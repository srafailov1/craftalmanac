### Chokecherry Syrup (No Pits)

`food-chokecherry-syrup` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Deep garnet syrup `#5a0f22`

> Chokecherries pucker the mouth raw but cook into a syrup the deep red of stained glass, long a staple across the northern plains. The one firm rule is the pit: like all wild cherries the stones hold amygdalin, which frees cyanide when crushed, so you juice gently and never blend or force the seeds. A food mill turns the fruit without cracking a single pit - a blender does the opposite, which is exactly what you must avoid.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `chokecherry`, `pin-cherry`, `black-cherry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Refrigerated 2-3 weeks or frozen for months. Shelf-stable only with an extension-tested canning process.
- **Timeline:** 1 hour active · Overnight strain passive
- **Yield:** About 2-3 cups of syrup from 4 cups fruit. Wild chokecherry is abundant and often overlooked.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe chokecherries (Prunus virginiana), stemmed** — 4 cups — Do not crush the pits at any stage. Pin cherry (P. pensylvanica) works the same way.
- **Water** — 3 cups
- **Sugar** — 3-4 cups — Chokecherry juice is very tart.
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 2 tbsp — Brightens and lowers pH.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large pot, Jelly bag or fine sieve, Food mill (optional)  ·  _Optional:_ Candy thermometer

**Steps**

1. Stem and rinse the cherries. Do not blend, mash hard, or force fruit through anything that could crack the pits.
2. Simmer whole cherries with water 20-25 minutes until soft, then rest 10 minutes.
3. Ladle into a jelly bag and let the juice drip through on its own - do not squeeze so hard you break seeds. Discard the pits and pulp.
4. Return the strained juice to the pot with sugar and lemon; simmer 8-12 minutes to a light syrup.
5. Cool and refrigerate. Serve over pancakes, in soda, or diluted as a drink.

**Variations & modifiers.** Add cinnamon or a vanilla bean to the simmer. Increase sugar and pectin for chokecherry jelly instead of syrup. The same pit-safe method works for pin cherry and wild black cherry.

**Preservation / curing.** Refrigerate 2-3 weeks; freeze for months. For canning, follow UMN Extension's tested chokecherry syrup/jelly recipe and process in a water bath.

**Beyond the recipe.** Once the syrup is made, the pulp and pits go to compost - never re-used, because of the seed toxin. The syrup itself glazes meats and sweetens the sumac-ade or berry shrub sodas in this collection.

**Safety**

- Cyanogenic pits: never crush, blend, or grind chokecherry, pin cherry, plum, or wild-cherry stones. Juice gently and discard whole.
- Cook the fruit; do not eat raw pits or bitter kernels.
- For shelf-stable canning use a tested extension recipe and correct water-bath time.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

**Sources**

- [Chokecherry jelly and syrup made from wild fruit - UMN Extension](https://extension.umn.edu/preserving-and-preparing/chokecherry-jelly-made-wild-fruit)
- [Classic Chokecherry Syrup - Forager Chef](https://foragerchef.com/chokecherry-syrup/)
- [Chokecherry Syrup - Practical Self Reliance](https://practicalselfreliance.com/chokecherry-syrup/)

### Cold-Steep Sumac-ade

`food-sumac-ade` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Rosy tart infusion `#c8506a`

> The upright crimson cones of staghorn sumac are furred with malic acid, and a rain the night before can wash the tartness away, so pick after a dry spell. Steep the berries in cold water - never hot, which drags out bitter tannins - and in a few hours you have a drink the pink of watermelon rind and just as tart. The rule that keeps you safe is simple: red cones held upright are edible Rhus; drooping white berries are poison sumac, and they grow in swamps, not the dry roadsides true sumac loves.

- **Maps to:** `sumac`
- **Keeping / durability:** _fugitive_ — Drink within 2-3 days refrigerated; a fresh cold infusion, not a preserve.
- **Timeline:** 15 minutes active · 2-4 hour steep passive
- **Yield:** About 8 cups of sumac-ade from 4-6 cones. Wildly abundant along dry roadsides and field edges.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe staghorn or smooth sumac cones (Rhus typhina/glabra)** — 4-6 cones — Deep red, upright, fuzzy. Taste a berry - it should be sour. Pick after several dry days.
- **Cold water** — 8 cups — Cold or room temperature only.
- _(optional)_ **Honey or sugar** — to taste — Balances the tartness.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large jar or pitcher, Fine cloth or coffee filter  ·  _Optional:_ Muddler

**Steps**

1. Confirm identification: RED upright cones with fuzzy stems = edible Rhus. Avoid white, drooping berries in wet ground - that is poison sumac.
2. Break the cones apart into a jar and cover with cold water. Gently crush to release the acid.
3. Steep 2-4 hours in the fridge or on the counter, tasting until it is pleasantly tart.
4. Strain through a fine cloth or coffee filter - twice if needed - to remove all the fine hairs and seeds.
5. Sweeten to taste and serve over ice.

**Variations & modifiers.** Add mint or a strip of ginger to the steep. Reduce a sweetened batch to a tart syrup for sodas, or freeze into a sorbet base. Ground dried cones become the classic za'atar spice.

**Preservation / curing.** Fresh only - refrigerate and drink within 2-3 days. For longer keeping, cook into a sweetened syrup and refrigerate up to two weeks.

**Beyond the recipe.** Dry and grind the strained cones for sumac spice - the same berries season fish, salads, and hummus. Sumac is an abundant native, so a light harvest leaves plenty for wildlife.

**Safety**

- Identify with certainty - poison sumac (Toxicodendron vernix) has white drooping berries and grows in wetlands; edible Rhus has red upright cones in dry ground.
- Always strain through fine cloth to remove irritating fine hairs and seeds.
- Sumac is related to mango and cashew - those with anacardiaceae allergies should be cautious.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

**Sources**

- [Staghorn Sumac Lemonade (cold-steep method) - Forager Chef](https://foragerchef.com/staghorn-sumac-lemonade/)
- [Staghorn Sumac: Identification, Uses, and Benefits - Practical Self Reliance](https://practicalselfreliance.com/staghorn-sumac/)
- [Wild Edibles: Yes, There Is a Sumac Berry You Can Eat - Farmers' Almanac](https://www.farmersalmanac.com/wild-edibles-sumac-berries)

**Reviewer check:** ✅ sources ✅ safety ✅ technique

- _Flags:_
  - Factual error in 'modifiers': ground sumac is an INGREDIENT in za'atar, not 'the classic za'atar spice' itself; commercial culinary sumac is also usually Rhus coriaria, not typhina/glabra (a fine substitute, but the claim as written is wrong).
  - Ratio is weak versus the cited key source: Forager Chef recommends ~1-1.5 clusters per pint (8-12 clusters per 8 cups) steeped overnight; the card's 4-6 cones per 8 cups with a 2-4 hour steep will often yield a thin drink, though the 'taste until pleasantly tart' step partially mitigates.
  - Card twice encourages harvesting from 'dry roadsides' with no caution about roadside contamination — vehicle exhaust residue and highway-department herbicide spraying are the standard foraging cautions for exactly this habitat, and the surface-acid coating that makes sumac work cannot be washed off before use.
  - 'Taste a berry' appears in the ingredient note before the ID-confirmation step in the instructions; taste-testing should be explicitly conditioned on first confirming red/upright/fuzzy Rhus (low real-world risk since poison sumac is white and drooping, but ordering matters in safety copy).
  - Sources are all foraging blogs (credible and accurate here, but no .edu/extension anchor); a university extension page on staghorn sumac would strengthen the citation set.

- _Fixes to apply before adding:_
  - Reword modifiers/beyondInk: 'Dry and grind the strained cones into sumac spice — a key ingredient in za'atar blends' (and note to sift out the hard seeds after grinding).

  - Add a safety bullet: 'Avoid plants along busy roads or mowed/sprayed rights-of-way — the tart coating you steep cannot be washed before use, so pick from clean field edges.' Also soften the two 'roadsides' mentions in hook/yield to 'field edges and clearings'.
  - Move the taste-test into the note as: 'After confirming red, upright, fuzzy cones, taste one berry — it should be sour.'
  - Optionally add a university extension citation on Rhus typhina/glabra identification (e.g., a state .edu extension foraging or plant-ID page) alongside the blog sources.

### Morel Cream Saute

`food-morel-cream` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Golden pan cream `#c9a15a`

> Morels are the one wild mushroom on this map, and spring's most sought-after: hollow, honeycombed caps that a good butter saute turns nutty and meaty. Two rules keep them safe. Cook them thoroughly - even true morels are mildly toxic raw and cause GI upset. And know the deadly imposter: false morels (Gyromitra) are chambered and cottony inside, not hollow, and carry a rocket-fuel toxin that cooking does not reliably remove.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `morel`
- **Keeping / durability:** _fugitive_ — Cook and eat within a day or two of foraging; fresh morels are highly perishable. The finished dish keeps a couple days refrigerated.
- **Timeline:** 30 minutes active · Optional soak passive
- **Yield:** Serves 2-3 as a side. Morels fruit briefly and unpredictably in spring.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Fresh true morels (Morchella), halved lengthwise** — 1/2 lb — Cut in half to confirm a single hollow chamber and to check for insects. Discard any that are solid/cottony inside.
- **Butter** — 3 tbsp
- _(optional)_ **Shallot or ramp, minced** — 1 — Another spring forage pairing.
- **Heavy cream** — 1/2 cup
- _(optional)_ **Salt, pepper, fresh thyme** — to taste

**Tools** — _Required:_ Knife, Skillet, Colander  ·  _Optional:_ Salt-water soak bowl

**Steps**

1. Halve every morel lengthwise and inspect: true morels are completely hollow. If it is chambered or cottony inside, discard it - that is a false morel.
2. Rinse briefly or soak in lightly salted water to flush grit and insects from the honeycomb; drain and pat dry.
3. Melt butter and saute the morels over medium heat until browned and fully cooked through - 8-10 minutes. Never serve them rare.
4. Add shallot, then cream and thyme; simmer a few minutes to a glossy sauce.
5. Season and serve over toast, pasta, or steak. Try a small portion the first time you eat morels - some people react even to well-cooked ones.

**Variations & modifiers.** Deglaze with a splash of the wild-grape verjus from this set instead of wine. Finish with parsley; add a little sherry for depth.

**Preservation / curing.** Best fresh. Cooked morels keep 2-3 days refrigerated. To store longer, saute and freeze, or dry raw morels (rehydrate and cook fully before eating).

**Beyond the recipe.** Strained mushroom-soak water can be reduced (carefully, after cooking) into a stock. Pair with the ramps and other spring forages the map may show nearby.

**Safety**

- Never eat morels raw or undercooked - even true morels are mildly toxic raw and can cause GI upset. Cook thoroughly.
- Deadly lookalike: false morels (Gyromitra) contain gyromitrin/MMH; they are chambered and cottony inside, not hollow. When in doubt, throw it out.
- Do not combine morels with alcohol until you know your tolerance; some people react. Eat a small amount the first time.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access and positive ID before collecting.

**Sources**

- [False Morel Poisoning - WebMD](https://www.webmd.com/first-aid/false-morels-poisoning)
- [On Cooking False Morels / Gyromitra - Forager Chef](https://foragerchef.com/on-cooking-false-morels-gyromitra/)
- [Gyromitra esculenta (false morel) - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyromitra_esculenta)

**Reviewer check:** ✅ sources ✅ safety ✅ technique

- _Flags:_
  - Sourcing is acceptable but could be stronger: WebMD is a consumer-health page, and the Forager Chef article actually argues that some Gyromitra species (G. caroliniana, brunnea, korfii) are low-toxin and edible to experienced foragers — it does NOT give a blanket 'never eat false morels' message, so citing it to support 'when in doubt throw it out' is a slight mismatch. Consider adding a .edu/extension or NAMA source (e.g., Michigan State/Minnesota DNR morel guides, North American Mycological Association) as the primary authority.
  - ID completeness: the hollow-vs-chambered cut test is the correct primary discriminator and is stated well, but it should be paired explicitly with the whole-cap gestalt (pitted honeycomb ridges fused directly to the stem, symmetric) — the cut test alone is necessary but not sufficient. The hook mentions honeycomb caps, so this is minor.
  - Nuance (not a safety defect): the card treats all Gyromitra as carrying the 'rocket-fuel toxin.' Gyromitrin content varies dramatically by species, but the card's conservative 'not hollow = discard' rule is the correct and safest guidance for a general audience, so this is fine to leave as-is.
  - The soak-water-into-stock suggestion is safe as written because it directs cooking first, but it is a low-value/edge suggestion for a toxin-bearing raw mushroom; could be dropped to avoid any impression of using raw-morel liquid.
- _Fixes to apply before adding:_
  - Add or swap in an authoritative source: North American Mycological Association, or a university extension/.edu morel-vs-false-morel guide, as the lead citation; keep WebMD/Wikipedia only as secondary.
  - In the ID step, add the full true-morel gestalt alongside the hollow test: caps with pitted/honeycombed ridges attached directly and continuously to the stem, roughly symmetric — reject wavy/brain-like or wrinkled 'saddle' caps even if they seem hollow (also excludes Verpa, whose cap hangs free and whose stem is cottony-stuffed).
  - Optionally soften the Forager Chef citation context or note that species-level toxicity varies but the safe rule for foragers is to discard anything not clearly a hollow true morel.

### Pawpaw Custard Ice Cream

`food-pawpaw-icecream` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Pale gold custard `#e7d488`

> Pawpaw is the largest fruit native to North America and tastes like a banana crossed with mango and custard - which is why it never travels: the ripe pulp browns and ferments within days of falling. That short window is the whole reason to eat it fresh, folded raw into a cooked custard base so its perfume survives. Peel and seed carefully; the skin and the big brown seeds are not eaten, and some people react to the skin.

- **Maps to:** `pawpaw`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Ice cream keeps frozen for weeks; the raw pulp itself must be used within a day or two of ripeness or frozen immediately.
- **Timeline:** 1 hour active · Chill + churn several hours passive
- **Yield:** About 1 quart of ice cream. Pawpaw fruits heavily but ripens all at once and briefly.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe pawpaw pulp (Asimina triloba), peeled and seeded** — 1.5 cups — Fully soft, fragrant fruit only. Discard skin and the large brown seeds - not eaten.
- **Egg yolks** — 5 — For the cooked custard base.
- **Whole milk** — 1.5 cups
- **Heavy cream** — 1.5 cups
- **Sugar** — 3/4 cup
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 1 tbsp — Slows browning of the pulp.
- _(optional)_ **Vanilla** — 1 tsp

**Tools** — _Required:_ Saucepan, Whisk, Fine sieve, Ice cream maker  ·  _Optional:_ Blender, Double boiler

**Steps**

1. Slice ripe pawpaws into disks, then lift the skin and pop out the large seeds; keep only the soft pulp. Puree with the lemon juice.
2. Make a custard: whisk yolks and sugar, temper in hot milk, and cook gently over low heat, stirring, until it coats the back of a spoon (do not boil).
3. Strain the custard, cool it fully, then whisk in the cream, vanilla, and pawpaw puree.
4. Chill the base thoroughly, then churn in an ice cream maker.
5. Freeze until firm and serve within a few weeks for best flavor.

**Variations & modifiers.** Skip the eggs for a quick Philadelphia-style base (pulp, cream, sugar) if you want to keep the fruit's flavor front and center. A spoon of the elderberry or chokecherry syrup from this set makes a fine ripple.

**Preservation / curing.** Keep frozen; texture is best within a few weeks. Freeze extra raw pulp in bags immediately - it does not keep fresh past a couple of days.

**Beyond the recipe.** Extra pulp freezes for pawpaw bread or the persimmon-style pudding. Seeds and skin are discarded (skin can irritate; seeds are not eaten).

**Safety**

- Do not eat the skin or seeds - only the ripe pulp. Some people get GI upset or skin reaction from the skin.
- Use fully ripe fruit; hard unripe pawpaw is unpleasant and less digestible.
- Standard cooked-custard care: cook gently, cool promptly, keep cold.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

**Sources**

- [Recipes and Uses (Pawpaw) - Kentucky State University](https://www.kysu.edu/academics/college-ahnr/school-of-anr/pawpaw/recipes-and-uses.php)
- [Pawpaw Ice Cream - Purdue Extension FoodLink](https://extension.purdue.edu/foodlink/recipe.php?recipe=Pawpaw+Ice+Cream)
- [Paw Paw Ice Cream - Hunter Angler Gardener Cook](https://honest-food.net/paw-paw-ice-cream/)

**Reviewer check:** ✅ sources ✅ safety ✅ technique

- _Flags:_
  - Purdue FoodLink source is real but is a fold-pulp-into-commercial-ice-cream shortcut, not a custard recipe; it only weakly supports the card's method (KSU and honest-food.net carry the method).
  - Safety bullet attributes reactions solely to the skin; a documented minority of people get GI upset/nausea from peeled ripe pulp itself (noted in KSU pawpaw literature) - card should tell first-time eaters to start with a small amount.
  - Hook states pulp 'browns and ferments within days' but safety list never says to discard fermented or off-smelling pulp - an obvious follow-through gap.
  - Custard step gives only the coats-the-spoon cue with no temperature target; 170-180 F (77-82 C) would anchor both egg safety and curdle avoidance.
- _Fixes to apply before adding:_
  - Broaden safety bullet 1 to: skin and seeds are never eaten, and some people react to the ripe pulp itself with GI upset - try a small amount first if new to pawpaw.
  - Add a safety line: use fruit that smells sweet, not boozy or sour - discard pulp that has begun to ferment or turned deep brown/off.
  - In step 2, add a temperature target: cook to 170-180 F (77-82 C), until it coats the back of a spoon; do not boil.
  - Optionally note in sources that the Purdue entry documents a simpler no-churn variant, or swap it for another .edu custard-method source (e.g., an Ohio State or KSU pawpaw publication) so all three back the stated method.

### Wild Berry Drinking Shrub

`food-berry-shrub` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Jewel-toned drinking vinegar `#6b1533`

> A shrub is what colonists made when they had more berries than they could eat and no refrigerator: fruit, sugar, and vinegar in rough balance, coaxed into a syrup that keeps for months and tastes electric in soda water. The acid is the whole point - it preserves the fruit and gives the drink a bright, mouth-watering snap. Reach for the invasives first: wineberry and Himalayan blackberry are aggressive spreaders, and picking them hard is a small act of restoration.

- **Maps to:** `blackberry`, `raspberry`, `wineberry`, `himalayan-blackberry`, `blueberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — The vinegar and sugar make it self-preserving; refrigerated it keeps 3-6 months and improves for the first weeks.
- **Timeline:** 20 minutes active · 1-7 day maceration passive
- **Yield:** About 2-3 cups of shrub syrup from 2 cups berries - dozens of drinks.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe wild berries (blackberry, raspberry, wineberry, blueberry)** — 2 cups — Favor invasive wineberry and Himalayan blackberry. Slightly overripe fruit is fine.
- **Sugar** — 1-2 cups — Equal-to-fruit for the classic ratio; less for a sharper shrub.
- **Vinegar (apple cider, red wine, or a blend)** — 1-2 cups — Around 5% acidity.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Jar with lid, Fine strainer, Bottle  ·  _Optional:_ Muddler, Kitchen scale

**Steps**

1. Combine berries and sugar in a jar, mash lightly, cover, and refrigerate 1-2 days until the sugar draws out a syrup.
2. Strain out the solids, pressing gently; reserve the syrup.
3. Stir the vinegar into the syrup to taste - start near equal parts and adjust.
4. Rest in the fridge a few days to a week to let the sharp vinegar edge mellow and marry.
5. Bottle. To serve, mix about 1 part shrub with 6-8 parts sparkling water over ice.

**Variations & modifiers.** Cold-process (above) keeps flavors fresh; a hot-process version simmers fruit and vinegar for a deeper, cooked note. Add thyme, basil, or black pepper to the maceration. Any mapped berry works - try huckleberry, thimbleberry, or serviceberry.

**Preservation / curing.** Refrigerated 3-6 months thanks to the vinegar. Watch for mold on the surface or off smells; discard if either appears.

**Beyond the recipe.** The strained macerated berries are excellent spooned over the pawpaw ice cream or stirred into yogurt. Shrub also makes a bright vinaigrette base or a cocktail mixer.

**Safety**

- Use fresh, food-grade vinegar at about 5% acidity for reliable preservation.
- Wash foraged berries; avoid roadside or sprayed patches.
- Refrigerate and discard at any sign of mold or fermentation gone wrong.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

**Sources**

- [Blackberry-Raspberry Shrub (drinking vinegar) - Blossom to Stem](https://www.blossomtostem.net/blackberry-raspberry-shrub-drinking-vinegar-fans-sour/)
- [Black Raspberry Shrub - Food in Jars](https://foodinjars.com/recipe/drink-week-day-four-black-raspberry-shrub/)
- [How to Make Shrubs, or Drinking Vinegar - Homestead Honey](https://homestead-honey.com/shrubs-or-drinking-vinegar-recipe/)

- _Flags:_
  - All three sources are real, reachable, and match the card's ratios/method, but all are hobby food blogs — no authoritative (.edu/extension) anchor for the preservation claims.
  - Safety list omits a positive-identification reminder; Rubus brambles are unmistakable, but wild blueberries/huckleberries have inedible lookalikes for beginners.
  - The 'preservation' claim of 3-6 months refrigerated is at the optimistic end of the cited blogs (Blossom to Stem says 'at least a month'), though Ask Extension independently supports up to 6 months refrigerated, and the card includes discard-on-mold guidance, so risk is low.

### Cold-Steeped Sumac 'Lemonade'

`food-sumac-drupe-rinse` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Rosy pink infusion `#C24A5B`

> The tartness on staghorn sumac's fuzzy red cones isn't inside the seed — it's a dust of malic and ascorbic acid on the outside of the drupes, which is why you steep them in cold water, never hot. Hot water drags bitter tannin out of the seeds; cold water lifts only the bright, lemony acid. It's the whole-use lesson in one glass: use the surface, leave the seed, waste nothing.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

> 🔁 **Dedup:** Near-duplicate of **Cold-Steep Sumac-ade** (`food-sumac-ade`). Merge into one card, or keep as an explicit variant.

- **Maps to:** `sumac`
- **Keeping / durability:** _fugitive_ — Drink fresh — the infusion is best within a day or two refrigerated; it's a fresh beverage, not a preserve.
- **Timeline:** 10 minutes active · 2-6 hours cold steeping passive
- **Yield:** About 1 quart of tart pink 'lemonade' per batch. Expect a bright, citrus-like drink — cloudier and more rustic than lemonade, with a rosy blush.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe staghorn/smooth sumac drupe clusters (true Rhus)** — 3-4 clusters per quart of water — Must be upright, dense, deep-red, velvety cones. NOT poison sumac (Toxicodendron vernix), which has drooping white/green berries in wet ground.
- **Cold non-chlorinated water** — 1 quart — Cold only — hot water extracts bitter seed tannins.
- _(optional)_ **Honey or sugar** — to taste — Sumac is intensely tart on its own.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large jar or bowl, Fine strainer, Coffee filter or muslin  ·  _Optional:_ Wooden spoon to bruise clusters, Ice

**Steps**

1. Identify carefully: harvest only upright, dense, red, velvety-hairy cones of true Rhus sumac. Poison sumac has smooth stems and drooping white berries and grows in wet swampy ground — if in any doubt, don't pick.
2. Choose clusters that haven't been rained on recently (rain washes off the surface acid). Do NOT wash the clusters — the flavor lives on the outside.
3. Break the clusters into a jar of cold water. Gently bruise or rub them to release the acid; more rubbing gives more tartness (and more tannin).
4. Cover and steep in the refrigerator 2-6 hours — cold only. Taste as it goes; pull it when it's pleasantly tart.
5. Strain first through a coarse sieve, then through a coffee filter or muslin to catch the fine irritating hairs and seed debris.
6. Sweeten to taste and serve cold over ice. Drink within a couple of days.

**Variations & modifiers.** Longer steeping and more bruising intensify tartness and pull more astringency; back off for a softer drink. A sprig of mint, lavender, or a little rose-hip syrup rounds it out. Concentrate a strong batch as a 'sumac shrub' base by adding a splash of the apple-scrap vinegar from the other card.

**Preservation / curing.** A fresh beverage — refrigerate and drink within 1-2 days; it does not keep. To store the flavor, dry whole clusters and grind the drupe surface into ground sumac spice (sieve out seeds and hairs), which keeps for months in a jar.

**Beyond the recipe.** Strained drupes and seeds compost. Dried clusters become ground sumac spice (a za'atar staple); the same true-Rhus tannin also makes a natural dye and, historically, a tanning agent — a bridge to the ink/dye cards.

**Safety**

- Safety first — identification: never confuse edible red-coned true Rhus sumac with poison sumac (Toxicodendron vernix), which has drooping white berries and causes severe rashes. When unsure, do not harvest.
- Always filter through fine cloth or a coffee filter to remove the tiny irritating hairs on the drupes.
- Steep cold, not hot, and drink in moderation — sumac is high in tannins, which are astringent and can upset some stomachs.
- Occurrence is not permission: a mapped sumac stand isn't harvest consent; sumac spreads readily, so light picking leaves plenty for wildlife.

### Cooked Elderberry Cordial

`food-elderberry-cordial` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Deep ruby-black cordial `#3b0a1e`

> The dark umbels of Sambucus canadensis carry a hard rule baked in: cook them, always. Raw berries and every green part hold cyanogenic glycosides that release cyanide during digestion, and hospital-documented cases trace back to raw juice. Ten minutes at a simmer breaks those compounds down and leaves a cordial the color of a garnet held to candlelight.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

> 🔁 **Dedup:** Related to **Cooked Elderberry Syrup** (`food-elderberry-syrup-cooked`). Distinct (fresh drink vs. keeping syrup) — keep both, cross-link.

- **Maps to:** `elderberry`, `red-elderberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Refrigerated cordial keeps 2-4 weeks; freeze in portions for months. Not shelf-stable without a tested canning recipe.
- **Timeline:** 45 minutes active · Overnight cool passive
- **Yield:** About 3-4 cups cordial from 4 cups berries. Expect real shrinkage; wild umbels are seedy.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe blue elderberries (Sambucus canadensis/nigra), destemmed** — 4 cups — Only fully ripe dark-blue/black berries. Never red elderberry unless deseeded and cooked - it is more toxic.
- **Water** — 2 cups
- **Sugar** — 1.5-2 cups — Adjust after juicing.
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 2 tbsp — Brightens flavor, lowers pH.
- _(optional)_ **Ginger, cinnamon, cloves** — to taste — Traditional warming spices.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large pot, Fine strainer or jelly bag, Fork or berry comb  ·  _Optional:_ Food mill, Immersion blender

**Steps**

1. Comb every berry off the green stems - stems and unripe fruit are toxic. Discard red, green, or unripe berries.
2. Simmer berries with water 15-20 minutes until fully collapsed. This cooking step is the safety step: it destroys cyanogenic glycosides.
3. Mash and strain through a jelly bag or fine sieve; discard solids.
4. Return juice to the pot, add sugar and lemon, simmer 5-10 minutes to dissolve and thicken slightly.
5. Cool, bottle, and refrigerate. Dilute to taste with sparkling or still water.

**Variations & modifiers.** Add a cinnamon stick and star anise for a mulled winter cordial; a splash extracts beautifully into hot water as a tea base. For red elderberry (Sambucus racemosa), you must both deseed and cook - treat it as a separate, more cautious project.

**Preservation / curing.** Refrigerate 2-4 weeks; freeze in jars or cubes for months. For shelf-stable syrup use an NCHFP-tested elderberry recipe and process accordingly - do not water-bath an untested formula.

**Beyond the recipe.** Strained pulp can be simmered again for a weaker second-run tea, then composted. The cordial base doubles as a topping for the cooked pawpaw ice cream or persimmon pudding in this collection.

**Safety**

- Cook thoroughly - raw elderberries, juice, leaves, stems, and unripe berries contain cyanogenic glycosides that can cause nausea, vomiting, and cramping.
- Destem completely; never include green plant parts.
- Do not substitute red elderberry casually - it is more toxic and requires deseeding plus cooking.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm you may legally collect where you forage.

### Mesquite Pod Atole

`food-mesquite-atole` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Cinnamon-tan porridge drink `#b58a5c`

> Mesquite pods are naturally sweet - the sugar is in the pod, not a seed - and desert peoples have milled them into flour for thousands of years, whisked warm into atole. The pods must be bone-dry: research from the University of Arizona tied Aspergillus mold and its carcinogenic aflatoxin directly to rain, so you harvest before the summer monsoons and never wet or wash the pods. Snap one in half; it should crack crisply, with no dark streaks or musty smell.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mesquite-bean`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Dry-milled mesquite flour is shelf-stable for months in a sealed jar; the finished atole is drunk fresh.
- **Timeline:** 1 hour active · Days of drying + milling passive
- **Yield:** About 5 gallons of pods mills down to roughly 1 gallon (5 lb) of flour; plan on hours of work.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Fully dry mesquite pods (Prosopis species), snap-crisp** — 1 lb pods — Harvest before summer rains. Never wash or wet. Discard any pods that are damp, dark-streaked, or musty (aflatoxin risk).
- **Water or milk** — 4 cups
- _(optional)_ **Cinnamon** — 1 stick — Traditional.
- _(optional)_ **Honey or piloncillo** — to taste — Mesquite is already sweet; add sparingly.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Grain mill or heavy-duty blender, Fine sieve, Whisk, Saucepan  ·  _Optional:_ Dehydrator or warm oven

**Steps**

1. Harvest and dry only sound, snap-crisp pods before the monsoon. Break one: it should shatter dryly with no mustiness. Dry further in a warm oven/dehydrator if at all bendy.
2. Break pods into pieces and mill to a coarse meal; sift out the hard seeds and woody fiber, re-milling the pulp for a finer flour.
3. Whisk 1/2-3/4 cup mesquite flour into cold water or milk until smooth to prevent lumps.
4. Heat gently, stirring constantly, and simmer 5-10 minutes to a thin, drinkable porridge; add cinnamon.
5. Sweeten lightly and drink warm.

**Variations & modifiers.** Blend with masa or oat flour for a heartier atole. Toast the meal lightly before whisking for a deeper flavor. The same flour sweetens pancakes and quick breads.

**Preservation / curing.** Store the dry flour in an airtight jar, cool and dark, for months (freeze for longer). Keep it dry - moisture invites the very mold you screened for. Atole is served fresh.

**Beyond the recipe.** Sifted-out seeds and fiber compost. Mesquite flour is a pantry staple beyond this drink - breads, cookies, and a natural sweetener.

**Safety**

- Aflatoxin: harvest before summer rains; never wash or wet pods; discard any damp, dark-streaked, or musty pods and dry thoroughly before milling.
- Do not crush the hard seeds into the flour - sift them out; the sweet edible part is the pod pulp.
- Milling tough pods can damage weak blenders - use a grain mill and eye protection.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

**Ingredients / materials**

`food-grape-verjus` — **Viability 3/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Pale green tart juice `#c4cf8a`

> Before lemons were cheap, cooks reached for verjus - the sour juice of unripe grapes - to brighten a sauce without the sledgehammer of vinegar. Wild grapes hang in reach across much of the country, and you catch them just as a few berries begin to blush, when the acid is high and the sugar barely started. One caution travels with wild grape: learn to tell it from the toxic lookalike Canada moonseed, whose single crescent seed and un-branched tendrils give it away.

- **Maps to:** `grape`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Refrigerated 2-3 weeks fresh; up to about 6 months with a little citric acid added. Freezes well.
- **Timeline:** 45 minutes active · Overnight settle passive
- **Yield:** About 2-3 quarts from 3-4 lb of grapes. Wild grape is abundant and vigorous.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Unripe wild grapes (Vitis species), stemmed** — 3-4 lb — Pick when hard and green, as the first few berries just start to color. Confirm true grape, not moonseed.
- _(optional)_ **Citric acid** — 1/2 tsp per pint — Extends refrigerated life to months.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Food mill or masher, Jelly bag or fine strainer, Bottle  ·  _Optional:_ Cheesecloth

**Steps**

1. Confirm identification: wild grapes have branched tendrils, several seeds per berry, and shreddy bark. Moonseed has a single flat crescent seed and no tendrils - discard anything uncertain.
2. Crush or run the green grapes through a food mill to release the juice.
3. Strain first through a coarse sieve, then through a jelly bag for a clean juice.
4. Let it settle overnight in the fridge, then pour off the clear juice from any sediment.
5. Bottle. Add 1/2 tsp citric acid per pint to keep it for months. Use anywhere you would use lemon juice.

**Variations & modifiers.** Use it to deglaze the morel cream saute, to dress salads, or to make a nonalcoholic spritz. Reduce with honey for a shrub-like syrup.

**Preservation / curing.** Refrigerate 2-3 weeks plain, or up to ~6 months with citric acid. Freeze in ice-cube trays for single-splash portions.

**Beyond the recipe.** Pressed skins and seeds compost. Verjus is a house-acid that ties this whole collection together - it replaces lemon in the sumac-ade, shrub, or morel pan.

**Safety**

- Distinguish wild grape from toxic Canada moonseed (Menispermum canadense): moonseed has one crescent seed, no tendrils, and is poisonous. When unsure, do not use.
- Green grapes are safe cooked or raw as juice; this is a condiment, not a beverage to chug.
- Standard clean-handling and cold storage.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

### Tray-frozen (IQF) wild berries

`food-iqf-frozen-berries` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Frost-dusted berry `#3b4a7a`

> The simplest, lowest-risk preservation there is: spread wild berries in a single layer, freeze them hard, then pour them loose into bags. Tray freezing (the home version of commercial IQF, 'individually quick frozen') keeps berries from clumping into a solid brick, so you can pour out a handful at a time all winter. No canner, no botulism math — just a freezer.

- **Maps to:** `blueberry`, `blackberry`, `raspberry`, `serviceberry`, `huckleberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Keeping-quality: frozen, not shelf-stable. Properly tray-frozen and well-wrapped berries hold good quality for roughly 8–12 months at 0°F, then slowly lose texture and color to freezer burn. Once thawed they're soft and best used in cooking or baking rather than eaten out of hand.
- **Timeline:** 15–30 min active · 2–4 hr to freeze solid passive
- **Yield:** Scales freely — a tray holds a few cups; freeze as many trays as your harvest and freezer allow.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild berries (blueberry, blackberry, raspberry, serviceberry, huckleberry)** — as much as you pick — sort out leaves, stems, and any bruised or unripe fruit

**Tools** — _Required:_ Shallow rimmed trays or baking sheets, Freezer at 0°F (-18°C), Freezer bags or rigid moisture-vapor-resistant containers, Parchment (optional liner)  ·  _Optional:_ Vacuum sealer, Permanent marker for dating, Colander (if rinsing)

**Steps**

1. Forage only where permitted; harvest a light share and confirm the identification of every berry — some wild berries are toxic look-alikes.
2. Sort and remove leaves, stems, and bruised or immature berries. Ideally don't wash delicate berries — wet skins toughen in the freezer. If you must rinse, dry them completely first.
3. Spread the berries in a single layer on shallow trays so they don't touch too much.
4. Freeze until solid, about 2–4 hours.
5. Promptly scoop the loose frozen berries into freezer bags or rigid containers, press out excess air, seal, and date.
6. Return to 0°F storage. Pour out what you need and reseal — they stay loose, not clumped.
7. Use from frozen in baking, sauces, and smoothies; thawed berries are soft and juicy, best cooked.

**Variations & modifiers.** Sugar pack or syrup pack (NCHFP methods) protect texture and color better for berries you'll eat thawed, at the cost of added sugar. Purée and freeze very soft or overripe berries instead. Vacuum sealing after tray-freezing extends quality life and cuts freezer burn.

**Preservation / curing.** Store at a steady 0°F; quality holds ~8–12 months. Keep air out (freezer burn is the main enemy) and date every bag. Don't refreeze fully thawed berries for quality — cook them into a jam, syrup, or sauce instead.

**Beyond the recipe.** Tray-frozen fruit is the feedstock for everything else in this set: pull a bag to make jam, syrup, leather, or smoothies later. Freezing is also the honest fallback when a canning recipe for a given fruit isn't tested — freeze it instead of guessing at a process.

**Safety**

- Identify every wild berry with certainty — some wild berries are toxic; when in doubt, leave it out.
- Freezing preserves but does not kill everything; keep berries clean, freeze promptly, and use good hygiene.
- Don't refreeze berries that have fully thawed to warm temperatures; cook them instead.
- Occurrence is not permission — forage only where legal and take a light share, leaving fruit for wildlife.

### Wild & beach plum jam

`food-plum-jam` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Deep magenta-red `#7c1f3a`

> Beach and wild plums (Prunus maritima and its cousins) are among the most rewarding foraged fruits for the canner: small, mouth-puckeringly tart, and reliably acidic enough for safe boiling-water processing. The NCHFP tested plum jam needs no commercial pectin — plum skins and slightly underripe fruit carry their own. Follow a tested recipe exactly and the pits stay whole and discarded, never crushed.

- **Maps to:** `plum`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Keeping-quality, not color: correctly water-bath-canned plum jam is shelf-stable for a year-plus unopened in a cool dark cupboard. Plums are naturally acidic (typically pH ~2.8–4.6), and the tested recipe's added lemon juice keeps it safely in the high-acid, water-bath-canner zone. Refrigerate after opening and use within weeks.
- **Timeline:** 60–90 min active · 12–24 hr to set/seal-check passive
- **Yield:** About 8 half-pint jars from 4 lb of fruit.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Chopped tart wild or beach plums (pitted, skins on)** — 2 quarts chopped (about 4 lb whole fruit) — use ~3/4 ripe, 1/4 slightly underripe for natural pectin; discard whole pits — never crush them
- **Sugar** — 6 cups — sugar level is part of the tested set and shelf stability — don't cut it freely
- **Water** — 1½ cups
- **Bottled lemon juice** — ¼ cup — adds guaranteed acidity plus pectin activation; use bottled for a known, consistent pH

**Tools** — _Required:_ Boiling-water canner or deep stockpot with rack, Half-pint canning jars, new lids, bands, Large heavy non-reactive pot, Jar lifter and canning funnel, Ladle and long spoon, Candy/jelly thermometer  ·  _Optional:_ Cherry/plum pitter, Potato masher, Kitchen scale, Timer

**Steps**

1. Forage plums only where harvesting is permitted — occurrence is not permission. Take a light share and leave fruit for wildlife.
2. Wash, halve, and pit the plums, keeping skins on. Discard the whole pits; do not crush or blend them (the kernels are cyanogenic). Chop to measure 2 quarts.
3. Sterilize half-pint jars; keep lids and jars hot.
4. Combine plums, sugar, water, and ¼ cup bottled lemon juice in the pot. Bring slowly to a boil, stirring until the sugar dissolves.
5. Boil rapidly, stirring constantly to prevent sticking, until it reaches the jellying point — 220°F at sea level, or 8°F above your local boiling point.
6. Ladle hot jam into hot sterile jars leaving ¼-inch headspace. Wipe rims with a clean damp cloth, set lids, and finger-tighten bands.
7. Process in a boiling-water canner: 5 minutes at 0–1,000 ft; 10 minutes at 1,001–6,000 ft; 15 minutes above 6,000 ft.
8. Remove jars, cool undisturbed 12–24 hr, and check that every lid sealed (no flex). Refrigerate any that didn't seal and use those first.

**Variations & modifiers.** Beach plums run smaller and tarter than inland wild plums but behave the same. A pouch or box of commercial pectin lets you shorten the boil and use the pectin-added ratios if your fruit is very ripe. A split vanilla bean or a cinnamon stick during the cook reads well against the tartness; a mix of wild plum species deepens the color.

**Preservation / curing.** Water-bath canning is the whole point: sealed jars keep 12+ months in a cool dark cupboard. Do not reduce the sugar or skip the lemon juice below the tested recipe — both underpin the safe acidity and shelf stability. Once opened, refrigerate and use within about 3 weeks. Discard any jar that unseals, leaks, spurts, or smells off.

**Beyond the recipe.** Pit-in wild plum 'jam' variants and thick plum butter use the same fruit; the strained skins and cooked pulp can go into fruit leather. Slightly underripe plums are the best natural-pectin source, so save a handful of green ones to help less-pectic fruit set.

**Safety**

- Plum, cherry, and apricot pits/kernels contain cyanogenic compounds — discard whole pits and never crush, grind, or blend them into the jam.
- This is a tested water-bath recipe because plums are high-acid; do not lower the sugar or omit the lemon juice, which keep it safely acidic.
- Occurrence is not permission — forage only where legal and take a light, sustainable share.
- Use the correct headspace and processing time for your altitude; discard any jar that fails to seal or shows signs of spoilage.

**Sources**

- [Plum Jam without Pectin — National Center for Home Food Preservation (UGA)](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/make-jam-jelly/jams/plum-jam-without-pectin/)
- [General Information on Canning Jams, Jellies, and Marmalades — NCHFP](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/make-jam-jelly/jams-jellies-general-information/general-information-on-canning-jams-jellies-and-marmalades/)

### Wild Grape & Crabapple Jelly

`food-wild-grape-crabapple-jelly` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Deep ruby jelly `#6e102a`

> Wild fox and riverbank grapes (Vitis) are too tart and seedy to eat by the handful, but that same tartness — pH near 3.2 — is exactly what jelly wants. They carry their own pectin, and a few underripe crabapples or hawthorn haws thrown in push the set over the line so you can skip the box pectin entirely. The catch every forager learns: green wild grape juice can crystallize into gritty tartrate crystals, so the juice needs an overnight rest in the cold before it becomes jelly.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `grape`, `apple`, `hawthorn`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Water-bath processed, sealed jars keep 12+ months in a cool dark pantry; refrigerate opened jars.
- **Timeline:** 2–3 hours over two days active · Overnight juice settling + drip; 12–24 hr seal check passive
- **Yield:** About 5–6 half-pints from ~3½ lb grapes. Wild grapes are seedy and low-yield per pound — plan on more fruit than a cultivated recipe calls for.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild grapes (Vitis), part slightly underripe** — ~3½ lb for ~5 cups juice — a quarter underripe grapes boosts natural pectin and acidity
- _(optional)_ **Crabapples or hawthorn haws (optional pectin boost)** — 1–2 cups, quartered — both are high-pectin; hawthorn haws are a mapped material and set beautifully
- **Granulated sugar** — ~¾ cup per cup of juice (per NCHFP no-pectin jelly) — follow the tested juice-to-sugar ratio for a reliable set
- _(optional)_ **Bottled lemon juice** — 2 Tbsp if fruit is fully ripe — adds acid and pectin help if you couldn't get underripe fruit

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large heavy pot, Jelly bag or cheesecloth-lined colander, Boiling-water canner + rack, Half-pint jars, lids, bands, Jar lifter, funnel, ladle  ·  _Optional:_ Candy thermometer (220°F / gel point at sea level), Food mill, Fine chinois, Second pot for refrigerating settled juice

**Steps**

1. Harvest grapes only where permitted; wild grape is important wildlife and pollinator habitat, so take lightly. NEVER confuse wild grape with Canada moonseed, whose single crescent seed and toxic berries mimic grape clusters — real grapes have 2–4 teardrop seeds and true tendrils.
2. Stem, wash, and lightly crush grapes (and crabapples/haws if using). Add just enough water to prevent scorching and simmer ~10 minutes until soft.
3. Pour into a jelly bag and let drip undisturbed — do not squeeze, or the jelly turns cloudy. Refrigerate the collected juice overnight so tartrate crystals settle out.
4. Carefully pour off the clear juice, leaving the gritty sediment behind. Measure the juice.
5. Sterilize jars; heat lids. In a wide pot, combine juice with sugar per the tested ratio (and lemon juice if needed).
6. Boil hard, stirring, until it reaches gel point (220°F at sea level, or a sheeting spoon test). Skim foam.
7. Fill hot jars to ¼-inch headspace, wipe rims, cap fingertip-tight, and process in the boiling-water bath 10 minutes (adjust for altitude).
8. Cool 12–24 hours undisturbed and check seals. A jelly that stays runny is still perfect as a syrup or glaze — no waste.

**Variations & modifiers.** Straight wild grape often sets soft; a quarter crabapple or a handful of hawthorn haws makes it dependable without commercial pectin. For a guaranteed set, switch to the NCHFP grape jelly recipe with powdered or liquid pectin. A little hawthorn adds a rosy, apple-blossom note; cornelian cherry can stand in for a tarter, brighter jelly.

**Preservation / curing.** Sealed and processed: 12+ months cool and dark. The overnight cold settle is what prevents the harmless-but-unpleasant crystalline tartrate grit that plagues wild grape jelly. Refrigerate after opening; discard any unsealed or off jar.

**Beyond the recipe.** The spent grape pulp still holds pigment — it's a mapped ink material too, and the leftover skins can start a wild grape shrub or vinegar. Tartrate crystals scraped from the settling jar are cream-of-tartar-adjacent, a fun byproduct to note.

**Safety**

- Grapes are high-acid and water-bath safe, but confirm identity: Canada moonseed (Menispermum) is toxic and mimics wild grape — check for grape's multiple teardrop seeds and forked tendrils.
- Use a tested juice-to-sugar ratio; unbalanced ratios can under-acidify or fail to seal.
- Do not squeeze the jelly bag if you want clarity, and always process in a true water bath — inversion sealing is not safe canning.
- Discard any jar that didn't seal or shows mold/off-smell; never taste-test suspect canned goods.
- Hawthorn haws are safe cooked flesh, but never crush or eat the seeds — like all pome/stone pits they are cyanogenic. Strain them out.

**Sources**

- [Crabapple Jelly without Pectin — NCHFP](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/make-jam-jelly/jellies/crabapple-jelly-without-pectin/)

### Wild apple butter & dried apple rings

`food-apple-butter` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Deep burnished brown `#6b3a1e`

> Wild apples and crab apples are usually too tart or small to eat out of hand, but they cook down into a dark, spiced apple butter that's better than anything from the store. It's a long, slow reduction — cider and vinegar, then hours of gentle cooking until a spoonful mounds and no liquid weeps at the edge. The peels and cores that come off go straight into a scrap batch of cider or jelly.

- **Maps to:** `apple`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Keeping-quality: NCHFP-tested apple butter is water-bath-canned and shelf-stable for a year-plus sealed in a cool dark cupboard. Apples plus the added vinegar keep it high-acid. It can also simply be frozen. Dried apple rings, dried until leathery, keep for months in an airtight container.
- **Timeline:** 1–2 hr active · hours of slow cooking; overnight for rings passive
- **Yield:** About 9–10 pints of apple butter from 8 lb apples; dried rings scale with what you slice.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild apples and/or crab apples** — 8 lb — tart, high-pectin fruit is ideal; wash and quarter, no need to peel for the butter
- **Apple cider** — 2 cups
- **Vinegar** — 2 cups — part of the tested recipe; also reinforces acidity
- **White sugar** — 2¼ cups
- **Packed brown sugar** — 2¼ cups
- _(optional)_ **Ground cinnamon** — 2 Tbsp
- _(optional)_ **Ground cloves** — 1 Tbsp

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large heavy non-reactive pot, Food mill, sieve, or colander, Boiling-water canner, Half-pint or pint jars, lids, bands, Ladle and funnel, Long spoon  ·  _Optional:_ Slow cooker (for the long reduction), Dehydrator or low oven (for rings), Immersion blender, Ascorbic acid or lemon water (to keep rings pale)

**Steps**

1. Forage apples where permitted. Wash and quarter (cores in is fine for the butter); no peeling needed since you'll sieve.
2. Cook the fruit slowly in the cider and vinegar until soft, then press through a food mill or sieve to make a smooth pulp; discard skins, cores, and seeds.
3. Return the pulp to the pot with both sugars and the spices. Cook slowly, stirring frequently to prevent sticking, until thick.
4. Test doneness: spoon a little onto a plate — when no rim of liquid separates around it (or it mounds on a spoon held from the steam for 2 minutes), it's ready.
5. Fill hot into sterile half-pint or pint jars, ¼-inch headspace. Wipe rims, set lids, finger-tighten bands.
6. Process in a boiling-water canner: 5 minutes for half-pints/pints at 0–1,000 ft (add altitude time as needed). Or skip canning and freeze the butter in containers.
7. For dried apple rings: peel/core and slice sound apples ~¼-inch, optionally dip in lemon or ascorbic-acid water to stay pale, then dry at ~135°F until leathery and pliable, and store airtight.

**Variations & modifiers.** Crab apples add tartness and extra pectin; a mix of wild varieties gives complex flavor. Swap in maple syrup for some sugar, or add allspice/nutmeg. Reduce further for a stiffer 'apple leather.' A slow cooker with the lid ajar makes the long reduction hands-off.

**Preservation / curing.** Two safe paths: water-bath-can the butter (¼-inch headspace, altitude-corrected times) for pantry storage a year-plus, or freeze it. Dried rings keep for months airtight and dark; condition them a week and check for moisture. Refrigerate opened jars and use within a few weeks.

**Beyond the recipe.** This is a whole-fruit, low-waste project: butter uses the pulp, rings use sound slices, and the peels/cores can be simmered into scrap-apple jelly or fermented into vinegar. Pomace after pressing can go to compost or animals.

**Safety**

- Follow the tested NCHFP ratios — the cider, vinegar, and sugar keep apple butter safely acidic for water-bath canning; don't cut the acid or sugar freely.

### Cooked Elderberry Syrup (Freezer & Fridge Keeping)

`food-elderberry-syrup-cooked` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Inky purple-black syrup `#1c0d24`

> Elderberry is the one wild fruit where the recipe is a safety instruction: raw berries, stems, and leaves carry cyanogenic glycosides, and people have been hospitalized from raw elderberry juice. Cooking is the fix — research shows processing drops those compounds sharply (up to 96% in cooked spreads and liqueurs). This syrup is built around a hard simmer and total stem removal. Blue elderberry (Sambucus canadensis/cerulea) is the workhorse; red elderberry is more toxic and must be both cooked AND deseeded.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

> 🔁 **Dedup:** Related to **Cooked Elderberry Cordial** (`food-elderberry-cordial`). Keep both, cross-link.

- **Maps to:** `elderberry`, `red-elderberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — This is a fridge/freezer syrup: ~2–3 weeks refrigerated, or freeze in portions for months. It is NOT a tested shelf-stable canning recipe.
- **Timeline:** 45–60 min active · Cooling; optional freezing passive
- **Yield:** About 2–3 cups syrup from 3–4 cups berries. Elderberries are small and seedy; a large harvest reduces to a modest yield.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe blue elderberries, fully stemmed** — 3–4 cups — green berries, stems, and leaves are toxic — use only dark-ripe fruit, no green
- **Water** — 3 cups
- **Honey or sugar** — ~1 cup, added after cooking — sweetener helps preserve and rounds the tannic edge; add off heat if using honey
- _(optional)_ **Fresh ginger, cinnamon, cloves** — To taste — traditional aromatics; optional
- _(optional)_ **Bottled lemon juice** — 1–2 Tbsp — brightens flavor; helps color

**Tools** — _Required:_ Pot, Fine sieve or jelly bag, Fork or berry comb for destemming, Clean jars or freezer containers, Gloves (elderberry stains)  ·  _Optional:_ Food mill (essential for red elderberry deseeding), Masher, Funnel

**Steps**

1. Harvest only fully ripe, dark berries where permitted. Comb every berry off the green stems and umbels — the green plant parts are the most toxic. Discard any green or underripe fruit.
2. For RED elderberry only: you must also remove the seeds. Cook then pass through a food mill; never use red elderberry seeds. Blue elderberry seeds are fine once cooked.
3. Put stemmed berries and water in a pot. Bring to a boil, then simmer hard at least 15–20 minutes — this is the step that reduces the cyanogenic compounds. Mash as it cooks.
4. Strain through a fine sieve or jelly bag, pressing to extract juice. Discard solids.
5. Return juice to the pot; simmer to reduce slightly. Remove from heat and stir in honey or sugar (adding honey off-heat preserves its character) and any lemon juice.
6. Cool, bottle in clean jars, and refrigerate — or freeze in portions/ice-cube trays for long keeping.
7. Label clearly 'cooked elderberry — refrigerate/freeze.' Never bottle raw elderberry juice, and never leave green material in the batch.

**Variations & modifiers.** Blue elderberry gives a mellow, deep syrup; red elderberry (deseeded) is sharper and less common. Add ginger/cinnamon for a spiced version, or reduce further with more sugar toward a pourable jam. For long shelf storage, freeze — do not attempt open-kettle canning of this syrup, as it isn't a tested water-bath recipe.

**Preservation / curing.** Refrigerate and use within ~2–3 weeks; freezing in small portions extends it for months with little quality loss. Dried and frozen elderberries also store well raw — but must still be cooked before eating. Discard if it ferments or smells off.

**Beyond the recipe.** Cooked elderberry is also a superb natural ink and textile dye (it appears on the ink map), so a craft batch and a food batch come from the same simmer — just keep food and craft vessels separate. The spent pulp composts or, if blue and cooked, sweetens into a fruit-leather base.

**Safety**

- MUST cook: raw elderberries, stems, leaves, and unripe fruit contain cyanogenic glycosides; raw juice has caused hospitalizations. Simmer 15–20+ minutes.
- Red elderberry is more toxic — cook AND remove the seeds via a food mill; never eat red elderberry seeds.
- Use only fully ripe, dark berries; discard all green berries and any green plant material.
- This is a refrigerator/freezer product, not a tested shelf-stable canning recipe — do not store at room temperature.
- Educational/culinary use only; elderberry syrup is not a medical treatment. Keep clearly labeled and out of reach of children.

**Sources**

### Cornelian cherry preserves & sharbat

`food-cornelian-cherry-preserves` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Bright ruby-red `#a51830`

> Cornelian cherry (Cornus mas) is a dogwood, not a true cherry — olive-shaped, brilliant red, and startlingly tart when ripe. Its flesh is among the most pectin-rich of any fruit, so a jam can set almost too fast, turning to a firm jelly if the syrup is overcooked. One pot gives you two things at once: a whole-fruit preserve (moraba) and a clear ruby sharbat syrup for cordials and soda.

- **Maps to:** `cornelian-cherry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Keeping-quality: high sugar plus the fruit's own acidity make cornelian cherry preserves and syrup shelf-stable for many months in sealed jars, longer refrigerated. Because it is an acidic, high-sugar preserve it water-bath cans like other jams; treat the syrup as a refrigerated or frozen product unless you follow a tested canning process.
- **Timeline:** 45–75 min active · overnight to macerate, plus seal-check passive
- **Yield:** Roughly 3–4 half-pints of preserves, or a preserve plus a couple cups of syrup, from 2 lb fruit.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ripe cornelian cherries (soft, deep red)** — 2 lb — must be fully ripe/soft — underripe fruit is mouth-drying and very sour; pits stay whole and are strained out
- **Sugar** — 3–4 cups (to taste and set) — the fruit is very tart; higher sugar also supports shelf stability
- **Water** — 2 cups — for extracting flesh from the large pits
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 1–2 Tbsp — brightens and reinforces acidity; the fruit is already acidic
- _(optional)_ **Ground cardamom** — ¼ tsp — traditional in Persian sharbat

**Tools** — _Required:_ Heavy non-reactive pot, Food mill, sieve, or jelly bag, Canning or storage jars with lids, Ladle and long spoon  ·  _Optional:_ Boiling-water canner (for shelf-stable jars), Candy thermometer, Bottles for syrup, Cheesecloth

**Steps**

1. Forage only where permitted; cornelian cherry is often a planted ornamental, so confirm you may harvest.
2. Wash the fruit and simmer with the water, covered, 20–40 minutes until the flesh softens and loosens from the pits.
3. Press through a food mill or sieve to separate the ruby pulp/juice from the large whole pits. Discard the pits.
4. For preserves: return pulp to the pot with sugar, bring to a boil, and cook — watching closely — just to a soft set. The pectin is very high, so stop early to avoid a hard jelly.
5. For sharbat syrup: simmer the strained juice with sugar (roughly equal volumes) a few minutes to a pourable syrup; add cardamom and a squeeze of lemon at the end.
6. Jar hot with ¼-inch headspace. Refrigerate, freeze, or — for shelf-stable jam — process in a boiling-water canner per the general NCHFP jam guidance for your altitude.
7. Dilute sharbat with cold water or soda to drink; use preserves on yogurt, cheese, or toast.

**Variations & modifiers.** Leave some fruit whole for a chunkier moraba-style preserve. Cook the syrup a touch longer and it sets into jelly — a feature, not a bug, if that's what you want. Blends beautifully with tart apple or quince (both high-pectin) to stretch a small harvest.

**Preservation / curing.** High sugar and acidity carry it: refrigerated syrup and preserves keep for months; frozen syrup keeps longer. For pantry storage, water-bath-can jars of the jam following tested jam headspace (¼ inch) and processing times. Treat homemade syrup as fridge/freezer unless canned to a tested process.

**Beyond the recipe.** Sharbat syrup becomes cordials, sodas, and cocktail mixers; the fruit is also traditionally dried, pickled, or fermented. The strained skins and pulp can be dried into a tart fruit leather. Traditional across Persian, Turkish, and Eastern European kitchens.

**Safety**

- Use fully ripe (soft, deep red) fruit; underripe cornelian cherries are intensely astringent, not dangerous but unpalatable.
- Strain out and discard the whole pits; do not crush them into the preserve.
- For shelf-stable jars, follow a tested boiling-water jam process (¼-inch headspace, altitude-correct times) — otherwise refrigerate or freeze.
- Occurrence is not permission — many cornelian cherries are ornamental plantings; confirm you may harvest.

**Sources**

- [General Information on Canning Jams, Jellies, and Marmalades — NCHFP (UGA)](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/make-jam-jelly/jams-jellies-general-information/general-information-on-canning-jams-jellies-and-marmalades/)

- [Cornus mas — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornus_mas)

### Cured & Frozen Black Walnuts (and Pinyon, Hickory)

`food-black-walnut-curing-storage` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Rich amber-brown kernels `#4a2c17`

> A fresh-fallen black walnut is a bomb of staining juglone wrapped in a rock-hard shell — and the nutmeat inside is bland and moist until it cures. Two to three weeks of airflow condenses the flavor into the intense, wine-and-earth taste black walnut is prized for. The other truth every gatherer learns: those oily kernels go rancid fast at room temperature, so once shelled they belong in the freezer, where they keep almost indefinitely. The same curing-then-freezing logic saves hickory, pinyon, hazelnut, and chinquapin.

- **Maps to:** `black-walnut`, `hickory`, `pinyon-pine-nut`, `hazelnut`, `chinquapin`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — In-shell cured nuts keep months in a cool dry place; shelled nutmeats keep almost indefinitely frozen. At room temperature the oils go rancid within weeks to a couple months.
- **Timeline:** 2–4 hours (hulling, shelling) active · 2–6 weeks curing passive
- **Yield:** Black walnut and hickory have low meat-to-shell ratios — expect a modest cup of meats from a large bucket of nuts, and real effort cracking. Hazelnut and chinquapin crack far easier for their yield.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Fallen black walnuts in the hull (Juglans nigra)** — As many as you'll process promptly — hull immediately — juice from the hull discolors and off-flavors the meat
- _(optional)_ **Hickory, pinyon, hazelnut, or chinquapin nuts** — Any amount — same cure-then-freeze approach; pinyon and hazelnut are especially oil-rich and rancid-prone

**Tools** — _Required:_ Gloves (juglone stains skin for weeks), Hulling method (foot/board, or drive over on a tarp), Bucket of water for washing, Mesh bags or crates for curing, Heavy nutcracker or vise, Freezer bags/containers  ·  _Optional:_ Dedicated non-food surfaces (walnut stains permanently), Nut pick, Baking sheet (light toast before freezing hickory/pinyon), Vacuum sealer

**Steps**

1. Gather fallen nuts where permitted — walnut and pinyon are heavily used wildlife food, so leave a generous share and take lightly.
2. Hull black walnuts immediately, gloved: leaving the hull on stains the meat and makes it strong/off-tasting. Wash the hulled nuts to remove hull debris and juice. (Save hulls — they're a mapped ink material.)
3. Float-test: discard any nuts that float (often empty or spoiled).
4. Cure the washed in-shell nuts 2–6 weeks in mesh bags or crates somewhere dry with good air circulation, stirring occasionally. Curing dries the meat and deepens flavor.
5. Crack and pick out the nutmeats (black walnut and hickory shells are famously hard — a vise or heavy cracker helps). Inspect and discard any moldy or shriveled kernels.
6. For hickory and pinyon, a light toast improves flavor and drives off moisture before storage — cool completely first.
7. Freeze the shelled nutmeats in airtight bags/containers. Frozen, they keep almost indefinitely; at room temperature, oily nuts (especially pinyon, hazelnut) go rancid within weeks.

**Variations & modifiers.** Toast lightly before freezing for deeper flavor and better keeping (do not toast then leave at room temp long). In-shell storage in a cool spot buys months without freezer space. Chinquapin and hazelnut, being sweeter and thinner-shelled, need less curing but the same cold storage. Pinyon's high oil content makes freezing essential.

**Preservation / curing.** In-shell cured nuts: months in a cool, dry, airy place. Shelled nutmeats: freeze for near-indefinite keeping; refrigerated they last a few months; at room temperature the oils turn rancid quickly, especially pinyon and hazelnut. Rancid nuts smell like paint/crayon — discard.

**Beyond the recipe.** Black walnut hulls are a premier natural ink and brown dye (on the ink map) — never waste them; the same harvest gives food and craft. Cracked shells make a natural abrasive/tumbling grit and a mulch. Rancid-risk trimmings go to wildlife or compost, not storage.

**Safety**

- Juglone from black walnut hulls stains skin near-black and is a plant/horse toxin — glove up and keep hull water away from gardens and livestock.
- Tree-nut allergen: obvious but worth labeling. The long walnut ink simmer also aerosolizes nut proteins — a real hazard for allergic people.
- Rancidity is the main food-safety issue: freeze shelled oily nuts; discard any that smell like paint or taste bitter/soapy.
- Discard floaters and any moldy kernels — mold on nuts can mean aflatoxin.
- Occurrence is not permission, and walnut/pinyon are important wildlife foods — harvest fallen nuts lightly and leave a share.

**Sources**

- [How to Harvest, Crack, and Store Black Walnuts — Iowa State Extension](https://yardandgarden.extension.iastate.edu/how-to/how-harvest-crack-and-store-black-walnuts)
- [Preparing Black Walnuts for Eating — Illinois Extension](https://extension.illinois.edu/blogs/extensions-greatest-hits/1999-10-02-preparing-black-walnuts-eating)

### Dried Morels (Cook-Before-Eating)

`food-dried-morel-mushrooms` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Deep tan honeycombed caps `#5a3a1e`

> Morels dry better than almost any mushroom — their hollow, honeycombed caps have little water to lose, and drying actually deepens their nutty, smoky flavor. A string of dried morels in a jar is spring captured for winter. Two hard rules travel with them: morels must be cooked before eating (raw or undercooked morels cause GI illness, and they're never safe with alcohol raw), and the honeycomb hides grit and the occasional insect, so they get a careful inspection and rinse before drying.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `morel`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Fully dried and kept airtight with a desiccant: 6–12+ months; freeze the jar for longer. Any moisture reabsorption invites mold — dry thoroughly.
- **Timeline:** 20–30 min prep active · 1–3 days air-dry, or 6–12 hrs in a dehydrator passive
- **Yield:** Morels shrink dramatically — expect roughly 1 ounce dried from 8–10 ounces fresh. A big flush becomes a small, precious jar.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Fresh morels, positively identified** — Any amount — true morels are hollow throughout and pitted; 'false morels' (Gyromitra) are toxic and must be discarded

**Tools** — _Required:_ Sharp knife, Soft brush, Dehydrator OR needle-and-thread / drying screen for air-drying, Airtight jars  ·  _Optional:_ Food-safe desiccant packet, Fan (for humid rooms), Fine mesh bag

**Steps**

1. Harvest only where permitted and only morels you have positively identified. Slice each lengthwise: true morels are completely hollow inside and the cap is attached to the stem; discard anything with a cottony/chambered interior or a brain-like cap (false morel / Gyromitra — toxic).
2. Brush off debris. Slice lengthwise in half (or leave small ones whole) to expose the hollow interior — this speeds drying and lets you check for insects and grit.
3. If gritty, rinse briefly and shake dry; blot well. Excess surface water just lengthens drying.
4. Air-dry by threading them on a string in a warm, airy spot, or lay on a screen; alternatively dehydrate at ~110–125°F. Good airflow is everything.
5. Dry until crisp and brittle — they should snap, not bend. Any leathery pieces need more time.
6. Cool completely, then pack into airtight jars (a desiccant packet helps in humid climates). Store in a cool, dark spot.
7. To use: rehydrate in warm water 20–30 min, then always COOK thoroughly. Save the strained soaking liquid as a rich stock — but strain out the grit.

**Variations & modifiers.** Whole small morels rehydrate into showpieces; halved ones dry faster and clean better. The mushroom soaking liquid is a flavor bomb for risotto and sauces — strain it through a coffee filter to catch grit. Powder over-dried scraps into a morel dust for seasoning.

**Preservation / curing.** Crisp-dry morels in an airtight jar keep 6–12 months or more; freezing the sealed jar extends them further. The failure mode is reabsorbed moisture, so re-crisp in a low oven if they soften and always store airtight. Discard any that smell musty or show mold.

**Beyond the recipe.** The grit-strained rehydrating liquid becomes a premium mushroom stock. Dried morel powder seasons salts, butters, and rubs. Stems too tough to dry well can simmer into that same stock rather than being tossed.

**Safety**

- MUST be cooked before eating — raw or undercooked morels cause GI illness. Never eat them raw, and avoid alcohol with undercooked morels.
- Positive ID is non-negotiable: true morels are fully hollow and pitted; toxic false morels (Gyromitra) have brain-like, chambered caps. When unsure, don't eat it.
- Per project rules, no new fungi taxa without a species-level whitelist — morel is the whitelisted edible here; do not extend this to other wild mushrooms.
- Even correctly ID'd morels disagree with some people; try a small cooked portion the first time.
- Dry fully to prevent mold; discard any musty or moldy pieces.

### Dried Sumac & Juniper Spice

### Gooseberry & wild currant jelly

`food-gooseberry-currant-jelly` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Translucent rose to garnet `#8f1d3b`

> Currants and gooseberries (Ribes) are the jelly-maker's easy win: tart, acidic, and so naturally high in pectin that they set firm with nothing but sugar and heat. - **Timeline:** 45–75 min active · overnight to drip the juice passive
- **Yield:** About 4–5 half-pints from ~3 lb fruit, depending on juice yield.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Currants and/or gooseberries** — about 3 lb (mix ~3/4 ripe, 1/4 underripe) — underripe fruit carries the most pectin; wild currants work the same as cultivated
- **Water** — to just cover (for extraction)
- **Sugar** — about 1 cup per cup of extracted juice — measure the juice first, then match sugar for the set

**Tools** — _Required:_ Heavy non-reactive pot, Jelly bag or several layers of cheesecloth, Boiling-water canner, Half-pint jars, lids, bands, Candy/jelly thermometer, Ladle and funnel  ·  _Optional:_ Potato masher, Kitchen scale, Jar lifter

**Steps**

1. Forage or pick fruit where permitted. Use only firm fruit and include some slightly underripe berries for extra pectin.
2. Wash and de-stem, then crush the fruit in the pot, add just enough water to prevent scorching, and simmer until soft and juicy (~10–15 min).
3. Ladle into a jelly bag and let it drip overnight; don't squeeze if you want crystal-clear jelly. Measure the juice.

Gooseberry's high pectin also makes it a natural 'helper' fruit to set lower-pectin jams. **Safety**

- This is a tested-style water-bath jelly because Ribes are high-acid; keep the sugar-to-juice ratio and processing times to get a safe seal and set.

- Occurrence is not permission — forage only where legal, and check local Ribes-planting restrictions tied to white pine blister rust.

### Mesquite Pod Flour (Dry-Milled)

`food-mesquite-flour-drying` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Cinnamon-tan sweet flour `#a8763f`

> Mesquite pods are a legume, not a bean pit — the sweet, cinnamon-caramel flavor lives in the dry pulp around the seeds, and desert peoples have milled them into flour for millennia. But this is the card where drying is a food-safety mandate: University of Arizona research tied aflatoxin (a carcinogen from Aspergillus mold) directly to rainfall, finding pods harvested after the monsoon can exceed the 20 ppb safe limit. The rule that makes mesquite flour safe is simple and non-negotiable — harvest bone-dry pods before the rains, never wet them, and reject anything moldy.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mesquite-bean`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Fully dry-milled flour keeps months airtight; freezing extends it. Any humidity risks mold and aflatoxin — keep bone-dry, cool, and sealed.
- **Timeline:** 1–2 hours milling active · Days-to-weeks of extra pod drying before milling passive
- **Yield:** A grocery bag of dry pods yields perhaps a few cups of flour after sifting out seeds and woody bits. Milling is the bottleneck — community events process far more than home tools.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Bone-dry, mold-free mesquite pods (Prosopis)** — As much as you can snap crisp — harvest before summer rains; pods must snap cleanly. Any mold, dark spots, or dampness = discard

**Tools** — _Required:_ Hammer mill or heavy-duty grain mill (community milling event ideal), Fine sieve/screen, Oven or dehydrator for a final crisp-dry, Airtight jars or freezer bags  ·  _Optional:_ High-power blender (small batches only — mesquite is hard on blades), Baking sheet, Gloves

**Steps**

1. Harvest pods off the tree or freshly fallen, in the dry pre-monsoon window. Choose pods that snap crisply; reject any that bend, are dark-blotched, discolored, or smell musty — those may carry aflatoxin.
2. Do NOT wash the pods. Instead, ensure they're thoroughly dry — finish them in a low oven (~150–175°F) or dehydrator for a couple hours to drive off any residual moisture and any lurking insects. This dryness is the whole safety margin.
3. Break pods into pieces. Discard the hard seeds' role — the flavor is in the pod pulp; commercial mills grind pod and seed together, but many home methods sift out the woody seeds.
4. Mill in a hammer mill or grain mill — a community mesquite-milling event is the safest, most effective option, since home blenders struggle and dull. Bring only clean, mold-free, debris-free pods.
5. Sift the milled flour through a fine screen; re-mill the coarse fraction. The fine, sweet flour is the goal.
6. Give the finished flour a brief final dry, cool it, then pack airtight.
7. Store cool and dry, or freeze. Use as up to ~25–30% of the flour in baked goods for a sweet, malty flavor (it has little gluten).

**Variations & modifiers.** Blend mesquite flour with wheat or other flours at ~1:3 for pancakes, breads, and cookies — it's naturally sweet, so cut added sugar. Toasting pods lightly before milling deepens the caramel notes. Screcurse coarser grinds work as a hot-cereal or drink base (mesquite atole).

**Preservation / curing.** Bone-dry flour stored airtight keeps several months in a cool pantry and much longer frozen. Moisture is the enemy on two fronts — clumping and, more seriously, mold/aflatoxin — so keep it sealed and dry and freeze for long storage. Discard any flour that smells musty or clumps damply.

**Beyond the recipe.** Sifted-out woody seed hulls can steep into a mild sweet tea or go to compost. Coarse siftings become a rustic hot cereal. Mesquite's honey-scented flowers (a separate harvest) are a bee favorite worth leaving in place.

**Safety**

- AFLATOXIN is the headline hazard: harvest only dry, pre-monsoon pods, never wet or wash them, and discard any moldy, discolored, or musty pods. Aflatoxin is a carcinogen with a 20 ppb safe limit.
- Do a hard final dry before milling; residual moisture invites mold in storage.
- Mesquite is very hard — protect blades/mills and hands; community milling events are safer and more effective than home blenders.
- Store bone-dry and sealed; re-inspect for musty smell before each use.
- Occurrence is not permission — many mesquite stands are on tribal, private, or managed land; get consent.

`food-rose-hip-flesh-syrup` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Warm orange-red syrup `#C0512A`

> A rose hip is really two foods stacked together: a tart, vitamin-C-rich flesh you want, wrapped around a core of seeds and fine hairs you very much don't. - **Ripe rose hips (after first frost is sweetest)** — 4 cups — From any mapped Rosa; deep red/orange and slightly soft.

**Beyond the recipe.** The strained seed-and-hair mash goes to the compost, NOT into leather or tea (the hairs irritate). Dried de-seeded hip flesh can be powdered into a tart vitamin-C sprinkle; the rose-hip flesh also pairs into fruit leather once fully de-haired.

### Wild Berry & Pawpaw Fruit Leather

`food-fruit-leather-drying` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Translucent amber-to-magenta sheet `#b5432f`

> Fruit leather is the oldest, simplest way to bank a glut of soft fruit: purée it, pour it thin, and let low heat pull the water out until it turns leathery. Pawpaw — the custard-fleshed native that tastes like banana crossed with mango — makes a leather so rich it barely needs anything added, while invasive wineberry and Himalayan blackberry are best harvested hard and turned into sheets you can roll and store. NCHFP dries leathers at 140°F; done right they keep a month at room temperature and a year frozen.

- **Maps to:** `blackberry`, `raspberry`, `wineberry`, `pawpaw`, `serviceberry`, `wild-strawberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Properly dried and wrapped: ~1 month at room temperature, up to 1 year frozen. Not indefinitely shelf-stable — moisture and warmth invite mold.
- **Timeline:** 30–45 min active · 6–12 hrs drying (dehydrator or low oven) passive
- **Yield:** One standard tray of ~2 cups purée yields a single rolled sheet (4–6 snack portions). Berries lose a lot of volume to water — plan several trays for a real stash.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Soft ripe fruit (berries, pawpaw, serviceberry, strawberry)** — ~2 cups purée per tray — pawpaw seeds and skin are removed and NOT eaten — use only the pulp
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 1–2 tsp — brightens color and slows browning, especially with pawpaw
- _(optional)_ **Honey, sugar, or maple** — To taste (optional) — tart fruit like wineberry may want a little; ripe pawpaw usually doesn't

**Tools** — _Required:_ Blender or food mill, Dehydrator with leather trays OR oven + parchment/silicone mat, Rimmed baking sheet, Plastic wrap or parchment for rolling  ·  _Optional:_ Fine sieve (to remove blackberry/raspberry seeds), Offset spatula, Airtight jars or vacuum bags

**Steps**

1. Harvest ripe fruit where permitted; favor the invasives (wineberry, Himalayan blackberry) for heavy picking and take natives lightly. For pawpaw, use only ripe, soft fruit and discard the large seeds and skin.
2. Purée the fruit smooth. For seedy berries, pass through a sieve if you want a smoother leather. Stir in lemon juice and any sweetener.
3. Pour the purée about ⅛-inch thick onto a lined dehydrator tray or parchment/silicone-lined baking sheet. Spread evenly — thin edges over-dry, thick centers stay tacky.
4. Dry at 140°F. In a dehydrator this is 6–12 hours; in an oven, use the lowest setting with the door cracked and check often.
5. It's done when the surface is no longer sticky and you can peel the whole sheet cleanly with no wet spots. Test the center, not the edge.
6. While still warm and pliable, roll the leather in parchment or plastic wrap, then cut into portions.
7. Store rolled leathers in an airtight container. Refrigerate or freeze for the longest keeping, especially in humid climates.

**Variations & modifiers.** Blend fruits to balance sweetness and pectin: pawpaw or serviceberry smooths out tart wineberry; a little applesauce base makes any thin purée pour and dry more evenly. Add warm spices (cinnamon, ginger) or a swirl of a second fruit for a two-tone sheet. Thicker pours take much longer and risk a gummy center — keep it thin.

**Preservation / curing.** Well-dried, wrapped leather keeps ~1 month at room temperature, several months refrigerated, and up to a year frozen. If any piece feels tacky or sticky in storage, it wasn't dry enough — refrigerate and use quickly. Condensation in the jar means return it to the dehydrator.

**Beyond the recipe.** Trimmed dry edges and scraps blitz into a fruit powder for smoothies or yogurt. Over-dried, brittle leather crumbles into 'fruit dust' — a natural sprinkle. Pawpaw skins and seeds are not eaten (seeds are mildly toxic) and go to compost, well away from where pets forage.

**Safety**

- Pawpaw: eat only the pulp of ripe fruit. Do not eat the seeds (mildly toxic) or skin; some people get GI upset even from the flesh — try a small amount first.
- Dry to fully non-tacky. Under-dried leather harbors mold and, in humid storage, can spoil — when in doubt, refrigerate or freeze.
- Wash all fruit and work surfaces; drying does not sterilize, so start clean.
- Favor invasive wineberry and Himalayan blackberry for heavy harvest; leave native berries for wildlife.
- Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission — get landowner/land-manager consent before picking.

### Wild Blueberry & Huckleberry Jam (Water-Bath Canned)

`food-blueberry-jam-waterbath` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Deep blue-violet set jam `#2e1a47`

> A wild blueberry the size of a peppercorn carries twice the flavor of the cultivated kind, and huckleberries — which refuse domestication and are still hand-picked off mountain slopes — carry more. The good news for the canner: berries in the Vaccinium and Ribes families are naturally acidic, pH roughly 3.1 to 3.4, which puts them safely inside the boiling-water-bath window with no acid math to worry about. Follow a tested NCHFP jam recipe and you get jars that hold on a pantry shelf for a year; improvise ratios and you get a soft set, mold, or worse.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `blueberry`, `huckleberry`, `ribes`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Sealed, processed jars are shelf-stable 12+ months in a cool dark pantry; refrigerate after opening and use within ~1 month.
- **Timeline:** 1.5–2 hours active · 12–24 hrs to cool and seal; check seals next day passive
- **Yield:** About 7–8 half-pint jars from ~6 cups crushed berries. Expect real work per cup with wild berries — a full afternoon of picking for one canner load is normal.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild blueberries and/or huckleberries** — About 6 cups crushed (start ~9 cups whole) — huckleberries and Ribes currants can substitute or blend; they sit in the same high-acid range
- **Granulated sugar** — Per your tested recipe (roughly 4–7 cups) — sugar is a preservative and gel component here, not just sweetness — don't cut it drastically in a set recipe
- **Powdered pectin** — 1 package (or per recipe) — use the pectin the tested recipe specifies; low/no-sugar pectin needs its own tested recipe
- **Bottled lemon juice** — 2 Tbsp (per NCHFP blueberry-spice jam) — bottled, not fresh — bottled has a known, consistent acidity; guarantees safe pH
- _(optional)_ **Ground cinnamon or nutmeg** — ½–1 tsp — the NCHFP blueberry-spice variant; optional flavor only

**Tools** — _Required:_ Boiling-water-bath canner or deep stockpot with rack, Half-pint canning jars, new lids, bands, Jar lifter, Large heavy-bottom pot, Ladle and canning funnel, Headspace tool or ruler  ·  _Optional:_ Potato masher, Candy/jam thermometer, Chinois or food mill for a seedless Ribes set, Timer

**Steps**

1. Confirm you're harvesting where it's permitted — occurrence on the map is never harvest permission, and huckleberry patches on public land often have their own picking rules. Leave a generous share; bears and birds depend on these.
2. Sort and rinse berries; discard any moldy or crushed ones. Crush thoroughly one layer at a time so the pectin can set evenly.
3. Sterilize jars and heat lids per your canner's directions; keep jars hot so they don't crack on filling.
4. In a large pot, combine crushed berries, bottled lemon juice, any spice, and powdered pectin. Bring to a full rolling boil that can't be stirred down.
5. Add all the sugar at once, return to a full rolling boil, and boil hard exactly 1 minute, stirring constantly. Skim foam.
6. Ladle hot jam into hot jars leaving ¼-inch headspace, wipe rims, apply lids and bands fingertip-tight.
7. Process in the boiling-water bath 10 minutes (add 5 min above 6,000 ft). Keep jars covered by 1–2 inches of water the whole time.
8. Turn off heat, wait 5 minutes, lift jars out, and cool undisturbed 12–24 hours. Confirm each lid is concave and won't flex — any that didn't seal go straight to the fridge.

**Variations & modifiers.** Blend in Ribes currants or gooseberries for a brighter, higher-pectin set. A blueberry-currant jam (NCHFP) uses the currant pulp for natural pectin and cuts added pectin. For softer fruit spreads, a low-sugar tested recipe with modified pectin lets you drop sugar without unsafe improvisation. Do not scale a jelly/jam recipe up more than doubled — set and heat penetration both fail.

**Preservation / curing.** Properly sealed and processed: 12+ months on a cool, dark, dry shelf; quality (color, flavor) is best in the first year. Any unsealed jar refrigerates and keeps a few weeks. Discard on sight for a bulging lid, spurting liquid, off smell, or mold — never taste-test a suspect jar.

**Beyond the recipe.** The skins and cores strained from a seedless Ribes batch make a quick cordial base or go into the compost. Under-set 'failed' jam is excellent stirred into yogurt, swirled into oatmeal, or used as a pancake syrup — nothing is wasted, it's just relabeled as sauce.

**Safety**

- Water-bath canning is only safe for high-acid foods (pH ≤ 4.6). Berries qualify, but the bottled lemon juice is your acid insurance — don't omit it.
- Use a tested recipe (NCHFP/USDA/Ball). Improvised sugar/fruit ratios can drop acidity or prevent a proper vacuum seal, risking spoilage and, in low-acid mistakes, botulism.
- Never can jam in the oven, dishwasher, or by 'inversion' — only a real boiling-water bath sanitizes and seals reliably.
- Discard, don't taste, any jar that failed to seal, smells fermented, or shows mold; botulinum toxin is odorless and can't be seen.
- If using Ribes/currants, note some Western public lands restrict Ribes to control white pine blister rust — check local rules before picking.

- **Yield:** About 2–3 cups of cordial from 2 cups fruit and 2–3 cups spirit. A little goes a long way — it's a sipping/mixing liqueur.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Whole wild cherries (sour, black, or chokecherry), stemmed** — ~2 cups — leave whole — do NOT crush or grind pits (cyanogenic); infuse a limited time, then strain
- **Neutral spirit, brandy, or whiskey (~40% ABV)** — ~2–3 cups — the alcohol both extracts and preserves
- **Sugar** — ~½–1 cup — sweetens and helps preserve; a cordial by definition is sweetened
- _(optional)_ **Rose hips, prickly-pear fruit, or spices** — To taste — rose hips add tartness/color; strain rose hips well (irritant internal hairs)

**Tools** — _Required:_ Clean glass jar with tight lid, Fine strainer + cheesecloth, Bottles for the finished cordial, Funnel  ·  _Optional:_ Gloves and tongs for prickly pear (glochids), Kitchen scale, Labels

**Steps**

1. Harvest ripe cherries where permitted; leave a share for wildlife. Stem and rinse. Keep the fruit WHOLE — do not pit-crush, grind, or blend, because the pits are cyanogenic.
2. For prickly-pear tuna: singe/scrub off the glochids and peel first — never handle bare-fingered. For rose hips, trim and de-seed or plan to strain out the irritant internal hairs later.
3. Layer fruit and sugar in a clean jar, then cover completely with spirit. Seal.
4. Store in a cool, dark place and shake gently every few days to dissolve the sugar.
5. Infuse 1–3 months. Because the pits are present, keep the infusion time moderate (a few months, not open-ended) and always strain the fruit out at the end rather than leaving pits sitting indefinitely.
6. Strain through cheesecloth (double-strain rose-hip versions to catch every fine hair). Taste and adjust sweetness.
7. Bottle the clear cordial, label, and store. It only improves with a little age.

**Variations & modifiers.** Sour and chokecherry give the brightest garnet and tartness; black cherry is softer and winier. Add a strip of rose hip or a splash of prickly-pear juice for color and tang, or warm spices (cinnamon, clove, vanilla) for a holiday cordial. Use less sugar for a drier 'ratafia' style. Cornelian cherry makes an excellent tart substitute.

**Preservation / curing.** Finished, strained cordial is shelf-stable for a year or more thanks to alcohol and sugar — store cool and dark. Straining the fruit out is both a flavor and a safety step (keeps pit compounds from accumulating over long storage). No refrigeration required, though it doesn't hurt.

**Beyond the recipe.** The alcohol-soaked strained cherries (pits still in — do not eat around the pits carelessly, and never eat the pits) can flavor desserts or be discarded; the safest path is to compost the spent fruit. Prickly-pear peels and rose-hip strainings compost. The cordial itself is a base for shrubs, cocktails, and glazes.

**Safety**

- NEVER crush, grind, or blend cherry/chokecherry/plum pits — they are cyanogenic. Infuse whole fruit only, for a limited time, then strain and discard the pits.
- Chokecherry and pin cherry especially: cook or infuse only; never eat raw pits or foliage.
- Prickly-pear glochids: singe/peel before handling; the tiny barbed hairs embed in skin. Rose hips: strain out the irritant internal hairs (double-strain).
- This is an alcoholic product — not for children, pregnant people, or anyone avoiding alcohol; label clearly.
- Occurrence is not permission — harvest where allowed and leave a wildlife share.

### Hoshigaki hand-massaged dried persimmon

`food-persimmon-hoshigaki` — **Viability 3/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Amber with sugar bloom `#c8651f`

> Hoshigaki is the patient art of turning astringent persimmons into candy. You peel firm, fully colored astringent fruit (American persimmon or Hachiya-type), hang it by the stem, and massage it gently by hand every day for weeks. Drying strips the mouth-drying astringency, the daily coddling breaks up the pulp and pushes air pockets out where mold would start, and after a month or two natural sugars frost the leathery surface white.

- **Maps to:** `persimmon`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Keeping-quality: finished hoshigaki are surface-dry and leathery, not shelf-stable canned goods. Kept cool, dry, and wrapped they hold for weeks to a few months; refrigerate or freeze for longer. Mold is the main failure mode, so they need airflow while drying and dry storage afterward.
- **Timeline:** 10–15 min/day of massage active · 3–6 weeks of air-drying passive
- **Yield:** Each persimmon yields one dried fruit, shrunk to a fraction of its fresh size.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Astringent persimmons, firm and fully orange** — as many as you can hang — must be astringent-type and firm (not yet soft-ripe) with a stub of stem/T-branch left on for hanging

**Tools** — _Required:_ Sharp peeler or paring knife, Cotton string, A cool, dry, well-ventilated spot with airflow, Clean hands  ·  _Optional:_ A fan (for humid climates), Food-grade alcohol or a quick boiling-water dip to sanitize before hanging, Screen/rack (if drying flat)

**Steps**

1. Select firm, fully orange astringent persimmons with the calyx and a short stem or T-shaped branch stub left on for tying. Harvest only where permitted.
2. Peel each fruit completely, leaving the stem and calyx intact. Optionally dip briefly in boiling water or wipe with alcohol to reduce surface microbes.
3. Tie a string to the stem/branch and hang the fruit so pieces don't touch, in a cool, dry, breezy spot out of direct sun and rain.
4. After several days a skin forms. Begin gentle daily massage — squeeze and knead each fruit softly to break the interior pulp and force out air pockets, which also prevents wrinkles that trap moisture and mold.
5. Massage daily for 3–6 weeks. Watch for mold: any fuzzy growth means discard that fruit and improve airflow.
6. They're done when leathery-but-pliable and often frosted with a natural white sugar bloom on the surface.
7. Wrap and store cool and dry; refrigerate or freeze to keep longer.

**Variations & modifiers.** American persimmon (Diospyros virginiana) fruits are small — hoshigaki works but is fiddly; large Hachiya-type fruit is the classic choice. In humid regions, run a fan and consider a brief boiling-water or alcohol dip to cut surface microbes. Non-astringent varieties can be dried but won't behave the same; astringent types are the traditional pick because drying removes their astringency.

**Preservation / curing.** Airflow during drying and dry storage after are everything — this is an uncanned dried food, so mold is the risk. Finished fruit keeps weeks to months wrapped and cool; refrigerate or freeze for longer. Discard any fruit that develops fuzzy or off-colored mold (surface sugar bloom is fine and desirable).

**Beyond the recipe.** Trimmed peels can be dried and used to sweeten pickles or tea (a traditional use). Soft-ripe astringent persimmons that aren't firm enough to hang are perfect for pulp freezing instead — see the persimmon & pawpaw pulp card.

**Safety**

- Use only astringent persimmons that are firm and fully colored; unripe-astringent fruit eaten fresh is intensely mouth-drying, and drying is what removes the astringency.
- Mold is the main hazard of open-air drying — give constant airflow, massage to expel air pockets, and discard any fruit with fuzzy or colored mold (white sugar bloom is harmless).
- Keep everything clean; a boiling-water or alcohol dip before hanging helps in humid climates.
- Occurrence is not permission — forage or harvest only where legal.

### Wild-Fermented Berry Soda

`food-berry-soda-wild-fermentation` — **Viability 3/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Bright fizzing ruby soda `#9e1f4d`

> Long before bottled soda, fizz came from wild yeast eating fruit sugar and exhaling carbon dioxide. A ginger bug or the yeasts living on a handful of raspberries will happily carbonate a sweet berry juice in a day or two on the counter. It's the most magical — and most dangerous-to-ignore — of the ferments: sugar-and-yeast bottle-conditioning builds real pressure, and un-burped bottles genuinely explode. The whole craft is balancing lively fizz against a hard, over-pressured bottle.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `blackberry`, `raspberry`, `wild-strawberry`, `serviceberry`, `grape`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — This is a fresh drink: refrigerate once fizzy and drink within ~1–2 weeks. Refrigeration slows fermentation but doesn't stop it — keep burping even cold bottles.
- **Timeline:** 30–45 min active · 1–3 days primary + 12–72 hrs bottle carbonation passive
- **Yield:** About 4–5 cups of soda per batch. Expect variability — wild ferments are alive and each batch behaves a little differently.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Berry juice or lightly crushed berries (blackberry, raspberry, serviceberry, wild strawberry)** — ~4 cups juice — wild grape works too; use ripe, clean fruit
- **Sugar** — ~½–¾ cup — food for the yeast and the source of the CO2; don't overdo it or pressure spikes
- _(optional)_ **Ginger bug or active wild starter** — ~½ cup — a ginger bug gives a reliable, safer, faster ferment than relying on wild yeast alone
- _(optional)_ **Unchlorinated water** — To dilute to taste — chlorine inhibits the culture

**Tools** — _Required:_ Jar or jug for primary ferment, Cloth cover + band, Strong flip-top (Grolsch-style) bottles or plastic soda bottles, Funnel, Fridge  ·  _Optional:_ Ginger bug (ginger + sugar + water, fed a few days), Plastic bottle as a 'tester' (squeeze to gauge pressure), Box/cooler to contain bottles

**Steps**

1. Harvest ripe berries where permitted; favor invasives (wineberry, Himalayan blackberry) for heavy picking. Rinse gently — but the wild yeast lives on the fruit, so don't scrub it away if relying on wild ferment.
2. Make a sweet berry base: combine berry juice/crushed berries, sugar, and (optionally) a ginger bug in a jar. Cover with cloth.
3. Ferment on the counter 1–3 days, stirring daily, until it's actively bubbling and smells lightly tangy and yeasty (not boozy or off).
4. Strain into strong bottles, leaving headspace. Use flip-top glass or sturdy plastic soda bottles — never thin glass.
5. Cap and leave at room temperature 12–72 hours to build carbonation. BURP each bottle at least once a day — crack it briefly to vent pressure. A plastic 'tester' bottle: when it's rock-hard, carbonation is done.
6. As soon as it's fizzy, refrigerate to slow fermentation. Keep bottles in a box or cooler as a burst shield.
7. Open cold, over a sink, pointed away from your face — it can gush. Drink within a couple weeks and keep burping any bottles you're holding.

**Variations & modifiers.** A ginger bug makes the ferment faster, fizzier, and more predictable than wild-only. Blend berries for balance; serviceberry and wild strawberry add floral sweetness, wild grape adds body. Less sugar and colder storage = gentler, safer fizz. For a non-fizzy option, refrigerate before bottle-conditioning to make a still, tangy 'fresh' drink.

**Preservation / curing.** A fresh drink, not a preserve: refrigerate once carbonated and drink within ~1–2 weeks. Cold slows but never fully stops fermentation, so pressure keeps building slowly — keep burping. This is not shelf-stable and should not be sealed and stored warm.

**Beyond the recipe.** Over-fermented soda becomes a berry vinegar or shrub base — nothing wasted. The ginger bug is a perpetual starter for future sodas. Strained berry pulp goes into leather or compost.

**Safety**

- EXPLOSION RISK is real: sugar-yeast bottle conditioning builds high pressure. Use only strong flip-top or plastic bottles, burp daily, refrigerate once fizzy, and store in a box/cooler as a shield.
- Open cold bottles slowly over a sink, face away — they can gush or spray glass if over-pressured.
- This is a live, low-alcohol ferment (a trace of alcohol forms); it's not for anyone avoiding alcohol and not for children in large amounts.
- Discard batches that smell of solvent/rot or grow fuzzy mold; a clean ferment smells tangy and yeasty.
- Cook elderberry if you ever use it here — but this recipe uses non-toxic berries; keep it to the safe fruits listed. Occurrence is not permission.

**Sources**

### Jelly-Bag Berry-Pulp Fruit Leather

`food-berry-pulp-fruit-leather` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Dark garnet leather `#7A1E32`

> Every jelly maker knows the guilt of the jelly bag: after the clear juice drips out, you're left with a bowl of intensely flavored pulp that usually hits the compost. But the NCHFP itself notes that leftover jelly pulp can be blended and dried into fruit rolls. The 'waste' is the best part — all the fruit body and flavor, minus the sugar-water you already used.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `blackberry`, `blueberry`, `grape`, `raspberry`, `elderberry`, `serviceberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Properly dried leather keeps ~1 month at room temp, longer refrigerated and up to a year frozen; it is not shelf-stable indefinitely.
- **Timeline:** 30 minutes active · 6-18 hours drying passive
- **Yield:** About 100 g of leather per cup of concentrated pulp; two to three trays from a typical batch of jelly leftovers. Expect deeply-flavored, tart-forward strips rather than candy-sweet ones.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Leftover berry/fruit pulp from making jelly** — 2-3 cups — Elderberry pulp must come from COOKED elderberries; red elderberry must be cooked AND deseeded. Grape/blackberry/blueberry pulp is straightforward.
- _(optional)_ **Water or a little fruit puree** — as needed to loosen — Jelly-bag pulp is dry; thin it to a spreadable puree.
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 1-2 tsp — Brightens color and flavor.
- _(optional)_ **Honey or sugar** — to taste — Optional — the pulp is already tart and concentrated.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Blender or food processor, Fine sieve, Dehydrator with fruit-leather sheets OR oven with parchment, Spatula  ·  _Optional:_ Kitchen scale, Airtight jars, Parchment for rolling

**Steps**

1. Start with pulp left in the jelly bag after juice extraction. Push it through a fine sieve or food mill to remove seeds, skins, and (critically) any stone-fruit pits or hard cores — you want smooth puree only.
2. For elderberry pulp, confirm the berries were fully cooked before jelly-making; for red elderberry, confirm they were cooked and the seeds removed. Never dry raw elderberry pulp.
3. Blend the sieved pulp with a splash of water or fruit puree until smooth and spreadable, adding optional lemon and a little sweetener to taste.
4. Spread ⅛-¼ inch thick on a dehydrator fruit-leather sheet or parchment-lined tray, keeping edges slightly thicker (they dry fastest).
5. Dry at 140°F in a dehydrator (about 6-8 hours) or a low oven (up to ~18 hours). It's done when the surface is no longer tacky and it peels cleanly with a leathery, not brittle, texture.
6. Cool, then roll in parchment or cut into strips. Store in airtight containers.

**Variations & modifiers.** Blend two pulps for depth — wild grape plus blackberry, or blueberry plus serviceberry. A pinch of warm spice (cinnamon, a little rose-hip puree) reads well. Thicker spreads chew like taffy; thinner ones crisp. Sour pulps (chokecherry — pits removed, or currant) may want a touch more honey.

**Preservation / curing.** Store cooled leather airtight at room temperature ~1 month, refrigerated longer, or frozen up to a year. If any piece feels damp or shows mold, discard it — under-dried leather can spoil. Rolling each piece in parchment keeps them from sticking.

**Beyond the recipe.** This is itself a 'byproduct' recipe — it rescues the jelly bag's leftovers. Any pulp too seedy to sieve smoothly can still go to the compost. Powder over-dried, brittle leather in a grinder for a tart fruit-dust to finish yogurt or oatmeal.

**Safety**

- Safety first — elderberry: only use pulp from cooked elderberries; red elderberry must be cooked AND deseeded (it's more toxic). Never dry raw elderberry pulp.
- Sieve out ALL pits and hard seeds from chokecherry, wild plum, and cherry pulp — cyanogenic kernels must never be crushed or eaten. Only use pulp from recipes where pits were removed before cooking.
- Dry to a non-tacky, leathery finish; under-dried leather retains enough moisture to mold. Discard any damp or moldy pieces.
- Occurrence is not permission — mapped berries and grapes may be on land with their own harvest rules, and wineberry/Himalayan blackberry are invasive (harvest freely where removal is welcomed).

### Apple & Crabapple Pectin Stock

`food-apple-core-pectin-stock` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Pale gold pectin liquid `#E4C36B`

> Commercial pectin comes in a foil packet; the same setting power is already hiding in the cores, peels, and underripe drops you'd throw away. Tart apples and crabapples are so loaded with pectin that two pounds of sliced crabapples in three cups of water can make enough liquid to gel jellies for weeks. Save the scraps from a season of pie-making and you never buy pectin again.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `apple`, `hawthorn`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Fresh pectin stock keeps ~1 week refrigerated or months frozen; it can be water-bath canned (½-inch headspace) but freezing best preserves setting strength.
- **Timeline:** 1 hour active · up to overnight to drip and cool passive
- **Yield:** Roughly 2-3 cups of pectin stock per 2 lb of scraps — enough to help set several small batches of low-pectin fruit jelly (elderberry, strawberry, wild grape).

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Tart apple / crabapple cores, peels, and slices** — about 2 lb of scraps, or 2 lb sliced crabapples — Underripe and tart fruit has far more pectin than sweet, ripe apples. Hawthorn haws can add pectin too.
- **Water** — about 3 cups (just to cover) — Barely cover the fruit — too much water dilutes the set.
- _(optional)_ **Lemon juice** — 1-2 Tbsp (optional) — A little acid helps extract pectin.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Large pot, Colander or jelly bag, Cheesecloth, Bowl, Clean jars or freezer containers  ·  _Optional:_ Candy/jelly thermometer, Rubbing alcohol for the pectin-strength test, Water-bath canner

**Steps**

1. Put tart apple/crabapple scraps (or sliced crabapples) in a pot with just enough water to cover, ~3 cups. Do not peel or seed further — the peel and core are where the pectin lives.
2. Bring to a boil, then simmer 30-40 minutes until the fruit is very soft and the liquid has thickened slightly.
3. Ladle into a cheesecloth-lined colander or jelly bag set over a bowl and let it drip. For clear stock, don't squeeze — squeezing gives more yield but cloudier stock.
4. Test the strength: mix 1 tsp cooled stock with 1 tsp rubbing alcohol (do NOT taste this test). A firm, single clot means strong pectin; scattered small clots mean simmer the stock down further to concentrate it.
5. Use immediately in low-pectin fruit jellies (start around 4-6 Tbsp stock per cup of prepared juice), or preserve for later.
6. To can: reheat stock to a boil, jar with ½-inch headspace, and process in a boiling-water bath per a tested recipe. To freeze: cool and pack in labeled containers.

**Variations & modifiers.** Crabapple gives the strongest set and a rosy tint; regular apple scraps stretch a crabapple batch. Underripe fruit beats ripe every time. Reduce the strained stock by simmering to concentrate weak pectin. Note the alcohol test is a quick strength gauge — discard the test sample.

**Preservation / curing.** Refrigerate finished stock ~1 week, freeze several months (best for keeping the set), or water-bath can with ½-inch headspace using a tested process time. Label with the batch strength so you know how much to use later. Discard if it smells fermented or shows mold.

**Beyond the recipe.** The cooked-out pulp can be pushed through a food mill for a rustic apple butter base (re-sweeten and cook down). Spent solids compost well. Pair this with any low-pectin mapped fruit — elderberry, wild strawberry, wineberry — to gel jellies without store pectin.

**Safety**

- Safety first — canning: only water-bath can this in a tested, correctly-acidified jelly recipe. Improperly processed low-acid or under-acidified home canning risks botulism; when unsure, freeze instead.

### Apple-Scrap Vinegar

`food-apple-scrap-vinegar` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Cloudy amber vinegar `#C8862E`

> The parts you'd toss carry the whole recipe: wild yeasts riding on the peel eat the fruit sugars into alcohol, then Acetobacter breathe that alcohol into acetic acid. A quart jar of cores and peels that were headed for the compost becomes a season's worth of tart, mother-thick vinegar. It's the oldest zero-waste trick in the pantry, and it costs nothing but patience and a scrap of cloth.

> 🔁 **Dedup:** Overlaps **Wild Apple & Fruit-Scrap Vinegar** (`food-fruit-scrap-vinegar-fermentation`). Merge into a single scrap-vinegar card.

- **Maps to:** `apple`, `pear`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Finished vinegar is shelf-stable for a year or more if it reaches a sour, stable acidity; store capped and dark. It is NOT for canning — home vinegar has an unknown, unreliable pH.
- **Timeline:** 20 minutes over several days active · 3-5 weeks fermenting passive
- **Yield:** About 2-3 cups of vinegar per quart jar of scraps. Expect a real vinegar for dressings, marinades, cleaning, and hair rinses — but a milder, less predictable acidity than store-bought.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Apple (or pear) peels and cores** — enough to loosely fill a quart jar (~2-3 apples' worth) — Clean, ripe, or slightly bruised — never rotten or moldy. Skip commercial waxed/sprayed peels if you can.
- **Sugar** — 1 Tbsp per cup of water — Feeds the initial fermentation; most is consumed.
- **Non-chlorinated water** — enough to submerge scraps — Chlorine can stall wild fermentation; let tap water sit out or use filtered.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Wide-mouth quart jar, Breathable cover (cloth + rubber band), Fermentation weight or small clean jar to hold scraps under  ·  _Optional:_ pH strips (to sanity-check sourness, not for canning safety), Second jar for the aging phase, Funnel and bottles

**Steps**

1. Fill a clean quart jar loosely with apple peels and cores. Dissolve 1 Tbsp sugar per cup of water and pour over the scraps until submerged.
2. Weight the scraps down so nothing floats above the waterline — exposed fruit is where mold starts. Cover with cloth so it breathes but keeps out fruit flies.
3. Keep at room temperature out of direct sun. Stir daily for the first week; it will fizz and smell yeasty-alcoholic as wild yeast works (this is the cider stage).
4. After 1-2 weeks, strain out the scraps (compost them). Return the cloudy liquid to a clean jar, re-cover with cloth, and let it sit undisturbed.
5. Over the next 2-4 weeks the alcohol oxidizes to acetic acid and a gelatinous 'mother' may form on top. Taste weekly with a clean spoon — it's done when it's sharply, cleanly sour.
6. If you ever see fuzzy mold (not the flat, gelatinous mother) or it smells rotten rather than sour, discard the whole batch. When it tastes right, bottle, cap, and label.

**Variations & modifiers.** A splash of raw unpasteurized vinegar or a piece of a live mother at the start speeds and steadies the acetic stage. Warmer rooms (70-80°F) ferment faster; cool rooms drag it out and raise mold risk. Pear, quince, or crabapple scraps all work — crabapple gives a tarter, more astringent vinegar.

**Preservation / curing.** Once fully soured, apple-scrap vinegar keeps a year-plus capped in a cool, dark cupboard; a harmless mother may re-form and can be reused. IMPORTANT: because its acidity is unmeasured and variable, never use homemade vinegar for pickling, canning, or any recipe where a guaranteed 5% acidity keeps food safe — use commercial 5% vinegar for that.

**Beyond the recipe.** The spent scraps go to the compost or worm bin. The mother can be saved to inoculate the next batch, and diluted finished vinegar makes a fine window and counter cleaner or an acidic hair rinse.

**Safety**

- Occurrence is not permission: mapping apple or pear trees is not consent to harvest — many are on private, park, or historic-orchard land with their own rules.
- Keep all scraps submerged and toss any batch with fuzzy mold or a rotten (vs. sour) smell — surface mold means the batch is unsafe.
- Do NOT use homemade vinegar for canning or pickling; its pH is unknown and unreliable, and under-acidified home-canned goods risk botulism. Use commercial 5% vinegar for preserving.
- Use clean jars, utensils, and a cloth cover; a breathable cover prevents pressure buildup and keeps flies out.

**Sources**

- [Practical Self Reliance — How to Make Apple Scrap Vinegar](https://practicalselfreliance.com/how-to-make-apple-scrap-vinegar/)
- [NCHFP — Making Vinegar at Home (safety & acidity caveats)](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_06/making_vinegar.html)
- [Grow a Good Life — Homemade Apple Scrap Vinegar](https://growagoodlife.com/apple-scrap-vinegar/)

### Hickory-Shell & Bark Syrup

`food-hickory-shell-syrup` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Smoky mahogany syrup `#6B3410`

> Unlike maple, hickory syrup isn't tapped — it's brewed. You roast the loose, shaggy bark and cracked nutshells until they're fragrant, steep them into a dark tea, and boil that down with sugar into a syrup that tastes like a campfire made sweet. It's a rare thing: a syrup you can make from the parts of the nut harvest you'd otherwise burn, without harming a single tree.

- **Maps to:** `hickory`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Cooked to ~219-220°F, the sugar-dense syrup is shelf-stable for months sealed; refrigerate after opening.
- **Timeline:** 1.5 hours active · 30-45 minutes steeping passive
- **Yield:** Roughly the volume of sugar you added, minus a little — about 1 cup syrup per cup of tea plus sugar. Expect a distinctly smoky, maple-adjacent syrup, not a maple clone.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Shagbark hickory bark (loose pieces) and/or cracked hickory shells** — about 1 lb — Take only loose, peeling bark that comes away easily — this doesn't harm the tree. Shells left from cracking nuts work too.
- **Water** — enough to just cover the bark/shells
- **Sugar** — equal volume to the finished tea (1 cup sugar per 1 cup tea) — The syrup relies on sugar concentration for both flavor and shelf life.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Baking sheet, Oven, Saucepan, Fine strainer, Candy thermometer  ·  _Optional:_ 8-inch heavy pot for the final boil, Sterilized bottles, Cheesecloth

**Steps**

1. Break bark into ~2-inch pieces; rinse off grit. Spread bark and/or cracked shells on a baking sheet and toast at 350°F for about 20 minutes, until very fragrant and just slightly darkened — don't scorch.
2. Put the toasted bark/shells in a saucepan and add just enough water to cover. Bring to a gentle simmer (keep it below a hard boil) and steep 30-45 minutes; a rolling boil pulls out bitterness.
3. Cool slightly, then strain the tea through a fine strainer (cheesecloth for clarity), discarding the bark and shells.
4. Measure the tea and return it to a heavy pot with an equal volume of sugar. Stir to dissolve.
5. Bring to a boil and cook to 219-220°F on a candy thermometer, where it thickens to syrup consistency. Watch it — it climbs fast at the end.
6. Bottle hot into sterilized bottles and cool. Label.

**Variations & modifiers.** More bark or a longer (still gentle) steep gives a stronger, smokier tea. A splash of the tea reserved un-boiled can adjust final consistency. Cracked shells alone give a milder, nuttier note than shaggy bark. Cook slightly past 220°F for a thicker, more caramel syrup; stop earlier for a pourable one.

**Preservation / curing.** The high sugar content makes it shelf-stable for many months sealed and stored cool; refrigerate after opening and use within a few months. If it crystallizes, gently rewarm. Discard if it ferments or molds (a sign it was under-concentrated).

**Beyond the recipe.** Spent bark and shells go to the compost or the smoker (they'll perfume grilled food). Cracked black-walnut and hickory shells left over from nut harvest also make a slow-to-break-down mulch or tumbling abrasive — see the black-walnut shell card.

**Safety**

- Positively identify shagbark hickory (Carya) before harvesting bark, and take only loose, already-peeling pieces so you don't wound the tree.
- Rinse bark well; forest bark carries grit, insects, and lichen. Toasting and straining handle most of it, but start clean.
- Hot sugar syrup causes severe burns — use a deep pot, a thermometer, and pour carefully.
- Occurrence is not permission: mapping a hickory is not consent to strip bark; many stands are on park or private land with their own rules.

### Persimmon & pawpaw pulp freezing

`food-glut-pulp-freezing` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Golden-orange purée `#d08a2c`

> Persimmons and pawpaws share a problem: they ripen all at once, go from perfect to mush in days, and have too short a window to eat fresh. The standard save is to screen the ripe pulp and freeze it in portions. It's the single most useful waste-reduction move for these short-season natives — capture a glut at its peak and bake, blend, or churn it all winter.

- **Maps to:** `persimmon`, `pawpaw`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Keeping-quality: frozen, not shelf-stable. Persimmon and pawpaw purées hold good quality about 6 months (up to a year for well-packed persimmon) at 0°F. A little ascorbic acid keeps the color from browning. These low-acid pulps are not safe to home-can, so freezing is the correct route.
- **Timeline:** 20–40 min active · freezes overnight; keeps ~6 months passive
- **Yield:** Scales with the glut — a good picking easily fills several 1–2 cup portions.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Fully soft-ripe persimmons and/or pawpaws** — as much as your glut — must be dead-ripe: astringent persimmons soft and jelly-like, pawpaws fragrant and yielding
- _(optional)_ **Ascorbic acid (vitamin C powder)** — ⅛ tsp (about 375 mg) per quart of purée — prevents browning; more effective than lemon juice, and useful for near-neutral-pH pawpaw

**Tools** — _Required:_ Sieve, food mill, or colander (to screen out seeds/skin), Bowl and spoon, Freezer containers or bags with headspace, Freezer at 0°F  ·  _Optional:_ Blender (for smooth pulp), Ice-cube trays or portion containers, Labels for date and portion size

**Steps**

1. Forage/harvest where permitted, and only fully soft-ripe fruit — under-ripe astringent persimmon is mouth-drying, and pawpaw is only worth eating dead-ripe.
2. For persimmon: sort, wash, peel, and press the soft flesh through a sieve to a smooth purée, leaving skin and seeds behind.
3. For pawpaw: cut, scoop the custardy flesh, and press through a screen to remove the large toxic seeds and the skin; keep only the pulp.
4. Optional but recommended: stir ⅛ tsp ascorbic acid per quart into the purée to prevent browning (especially for near-neutral pawpaw).
5. Portion into freezer containers or bags, leaving ½-inch headspace for expansion; freezing in small usable portions (cups or cubes) means you thaw only what you need.
6. Label with fruit and date, and freeze at 0°F.
7. Use within about 6 months for best quality: persimmon pulp for puddings, breads, and cookies; pawpaw pulp for ice cream, custards, and quick breads.

**Variations & modifiers.** Freeze in recipe-sized portions (1-cup blocks, or ice-cube-tray dollops for small amounts). Persimmon from native varieties needs no sugar; cultivated persimmon or pawpaw can be packed with 1 cup sugar per quart if you like. Purée is also the honest fallback for any low-acid fruit that has no tested canning recipe.

**Preservation / curing.** Freezing only — both fruits are low-acid and not safe to home-can as pulp. Keep at a steady 0°F; persimmon holds up to ~6–12 months, pawpaw about 6 months. Ascorbic acid guards color; press air out and leave ½-inch headspace. Don't refreeze fully thawed pulp for quality.

**Beyond the recipe.** This is the waste-reduction backbone: it turns a two-week glut of the most perishable native fruits into a year of ingredients. Screened-out persimmon peels can be dried; pawpaw skin and seeds are discarded (seeds are toxic and never eaten). Pairs with the hoshigaki card — firm persimmons get dried, soft ones get pulped.

**Safety**

- Use only fully soft-ripe fruit; under-ripe astringent persimmon is intensely mouth-drying (not dangerous, but unpleasant).
- Pawpaw seeds and skin are not food — screen them out and discard; the seeds contain toxic acetogenins. Some people get stomach upset from pawpaw, and pawpaw flesh contains annonacin, so eat it in moderation rather than in large daily quantities.
- These low-acid pulps are not safe to home-can — freeze them; do not water-bath or 'cold pack' pulp for the pantry.
- Occurrence is not permission — forage only where legal and take a light, sustainable share.

**Sources**

- **Water** — to cover generously (~6-8 cups)

- _(optional)_ **Salt** — to taste at the end

**Tools** — _Required:_ Freezer bag, Large pot, Fine strainer, Storage containers  ·  _Optional:_ Cheesecloth, Ice-cube trays for concentrated portions

- Occurrence is not permission: a mapped morel spot isn't harvest consent, and morels should be gathered lightly.

### Whole-Plant Prickly Pear: Pads + Tunas

`food-prickly-pear-whole-pad-fruit` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Olive nopal + magenta tuna `#8AA84B`

> Most foragers take the jewel-toned tuna fruit and walk past the rest of the plant — but the young pads (nopales) are a vegetable in their own right, tart and green-bean-like once the glochids are scraped away and they're cooked to olive green. Using both the pad and the fruit is whole-plant eating from a single abundant, drought-hardy cactus. The only toll is respect for the glochids, the near-invisible barbed hairs that make careless harvest miserable.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `prickly-pear`
- **Keeping / durability:** _fugitive_ — Fresh cooked nopales keep a few days refrigerated; tuna fruit is best eaten fresh or turned promptly into juice/syrup.
- **Timeline:** 40 minutes active · 10-20 minutes cooking passive
- **Yield:** 3-4 pads make a generous side or filling for several tacos; fruit yield depends on the stand. Expect a tart, okra-like vegetable and an intensely sweet-tart fruit.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Young prickly-pear pads (nopales)** — 3-4 pads — Young, bright, tender pads eat best; older pads toughen.
- _(optional)_ **Ripe prickly-pear tunas (fruit)** — as gathered — The magenta/orange fruit — deglochid before handling.
- _(optional)_ **Salt / lime / aromatics** — to taste — For finishing the nopales.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Thick gloves, Tongs, Sharp knife or vegetable peeler, Cutting board, Skillet or pot  ·  _Optional:_ Kitchen torch or gas flame (to singe glochids), Sieve for fruit juice

**Steps**

1. Wear gloves and use tongs — never bare hands. Do NOT rinse pads or fruit before deglochiding; water just spreads the barbed hairs.
2. For pads: hold with tongs and scrape the whole surface (both sides and the rim) with the dull edge of a knife or a peeler to remove every glochid cluster and spine. Trim the cut edge.
3. Only after deglochiding, rinse the pads, then dice. Cook by boiling, grilling, or sautéing until they turn from bright to olive green (they exude a slick 'baba' that firms up as they cook).
4. Finish nopales with salt, lime, onion, or fold into eggs and tacos.
5. For the fruit: deglochid the tunas the same way (scrape, or singe over a flame), then peel. Eat fresh, or press and double-strain the pulp for juice/syrup, discarding the hard seeds.
6. Reserve fruit pulp/juice for other cards (syrup, crackers), keeping the seeds and skins out.

**Variations & modifiers.** Grilling the pads whole (deglochided) before dicing adds char. Boiling reduces the slickness most; quick sautéing keeps more. The fruit ranges from magenta to gold by species — magenta stains everything. Combine pad and fruit in a single dish for a true whole-plant plate.

**Preservation / curing.** Cooked nopales keep 3-4 days refrigerated; they can also be pickled with commercial 5% vinegar per a tested recipe. Tuna fruit is fugitive — eat fresh within days or turn promptly into strained juice, syrup, or leather (see other cards).

**Beyond the recipe.** The vivid magenta juice of red tunas is a natural food color and, historically, a dye. Fruit skins and seeds compost. The pad's mucilage has been used as a folk binder and even a natural water clarifier — a bridge to craft uses.

**Safety**

- Safety first — glochids: the tiny barbed hairs embed in skin and mucous membranes and are painful to remove. Always use gloves and tongs, deglochid before rinsing, and singe or scrape thoroughly. If glochids embed, remove with tape or tweezers.
- Cook pads before eating (raw nopal is very slimy and hard to digest for some); discard tuna seeds rather than crushing them.
- If pickling nopales for storage, use commercial 5% vinegar and a tested recipe — not homemade vinegar of unknown acidity.
- Occurrence is not permission: cactus stands may be on protected desert land; harvest young pads sparingly so the plant keeps growing.

**Sources**

- [University of Nevada, Reno Extension — Eating Cactus: Prickly Pear for Food](https://extension.unr.edu/publication.aspx?PubID=2157)
- [Pati Jinich — Cleaning and Cooking Cactus Paddles (Nopales)](https://patijinich.com/cleaning_cactus_paddles_or_nopales/)

### Wild Apple & Fruit-Scrap Vinegar

`food-fruit-scrap-vinegar-fermentation` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Cloudy golden-amber vinegar `#c69a3a`

> Vinegar is what happens when you let fermentation run past the alcohol stage: wild yeasts on the fruit turn sugar to alcohol, then Acetobacter bacteria — with oxygen — turn that alcohol to acetic acid, growing the gelatinous 'mother' as they work. Wild crabapples and pears are perfect fuel; even the cores and peels left from another project will do. The honest caveat that keeps this safe: homemade vinegar's acidity is unpredictable, so it's a condiment and drinking vinegar — not a canning acid — unless you verify it.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

> 🔁 **Dedup:** Overlaps **Apple-Scrap Vinegar** (`food-apple-scrap-vinegar`). Merge; the scrap-vinegar method is one card with fruit variations.

- **Maps to:** `apple`, `pear`, `grape`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Finished, strained vinegar is self-preserving and keeps a year+ at room temperature. But do NOT rely on unmeasured homemade vinegar for canning acidity — use commercial 5% vinegar for preserving.
- **Timeline:** 20–30 min over several weeks active · 4–6 weeks total fermentation passive
- **Yield:** A quart jar of scraps yields roughly a pint-plus of vinegar. Yield depends on how much sugar/juice the fruit contributed.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild apple/crabapple or pear scraps (peels, cores) or whole chopped fruit** — Enough to fill a jar ~⅔ — wild grape works too; use clean, unsprayed fruit
- **Sugar** — ~1 Tbsp per cup of water — feeds the first (alcoholic) fermentation
- **Unchlorinated water** — Enough to submerge the fruit — chlorine can inhibit the wild cultures
- _(optional)_ **Splash of raw vinegar with mother (optional)** — 2–3 Tbsp — jump-starts acetic fermentation and lowers spoilage risk

**Tools** — _Required:_ Wide-mouth glass jar or crock, Breathable cover (cloth + band), Spoon for daily stirring, Fine strainer, Bottles for finished vinegar  ·  _Optional:_ pH strips or meter, Second jar for the mother, Funnel

**Steps**

1. Fill a clean jar about two-thirds with wild apple/pear scraps or chopped fruit. Use unsprayed fruit — the wild yeast lives on the skins.
2. Dissolve sugar in unchlorinated water (~1 Tbsp per cup) and pour over to submerge the fruit. Add a splash of raw vinegar-with-mother if you have it.
3. Cover with cloth (never sealed — acetic fermentation needs oxygen) and keep at 70–80°F.
4. Stir 2–3 times daily for the first week to keep fruit submerged and discourage surface mold. It will smell yeasty/boozy as alcohol forms.
5. After ~1–2 weeks, strain out the fruit. Return the liquid to the jar, re-cover, and let acetic fermentation continue.
6. Over the next few weeks a 'mother' forms and the smell shifts sharply vinegary. Taste weekly; total time is roughly 4–6 weeks.
7. When it tastes fully sour, strain and bottle. Keep the mother to start the next batch. Refrigerate to halt further souring.

**Variations & modifiers.** Wild grape scraps make a fruity red wine vinegar; pear gives a soft, floral vinegar. Infuse the finished vinegar with rose hips, sumac, or berries for a drinking vinegar/shrub. Warmer temps speed it; a mother from a previous batch makes it faster and safer. For a sweet-tart shrub, blend finished vinegar with fruit and sugar and refrigerate.

**Preservation / curing.** Finished, strained vinegar is self-preserving and keeps a year or more at room temperature; refrigeration stops it from souring further or forming new mother. Discard a batch that grows fuzzy/colored mold (not the smooth mother) or smells of nail polish/rot rather than clean vinegar.

**Beyond the recipe.** The mother is a living starter you pass to friends or the next batch. Strained-out fruit can go to compost or, if still sweet, a quick second extraction. The vinegar itself becomes shrubs, salad dressings, and pickling brine (for refrigerator pickles, not canning).

**Safety**

- Do NOT use homemade vinegar of unknown acidity for water-bath canning or pickling — its pH is unreliable. Use commercial 5% vinegar for any canned/shelf-stable pickle.
- Ferment uncovered-but-cloth-covered and stir often; a smooth mother is good, but fuzzy, green/black, or hairy mold means discard the batch.
- A nail-polish-remover (solvent) smell can indicate a stalled/off ferment — trust your nose and discard if it smells wrong.
- Use clean, unsprayed fruit and unchlorinated water; keep tools and hands clean.
- Occurrence is not permission — harvest wild apples/pears where allowed.

**Sources**

- [How to Make Apple Scraps Vinegar — Chelsea Green](https://www.chelseagreen.com/2023/fermentation-apple-scraps-vinegar/)
- [Making Vinegar at Home — Penn State Extension](https://extension.psu.edu/lets-preserve-fermentation)
- [Homemade Vinegars & Canning Safety — NCHFP (Pickled Products/Ingredients)](https://nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_06/ingredients.html)

### Pulp-and-Seed Scrap Crackers

`food-pulp-seed-crackers` — **Viability 3/5** (solid) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Toasty seeded crisp `#B98A4E`

> When you juice or strain fruit, the pulp and seeds left behind still hold most of the fiber and a lot of flavor — and dried thin, they crisp into a savory cracker. It's the seed-cracker principle: bind leftover pulp with a few whole seeds, spread it paper-thin, and dehydrate. Roughly a cup of tomato-style pulp yields about 100 grams of finished cracker, turning yesterday's 'waste' into a shelf snack.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `prickly-pear`, `grape`, `elderberry`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Fully-crisp crackers keep a few weeks in an airtight jar; re-crisp in a low oven if they soften. Not indefinitely shelf-stable.
- **Timeline:** 30 minutes active · 6-12 hours drying passive
- **Yield:** About 100 g of cracker per cup of pulp — a tray or two per batch. Expect rustic, seed-studded crisps, best as a base for dips or cheese rather than a delicate wafer.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Leftover fruit/vegetable juicing or straining pulp** — 2 cups — Prickly-pear tuna pulp must be fully de-glochided and strained; elderberry pulp must be from COOKED berries only.
- **Whole seeds (pumpkin, sunflower, flax)** — ½-1 cup — Flax or chia also help bind.
- _(optional)_ **Salt and seasonings** — to taste — Herbs, pepper, ground sumac spice.
- _(optional)_ **Water or a little puree** — as needed — To reach a spreadable paste.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Blender or bowl, Dehydrator with solid sheets OR oven with parchment, Spatula, Airtight container  ·  _Optional:_ Pizza cutter (to score), Kitchen scale

**Steps**

1. Start with clean pulp from juicing or jelly-straining. For prickly-pear pulp, be certain all glochids are removed and it's well strained; for elderberry, use only pulp from cooked berries.
2. Lightly blend the pulp (leave some texture), then stir in whole seeds, ground flax/chia to bind, salt, and seasonings. Add a little water only if needed for a spreadable paste.
3. Spread ¼ inch thick and even on a solid dehydrator sheet or parchment; score into cracker shapes so they snap apart cleanly later.
4. Dry low and slow: a common approach is ~2 hours at 160°F then several hours at ~130°F, or a longer single stage around 130-140°F, until fully crisp with no soft center.
5. Flip once partway if drying in an oven. They're done when they snap rather than bend.
6. Cool completely, break along the scores, and store airtight.

**Variations & modifiers.** Green/vegetable pulp (celery, carrot, greens) makes a savory cracker; fruit pulp makes a sweeter one — keep sweet and savory batches separate. More seeds = crunchier and sturdier. A spoon of ground flax or chia in water is the vegan binder. Finish with the site's ground sumac spice for a tart edge.

**Preservation / curing.** Store fully-crisp crackers airtight at room temperature a few weeks; refrigerate in humid climates. If they go leathery, re-crisp in a 200°F oven. Under-dried crackers can mold — dry until they snap. Discard any that smell off.

**Beyond the recipe.** This card is itself a byproduct rescue. Over-dried crumbs become a seasoned 'cracker dust' topping; any pulp too seedy to spread thin can still compost. Pairs with the mushroom stock and apple-scrap vinegar for a zero-waste snack board.

**Safety**

- Safety first — prickly pear: pulp must be completely de-glochided; the tiny barbed glochids embed in skin and mouth. Handle pads/fruit with gloves and tongs, scrape/strain thoroughly.
- Elderberry pulp must come from COOKED berries only; never dry raw elderberry pulp (and deseed red elderberry).
- Strain out any stone-fruit pits and hard cores before drying — cyanogenic kernels must never be crushed or eaten.
- Dry until crackers truly snap; under-dried crackers retain moisture and can mold. Occurrence is not permission for the mapped plants used.

**Sources**

- [Brod & Taylor — Tomato Pulp Crackers (drying method & yield)](https://brodandtaylor.com/blogs/recipes/tomato-pulp-crackers)
- [Food Revolution Network — Dehydrated Juice Pulp Crackers](https://foodrevolution.org/recipes/dehydrated-juice-pulp-crackers/)
