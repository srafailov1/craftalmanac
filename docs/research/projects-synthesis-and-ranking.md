# Projects Research — Synthesis & Viability Ranking

_Companion to [`food-map-projects.md`](food-map-projects.md) and [`mineral-map-projects.md`](mineral-map-projects.md). Nothing here is live; this is the review packet for deciding what to build into the Food and Minerals Projects sections. Each candidate is written as a full project card in the ink/dye schema and was run past an adversarial food-safety or materials-safety reviewer. This file ranks them and flags what to fix first._

## How this was produced

A research workflow fanned out across the six requested topic areas (food: fresh use, preserve for later, waste reduction; minerals: clay bodies, glazes, other craft), grounded each card in live web sources, then sent every card to an independent skeptical reviewer — a food-safety scientist for the recipes, a materials/ceramics-safety expert for the stone and clay work — to check that sources were real and reachable, that hazards were correctly and completely handled, and that ratios/temperatures/times were sound. A completeness critic then named obvious high-value projects the first pass missed, and those were generated and verified in a second round. **75 cards survived** (none were rejected as dangerous or fabricated); the reviewer's flags and fixes travel with each card in the two project files and are summarized below.

**Totals:** 75 candidate cards — 39 food (13 fresh · 24 preserve · 2 waste), 36 mineral (8 clay bodies · 8 glazes · 20 other craft).

**Post-review viability spread:** 15 cards at 5/5, 53 at 4/5, 7 at 3/5. Scores are the reviewer's adjusted number where they revised it down, otherwise the researcher's.

## What I found (headlines)

- **The material fit is real, not forced.** Every food card is built on a mapped species, and the mineral cards lean hard on mapped stone — clay appears in 14 cards, silica/quartz/chert in ~15, limestone/marble in ~13. The one previously-unused mapped material, petrified wood, now anchors a flagship lapidary card.
- **Food splits cleanly into your three asks.** Fresh-use drinks and dishes (shrubs, cordials, syrups, morel sauté, pawpaw ice cream), a deep preservation bench (tested water-bath jams and jellies, drying, freezing, fermenting, curing, cordials), and a genuinely strong waste-reduction set (scrap vinegar, pectin stock from cores, jelly-bag pulp leather, hickory-shell syrup, mushroom-scrap stock, whole-plant prickly pear, glut pulp-freezing).
- **Minerals go well beyond "clay + glaze."** The set covers wild-clay processing and body recipes, a full glaze range from wood-ash to calcium to celadon, the lime cycle (limestone/marble → quicklime → limewash/plaster), plaster-of-Paris and mold-making from gypsum, carving in soapstone/alabaster/serpentine, marble lettering, slate engraving, whetstone finishing, knapping, lapidary/tumbling/cabbing, and ochre pigment — with pipestone deliberately included as an educational "do not carve" card.
- **The cards reinforce the site's safety identity rather than fighting it.** The strongest food cards are ones where the safety rule *is* the recipe: chokecherry syrup (never crush the cyanogenic pits), cook-only elderberry, high-acid tested plum jam. On the mineral side, the asbestos, silica, and caustic-lime hazards are handled as first-class content.

## Cross-cutting safety patterns to fix before adding

The reviewers cleared all 75 cards as fixable, but three patterns recur and should be corrected consistently rather than card by card:

1. **Wild grape → Canada moonseed lookalike.** Several cards that use wild grape (verjus, fruit-scrap vinegar, wild-fermented berry soda, pulp-and-seed crackers) omit the warning about *Menispermum canadense* — a potentially fatal toxic vine that resembles wild grape. The existing food catalog already carries this warning on the `grape` species; every grape-using project card must repeat it, and note the ID tells (moonseed has a single crescent seed, no tendrils, untoothed leaves; true grape has 2–4 round seeds and tendrils). One card also mis-describes moonseed as having tendrils — that error must be removed.
2. **Dead NCHFP / extension URLs.** The National Center for Home Food Preservation redesigned its site, so many older `nchfp.uga.edu/how/can_0*/…` links now 404. Affected cards include the water-bath blueberry jam, apple-core pectin stock, fruit-scrap vinegar, and berry soda. Re-point each to the current `nchfp.uga.edu` path (or the USDA Complete Guide PDF) and re-verify the exact tested ratios, yields, headspace, and processing times before publishing any canning recipe — a few cards drifted from the tested numbers.
3. **Cyanogenic seeds beyond cherries.** The "never crush the pits" rule is applied well to cherries and plums but must also be stated wherever **hawthorn haws** and **apple/crabapple cores** are used (both carry cyanogenic seeds) — e.g. the pectin-stock and haw-jelly cards.

Mineral-specific fixes the reviewers flagged: the soapstone card lists an N95 respirator in tools while its own safety text (correctly) demands P100/HEPA for asbestos-risk stone — the tools list must match; the wild-clay slip glaze and high-clay glazes need a calcine/roast step to stop crazing and crawling; the ochre-pigment card must name crystalline silica/silicosis and require a dedicated (non-food) roasting vessel; and the plaster-of-Paris card must disambiguate gypsum alabaster (calcines to plaster) from calcite alabaster (does not).

## Recommended first launch sets

If you want a tight, safe, high-impact first release for each map rather than all 75 cards, these are the ones I'd ship first — each is a flagship-tier card with no blocking safety flag, chosen to span the sub-themes and the mapped species/materials.

**Food map — first 10:**
- **Chokecherry Syrup (No Pits)** (`food-chokecherry-syrup`, fresh) — Three mapped wild-cherry species, an extension-backed recipe, and a clear demonstration of the site's cyanogenic-pit safety anchor. Regional heritage appeal.
- **Cold-Steep Sumac-ade** (`food-sumac-ade`, fresh) — Abundant native, trivially easy, and a textbook ID-safety lesson (red vs white). Mapped species, universal appeal, and a satisfying instant result.
- **Jelly-Bag Berry-Pulp Fruit Leather** (`food-berry-pulp-fruit-leather`, preserve) — Textbook whole-use loop across many mapped berries, backed by an NCHFP recipe, and a natural vehicle for the elderberry/pit safety anchors the site must teach.
- **Morel Cream Saute** (`food-morel-cream`, fresh) — The map's only mushroom, huge forager appeal, and a life-or-death ID/cooking lesson that embodies the site's fungi safety policy. Perfect fit.
- **Pawpaw Custard Ice Cream** (`food-pawpaw-icecream`, fresh) — Beloved mapped native, strong university sourcing (KSU/Purdue), and the definitive 'eat it fresh, it won't keep' story. Distinctive and high-appeal.
- **Tray-frozen (IQF) wild berries** (`food-iqf-frozen-berries`, preserve) — The universal, foolproof preservation move; maps to five common wild berries and grounded in multiple NCHFP freezing pages. Zero safety math, high reuse as the on-ramp to every other card.
- **Wild & beach plum jam** (`food-plum-jam`, preserve) — Directly tested NCHFP recipe, abundant coastal/wild fruit, and a clean teaching moment on pit safety plus high-acid canning. Strongest anchor card of the set.
- **Wild apple butter & dried apple rings** (`food-apple-butter`, preserve) — Exact NCHFP tested recipe, ubiquitous wild/crab apples, and a strong whole-fruit low-waste story pairing butter with rings and scrap use. Anchor-quality.
- **Wild Berry Drinking Shrub** (`food-berry-shrub`, fresh) — Hits many mapped berries, actively rewards invasive removal, keeps for months, and needs no canning skill. Extremely broad appeal.
- **Wild Grape & Crabapple Jelly** (`food-wild-grape-crabapple-jelly`, preserve) — Three mapped materials (grape, crabapple/apple, hawthorn), classic forager project, strong lookalike safety note, and solid NCHFP sourcing on natural-pectin jelly.

**Mineral map — first 8:**
- **Agate & jasper tumbling** (`mineral-lapidary-tumbling`, craft) — Three mapped materials, hugely popular hobby, beginner-accessible, with a clean silica-safety message — ideal broad-appeal lapidary anchor.
- **Iron-Clay Celadon** (`mineral-iron-clay-celadon`, glaze) — Iconic, beautiful, and food-safe by design; maps clay, silica, and limestone/marble with a compelling historical hook and an honest atmosphere/CO safety note.
- **Petrified wood lapidary** (`mineral-petrified-wood-lapidary`, craft) — Directly activates a mapped material (petrified-wood) that had no primary craft use, reuses the agate/lapidary setup, and teaches the core wet-cutting silica lesson that anchors the whole minerals mode. Broadly doable by hobbyists with a modest cabbing rig.
- **Terra Sigillata from Local Clay** (`mineral-terra-sigillata-local-clay`, clay body) — A gorgeous, low-hazard, deeply historical use of mapped local clay with a clear siphoning method and honest food-safety caveat — high appeal and easy to source.
- **Wood-Ash Glaze (40/40/20)** (`mineral-wood-ash-glaze-40-40-20`, glaze) — Classic, evocative, and uses mapped clay + silica plus a true waste-stream flux; safety story (caustic + silica) is exactly the site's voice. Broadly appealing to any potter with a fireplace.
- **Novaculite whetstone finish** (`mineral-novaculite-whetstone`, craft) — Two mapped materials, a genuinely useful lifelong skill, low hazard, and impeccable sourcing — a natural anchor card for the sharpening cluster.
- **Agate & jasper cabochon** (`mineral-cabochon`, craft) — Uses three mapped materials and bridges tumbling to jewelry, but the equipment cost and technique push it to Intermediate, trimming broad appeal versus tumbling.
- **Chalk gesso / whiting ground** (`mineral-chalk-gesso`, craft) — Turns two mapped carving stones (marble, limestone) into a foundational, non-toxic studio material with deep historical grounding and strong sources. Broadly achievable; ranked below the lapidary flagship only because it needs a gilding/panel context to fully shine.

Species like `serviceberry`, `hazelnut`, and `pinyon-pine-nut` already appear as blend members but could each carry a lead card of their own.

## Notes for integrating into the app (when you're ready)

These are structured to drop into the existing pattern with minimal new plumbing — recorded here so the eventual build is mechanical, not a redesign:

- **Schema is 1:1 with `PROJECT_RECIPES`.** Each card already carries `id`, `name`, `teaser`, `difficulty`, `hook`, `ingredients` (with `required`/`note`), `toolsRequired`/`toolsOptional`, `steps`, `modifiers`, `preservation`, `yield`, `beyondInk`, `safety`, and `sources`. `lightfastness` is reused as keeping-quality (food) / durability & food-safety (ceramics); if that reads oddly you may want to rename the field or relabel it in `recipeDetailHTML`.
- **`sheetProjectsHTML` currently hard-codes ink/dye buckets and an "only pairs with Ink/Dye" scope note** (app.js ~11968). Adding food/mineral projects means keying the shelves and the `PROJECT_RECIPES.length` / "INK & DYE" header off `state.activeMap`, and giving each map its own bucket labels (e.g. Fresh · Preserve · Waste for food; Clay bodies · Glazes · Craft for minerals).
- **Spine colors:** food cards can reuse `FOOD_CATEGORY_COLORS`, mineral cards `MINERAL_CATEGORY_COLORS`, via `getProjectSpineColor`; each card includes a suggested `swatch` hex for the result chip.
- **Safety box:** cards marked `toxic: true` should render the top "Safety first" box exactly as ink cards do; `educationalOnly: true` (pipestone) should render the educational stamp.
- **Before shipping any canning card**, confirm the tested recipe against the current NCHFP/USDA source and encode the exact ratios, headspace, altitude-adjusted times, and yield — do not ship an approximated water-bath recipe.
- **`ATTRIBUTION.md`:** no new *data sources* are introduced (these are external recipe/technique references cited per card), but if you want the reference sites logged, add them there per the project's sourcing convention.
