**Cross-cutting safety anchors:** all silica-bearing work (clay, quartz, chert, agate, petrified wood, glaze mixing) is **processed wet / masked** — dry silica dust causes silicosis. **Soapstone and serpentine may host natural asbestos** — cut and grind wet with HEPA respiratory protection, or don't. **Quicklime and slaked lime are caustic.** Glazes are **food-contact**: only correctly-fired, leach-tested glazes are food-safe — **no lead, ever**; barium/lithium/copper need caution. **Occurrence is never collecting permission**, toolstone sources are often archaeological (ARPA), and **pipestone is culturally restricted**.

### Terra Sigillata from Local Clay

`mineral-terra-sigillata-local-clay` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Satin-to-glossy burnished surface `#C4653D`

> Roman red-gloss Samian ware got its mirror sheen without a glaze — just the very finest clay particles, floated off the top of a settled slurry and burnished to a shine. That siphoned fraction is terra sigillata, 'sealed earth.' A deflocculant makes the coarse particles sink and the finest ones hang in the upper water, so you can literally pour the shine off the top of a jar.

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — A burnished, low-fired terra-sig surface is durable and decorative but not glassy — it is not a food-safe liquid-holding surface on its own and can wear with heavy handling.
- **Timeline:** 1 hour to mix, 30 min to siphon active · 8-24+ hours settling passive
- **Yield:** 2 lb of dry clay yields roughly a pint to a quart of usable terra sig depending on how fine the clay is and how tightly you siphon — the finest, glossiest fraction is only a portion of the batch.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Dry local/wild clay (finely ground)** — 2 lb (≈900 g) — Redart-type iron clays give warm reds; light ball/kaolin clays give buff/white.
- **Water (cool)** — 1 quart (≈950 mL) per 2 lb clay — Standard ratio is 1 quart water : 2 lb dry clay.
- **Deflocculant (sodium silicate and/or soda ash)** — ≈0.5% of dry clay weight (e.g., 0.25% soda ash + 0.25% sodium silicate) — Too much flocculates it back — measure precisely.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Tall clear jar or graduated container, Scale accurate to grams, Siphon tube or turkey baster, Soft brush for application, Smooth stone / plastic sheet / soft cloth for burnishing  ·  _Optional:_ Hydrometer to target specific gravity ~1.15, Ball mill for smoother sig, pH strips

**Steps**

1. Dissolve the deflocculant (≈0.5% of the dry clay weight) into the measured water — a little in hot water first helps it disperse.
2. Stir the ground dry clay into the deflocculated water at ~1 quart per 2 lb of clay. Shake or stir vigorously, or ball-mill briefly.
3. Let the jar stand undisturbed and covered for 8-24 hours. The deflocculant drives the coarse particles to the bottom while the finest clay stays suspended in the upper layer.
4. Siphon or baster off only the fine, cloudy upper layer — do not disturb the sediment. That upper fraction is your terra sigillata.
5. Optionally concentrate it by letting it settle again and pouring off excess clear water; aim for a thin cream (specific gravity around 1.1-1.2).
6. Brush 2-4 thin coats onto leather-hard-to-bone-dry ware, letting each lose its wet sheen, then burnish with a soft cloth or smooth stone for gloss.
7. Bisque fire low (around cone 06-04); higher firing dulls the sheen as the surface begins to sinter.

**Variations & modifiers.** Iron-rich local clay = classic Roman red; ball clay/kaolin = buff to near-white; blend clays or add a little mason stain to the sig for color. Fire lower for more sheen, higher for durability at the cost of gloss. A smoke/pit firing over terra sig gives rich carbon blushes.

**Preservation / curing.** Store the finished sig sealed; it keeps for months (skim any mold, re-shake before use). On ware, a burnished-and-low-fired terra sig is a permanent decorative surface but stays slightly porous — seal food or decorative-only pieces with wax or keep them non-utilitarian.

**Beyond the recipe.** The coarse sediment left at the jar bottom is not waste — dry it and it is a serviceable plastic clay or slip. Colored terra sigs also make excellent brushable engobes and, thinned, a decorating slip for majolica-style low-fire work.

**Safety**

- Sodium silicate and soda ash are alkaline — wear gloves and eye protection and avoid getting mixing water in eyes.
- Ground dry clay is respirable silica — grind and handle the dry clay masked; make the slurry as soon as possible.
- Burnished terra sig is not a glassy, food-safe glaze; do not treat low-fired sig surfaces as safe for holding food or drink.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect the source clay only with the landowner's consent.

**Sources**

### Iron Clay: Earthenware vs. Stoneware

`mineral-iron-clay-earthenware-stoneware` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Terracotta red to toasty brown `#8C4A32`

> The same iron that stains a wild clay red is what caps how hot you can fire it: push an iron-rich terracotta past its range and it bloats, slumps, or turns to a black bubbled slag. Earthenware clays mature low (roughly cone 010-04); stonewares vitrify dense and waterproof higher (about cone 5-10). The only way to know which yours is, is to fire a ladder of test tiles and watch where it tightens up — and where it fails.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Fired to its correct maturing cone the body is durable; earthenware stays porous (needs a glaze liner to hold liquids), stoneware vitrifies waterproof. Food-safety still rides on a tested glaze.
- **Timeline:** 3-4 hours (tiles, marking, loading) active · Days of drying plus multiple firings passive
- **Yield:** This is a diagnostic card: a few pounds of clay yields a tile ladder plus a couple of trial pots. The payoff is knowing your clay's cone, not volume.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Processed iron-rich wild clay** — Enough for 6-10 test tiles + trial pots — Red/brown color signals iron; iron is a flux that lowers the melting point.
- _(optional)_ **Optional flux or temper** — To taste — A little talc or whiting can nudge a body; sand/grog opens it for thermal shock.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Test kiln with pyrometric cones or accurate controller, Test-tile molds or slab, Ruler + marker for shrinkage line (mark 10 cm), Scale for absorption test, P100 mask  ·  _Optional:_ Boiling setup for the fired-absorption test, Kiln shelf posts to catch bloating/melting drips

**Steps**

1. Make identical test tiles, each with a scored 10 cm reference line; record the wet length, then the bone-dry length to get drying shrinkage.
2. Fire tiles in steps across a range — e.g., cone 06, 04, 1, 5, 8 — placing each on a scrap shelf or in a catch-tray in case it melts.
3. After each firing, re-measure the line for fired shrinkage: shrinkage climbs to a maximum as the body densifies, then the body swells/bloats if overfired — that peak marks maturity.
4. Run the absorption test: weigh a fired tile, boil and soak it, weigh again; low absorption (roughly under 3%) means it is vitrifying like stoneware, high absorption means it is staying earthenware-porous.
5. Watch for failure signs: black coring, blistering, warping, or slumping mean you have passed the body's ceiling — back off to the last good cone.
6. Fire actual ware at that proven cone; glaze earthenware bodies to make them hold liquid, since they never fully vitrify.

**Variations & modifiers.** To extend an iron earthenware upward, blend in a more refractory light clay or reduce iron by blending. To lower a stubborn body, a touch of flux (talc, whiting, or feldspar) drops the maturing cone. Reduction firing deepens iron colors and can flux the body further; oxidation keeps brighter terracotta reds.

**Preservation / curing.** Unfired body stores wet and sealed. Correctly matured stoneware is waterproof and dishwasher-durable; earthenware stays porous and must be glaze-lined for utility. Overfired, bloated ware is scrap — recover only as grog.

**Beyond the recipe.** Overfired, bloated, or melted tiles crush into grog and even into a raw slip glaze (some iron clays melt to a glossy brown 'slip glaze' at stoneware temperatures — the origin of Albany-type slips). Well-matured scraps become the sig and temper feedstock for other cards.

**Safety**

- Firing unknown wild clay can bloat or melt onto the shelf — always fire test tiles on a scrap shelf or catch-tray and vent the kiln (fumes from organics and sulfur).
- Dry clay and any crushing of overfired scrap are silica hazards — process wet or masked.
- Do not assume an iron wild-clay pot is food-safe; earthenware needs a tested food-safe glaze liner, and no raw or crazed surface should hold food.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect the clay only with consent.

**Sources**

- [Ceramic Arts Network — A Simple Test Determines the Best Firing Temperature for a Clay Body](https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/daily/article/A-Simple-Test-Determines-the-Best-Firing-Temperature-for-a-Clay-Body)
- [Digitalfire — Earthenware (glossary)](https://digitalfire.com/glossary/earthenware)

### Limestone & Marble as Whiting Flux

`mineral-whiting-limestone-flux-note` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Warm, tightened low-fire body `#EDE6D2`

> Limestone and marble are the same molecule — calcium carbonate — the metamorphic one just recrystallized. Ground fine it becomes 'whiting,' the most common calcium flux in ceramics: about 5% in a porous earthenware body helps counter the moisture expansion that makes glazes craze, and it is the backbone calcium of countless glazes. Fire it and the carbonate burns off as CO2 (the lime cycle in miniature), leaving reactive calcium oxide behind.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-limestone`, `mineral-marble`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — As a body addition whiting improves glaze fit and durability; too much makes a body short/weak. Decorative-durable, with food-safety governed by the glaze, not the whiting.
- **Timeline:** 2-3 hours (grinding, calcining, blending, tiles) active · Days drying + firing passive
- **Yield:** A little goes far: hundreds of grams of whiting flux a large batch of glaze or body. Self-grinding limestone is laborious; this card is about a mapped source for a workhorse additive.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Whiting (calcium carbonate) ground from mapped limestone or marble** — ~5% as a body addition (much more as a glaze flux) — Grind fine (~200 mesh); marble and limestone are both CaCO3.
- **Base clay body** — Balance — Any porous earthenware body prone to moisture-expansion crazing benefits.
- **Water** — As needed — Process wet to control dust.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Crusher + ball mill/muller, 200-mesh screen, Scale, P100 respirator, Kiln + glazed test tiles  ·  _Optional:_ Acid-fizz test kit (vinegar) to confirm carbonate, Test kiln for glaze-fit ladders

**Steps**

1. Confirm the stone is carbonate: a drop of vinegar on limestone or marble fizzes (CO2). That fizz is the working chemistry.
2. Crush and grind to a fine powder (~200 mesh), processing wet or masked — calcium carbonate dust is a nuisance/respiratory irritant and often carries free silica.
3. For a body addition, blend ~5% whiting into a porous earthenware body to reduce delayed moisture-expansion crazing.
4. For glaze use, weigh whiting as the calcium flux in a tested glaze recipe (calcium is a high-temperature flux giving hardness and durability).
5. Fire and note that above ~1650-1700°F the carbonate decomposes, releasing CO2 and leaving calcium oxide — vent the kiln and don't seal thick calcium layers that could bloat.
6. Test glaze fit on tiles; adjust whiting to tune the craze/shiver balance.

**Variations & modifiers.** As a glaze flux, more calcium = harder, more matte, more durable glass (to a point); too much crawls or matte-crazes. As a body addition keep it low (~5%). Dolomitic limestone adds magnesium (different, more matte effect). Calcined lime is caustic — see safety.

**Preservation / curing.** Dry whiting keeps indefinitely, sealed. In fired ware calcium is a permanent, durable flux. Beware 'lime pops': coarse limestone grains left in a body absorb moisture after firing, slake, and pop chips off the ware — grind fine to avoid this.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same limestone/marble, burned hotter to quicklime and slaked, is the lime of lime plaster, limewash, and mortar — the full lime cycle. Marble also carves (the mineral-set carving card), and marble/limestone dust is a filler in gesso and clay-plaster.

**Safety**

- Grinding limestone/marble makes respirable dust that frequently contains free silica — grind wet, wear P100, never dry-sweep.
- Calcining carbonate to quicklime (CaO) produces a strongly caustic material that burns skin, eyes, and lungs and reacts violently with water — do not attempt without proper training and PPE.
- Coarse lime grains cause lime pops in fired ware — always grind fine.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped limestone/marble only with consent; glaze food-safety depends on a properly fluxed, tested glaze (never lead).

**Sources**

- [Digitalfire — Calcium Carbonate (material)](https://digitalfire.com/material/calcium+carbonate)
- [Digitalfire — Glaze Crazing (troubleshooting)](https://digitalfire.com/trouble/glaze+crazing)
- [Glazy — Major Oxides in Glazes](https://help.glazy.org/concepts/oxides)

### Pit-Fire Primitive Body

`mineral-pit-fire-primitive-body` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Smoke-blushed black, grey & flame-orange `#5A4636`

> The oldest fired pots on Earth came out of open fires and pits, and the bodies that survive that fast, uneven heat are heavily tempered and open. Load a wild clay with 20-30% sand or grog and it drains steam and shrugs off thermal shock; then the fire itself becomes the glaze, painting carbon-black, dove-grey, and flashed-orange blushes where flame, ash, and smoke touch the burnished surface.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-quartz`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Pit-fired ware is low-fired, porous, and decorative — durable as sculpture but NOT waterproof or food-safe for holding liquids; the smoke surface can rub with heavy handling.
- **Timeline:** 4-6 hours (building + tending the burn) active · Weeks to fully dry ware; a firing cools overnight passive
- **Yield:** A small pit fires several pieces at once; expect some losses to thermal cracking, especially early on. The reward is one-of-a-kind surfaces no glaze can copy.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Tempered wild-clay body (open, grogged)** — 20-30% aggregate — High temper = steam escape + thermal-shock survival.
- **Clean sand and/or grog (mapped silica/quartz)** — Included in the 20-30% — Process silica wet/masked.
- **Combustibles for the pit (sawdust, wood, dung, seaweed, banana peels, copper carbonate, salt for color)** — Fills the pit — These make the color/blush effects, not a glaze.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Fire pit or metal container (legal burn site), Sawdust/wood/combustibles, Burnishing stone, Metal tongs + heat gloves, Fire extinguisher/water on hand, P100 mask for clay processing  ·  _Optional:_ Terra sigillata for a pre-burnished surface, Copper/iron/salt for flashing color, Metal saggar/foil for controlled effects

**Steps**

1. Build with a well-tempered open body (20-30% sand/grog) so ware can take rapid, uneven heating without cracking.
2. Dry pieces very thoroughly — weeks if thick — and optionally coat with terra sigillata and burnish for smoother, shinier blush surfaces.
3. Pre-warm ware near the fire or in a low kiln to drive off all moisture; trapped water causes explosive spalling in a fast fire.
4. Nest the pots in sawdust in the pit, layer combustibles and colorants (copper for reds/greens, salt, seaweed, dung) around them, then build the fire on top.
5. Burn hot and let it fall to embers; the smoky, oxygen-starved smolder drives carbon into the porous body for blacks and greys, while flame contact flashes oranges.
6. Let the pit cool completely before unloading; wax or burnish the cooled ware to bring up the smoke colors.

**Variations & modifiers.** More temper = safer firing, coarser look. Copper carbonate gives reds/pinks; iron gives warm browns; table salt and seaweed give fumed halos; a foil saggar concentrates effects. Reduction-heavy smother = blacker; more air = more orange flashing. Terra-sig + burnish before firing yields the classic glossy blackware.

**Preservation / curing.** Pit-fired ware is porous and decorative — seal with wax or a food-safe post-fire treatment only for non-utilitarian use; it will not hold water reliably. Keep out of the dishwasher; the carbon surface is somewhat fragile. Store dry.

**Beyond the recipe.** The technique doubles as the traditional method behind Pueblo blackware and burnished redware. Leftover grog/temper feeds other bodies; the ash and charcoal from the pit can be washed and tested as a wood-ash glaze flux for future high-fire work.

**Safety**

- Open/pit firing needs a legal, controlled burn site, clear of overhangs and dry brush, with water/extinguisher on hand and attention to burn bans and air rules.
- Under-dried ware explodes in a fast fire — dry and pre-warm thoroughly; wear a face shield and stand clear when firing.
- Clay processing (sand, grog, dry clay) is a silica hazard — process wet or masked.
- Pit-fired ware is porous and low-fired — not food-safe for holding food or liquids; occurrence is not permission for the clay you dig.

- [Digitalfire — Grog (thermal-shock temper)](https://digitalfire.com/glossary/grog)
- [Linda Arbuckle — Earthenware Clays (handout)](https://www.lindaarbuckle.com/handouts/clay-earthenware.pdf)

### Silica & Quartz Body Opener

`mineral-silica-quartz-body-opener` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Pale, tighter-fitting body `#D8CFC4`

> Quartz is the quiet skeleton inside almost every clay body. Added as fine silica it fills the body, stiffens it against slumping, and — crucially — its own thermal expansion 'squeezes' the glaze on cooling to help keep it from crazing. Flint, chert, and Arkansas novaculite are all just microcrystalline SiO2; ground fine, they do the same job that bagged 'flint' does in a studio recipe.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-silica`, `mineral-quartz`, `mineral-novaculite`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Correctly proportioned silica improves glaze fit and body durability; too much free quartz can craze/dunt ware, so this is decorative-durable until glaze fit is proven food-safe.
- **Timeline:** 2-4 hours (crushing, grinding, screening, blending) active · Days for wet processing and drying passive
- **Yield:** A small test body (e.g., 2 kg) uses only 100-500 g of fine silica; grinding your own is slow, so this card is about a targeted additive, not bulk production. Expect meaningful loss to oversize during screening.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Mapped silica: chert / flint / quartz / novaculite (SiO2)** — 5-25% of dry body weight — Must be ground to a fine powder (target ~200 mesh) — coarse quartz causes dunting.
- **Plastic clay body (wild or ball clay)** — Balance of the recipe — Supplies plasticity and alumina the pure silica lacks.
- **Water** — As needed — Process everything wet to control silica dust.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Jaw crusher or heavy mortar (calcining optional to ease crushing), Ball mill or muller to grind fine, 200-mesh screen, P100 respirator + wet setup, Scale  ·  _Optional:_ Test kiln for fit tests, Dilatometer access (advanced) or simple craze/shiver test tiles

**Steps**

1. Identify the silica: clear/white quartz, banded chert/flint, or Arkansas novaculite (whetstone-grade microcrystalline quartz) all work; keep it clean of contaminants.
2. Crush coarse, then grind to a fine powder. Some potters heat (calcine) hard quartz to ~red heat and quench to make it crumble more easily — do this carefully and outdoors.
3. Screen to ~200 mesh; discard oversize back into the crusher. Coarse quartz particles left in the body cause cracks (dunting) on cooling.
4. Do all crushing, grinding, and screening wet or inside a respirator — SiO2 dust is the classic silicosis hazard.
5. Blend the fine silica into the plastic clay: start around 10% by dry weight for a filler/opener, wedging or slip-mixing to full uniformity.
6. Make craze/shiver test tiles glazed with your standard glaze; adjust silica up (less crazing) or down (less shivering/dunting) to dial in glaze fit.

**Variations & modifiers.** As an 'opener' quartz reduces shrinkage and warping like grog but is finer. Line-blend 5/10/15/20/25% silica against a fixed glaze to map the craze-to-shiver window. Chert and flint behave like quartz; novaculite is prized for its extreme fineness and purity. Free quartz above ~25% risks cristobalite/dunting problems at stoneware temperatures.

**Preservation / curing.** Dry silica powder keeps indefinitely, sealed and dry (mask when opening). In the fired body silica is permanent; correct proportions give durable, craze-resistant ware, while excess can leave ware prone to cracking under thermal or mechanical stress.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same fine silica is a glaze former (the primary glass-maker in most glazes) and a kiln-wash ingredient. Coarser rejected quartz becomes body temper or pit-fire aggregate. Novaculite's other life is as a whetstone — the sharpening card in the mineral set.

**Safety**

- Crystalline silica dust from quartz/chert/flint/novaculite is the leading silicosis hazard in ceramics — grind and screen wet, wear P100, and never dry-sweep.
- Calcining/quenching quartz throws hot fragments and can spall — do it outdoors with eye protection and heavy gloves.
- Too-coarse or too-much free quartz causes dunting (cracked ware) and cristobalite conversion; keep it fine and moderate.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped silica only with landowner consent; leave protected outcrops untouched.

### Talc Low-Fire Body (from Soapstone)

`mineral-soapstone-talc-lowfire-body` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Smooth chalky white low-fire body `#E7E2D6`

> Half the low-fire white clay in America is essentially talc and ball clay. Talc — the soft mineral that soapstone is mostly made of — raises a body's thermal expansion just enough to keep low-fire glazes from crazing, and a 50:50 talc-to-ball-clay slip is famously easy to cast with remarkably low shrinkage. But soapstone carries a warning glazed onto its own reputation: it can host tremolite asbestos, so any grinding of your own must be wet and respirated.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-soapstone`, `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — A talc body fired to cone 04 is durable and, with a well-fit glaze, craze-resistant; it stays porous (low-fire) so food-holding ware needs a tested food-safe glaze liner.
- **Timeline:** 2-3 hours mixing/testing active · Days drying; hazard-controlled grinding if self-milling talc passive
- **Yield:** A modest test body (2-4 kg) is plenty to prove the recipe. Self-grinding soapstone for talc is slow and hazardous; the honest recommendation is to buy milled talc and reserve mapped soapstone for carving.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Talc (commercially milled ceramic talc strongly preferred over self-ground soapstone)** — 40-60% of the body — Soapstone/steatite may contain asbestos and free quartz — buy asbestos-free ceramic talc unless you can verify and wet-grind a known-clean stone.
- **Ball clay** — Balance (≈50%) — Supplies plasticity; a classic hobby casting body is 50 talc : 50 ball clay.
- _(optional)_ **Deflocculant + water (for casting slip)** — As needed — For slip casting; a plastic version uses just water.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Scale, Mixing bucket / blunger for slip, P100 respirator + HEPA vacuum, Test tiles + glaze for craze testing, Kiln to cone 08-04  ·  _Optional:_ Wet lapidary/tile saw if cutting soapstone, Plaster casting molds, Dilatometer/craze-test setup

**Steps**

1. Source talc safely: for anything you will handle as powder, use commercially milled, asbestos-free ceramic talc. Only consider self-processing soapstone if you can verify it is asbestos-free (Vermont soapstones are often clean; New York and California stone are notorious for asbestos).
2. If cutting/grinding your own soapstone, do it wet with a respirator and HEPA control — never dry-grind steatite.
3. Weigh a starting body of 50% talc : 50% ball clay by dry weight (talc can range 40-60% depending on the effect you want).
4. For a casting slip, disperse with deflocculant and water; for a plastic body, wedge with just enough water to throw or hand-build.
5. Glaze test tiles and fire to cone 04 (talc bodies commonly mature cone 08-04); check that the raised body expansion has pulled the glaze into compression (no crazing).
6. Adjust talc up if glazes still craze, down if glazes shiver or the body gets too short/non-plastic.

**Variations & modifiers.** More talc = higher body expansion, better craze resistance, easier casting, lower shrinkage — but a shorter, less plastic body. Adding grog to a talc body improves thermal-shock resistance (a route to flameware). Talc also whitens the body. Do not fire talc bodies to stoneware temperatures — they are low-fire by design.

**Preservation / curing.** Dry talc keeps indefinitely, sealed and masked. Talc bodies dry and fire with notably low shrinkage, so they warp less; fired low-fire ware stays porous and needs glaze for utility. Cured plaster casting molds from these slips last many casts.

**Beyond the recipe.** Talc is also a low-fire glaze flux (a magnesium/silica source giving matte, buttery surfaces). Soapstone's better craft life is as a carving stone and a heat-retaining griddle/bakeware stone — see the soapstone carving card in the mineral set.

**Safety**

- Soapstone/steatite and serpentine can contain tremolite asbestos and free quartz — never dry-cut or dry-grind; work wet with a P100 respirator and HEPA vacuum, or avoid self-processing and buy asbestos-free ceramic talc.
- Cosmetic/industrial talc can be quartz- or asbestos-contaminated; use ceramic-grade talc from a supplier who certifies asbestos-free.
- Talc-body dust is still respirable silica/magnesium silicate — mask and wet-clean.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped soapstone only with consent; and low-fire ware needs a tested food-safe glaze for food use.

### Tempered Wild-Clay Body

`mineral-wild-clay-temper-body` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Speckled earthy stoneware `#9B6A4A`

> The oldest fix in pottery is also the simplest: a too-sticky, over-shrinking clay is opened up by mixing in something already fired — grog — or clean sand. The ancients crushed old pots back into their bodies for exactly this. Temper reduces drying shrinkage and cracking (one test dropped drying shrinkage from 8% to 6.5% with 20% aggregate) and buys the thermal-shock resistance a pit or raku fire demands.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-quartz`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A well-tempered, correctly fired body is durable and long-lived; food-safety still comes from a tested glaze and full firing, not from the temper.
- **Timeline:** 1-2 hours mixing and wedging per batch active · Overnight+ to let the mix homogenize; days to dry ware passive
- **Yield:** Yield ≈ input clay plus the added temper weight; 80:10:10 turns 8 lb of clay into ~10 lb of body. Expect the tempered body to feel firmer and shrink noticeably less than the untempered clay.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Processed wild clay body** — 80 parts by weight — From the wild-clay processing card, at wedgeable stiffness.
- _(optional)_ **Clean silica sand (mapped silica/quartz, crushed & screened)** — 10-20 parts — Screen to a consistent mesh (~40-70). Prepare wet — silica dust causes silicosis.
- _(optional)_ **Grog (crushed pre-fired clay/bisque)** — 10-20 parts — Combine sand + grog to roughly 20% total aggregate for hand-building/pit firing.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Scale, Screens for sizing sand/grog, Wedging surface (plaster or canvas), N95/P100 mask, Water for wet processing  ·  _Optional:_ Mortar & pestle or jaw-style crusher for making grog, Test-tile ruler, Pug mill for large batches

**Steps**

1. Decide the aggregate load: ~10% for smoother throwing bodies, up to ~20-30% for hand-built or pit/raku work needing thermal-shock resistance and low shrinkage.
2. Make grog by crushing scrap bisque or old pots, then screen to size; prepare or buy clean silica sand and screen it too. Do all crushing/screening wet or masked — this is pure silica dust.
3. Weigh clay and aggregate. A common starting recipe is clay:sand:grog of 80:10:10 by weight (i.e., ~20% total temper).
4. Wedge the aggregate evenly into the plastic clay until no dry pockets or streaks remain; add a little water if the mix stiffens.
5. Rest the tempered body a day so moisture equalizes, then re-wedge before use.
6. Fire test tiles alongside your first pieces to confirm the temper hasn't pushed the body too porous or shifted its maturing cone.

**Variations & modifiers.** Coarser sand/grog = more open, tooth-y body good for large forms and thermal shock, but rougher to throw. Finer temper = smoother but less shrink relief. Line-blend temper at 3%, 7%, 15%, and 20% on test tiles to find your sweet spot. Grog adds thermal-shock resistance more than plain sand and shows as speckle in the fired body.

**Preservation / curing.** Store sealed and moist like any clay body; tempered bodies age fine. Because temper opens the body, tempered ware dries more evenly and cracks less — but stays more porous, so it usually needs a glaze liner for any liquid-holding use.

**Beyond the recipe.** Leftover screened sand and grog store indefinitely for the next batch or for a coarse pit-fire/raku body. Very coarse grog also makes a good kiln-shelf wadding and a non-slip additive for wedging bats.

**Safety**

- Silica sand, quartz, chert, and grog are all crystalline silica — crush and screen wet or with a P100 respirator; silicosis is cumulative and irreversible.
- Never dry-sweep silica or grog dust; wet-mop or HEPA-vacuum.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped silica/quartz only with consent and never from protected land.
- A porous tempered body is not food-safe on its own; use a tested food-safe glaze liner for anything holding food or drink.

### Wild Clay: Field Test & Processing

`mineral-wild-clay-field-test-processing` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Buff-to-red plastic body `#B06B4A`

> Almost any exposed clay bank — a road cut, a creek meander, a builder's excavation — holds a clay that once made a local brick or roof tile. The test for whether it will hold a shape is beautifully simple: roll a coil and bend it around your finger. If it wraps without cracking, it's plastic enough to throw. Occurrence is not permission — a mapped deposit is not a dig site; get the landowner's or manager's yes before you take a bucket.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A properly wedged, dried, and bisque-fired wild-clay body is durable indefinitely; food-safety depends entirely on the glaze and full vitrification, not the raw clay.
- **Timeline:** 3-5 hours across several days (digging, slaking, screening, wedging) active · 3-14 days settling and drying passive
- **Yield:** A 2-gallon bucket of raw bank clay typically yields 8-15 lb of usable processed body after grit and water are removed — enough for a handful of small pots. Expect real losses to sand and stones; a sandy bank can lose half its volume.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild clay (mapped clay deposit)** — 1-2 buckets raw — Occurrence is not permission — collect only with the landowner/land manager's consent, and never in parks or protected areas.
- **Water** — 2-3x the clay volume — For slaking to a milk-thin slurry.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Buckets (2-3), Window screen + progressively finer sieves (window screen down to ~80-100 mesh), Stir stick or drill paddle, Plaster bat or old bedsheet on plaster/wood for drying, N95 or better dust mask  ·  _Optional:_ Ball mill or blender for a small test batch, Shrinkage ruler / test tiles, Test kiln access for fire tests

**Steps**

1. Field test first: dig a walnut-sized lump, remove pebbles and roots, and roll a pencil-thick coil. Wrap it around your finger — no cracks means it is plastic enough to use; heavy cracking means it needs a more plastic clay blended in or is too sandy.
2. Dry the raw clay completely — bone-dry clay slakes fastest and most fully.
3. Slake: drop the dry lumps into a bucket of water (clay into water, not water onto clay) and let them collapse on their own for hours to a day. Stir to a smooth, milk-thin slurry.
4. Screen out roots and gravel through a window screen, then work down through finer sieves. Wear a mask whenever the material is dry — all clay dust is respirable silica and causes silicosis.
5. Levigate to drop sand: keep the slurry thin, let it stand ~10 minutes so sand settles, and pour or siphon the fine clay-in-water off the top, leaving the grit behind. Repeat for a smoother body.
6. Concentrate: let the poured-off slip settle for a day or more, siphon the clear water, then dry the thick slip on a plaster bat or a sheet over plaster until it reaches wedgeable stiffness.
7. Wedge thoroughly to remove air, then make test tiles: mark a 10 cm line on each, measure after bone-dry and again after bisque and glaze firings to learn the body's shrinkage and maturing cone.

**Variations & modifiers.** Too sticky/short: blend 10-30% fine sand or grog as temper (see the temper card). Too sandy: levigate more aggressively or blend with a more plastic ball clay. A magnet dragged through dry, screened clay pulls out coarse iron. Blend two wild clays to average out their behavior.

**Preservation / curing.** Store wet clay sealed in plastic to age — aging improves plasticity as bacteria develop. Dried, screened clay powder keeps indefinitely sealed and dry (mask when handling). Fire only after test tiles confirm the maturing range so ware neither stays porous nor bloats.

**Beyond the recipe.** The levigated-out sand and grit is clean temper for the next batch or for a coarser pit-fire body. The finest suspended fraction, siphoned separately, is the starting stock for terra sigillata. Iron-stained wild clay can also be dried and ground as a slip or a raw glaze colorant.

**Safety**

- All clay dust is respirable crystalline silica — process wet whenever possible, wear N95/P100 when handling dry material, and never sweep dry (wet-mop or HEPA-vacuum).
- Occurrence is not permission: a mapped deposit conveys no right to dig. Get explicit landowner or land-manager consent; digging is prohibited in most parks and protected lands.
- Do not dig into stream banks or wetlands where it causes erosion or violates water rules; take small, backfill, and leave the bank stable.
- Unknown wild clay can contain natural contaminants — never assume a raw wild-clay pot is food-safe until it is fully vitrified and, for food surfaces, lined with a tested food-safe glaze.

### Iron-Clay Celadon

`mineral-iron-clay-celadon` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Translucent jade-green celadon `#7fa389`

> Celadon is what happens when you starve a glaze of oxygen: a whisper of iron — under 2% — that would read yellow-brown in an ordinary fire instead turns translucent jade-green when the kiln burns reduction. Song-dynasty potters prized it as a stand-in for carved jade, and the color still comes from the humblest ingredients — feldspar, silica, limestone whiting, and a pinch of iron, some of it hiding in the wild clay itself. Get the iron and the atmosphere right and glass becomes stone-green light.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-limestone`, `mineral-marble`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A properly fitted celadon fired to cone 10 is durable and food-safe; the low iron poses no leaching concern, but check fit to avoid crazing.
- **Timeline:** 2-3 hours mixing and glazing active · A reduction firing to cone 10 passive
- **Yield:** A 1000 g batch glazes ~18-22 pieces. Success hinges on the firing atmosphere more than the recipe — budget a couple of reduction firings to dial it in.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Feldspar (Custer/Kona)** — 27-56% — Alkali flux; higher soda/potash pushes celadon bluer.
- **Silica (quartz/flint)** — 30-33% — High silica supports the blue-green and glassiness.
- **Whiting (limestone/marble calcium carbonate)** — 14-20% — Calcium flux for the classic transparent melt.
- _(optional)_ **Kaolin (EPK)** — 0-20% — Alumina and suspension (as in the Alfred celadon).
- **Red or yellow iron oxide** — 0.6-2% — The colorant — keep low; more iron shifts toward olive then tenmoku.
- _(optional)_ **Iron-bearing wild clay** — optional partial substitution — A local red clay can supply some of both the clay and the iron — the mapped-material route.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator, Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve, Reduction-capable kiln (gas/wood), Gloves  ·  _Optional:_ Oxygen probe, Ball mill, Test tiles

**Steps**

1. Choose a tested base — e.g. Alfred celadon: silica 33 / whiting 20 / feldspar 27 / EPK 20, plus ~0.5-1% iron.
2. Weigh accurately; iron level is the whole game, so measure the colorant to a tenth of a gram.
3. Mix into water, sieve twice through 80-mesh for a flawless transparent film.
4. Glaze test tiles at two-three iron levels (0.6, 1, 2%) to bracket the green you want.
5. Fire to cone 10 in reduction: begin body reduction around cone 012-010 and hold moderate reduction to the end so the iron converts to its green ferrous state.
6. Cool and evaluate — oxidation flecks mean the reduction was too light or too late.
7. Check fit; add silica if it crazes. Blue celadons want high silica and low titanium.
8. Food-safe once fit is confirmed — the low iron does not leach.

**Variations & modifiers.** Iron drives color: ~0.5-1% = pale blue-green, ~2% = deeper olive-green, 3%+ = amber then black tenmoku. High soda + high silica + low titanium = bluer; more calcium and titanium = greener/greyer. A pinch of bone ash (1%) can add opalescence. Substitute a little wild red clay to source iron and clay from the map at once.

**Preservation / curing.** Store dry materials sealed. Mixed celadon keeps in a lidded bucket; re-sieve for a clean film. Fired ware is permanent and dishwasher-safe when well fitted.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same base without iron is a clear high-fire liner glaze. High-iron variants of the recipe become tenmoku and iron-saturate glazes — the color family runs straight into oil-spot and kaki.

**Safety**

- Reduction firing produces carbon monoxide — fire gas/wood kilns with excellent ventilation and a CO detector.
- Glaze mixing raises free silica from silica, feldspar, and clay — P100 respirator and wet-cleaning; silicosis risk.
- Grind any foraged limestone/marble whiting or quartz WET and masked.
- This glaze is food-safe by design (low iron, no toxic colorant) once fit is confirmed — but a crazed celadon should still get a liner or refit.
- Handle iron oxide with a mask; the fine powder is a nuisance dust.

### Wood-Ash Glaze (40/40/20)

`mineral-wood-ash-glaze-40-40-20` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Streaky celadon-to-amber ash glaze `#8a9b6c`

> For a thousand years before anyone measured a glaze, the wood ash that drifted onto pots in the kiln melted into an accidental glass — the first glazes were pure serendipity. Sieve out your fireplace ash, blend it 40:40:20 with feldspar and clay, and you rebuild that ancient accident on purpose: the calcium and potash in the ash flux the silica into a runny, streaky celadon that pools amber in the throwing marks. No two batches match, because no two fires burn the same wood.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-silica`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Fired to cone 9-10 and mixed with clean hardwood ash, a well-fitted ash glaze is durable and food-safe on liner surfaces; runny high-iron batches are decorative-only and should be tested for fit.
- **Timeline:** 2-3 hours mixing and glazing active · 1-2 weeks to soak-wash ash, plus a cone 10 firing passive
- **Yield:** A 1000 g dry batch glazes roughly 15-25 mugs depending on thickness. Expect real variability batch-to-batch; ash glazes reward test tiles over hope.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wood ash (hardwood, sieved 30-80 mesh)** — 40% by dry weight — Unwashed ash keeps more soluble flux but is caustic and variable; washed ash is gentler and more repeatable. Hardwood (oak, ash, fruitwood) gives more flux than softwood.
- **Potash or soda feldspar (e.g. Custer, Minspar)** — 40% — The alumina/silica backbone; substitute nepheline syenite to lower melt temp.
- **Ball clay or kaolin (EPK)** — 20% — Adds alumina, suspends the slurry, and stiffens a too-runny melt.
- _(optional)_ **Silica (quartz/flint)** — 0-15% (line-blend addition) — Add to raise melting point and reduce running; helps glaze fit to cut crazing.
- _(optional)_ **Red iron oxide** — 0-4% — Deepens celadon green in reduction; keep low for classic pale celadon.

**Tools** — _Required:_ NIOSH N95/P100 respirator, Chemical-resistant rubber gloves, Safety goggles, 80-mesh sieve, Gram scale, Buckets  ·  _Optional:_ Ball mill, Spray booth, Hydrometer, Test tiles

**Steps**

1. Collect ash only from clean, untreated wood — never painted, pressure-treated, or glued scrap, which carry heavy metals and chlorides.
2. Wearing a P100 respirator and goggles, dry-sieve ash through 30-mesh outdoors or under local exhaust to remove charcoal and nails. Dry ash is caustic and lung-damaging.
3. Optional wash: soak ash in water in a lidded bucket for a week, pour off the caustic (soapy-feeling) water, refill, and repeat until the water runs clear and non-slippery. Wear gloves; the wash water can be as caustic as lye.
4. Weigh 40 ash : 40 feldspar : 20 clay by dry weight. Add colorant/silica per your line blend.
5. Mix into water to a thin-cream consistency, sieve through 80-mesh, and hydrometer to a repeatable specific gravity.
6. Glaze bisqueware thinly — ash glazes run. Leave the bottom 1/2 inch bare and stilt or wad if needed.
7. Fire to cone 9-10; in reduction the iron shifts to celadon green, in oxidation toward amber/tan.
8. Test every new wood source and wash-state on tiles before committing pots.

**Variations & modifiers.** Line-blend the ash:feldspar:silica triangle to tune the melt: more ash = runnier and glossier; more clay = stiffer and more matte; more silica = less running and better fit. Softwood ash and straw/grass ash carry more silica and phosphorus for opalescent 'oil-spot' effects. Layer thin over an iron slip for classic hare's-fur streaking.

**Preservation / curing.** Store dry ingredients sealed and labeled; store mixed glaze in a lidded bucket and re-sieve if it settles. Fired ware is permanent. Do not store wash water — neutralize and dispose per local rules; it is caustic.

**Beyond the recipe.** Spent, neutralized ash-wash sludge can be composted in small amounts (it is essentially lime/potash). Sieved-out charcoal is fine for the garden. Leftover glaze slurry blends into other high-fire bases as a flux booster.

**Safety**

- Dry wood ash is CAUSTIC and contains fine free silica — always sieve outdoors or under exhaust wearing a P100 respirator, goggles, and gloves; silica dust causes irreversible silicosis.
- Wash water and wet ash are strongly alkaline (lye-like) — wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection; can burn skin and eyes.
- Never use ash from treated, painted, or manufactured wood — it concentrates arsenic, chromium, copper, and chlorides.
- Runny ash glazes can weld pots to shelves — glaze thinly and protect kiln shelves.
- Food-safety: high-iron or barium-bearing variants and crazed surfaces are decorative-only. Use a tested clear liner on food-contact areas.

### Limestone / Whiting Flux Glaze

`mineral-whiting-limestone-flux-glaze` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Glossy or calcia-matte clear-to-cream `#e6e0d0`

> Whiting — the humble white powder in nearly every stoneware glaze — is just finely ground limestone or marble: calcium carbonate, the same rock that builds cliffs and cathedrals. Above cone 8 that calcium is one of the most reliable, durable fluxes in ceramics; below it, the same material can go stony and matte instead. Learning to place calcium in a glaze is learning to place the mapped limestone of your own region into the melt.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-limestone`, `mineral-marble`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Calcium-fluxed glazes fired to maturity are among the most durable and food-safe surfaces; over-loaded calcia mattes can be less durable and should be tested.
- **Timeline:** 1-2 hours mixing active · Firing to target cone passive
- **Yield:** A 1000 g batch glazes ~15-20 pieces. A reliable clear base you'll reuse as the parent for many colored variants.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Whiting (calcium carbonate, ground limestone/marble)** — 8-22% — Higher amounts push toward calcia matte above cone 8; commercial whiting is purest, but clean marble/limestone can be milled fine.
- **Feldspar (potash/soda)** — 35-45% — Alkali flux + alumina/silica backbone.
- **Silica (quartz/flint)** — 20-30% — Glass former; balances thermal expansion for fit.
- **Kaolin / ball clay (EPK)** — 15-22% — Alumina for durability and suspension.
- _(optional)_ **Tin oxide or zircon** — 0-10% — Opacifier for a cream/white matte.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator, Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve, Buckets, Gloves  ·  _Optional:_ Ball mill, Hydrometer, Test tiles

**Steps**

1. Weigh a balanced base — a classic starting clear is ~feldspar 40 / silica 28 / whiting 20 / EPK 12 (Alfred-style), adjustable.
2. If milling your own limestone or marble, cut and grind WET to avoid dust, then dry and screen to a fine flour. Respirator on for any dry handling.
3. Mix dry ingredients into water to a light cream, sieve twice through 80-mesh.
4. Hydrometer to a repeatable specific gravity and glaze test tiles.
5. Fire to cone 6-10. Note that below ~cone 8 raw whiting may not fully melt and can produce a satin/matte or pinhole — adjust particle size or swap some calcium to a frit.
6. Increase whiting toward 20-22% (with high firing) to intentionally make a calcium matte; decrease toward 8-12% for a clean gloss.
7. Check fit for crazing; add silica to lower expansion if it crazes.
8. Leach-test any colored version before food use.

**Variations & modifiers.** This is the calcium-flux workhorse: 8-12% whiting reads glossy, 18-22% at cone 9-10 reads matte/stony. Swap part of the whiting for dolomite to add magnesium for a silkier matte. Add 1-3% iron for celadon, 0.5-2% copper (oxidation) for green — copper cautions apply to food surfaces.

**Preservation / curing.** Store dry ingredients sealed; keep milled limestone dry to prevent caking. Mixed glaze keeps indefinitely, lidded; re-sieve if settled. Fired result is permanent.

**Beyond the recipe.** Ground limestone/marble whiting is also the calcium source for glass, lime plaster, and a soil sweetener. Leftover clear base is the ideal liner glaze over decorative outer glazes.

**Safety**

- Grind limestone/marble WET and mask up — quartz/silica impurities and fine carbonate dust are respiratory hazards.
- All glaze mixing raises silica dust from the silica, clay, and feldspar — P100 respirator, wet-clean, never dry-sweep.
- Calcined lime (quicklime) is caustic; limestone glaze materials are not quicklime, but avoid heating raw limestone dust and skin contact with any lime.
- Colored variants (copper, cobalt, manganese) can leach — use tested liners on food contact.
- Zircon opacifiers are safe fired but the raw dust is a respiratory hazard; handle wet where possible.

### Raw Slip Decoration (Engobe)

`mineral-raw-slip-decoration` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Contrasting slip-trailed earth tones `#c9a06a`

> The oldest decoration on pottery is just clay on clay: a slip — liquid clay — brushed, trailed, or combed onto a leather-hard pot so a light body shows through a dark one, or the reverse. It is the slipware of English country pottery and the mishima of Korea, and it needs no glaze chemistry at all, only a clay that shrinks at the same rate as the pot beneath it. Dig one clay for the body and another for the slip and you can draw with the map itself.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-limestone`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Slip fired under a food-safe clear glaze is durable and food-safe; unglazed slip is porous and decorative-only. Fit (shrinkage match) is what keeps it from flaking.
- **Timeline:** 1-2 hours mixing; minutes to decorate active · Drying and firing passive
- **Yield:** A 1000 g dry batch of slip decorates dozens of pots — decoration uses little material. The limiting factor is fit, not quantity.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Base clay (ball clay/kaolin or wild clay)** — 50-90% (slip) / 25-50% (engobe) — Match the slip's shrinkage to the body; slips are higher-clay, engobes lower-clay with calcined additions.
- _(optional)_ **Calcined clay (calcined kaolin)** — 0-25% (engobe) — Reduces shrinkage so the slip fits drier ware without cracking.
- _(optional)_ **Silica (quartz/flint)** — 0-15% — Adjusts shrinkage and hardness.
- _(optional)_ **Feldspar / whiting (limestone)** — 0-15% — Flux for adhesion and to harden the slip at temperature.
- _(optional)_ **Colorant oxides or stains** — 2-15% — Slips need more colorant than glazes: ~10-15% stain for saturated color; iron, cobalt, and rutile are classic.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator (for dry mixing), Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve, Brushes / slip trailer, Gloves  ·  _Optional:_ Rubber comb, Sgraffito tool, Bulb syringe, Banding wheel

**Steps**

1. Mix a slip at 50-90% clay with water to a smooth pourable cream; sieve through 80-mesh. Wear a respirator while handling dry clay and oxides.
2. For color, blend in 2-15% oxide or stain (more than you'd use in a glaze).
3. Test the slip's fit: brush it on a scrap of the same body at the same moisture and dry it — if it flakes or crawls, add calcined clay/silica to cut shrinkage.
4. Apply to WET-to-leather-hard ware for best bond (high-clay slips can crack on bone-dry pots). Brush, trail, dip, or pour.
5. Decorate: trail lines with a bulb, comb through for feathering, or carve back through for sgraffito.
6. Dry slowly and evenly to avoid the slip pulling off at edges.
7. Bisque, then cover food surfaces with a tested food-safe clear glaze and glaze-fire.
8. Leach-test if you used copper/cobalt/manganese under a food surface.

**Variations & modifiers.** White slip on red body (or the reverse) is the classic contrast; combing two wet-trailed colors gives feathered slipware. Add more calcined clay/silica to apply on drier ware. Underglaze-style detail comes from higher stain loads. Wild-clay slips let the body and decoration both come from mapped material.

**Preservation / curing.** Store slip in a lidded container; re-sieve if it settles or smells sour (harmless, just re-blend). Unglazed fired slip is porous and decorative; glazed slip is permanent and food-safe under a sound clear.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same slip thinned becomes casting or joining slip for handles and attachments. Wild-clay slip doubles as the starting point for a wild-clay slip glaze at higher temperatures.

**Safety**

- Dry clay and oxide powders raise fine free silica and toxic metal dust — respirator (P100), mix wet where possible, wet-clean surfaces.
- Colorant oxides (cobalt, manganese, copper, chrome) are toxic — wear gloves and a mask; keep out of food-contact glaze layers or leach-test.
- Grind any foraged limestone/silica additions WET and masked.
- Unglazed slip is porous and not food-safe — glaze food surfaces with a tested clear.
- Occurrence is not permission: confirm rights before digging body or slip clays.

### Silica / Quartz Glass-Former Addition

`mineral-silica-quartz-glaze-fit` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Harder, brighter, craze-free glaze `#cfd6d8`

> Silica is the glass in glass — SiO2, the same building block of quartz crystal, chert, novaculite whetstones, and petrified wood. Alone it needs ~1700 degrees C to melt, so glazes borrow flux to coax it into liquid at kiln temperatures. But the silica you add does one more quiet, crucial job: it lowers the glaze's thermal expansion, pulling a crazing glaze back into compression on the pot. This isn't a recipe so much as the lever that makes every other glaze fit.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-silica`, `mineral-quartz`, `mineral-novaculite`, `mineral-petrified-wood`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Correcting silica raises hardness and durability and cures crazing, improving food-safety of an otherwise sound glaze; too much silica under-melts and dulls the surface.
- **Timeline:** 2-3 hours across a line blend active · One firing per test round passive
- **Yield:** A line blend is a handful of test tiles, not a batch of pots — but it can rescue an entire glaze recipe and every future pot glazed with it.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Silica (300-mesh quartz/flint)** — add in 5% steps, typically 20-35% of a base — Commercial 300-mesh quartz is safest; foraged quartz/chert/petrified wood must be milled extremely fine — a serious silica-dust job.
- **A crazing base glaze to correct** — remainder — Any glossy glaze that fine-line crazes is the candidate for a silica line blend.
- _(optional)_ **Kaolin (EPK)** — 0-5% — Add alongside silica to keep alumina/silica balanced and glaze glossy rather than dry.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator, Gram scale, Test tiles, 80-mesh sieve, Gloves  ·  _Optional:_ Ball mill (essential if milling foraged quartz), Thermal-shock kettle, Dilatometer (lab)

**Steps**

1. Identify a glaze that crazes (fine cracks) — that means its expansion is too high relative to the clay body.
2. Mix a line blend adding silica in 5% increments (e.g. +5, +10, +15%) to separate cups of the base.
3. If foraging quartz, chert, or petrified wood: cut and grind WET, then ball-mill to a fine flour. Dry silica milling is one of the most dangerous studio tasks for silicosis — commercial 300-mesh silica is strongly preferred.
4. Glaze test tiles from each blend and fire to the target cone.
5. Thermal-shock the fired tiles (heat, then plunge in cold water) and inspect for craze lines under a loupe.
6. Pick the lowest silica addition that eliminates crazing without dulling the surface (too much silica goes stony/under-melted).
7. If added silica makes the glaze dry, add a little more flux (feldspar/whiting) or a touch of kaolin to rebalance.
8. Recheck fit and, for food ware, leach-test the final recipe.

**Variations & modifiers.** Silica and kaolin added together in a ~2:1 ratio keep the alumina/silica balance so gloss survives. If you overshoot and the glaze under-melts (matte, pinholed), pull silica back or add flux. Coarser quartz can be used deliberately for micro-texture in art glazes.

**Preservation / curing.** Store silica sealed and dry. Mixed glaze keeps in a lidded bucket. Fired correction is permanent and improves ware longevity by preventing water ingress through craze lines.

**Beyond the recipe.** Fine milled quartz is also a body filler to open a clay body and reduce warping, and a polishing abrasive (novaculite is literally whetstone quartz). Screened-out coarse quartz makes grog.

**Safety**

- Free crystalline silica is the single biggest chronic hazard in a ceramics studio — inhalation causes irreversible silicosis. Use commercial pre-ground silica, work wet, wear a P100 respirator, and wet-clean surfaces.
- Never dry-mill foraged quartz, chert, novaculite, or petrified wood without full containment, HEPA, and P100 protection; these are near-pure silica.
- Do not dry-sweep glaze dust — it re-suspends respirable silica.
- Correcting fit improves food-safety (no craze-line bacteria harbor) but does not by itself make a colored glaze non-leaching — still leach-test.
- Wet-cut mapped stones (agate/chert/petrified wood) to avoid both silica dust and sharp fracture edges.

### Talc / Soapstone Matte Glaze

`mineral-talc-matte-glaze` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Silky magnesia-matte white/pastel `#ede9e2`

> Talc is soapstone ground to powder — the same soft, soapy stone people carve into bowls and stove liners, chemically a magnesium silicate. Load enough of that magnesium into a glaze and, on slow cooling, it grows microscopic crystals that scatter light into a soft, dry, buttery matte you want to touch. It is the mineral behind the best magnesia mattes at both cone 6 and cone 10 — but soapstone and its cousin serpentine can carry asbestos, so this is a wet-and-masked material from the very first cut.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-soapstone`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-clay`, `mineral-limestone`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — A well-formulated MgO matte (e.g. boron-fluxed) can be durable and food-safe, but under-fluxed talc mattes can be soft and stain-prone — test before food use.
- **Timeline:** 2-3 hours mixing and glazing active · Firing with a slow controlled cool passive
- **Yield:** A 1000 g batch glazes ~15-20 pieces. Expect to tune the cooling curve; the matte is made as much in the cool as in the recipe.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Talc (from soapstone/steatite)** — 12-20% — Commercial cosmetic-free ceramic talc is safest; foraged soapstone must be verified asbestos-free and milled WET.
- **Nepheline syenite or feldspar** — 25-35% — Alkali flux backbone.
- _(optional)_ **Ferro Frit 3134 (boron flux)** — 15-22% — Boron helps the MgO melt cleanly at cone 6 and improves durability/leach resistance.
- _(optional)_ **Whiting (limestone)** — 8-12% — Calcium co-flux.
- **Kaolin (EPK)** — 10-15% — Alumina and suspension.
- **Silica** — 8-12% — Glass former and fit control.
- _(optional)_ **Tin oxide or zircon** — 5-8% — Whitens/opacifies the matte.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator, Gloves, Goggles, Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve  ·  _Optional:_ Ball mill, Programmable kiln (for slow cool), Test tiles

**Steps**

1. If sourcing your own soapstone, verify it is asbestos-free — serpentine-associated soapstone can host asbestos. When in doubt, buy ceramic-grade talc.
2. Any cutting/grinding of soapstone must be WET with HEPA extraction and a P100 respirator — dry soapstone dust may release asbestos fibers.
3. Weigh a boron-fluxed magnesia base (e.g. G2934-style: talc ~15 / frit 3134 ~20 / nepheline ~30 / EPK ~13 / silica ~10 / whiting ~10, plus opacifier).
4. Mix into water to a smooth cream, sieve twice through 80-mesh.
5. Glaze test tiles evenly — mattes show application flaws.
6. Fire to cone 6 (or cone 10) and program a slow cool through the crystallization range to build the microcrystalline matte; a fast cool can go glossy or dry.
7. Check the surface: it should be silky, not chalky. Chalky/dry means under-fluxed — add frit.
8. Cutlery-mark and leach-test before food use.

**Variations & modifiers.** Slow cooling deepens the matte; fast cooling turns it satin. Trade some talc for dolomite to combine magnesium and calcium for a classic dolomite matte at cone 10. Colorants sit softly in magnesia mattes — cobalt goes lavender/pink, rutile breaks streaky. Keep boron/flux adequate so the matte is durable, not soft.

**Preservation / curing.** Store talc and dry materials sealed. Mixed glaze keeps lidded; re-sieve when settled. Fired matte is permanent; avoid acidic-food storage on any under-fluxed matte.

**Beyond the recipe.** Talc is also a low-expansion body flux for craze-resistant earthenware and ovenware bodies. Carved soapstone offcuts become bowls, beads, and stove tiles; scrap grinds (WET) into more talc.

**Safety**

- Soapstone/steatite and serpentine MAY contain asbestos — verify the source or use ceramic talc; cut and grind WET with HEPA and a P100 respirator, never dry.
- All dry glaze handling raises free silica — respirator and wet-clean; silicosis risk.
- Under-fluxed magnesia mattes can be soft and leach or cutlery-mark — leach-test before food contact and prefer boron-fluxed bases for dinnerware.
- Barium is sometimes used for mattes — avoid barium on food surfaces; this recipe uses MgO instead by design.
- Dispose of soapstone grinding slurry as potential asbestos waste per local rules if the source is unverified.

### Terra Sigillata Burnish

`mineral-terra-sigillata-burnish` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Satiny burnished earth-red or ivory `#b5623d`

> Long before glaze, Greek and Roman potters gave their ware a soft glassy shine with nothing but clay refined down to its finest particles — terra sigillata, 'sealed earth,' the sheen on ancient red-figure vases. You let a clay-and-water slurry settle, pour off only the cloud of ultrafine platelets floating on top, brush that on in whisper-thin coats, and buff it before it dries. Burnished and fired low, it becomes a lustrous skin — a glaze substitute made of pure, mapped clay.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — A durable decorative surface, but terra sig is porous and not vitrified — not a food-safe liner on its own; seal or use only on decorative/dry surfaces for food contact.
- **Timeline:** 1-2 hours to mix, plus burnishing active · 12-48 hours settling; a low bisque-range firing passive
- **Yield:** One jar of settled slurry yields plenty of sig for many pots — a thin coat goes a long way. Only the middle fraction is usable, so expect to discard the grit.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Ball clay (e.g. OM4) or red clay (Redart) or wild clay** — 1 part dry clay — White clays give ivory sig; iron clays (Redart, wild red clay) give the classic terracotta red.
- **Water (distilled preferred)** — ~2.5 parts by weight — Roughly a 2.5:1 water-to-clay ratio.
- **Sodium silicate (deflocculant)** — ~0.4% of dry clay weight — Keeps fine particles suspended so the coarse grit settles out; soda ash can partly substitute.
- _(optional)_ **Soda ash** — trace (with red clays) — Pinnell's red-clay method uses a little soda ash plus sodium silicate.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator (for dry clay), Gram scale, Tall clear jar or bucket, Soft brush, Turkey baster or siphon  ·  _Optional:_ Hydrometer, Smooth stone or plastic bag for burnishing, Spray bottle

**Steps**

1. Weigh clay and water at ~1:2.5. Wearing a respirator, add dry clay to water (not the reverse) to limit dust.
2. Dissolve the deflocculant (sodium silicate, ~0.4% of dry clay; add a little soda ash for red clays) and blend the slurry smooth.
3. Let it settle undisturbed 12-48 hours. It separates into a bottom grit layer, a middle usable layer, and a top water layer.
4. Siphon off the top water, then draw off ONLY the fine middle layer — that is the terra sig. Discard the heavy bottom sludge.
5. Adjust to a thin-milk consistency (hydrometer ~1.10-1.15).
6. Brush 2-3 whisper-thin coats onto leather-hard or bone-dry ware; over-thick sig flakes.
7. Before the last coat fully dries, burnish with a soft cloth, plastic bag, or smooth stone for the sheen.
8. Bisque-fire low (around cone 08-04); higher firing burns off the shine.

**Variations & modifiers.** Firing temperature controls sheen: the lower the fire, the waxier and shinier. Color the sig with 2-10% oxides/stains (more than a glaze needs). Layer different-colored sigs for terra-sigillata painting. Post-fire waxing or a pit/smoke firing deepens the surface. Wild red clay makes a beautifully local terracotta sig.

**Preservation / curing.** Store sig in a sealed jar; it keeps for months but re-check consistency (it keeps settling). Fired sheen is permanent but porous — protect food surfaces with a proper liner glaze or keep sig to decorative/exterior areas.

**Beyond the recipe.** The discarded coarse settlings return to the reclaim bucket for clay body. Terra sig also makes a fine resist/underglaze medium and a smoke-fire surface for raku and pit firing.

**Safety**

- Dry clay is fine free silica — add clay to water, wear a P100 respirator, and wet-clean; silicosis is cumulative and permanent.
- Sodium silicate and soda ash are alkaline — wear gloves and avoid eye contact.
- Terra sig is NOT a vitrified, food-safe glaze — it is porous; keep it off food-contact surfaces unless sealed with a fired liner.
- Oxide colorants (cobalt, manganese, copper) are toxic dusts raw — mask and handle wet.
- Verify collecting rights for any wild clay — occurrence is not permission.

**Sources**

### Wild-Clay Slip Glaze (Albany style)

`mineral-wild-clay-slip-glaze` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Glossy tobacco-brown slip glaze `#6b4a2f`

> For most of the 1800s and 1900s, American stoneware crocks wore a single dark glaze dug from one clay bank near Albany, New York — a naturally iron- and lime-rich clay that melts almost entirely on its own into a glossy tobacco brown. When that pit closed, potters spent decades reverse-engineering it. You can chase the same thing under your own boots: a high-iron, high-flux wild clay that becomes a glaze at 60-100% of the recipe.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-limestone`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A well-melted iron slip glaze fired to its clay's maturity is durable and food-capable, but every wild clay is unique — test for melt, fit, and leaching before food use.
- **Timeline:** 3-4 hours processing and testing active · Days to dry-process and settle, plus firing passive
- **Yield:** A 1000 g batch glazes roughly 15-20 pieces. Expect to spend more time testing than glazing; the reward is a glaze that costs almost nothing and is unmistakably local.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Wild iron/lime-rich clay (Albany-style)** — 60-100% — Dark red-brown clays from lake/glacial deposits melt best. Dry, crush, and screen through 80-100 mesh.
- _(optional)_ **Feldspar or nepheline syenite** — 0-25% — Add flux if the raw clay won't fully melt at your cone.
- _(optional)_ **Whiting (calcium carbonate, from limestone/marble)** — 0-15% — Boosts calcium flux for gloss and durability; mapped as ground limestone/marble.
- _(optional)_ **Ferro Frit 3195 or 3134** — 0-20% — The reliable modern route to bring a stubborn wild slip down to cone 6.
- _(optional)_ **Red iron oxide** — 0-3% — Deepens color toward near-black tenmoku territory.

**Tools** — _Required:_ P100 respirator, Gloves, Goggles, 80-100 mesh sieve, Gram scale, Mortar or crusher  ·  _Optional:_ Ball mill, Test tiles, Line-blend cups

**Steps**

1. Confirm you have permission to collect the clay — occurrence on a map is never harvest permission, and many banks are on protected or private land.
2. Fully dry the clay, then crush and screen it dry. Wear a P100 respirator: dry clay dust is fine free silica and causes silicosis.
3. Slake the powder in water and sieve through 80-100 mesh to remove grit and organics.
4. Run a melt test at your target cone: brush the pure slip on a test tile and fire. If it stays dry/matte, it needs added flux.
5. Line-blend flux additions (feldspar, whiting, or frit) in 5-10% steps until the tile gives a smooth glassy surface.
6. Check fit on a fired tile — flex and thermal-shock test; look for crazing or shivering.
7. Once dialed in, glaze ware, keeping feet clean, and fire to the tested cone.
8. Send a dialed-in food-contact recipe for leach testing before using on dinnerware.

**Variations & modifiers.** Higher clay % = darker, drier, more matte; more frit/feldspar = glossier and lower-firing. Add 2-4% titanium or rutile for streaky 'kaki' rust breaks. Layer a thin ash glaze over the top for hare's-fur. Substitute Alberta Slip (the commercial Albany stand-in, 80% slip + 20% Frit 3195 at cone 6) when you can't dig your own.

**Preservation / curing.** Store dry, screened clay in sealed labeled containers to control silica dust. Mixed glaze keeps indefinitely in a lidded bucket; re-sieve after settling. Fired glaze is permanent.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same wild clay, less refined, can temper a stoneware body or make a slip for decoration. Coarse grit screened out is good pathway fill or grog if re-fired and crushed.

**Safety**

- Dry clay processing releases fine free silica — respirator (P100), wet-clean surfaces, never sweep dry; silicosis is permanent and cumulative.
- Iron-rich wild clays can carry unknown minerals — leach-test before any food-contact use.
- Some regional clays contain gypsum/lime lumps that pop or lime-blow in the fire; screen thoroughly.
- Occurrence is not permission: verify legal collecting rights and leave the bank stable and un-eroded.
- Wear gloves; prolonged wet-clay contact dries and cracks skin.

### Agate & jasper tumbling

`mineral-lapidary-tumbling` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Glassy polished agate `#9c5a45`

> A rock tumbler is patience in a barrel: rough agate, jasper, and petrified wood ride for weeks through coarse-to-fine grit and water until abrasion rounds and polishes them to glass. The catch is that these are all silica (SiO2) — dumping a barrel or handling dry grit slurry can free respirable silica dust, so the whole process stays wet and the sludge never goes down the drain.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-agate`, `mineral-quartz`, `mineral-petrified-wood`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A polished stone is permanent. Agate, jasper, and silicified wood hold a glassy polish indefinitely.
- **Timeline:** 1-2 hours of tending across the run active · 3-5 weeks of tumbling per batch passive
- **Yield:** A barrel of polished stones — dozens of small pieces — per multi-week run. Expect some to chip or fail to polish.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Rough agate, jasper, quartz, or petrified wood** — fill barrel ~2/3 — Best at Mohs 6-8; keep pieces similar in hardness and size.
- **Silicon-carbide grit set (coarse 60/90, medium 120/220, fine 500/600)** — per barrel instructions — Each stage in its own sequence; never mix stages.
- **Polishing compound (cerium/tin/aluminum oxide)** — final stage — Optional pre-polish (1200) before the polish step.
- _(optional)_ **Ceramic media / plastic pellets** — as filler — Cushions stones and carries slurry in later stages.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Rotary (or vibratory) tumbler, Silicon-carbide grits + polish, Strainer (dedicated, not kitchen), N95 mask for handling dry grit/dust, Bucket  ·  _Optional:_ Plastic colander, Old toothbrush for cleaning stones, Settling bucket for slurry disposal

**Steps**

1. Sort rough by hardness and size; load the barrel about two-thirds full with stones plus water and coarse (60/90) grit.
2. Run the coarse stage ~1 week, then STOP: rinse stones and barrel thoroughly so no coarse grit contaminates the next stage.
3. Repeat with medium (120/220) grit, then fine (500/600), rinsing completely between every stage.
4. Run the polish stage with polishing compound and (optionally) cushioning media until the stones gleam.
5. Handle grit and slurry wet; wear an N95 when opening or cleaning anything that could have dried to powder.
6. Let slurry settle in a bucket and dispose of the solids in the trash — never pour silica sludge down the drain (it clogs and is a dust source when dry).

**Variations & modifiers.** Vibratory tumblers finish in days rather than weeks. Softer stones (obsidian) need gentler, shorter runs and lots of cushioning media. Agate and jasper are the most reliable beginners' stones; petrified wood polishes beautifully but can undercut along growth rings.

**Preservation / curing.** Polished stones need no upkeep. Store grits sealed and labeled by stage; cross-contamination ruins a batch.

**Beyond the recipe.** Polished stones become jewelry tumbles, cabochon blanks, mosaic and terrarium stone, or worry stones. Failed pieces reload into the next coarse batch.

**Safety**

- SILICA DUST: agate, jasper, quartz, and petrified wood are all SiO2. Keep every stage wet; mask when handling dry grit or dried slurry; dust causes silicosis.
- Never pour slurry down drains — settle and bin the solids.
- Keep the tumbler away from food-prep areas; dedicate strainers and buckets to lapidary.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect rough only where allowed, leave protected sites and quantities alone.

### Petrified wood lapidary

`mineral-petrified-wood-lapidary` — **Viability 5/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Agate-bright wood grain, glassy polish `#7a4a28`

> Petrified wood is a fossil that cuts like a gemstone: over millions of years silica-rich groundwater replaced the cellulose cell by cell, so what looks like a log is really solid chalcedony and agate at Mohs ~7. That means you are no longer cutting wood — you are cutting rock as hard as the blade of a pocketknife, and it takes a glassy, mirror polish that shows every growth ring. The catch is the silica: cut and grind it dry and you are making the same dust that gives quarry workers silicosis.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-petrified-wood`, `mineral-agate`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — As durable as an object gets — it is stone. A finished slab, bookend, or cabochon is chemically inert quartz that will not fade, corrode, or wear in any human timeframe; the only thing that dulls it is a scratch to the polish, which re-polishes out.
- **Timeline:** 3–8 hr per piece active · days, spread across grinding/polish stages passive
- **Yield:** One fist-sized block yields several cabochons or a matched pair of small bookends; a slabbing block yields a stack of slabs for cabs, coasters, and inlay.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Petrified wood rough (silicified wood / agatized wood)** — palm-sized chunk up to a slabbing block — denser, agatized pieces polish best; punky or chalky material crumbles
- **Water (cutting/grinding coolant)** — continuous flow or a full reservoir — the single most important safety input — it suppresses silica dust and cools the blade
- **Diamond blade + diamond grinding/polishing wheels or wet belts** — one blade, a grit progression (80 → 220 → 280 → 600 → 1200 → polish) — steel diamond wheels for coarse shaping, resin/soft wheels for fine polish
- _(optional)_ **Cerium oxide or diamond polishing compound** — a spoonful, as a slurry — for the final mirror on a felt or leather buff
- _(optional)_ **Dop wax + dop stick** — as needed — for holding a cabochon while shaping

**Tools** — _Required:_ Trim saw or slab saw with water/oil coolant, Cabbing machine or wet diamond wheels/belts, P100 respirator (for any incidental dry handling/cleanup), Eye protection / face shield, Waterproof apron and gloves  ·  _Optional:_ Tile saw (budget entry for small pieces), Flat lap for slabs, bookends, and spheres, Dop station / alcohol lamp, Ultrasonic or brush for cleaning slurry off the stone

**Steps**

1. Source legally — occurrence is not permission. On BLM land, casual personal collection of petrified wood is capped at 25 lb + one piece per day (250 lb/yr) and may not be sold; in National Parks (e.g., Petrified Forest NP) removing any is a federal offense. Buy from a dealer if you are unsure.
2. Slab it wet: feed the block slowly into a diamond saw running full coolant, never forcing the cut. Let the blade do the work — pushing hard overheats, cracks the stone, and defeats the water's dust control.
3. Choose and mark your cab or bookend face on a slab, showing the best grain/ring pattern; nip or trim to a rough preform.
4. Shape the dome/edges on the coarse steel diamond wheel (80, then 220 grit) with water flowing over the wheel the whole time. Keep the stone moving so you don't cut a flat.
5. Refine through the resin wheels (280, 600, 1200) with a light touch — soft resin compresses into softer bands of the wood if you press, undercutting the polish.
6. Final polish on felt/leather with cerium oxide or diamond slurry until the surface goes to a glass mirror.
7. For bookends/blocks: cut a flat base on the saw, grind the show-face flat on a wet flat lap through the grit progression, then polish. Leave the natural bark edge for contrast if the piece has one.
8. Clean, dry, and — importantly — wet-wipe the whole work area. Never dry-sweep or air-blow silica slurry; let it settle, then wet-clean and dispose of the sludge.

**Variations & modifiers.** Agatized and jasperized wood take a higher polish than opalized or 'punky' wood; the harder and more solidly silicified, the better. Palm wood (with its distinctive rod pattern), Arizona rainbow wood, and Oregon/Washington material each read differently across a slab. Oil coolant in a slab saw cuts cleaner and cooler than water but adds cleanup; water is fine and safer to breathe near.

**Preservation / curing.** The finished stone is permanent and needs no sealing. If a polish ever dulls, take it back through the last two fine wheels. Store the loose slurry/sludge sealed and dispose of it as inert waste — the hazard is only while it is airborne dust, not once it is wet or set.

**Beyond the recipe.** Byproduct silica sludge is inert once wet and can be dried into a filler. Off-cut slabs make coasters, clock faces, knife-scale liners, and inlay for the wild-clay and woodworking sides of the shop. The same wet-lapidary setup handles the agate, jasper, and chalcedony cabbing on the map.

**Safety**

- Crystalline silica is the headline hazard: dry cutting/grinding petrified wood makes respirable silica dust that causes silicosis (irreversible, sometimes fatal). ALWAYS cut and grind WET so no dust goes airborne; wear a P100 respirator for any dry handling or cleanup.
- Never dry-sweep, brush, or air-blow the dust or dried slurry — that re-suspends the silica. Let it settle, then wet-wipe and dispose of the sludge.
- Wet saws and grinders throw water and grit — wear eye/face protection and keep electrical cords and hands clear of the wet zone (GFCI outlet).
- The stone shatters into sharp shards if the blade binds or you force a cut; feed slowly and keep fingers well back of the blade.
- Occurrence is not permission: verify collecting rules (BLM caps, NPS total prohibition, private/claimed land) before taking any material.

- [Dangerous Dust: 5 Tips to Handle Lapidary Minerals Safely — Rock & Gem](https://www.rockngem.com/dangerous-dust/)

### Agate & jasper cabochon

`mineral-cabochon` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Domed polished cabochon `#a8452f`

> A cabochon is the oldest way to cut a stone: no facets, just a smooth polished dome that shows off agate banding, jasper landscapes, or the cell structure of petrified wood. You grind it wet on silicon-carbide or diamond wheels — and wet is not optional, because the water is both coolant and the only thing keeping silica dust out of your lungs.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-agate`, `mineral-petrified-wood`, `mineral-gemstone`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A cut-and-polished cabochon is permanent and set-ready. Silica gems hold their polish indefinitely.
- **Timeline:** 1-3 hours per stone active
- **Yield:** One cabochon per session as you learn; faster once dopping and doming feel natural.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Slab of agate, jasper, or petrified wood** — 1 — Pre-sawn slab, ~4-6 mm; choose banding/pattern for the dome face.
- **Cabochon template + aluminum dop stick and dop wax** — as needed — Wax-mount the stone to a stick to hold it against the wheels.
- **Water** — continuous flow — Wheels must run wet — coolant and dust suppression.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Cabbing machine or diamond wheels (coarse→fine), Diamond scribe/template, Trim saw or nippers, Dop stick + wax + alcohol lamp, Safety glasses, N95/P100 respirator  ·  _Optional:_ Flat lap for the back, Cerium/diamond polish + felt/leather wheel, Magnifier

**Steps**

1. Choose the pattern on the slab and scribe the outline with a template; trim close to the line with a saw or nippers.
2. Grind the outline true on a coarse diamond wheel, running water continuously.
3. Warm dop wax, mount the stone on a dop stick, and grind the dome — coarse to fine — checking the curve as you go.
4. Sand up through the fine wheels until all scratches vanish; keep everything wet and wear a respirator around any spray.
5. Polish on a felt/leather wheel with cerium or diamond compound to a mirror dome.
6. Release the stone from the dop (freezer or heat), clean off wax, and finish the flat back.

**Variations & modifiers.** Diamond wheels cut faster and cleaner than silicon-carbide but cost more. Softer or fractured stone needs lighter pressure. Petrified wood and banded agate reward orienting the slab to center the best pattern under the dome.

**Preservation / curing.** Finished cabs need no upkeep; store so domes do not rub. Set into wire or bezel jewelry when ready.

**Beyond the recipe.** Cab offcuts feed the tumbler; small scraps become inlay or mosaic. Practice cabs make sample sets for teaching lapidary.

**Safety**

- SILICA DUST: never grind these stones dry. Water is coolant AND dust control; wear a respirator around spray/mist.
- Wheels and saws — eye protection and careful finger placement; diamond wheels bite skin.
- Dop-wax alcohol lamp is an open flame — keep it clear of solvents.
- Occurrence is not permission — source slabs and rough responsibly, not from protected land.

### Alabaster carving (translucent)

`mineral-alabaster-carving` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Creamy translucent white stone `#e2d4bd`

> Alabaster is fine-grained gypsum soft enough to shape with a horseshoe rasp, yet polished thin it glows when you hold it to a window — the reason medieval carvers made lamp panels and small saints from it. It has no asbestos risk, but gypsum dust is still an irritant, and worked dry it makes a fine cloud you should not breathe.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-alabaster`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Durable decorative object, but gypsum is water-soluble — alabaster is for dry indoor pieces only and will etch and dull if left wet or outdoors.
- **Timeline:** 2-6 hours active · None; wax buffs immediately passive
- **Yield:** One small figure, dish, or lamp panel per block.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Alabaster block** — 1-4 lb — Sold as translucent white, pink, or veined stock; Mohs 3-4, softer than marble.
- **Microcrystalline or carnauba wax** — as needed — Brings up the translucent shine; deepens veining.
- _(optional)_ **Water** — small amount for wet-sanding — Use sparingly — too much water pits gypsum. Many carvers sand alabaster damp, not soaking.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Wood/horseshoe rasp, Riffler files, Wet/dry sandpaper 120-600, Dust mask (N95) or respirator, Safety goggles  ·  _Optional:_ Wooden mallet and flat chisel, 0000 steel wool, Small hand saw

**Steps**

1. Sketch your form. Remove bulk with a rasp — alabaster cuts fast, so go slow near finish lines.
2. Refine with rifflers. Use a wooden mallet, not steel, if chiseling; a hard blow drives cracks deep below the surface.
3. Sand damp from 120 up through 600 grit until the surface is glassy; keep dust down with a mask and light misting.
4. Wipe clean and let the stone dry fully — trapped moisture clouds the polish.
5. Buff on wax with a soft cloth and polish to a glow. Hold the finished piece to light to check translucency.
6. Clean the bench wet; do not dry-sweep gypsum dust.

**Variations & modifiers.** Thin walls (under ~1 cm) maximize translucency for lamp shades or votive shields. Pink and honey-veined alabasters carve identically to white. For crisper edges, step up to marble (Mohs 3-4 calcite) — harder, opaque, weatherproof.

**Preservation / curing.** Keep dry and indoors. Re-wax to restore luster. Never use alabaster where it meets water, acid, or weather — gypsum dissolves.

**Beyond the recipe.** Gypsum offcuts can be calcined and slaked into plaster of Paris for casting molds — the same CaSO4 chemistry potters use for slip molds (a plaster mold, not a glaze ingredient).

**Safety**

- GYPSUM DUST: irritating to lungs and eyes. Work damp, wear an N95, and keep ventilation moving.
- Use a wooden mallet; steel-chisel blows crack alabaster invisibly and ruin the polish.
- Occurrence is not permission — do not collect gypsum from mapped or protected sites.
- Wet-clean the workspace; bag dust rather than sweeping.

### Chalk gesso / whiting ground

`mineral-chalk-gesso` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Chalk white, hard and burnishable `#f4f1e8`

> Every gilded icon and gold-leafed frame starts with a humble white ground: calcium carbonate — whiting, chalk, powdered marble or limestone — bound in warm animal glue. Layered onto a panel and burnished, this gesso becomes a stone-smooth, chalk-hard surface that fills the wood grain and gives water-gilded gold a mirror to shine against. It is the oldest paint ground in the Western tradition and the reason a 600-year-old panel is still flat and bright.

- **Maps to:** `mineral-marble`, `mineral-limestone`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Durable and archival when made right: true chalk-and-glue gesso on a rigid panel is the ground beneath centuries-old paintings and gilding. It is rigid, so it belongs on stiff panels, not flexible canvas, where it can crack.
- **Timeline:** 1–2 hr to mix and lay active · overnight glue soak + a day to dry between coats passive
- **Yield:** A cup of mixed gesso grounds several small panels; scale the glue:whiting ratio up for larger work.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Whiting / powdered chalk (calcium carbonate)** — by volume, ~1 part to 1 part glue — finely ground marble or limestone dust = the classic 'whiting'; calcium carbonate, non-toxic
- **Hide glue (rabbit-skin glue) granules** — 1 part glue to ~15 parts cold water — the protein binder; soak cold overnight, then warm gently — never boil
- **Distilled water** — as the glue ratio requires
- **A rigid wood/MDF panel, sized with warm glue first** — one panel — seal (size) the panel with a coat of the warm glue and let it dry before gessoing

**Tools** — _Required:_ Double boiler or glue pot (heat glue without boiling), Sieve or fine strainer, Wide soft brush, Rigid panel, Fine sandpaper / burnishing cloth  ·  _Optional:_ Kitchen scale for repeatable glue strength, Thermometer (keep glue ~55–60°C, below a boil), Marble/limestone dust hand-levigated from your own stone, Fine wet-or-dry paper for the final polish

**Steps**

1. Soak hide glue in cold distilled water (about 1:15 by weight) overnight until it swells to a jelly.
2. Warm the swollen glue in a double boiler until liquid and hot but NOT boiling — boiling degrades the glue's strength.
3. Size the panel: brush one thin coat of the warm glue over the sealed, sanded panel and let it dry. This stops the gesso from soaking in unevenly.
4. Make the gesso: sift whiting into the hot glue gradually (roughly equal volumes), letting each addition sink and wet through before stirring, until you have a smooth mix the consistency of light cream. Keep it warm and skim any foam.
5. Lay the first coat warm and thin, working it into the grain. Let each coat set to touch-dry, then brush the next while the previous is still slightly damp, building 5–8 thin coats (alternate brush direction).
6. Let it dry fully (a day). It should feel chalk-hard and dead-matte.
7. Sand/scrape smooth, then burnish with a damp cloth or fine paper to a stone-smooth surface ready for bole and gilding, or for tempera/oil.
8. Wear a dust mask while sanding cured gesso — the fine dry chalk dust is a nuisance to breathe even though it isn't toxic.

**Variations & modifiers.** Calcium carbonate (chalk/whiting from marble or limestone) makes the classic Northern European 'chalk gesso'; calcium sulfate (from gypsum/alabaster) makes the Italian 'gesso sottile' — the same method, a slightly different feel and absorbency. Weaker glue = softer, more absorbent ground (good for burnished water gilding); stronger glue = harder, less absorbent. A little honey or glycerin adds flexibility.

**Preservation / curing.** Store dry whiting sealed against moisture. Prepared liquid gesso is perishable like any glue size — it gels cold, re-melts warm, and grows mold within days at room temperature; refrigerate short-term or make fresh. Finished dry gesso panels are archival if kept from damp and flexing.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same whiting is a mild polishing powder, a putty and paint filler, and the base for bole/gilding and traditional distemper paint. It ties the map's marble and limestone dust — including your own hand-ground stone waste — into a working ground; the studio ethic of 'one material's remnant becomes another's ingredient' lives here.

**Safety**

- Low-toxicity craft: calcium carbonate (chalk/whiting) is not poisonous, and hide glue is a traditional protein binder.
- Wear a dust mask when grinding stone into whiting or sanding cured gesso — fine dry particulate irritates lungs even when non-toxic (and marble/limestone dust in quantity is a nuisance-dust hazard).
- Do not boil the hide glue — overheating ruins its binding strength (a quality issue, and a scald risk from hot glue).
- Hot glue can burn skin; work over a double boiler and mind splashes.
- Prepared gesso spoils and molds within days — don't reuse smelly batches; keep it refrigerated or make fresh.

**Sources**

- [How to Prepare Gesso for Traditional Water Gilding — Gilded Planet](https://www.gildedplanet.com/gessoprep.html)
- [How To Make Gesso — Charles Douglas Gilding Studio](https://www.gildingstudio.com/a-gilders-journal/how-to-make-gesso)
- [Gesso (traditional chalk/glue ground) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gesso)

### Engraved slate coaster

`mineral-slate-engraving` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Pale line on black slate `#3a3a3f`

> Slate splits into flat tiles along its cleavage and scratches pale grey wherever a carbide point drags across the dark surface — the same reason schoolchildren once wrote on slate boards. A rotary tool or a hand scribe turns a coaster blank into a lettered keepsake in an afternoon, but slate is a silica rock, so its fine dust belongs out of your lungs.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-slate`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — The engraved line is permanent stone; oiled slate keeps its rich color. Fully functional and washable as a coaster.
- **Timeline:** 30 minutes to 2 hours active
- **Yield:** One finished coaster per blank; a set of four is an easy afternoon.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Slate coaster/tile blank** — 1+ — Pre-cut blanks avoid the dustiest cutting step.
- _(optional)_ **Food-safe mineral oil or stone sealer** — small — Rinse dust first, then oil to deepen the black and make the pale engraving pop.
- _(optional)_ **Transfer paper / stencil** — 1 — For lettering or logos.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Carbide-tip scribe OR rotary tool with diamond/carbide bit, N95 dust mask, Safety glasses, Damp cloth  ·  _Optional:_ Bench vise or non-slip mat, Fume/dust extractor for powered engraving, Fine sandpaper for edges

**Steps**

1. Transfer or draw your design onto the slate surface.
2. Scribe the outline with light passes; slate engraves pale, so shallow is enough. Wear a mask and glasses whenever a powered tool makes dust.
3. Deepen and shade lines as desired; use a dust extractor or work near ventilation with rotary tools.
4. Rinse the slate under water or wipe with a damp cloth to clear dust and reveal the finished engraving.
5. Optional: wipe on food-safe mineral oil to darken the stone and heighten contrast.
6. Add cork or felt feet to the underside so the coaster does not scratch furniture.

**Variations & modifiers.** Hand scribing gives a soft sketched line; rotary tools give bold controllable strokes. Oil finish maximizes contrast; leave dry for a chalkier look. Larger tiles become trivets or house-number plaques the same way.

**Preservation / curing.** Wash by hand and re-oil occasionally. The engraving never fades; only the oil finish needs refreshing.

**Beyond the recipe.** Slate offcuts become plant labels, chalkboard tags, or ground (wet) into a filler. Powdered slate has been used historically as a dark pigment extender.

**Safety**

- SILICA DUST: slate is a silica rock — powered engraving makes respirable dust. Mask up, use extraction, and rinse the work wet.
- Wear eye protection; carbide points and rotary bits fling chips.
- Secure the tile so the tool cannot skid off the edge.
- Occurrence is not permission — buy blanks rather than prying slate from mapped ledges.

### Flint-and-steel fire striking

`mineral-flint-steel-fire` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Orange sparks, glowing ember on char cloth `#c8541c`

> Long before matches, fire came from scraping a high-carbon steel down the sharp edge of a stone: the edge shaves off tiny curls of steel, friction ignites them, and a shower of 800°C sparks rains onto a scrap of char cloth. It is the most beginner-friendly way to use knapping toolstone — no flintknapping skill required, just a sharp edge, which you can dress with a single blow. Chert, flint, quartz, and agate all work; what you actually need is a hard stone with a fresh, acute edge to 'bite' the steel.

- **Maps to:** `mineral-silica`, `mineral-agate`, `mineral-quartz`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Durability of the tools, not a shelf life: a chert striker edge and a carbon-steel striker last for thousands of strikes. Re-dress the stone edge with a chip when it dulls; the steel eventually wears a groove and can be reground.
- **Timeline:** 5–20 min to a flame, once practiced active · ~30 min one-time to make char cloth passive
- **Yield:** One striker + one stone + a tin of char cloth is a lifetime fire kit — hundreds to thousands of lights.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Chert / flint / quartz / agate with a sharp edge** — a palm-fillable hand piece — any hard (Mohs ~7) silica stone with a fresh acute edge; freshly broken edge sparks best
- **High-carbon steel striker** — one C-shaped striker, or an old file/hacksaw blade — must be high-carbon (hardened) steel — stainless and mild steel won't spark
- **Char cloth (charred cotton)** — a few small squares — catches the spark as a glowing coal; make it from 100% cotton in a sealed tin
- **Tinder bundle (dry grass, jute, birch bark, cattail down)** — a bird's-nest handful — receives the glowing char and blows into flame

**Tools** — _Required:_ High-carbon steel striker, Sharp chert/flint edge, Char cloth, Dry tinder bundle, A safe, cleared fire spot or hearth  ·  _Optional:_ Small tin with a lid (to char cloth over coals), Leather glove for the stone hand, Tinder box to store char cloth dry, Second stone to dress the edge

**Steps**

1. Make char cloth (once): pack squares of 100% cotton into a small tin with a tiny vent hole, set it in a fire until the smoke stops jetting, then let it cool sealed. You get black, fragile charred cloth.
2. Dress a sharp edge on your stone if needed — one sharp tap with a hammerstone or the back of the striker to expose a fresh, acute ridge. This is the whole 'knapping' requirement.
3. Pinch a piece of char cloth against the top of the sharp stone edge with your thumb, so falling sparks land on it.
4. Grip the steel and strike down and across the sharp edge at a glancing angle, scraping shavings of steel off. Short, firm, repeated strokes throw the most sparks.
5. A spark catches the char cloth as a spreading orange glow — protect it from wind.
6. Fold the glowing char into your tinder bundle, cup it, and blow steadily. The coal grows and the bundle bursts into flame.
7. Transfer the flaming bundle to your fire lay. Keep water/dirt at hand to fully extinguish when done.

**Variations & modifiers.** Freshly fractured chert/flint sparks best; a smoothed or rounded edge won't bite — re-dress it. English flint, novaculite, jasper, quartz, and agate all work as long as they're hard and sharp. If sparks are weak, the steel may be too soft (test with a known carbon-steel file) or the edge too dull. Amadou (fungus) or true tinder fungus can replace char cloth in the traditional kit.

**Preservation / curing.** Keep char cloth and tinder bone-dry in a sealed tin — damp char won't catch. The stone and steel are effectively permanent; wipe the steel to prevent rust and re-dress the stone edge as it wears smooth.

**Beyond the recipe.** A gentle, non-destructive gateway to the map's toolstone (chert, flint, novaculite, obsidian) that needs no knapping skill or ARPA-sensitive artifact collecting — you use a broken hand piece, not a worked point. The same char-cloth-and-tinder skill supports the pit-fire and wild-clay cards' fire-starting.

**Safety**

- Sharp silica edges cut skin easily — handle the dressed stone carefully and consider a glove on the stone hand.
- Wear eye protection when dressing an edge: struck chert throws sharp flakes.
- You are making fire — clear a non-flammable area, keep water/dirt at hand, mind wind and dry fuels, and fully extinguish before leaving.
- Char cloth production makes smoke — do it outdoors or well ventilated.
- Collect toolstone responsibly: use naturally broken hand pieces, and never collect worked stone tools or artifacts (protected under ARPA). Occurrence is not permission.

**Sources**

### Gypsum Plaster: Molds & Wedging Bats

`mineral-plaster-molds-wedging-bats` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Beginner** · **Result:** Chalk-white absorbent plaster `#F4F1EA`

> Every plaster mold, drying bat, and wedging table in a pottery studio is calcined gypsum — the same soft stone as alabaster — ground, cooked to drive off water, and re-hydrated to set rock-hard in minutes. Pottery plaster is mixed by an exact recipe: 100 parts plaster to 70 parts water by weight. Get the ratio right and you get a strong, thirsty mold; get it wrong and it sets soft, weak, or too dense to pull the water out of clay.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-alabaster`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A correctly mixed, fully dried plaster mold or bat lasts for hundreds of uses; durability is 'good' as a studio tool — this is not a food or fired-ware surface.
- **Timeline:** 30-45 minutes to mix and pour active · 1 hour to set (warm); several days to a week to fully dry before use passive
- **Yield:** The 70:100 ratio makes a predictable volume: ~2.8 lb water + 4 lb plaster fills roughly a small drying bat. Mixed plaster has a short pot life (minutes) — mix only what you can pour before it stiffens.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Pottery/molding plaster (calcined gypsum; note: buy USG-type pottery plaster rather than self-calcining alabaster)** — 100 parts by weight — Alabaster is the natural gypsum stone; self-calcining is finicky, so studio pottery plaster is the reliable route.
- **Water (potable, 70-100°F)** — 70 parts by weight — Cold water = more expansion + slower set; warm = faster set, less expansion.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Accurate scale, Flexible mixing bucket, Cottle boards / mold form or bat ring, Mold soap or release for multi-part molds, Dust mask for handling dry plaster  ·  _Optional:_ Drill mixer (low speed), Surform/rasp for trimming, Sponge for finishing the working face

**Steps**

1. Weigh water and plaster to the 70:100 ratio (water:plaster by weight). This ratio sets strength and absorbency, so measure — don't eyeball.
2. Sift the plaster into the water by hand (plaster into water, never water onto plaster), letting each handful sink until a peak of dry plaster rises just above the surface — that indicates roughly the right ratio.
3. Let it slake undisturbed for a couple of minutes, then mix slowly and thoroughly, breaking up lumps without whipping in air.
4. Pour into your soaped mold form or bat ring while the slurry is still fluid (before it thickens); tap/vibrate to release bubbles.
5. Wait for the set exotherm — the plaster warms as it hardens; demold after it cools back down and is firm (about an hour).
6. Dry the finished mold/bat fully for several days to a week (a warm, ventilated spot, not so hot it calcines the surface) before putting it to work; wet plaster won't absorb from clay.

**Variations & modifiers.** More plaster (less water) = faster set, harder, less absorbent mold; more water = softer, more absorbent, weaker. Warm water speeds the set for small pours; cold water buys working time and more expansion for tight detail. Add a splash of vinegar to retard set, or a pinch of salt/potassium sulfate to accelerate — test first.

**Preservation / curing.** Cured, fully dried plaster molds and bats last for hundreds of uses if kept from chipping and from soaking too wet. Never let plaster crumbs get into a clay body — even a tiny lodged bit causes a lime-pop blowout in fired ware. Store dry; re-dry molds between heavy casting sessions.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same gypsum plaster makes press molds, hump/slump molds, and sprig molds, plus super-absorbent reclaim slabs for reconditioning slop clay. Natural alabaster's other craft life is carving (the alabaster carving card in the mineral set) — a soft, translucent stone.

**Safety**

- Never mix or immerse a hand in a large plaster batch that could set around it — plaster sets exothermically and hard, a documented burn/entrapment hazard; keep body parts out of setting plaster.
- Dry gypsum/plaster dust irritates lungs and eyes — wear a dust mask, don't dry-sweep, and never pour plaster slurry or washout down a drain (it sets and clogs pipes).
- Keep plaster out of clay bodies and off wedging clay surfaces used for other work — embedded plaster crumbs cause lime-pop blowouts in fired ware.
- Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped alabaster/gypsum only with consent; and this is a studio tool, not a food-contact surface.

### Lime cycle: limewash & putty

`mineral-lime-cycle` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Chalk-white breathable limewash `#f2efe6`

> The lime cycle is the oldest chemistry in building: burn limestone or marble (calcium carbonate) above ~900°C to drive off CO2 and get quicklime, slake that quicklime in water — a violently exothermic reaction that can boil and spit — into a smooth putty, then thin it to a limewash that slowly re-absorbs CO2 from the air and turns back to stone on the wall. It is beautiful and it is genuinely dangerous: quicklime and fresh putty are strongly caustic and will burn skin and eyes.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-limestone`, `mineral-marble`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Carbonated limewash and lime plaster are durable, breathable finishes that last years to decades outdoors and re-mineralize as they cure.
- **Timeline:** A day to slake and mix active · Putty matures weeks to months; limewash carbonates over days to weeks passive
- **Yield:** A few pounds of putty makes liters of limewash — enough for a small wall or several test panels.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Quicklime (calcium oxide) OR mature lime putty** — as needed — Most crafters start from purchased quicklime or ready putty rather than burning their own stone. Quicklime is the hazardous step.
- _(optional)_ **Limestone or marble** — for the burn (advanced only) — Calcining your own requires a lime kiln reaching ~900°C+ — a serious undertaking, not a casual step.
- **Clean water** — roughly 2-3x lime by volume for slaking — Always add lime to water in stages, never the reverse, to control the boil.
- _(optional)_ **Natural earth pigment (optional)** — up to ~5-10% by volume — Only alkali-stable pigments (ochres, oxides) survive lime's high pH.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Chemical goggles and face shield, Long nitrile/rubber gauntlets, P100 respirator, Non-reactive slaking tub (steel/plastic), Long stir stick, Full skin cover  ·  _Optional:_ Sieve for straining putty, Masonry brush for limewash, Sealed maturing bucket

**Steps**

1. SAFETY FIRST: goggles, face shield, gauntlets, respirator, covered skin, and running water nearby before any lime touches water.
2. Slaking (if starting from quicklime): add quicklime to water in small increments. It will hiss, steam, and can boil — stand back and stir; never lean over the tub.
3. Let the slaked putty rest under a layer of water; mature it sealed for weeks to months (older putty works better).
4. For limewash: dilute mature putty with water to a thin-milk consistency (roughly 1 part putty to several parts water), tint with alkali-stable pigment if desired.
5. Wet the wall, then brush on many thin coats, letting each carbonate before the next. Thin coats bond; thick coats crack.
6. Keep the fresh work damp and out of direct sun/frost so it carbonates slowly and hard.

**Variations & modifiers.** Thicker putty plus sand becomes lime plaster; on wet plaster, painting with lime-fast pigment is buon fresco. Add casein or linseed for durability tweaks. Pigment load stays low — high-pH lime kills fugitive and many organic colors, so stick to ochres and oxides.

**Preservation / curing.** Mature putty keeps indefinitely under water in a sealed tub. Cured limewash self-heals hairline cracks and can be recoated yearly. Store quicklime absolutely dry.

**Beyond the recipe.** Lime putty is also a mortar, a fresco ground, a tannery and hide-processing agent, and a soil amendment. Leftover carbonated wash is inert chalk.

**Safety**

- CAUSTIC: quicklime and fresh lime are strongly alkaline and cause chemical burns to skin and eyes; slaking is exothermic and can boil and spatter. Full PPE is non-negotiable.
- Always add lime to water in stages — never water to a mass of quicklime.
- P100 respirator against lime dust; never breathe quicklime powder.
- Home calcining requires a real high-temperature kiln — most should buy quicklime or putty, not burn stone.
- Occurrence is not permission — do not quarry limestone or marble from mapped or protected land.

### Marble & limestone lettering

`mineral-marble-lettering` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Crisp V-cut incised letters `#d9d4c8`

> Incised V-cut lettering is why Roman inscriptions still read cleanly two thousand years on: a chisel-cut letter catches shadow instead of paint, so it survives long after any pigment weathers off. Soft limestone freestone and fine marble both take the cut, but both are calcite/silica-bearing stone — cut them wet or masked, because the dust is a lung hazard.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-marble`, `mineral-limestone`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A properly cut inscription in sound stone is effectively permanent; limestone weathers faster than marble outdoors.
- **Timeline:** Many hours per line of text active
- **Yield:** One plaque, address stone, or memorial line. Lettering is slow — budget hours per word.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Limestone or marble slab** — sized to text — Fine, even-grained 'freestone' limestone or statuary marble cuts cleanest. Avoid fossil-packed or crumbly stock.
- **Chalk or graphite for layout** — small — Draw the letters full-size before any cut.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Lettering chisels (flat and 'dummy' mallet), Stone mallet, Straightedge and set square, Safety goggles, N95/P100 respirator, Water spray or wet setup  ·  _Optional:_ Skew chisels, Pencil grid / pounced pattern, Diamond hand pads for cleanup

**Steps**

1. Draw the full inscription on paper, then transfer it life-size to the stone with chalk or a pounced pattern.
2. Cut a central guide line down each stroke, then angle chisel cuts from both sides to meet in a clean V at the bottom of the stroke.
3. Work with the stone damp or spray-cut to keep silica/calcite dust out of the air; wear a respirator regardless.
4. Deepen and true each stroke, keeping the V symmetrical so both faces catch light evenly.
5. Cut serifs and terminals last, then clean the grooves with a fine chisel.
6. Rinse the stone; wet-clean the bench. Do not dry-sweep the dust.

**Variations & modifiers.** Limestone forgives beginners (softer, quieter cut); marble rewards experience with crisper, longer-lasting edges. Painted or gilded letter interiors add contrast but the incision alone survives weathering. Practice on limestone offcuts before committing to marble.

**Preservation / curing.** Indoor inscriptions last indefinitely. Outdoors, marble outlasts limestone; a breathable stone (not film-forming) sealer slows acid-rain etching.

**Beyond the recipe.** Chips and dust are pure calcium carbonate — the same whiting that fluxes glazes and feeds the lime cycle. Save clean marble dust as a gesso and glaze filler.

**Safety**

- SILICA/CALCITE DUST: cut wet or masked with a P100; chronic stone dust causes lung disease.
- Flying chips — always wear impact goggles.
- Mallet work is repetitive-strain heavy; pace yourself and support the stone well.
- Occurrence is not permission — source slabs from suppliers, not protected outcrops.

### Novaculite whetstone finish

`mineral-novaculite-whetstone` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Honed mirror edge `#b8bcc0`

> Arkansas whetstones are novaculite — a dense microcrystalline quartz whose 1-to-5-micron grains act like a natural 600-to-1200-grit abrasive, the classic final stone for putting a shaving edge on a blade. Where a coarse stone shapes steel, the hard black or translucent Arkansas refines it to a polish, and truing a slightly dished stone flat is the quiet skill that makes the whole thing work.

- **Maps to:** `mineral-novaculite`, `mineral-silica`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A novaculite stone lasts generations; the 'result' is a durable honed edge on your tool, renewable on demand.
- **Timeline:** 20-60 minutes to hone; longer to true a stone active
- **Yield:** One well-honed edge per session; a single stone serves for decades.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Novaculite (Arkansas) stone — soft/hard/black/translucent** — 1 — Grade by density: Washita/soft is coarser; hard, black, and translucent are finishing stones.
- **Honing oil or water** — as needed — Mineral oil is traditional; the stone can also run with water. Never dry-hone.
- _(optional)_ **Loose silicon-carbide grit + flat plate (for truing)** — small — For flattening a dished stone.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Novaculite stone, Lubricant, Blade/tool to sharpen, Clean cloth  ·  _Optional:_ Coarser bench stone for setting the initial bevel, Flat granite/glass plate and SiC grit for truing, Strop and compound for final polish

**Steps**

1. Set the bevel on a coarser stone first if the edge is dull or damaged — novaculite refines, it does not do heavy metal removal.
2. Flood the Arkansas stone with oil or water so swarf floats and the pores do not clog.
3. Hold a consistent bevel angle and draw the edge across the stone in even strokes, alternating sides, wiping swarf as you go.
4. Progress from soft to hard/black/translucent to walk the edge up to a polish; test on paper or hair as the burr disappears.
5. Truing: rub a dished stone on a flat plate charged with silicon-carbide grit and water until a pencil grid across the face wears off evenly.
6. Clean the stone after use — oil it, rub, and wipe the excess so it stays unclogged.

**Variations & modifiers.** Oil versus water is a preference; pick one and stay consistent, since oil-soaked stones resist later water use. Coarser Washita for kitchen knives, hard/translucent for razors and fine tools. A leather strop after the stone chases the last wire edge.

**Preservation / curing.** Clean and lightly oil after each session; store dry and flat. Re-true whenever the face dishes. The stone is effectively permanent.

**Beyond the recipe.** Fine novaculite is also lapped into pen-tip and instrument-finishing stones. Spent silicon-carbide truing slurry is disposed of, not reused.

**Safety**

- Sharp edges — hone away from your body and keep fingers clear of the stroke path.
- Keep the stone wet with oil/water; dry honing raises fine silica dust and clogs the stone.
- Truing with loose SiC grit is a wet process — rinse, don't sand dry.
- Occurrence is not permission — buy graded stones rather than collecting from the Novaculite Uplift.

**Sources**

- [Novaculite (Silica Stone) — Arkansas Geological Survey](https://geology.arkansas.gov/minerals/industrial/novaculite-silica-stone.html)

### Ochre pigment from iron clay

`mineral-ochre-pigment` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Warm iron-oxide earth red `#a1552f`

> The oldest pigment humans ever used is iron-stained clay: goethite and hematite tinting a kaolin-and-silica earth into yellows, reds, and browns — the ochres of cave walls and Renaissance flesh tones alike. You can wash a lump of iron-bearing clay into fine pigment by levigation (float off the fines, settle out the grit), then bind it with egg yolk into tempera that cures to a durable film. Roasting yellow ochre drives it toward warm red.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — Iron-oxide earths are among the most lightfast pigments known. Bound in egg tempera the paint cures to a durable, water-resistant film.
- **Timeline:** 2-4 hours across a couple of days active · Days to settle and dry passive
- **Yield:** A cupful of raw clay yields a few tablespoons of fine pigment — enough for many small paintings.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Iron-bearing clay / ochreous earth** — a few handfuls — Yellow, red, or brown iron-stained clay. Clay is a mapped material — collect only where allowed.
- **Water** — several liters — For levigation (repeated wash-and-settle separation).
- _(optional)_ **Egg yolk (binder)** — 1 yolk + a little water — For egg tempera; roughly 3 parts yolk to 1 part water, then mixed into pigment paste.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Buckets/jars for settling, Sieve or cloth strainer, Muller & slab OR mortar and pestle, N95 dust mask (for dry pigment), Drying trays  ·  _Optional:_ Cast-iron pan or crucible for roasting, Palette knife, Gum arabic (alternate binder for watercolor)

**Steps**

1. Break up the dry clay and soak it in water; stir to a thin slurry.
2. Levigate: let the coarse sand/grit settle for a minute, pour the still-cloudy fine suspension into a second container, and discard the grit.
3. Let the fine suspension settle fully (hours to days), then pour off the clear water and dry the pigment paste into a cake.
4. Grind the dry cake to a fine powder with a muller or mortar — wear a mask, dry pigment is a dust hazard.
5. To make red from yellow: roast the dry ochre in a pan/crucible until it shifts from yellow to warm red (hematite), then cool and regrind.
6. Bind: mull the pigment powder with egg yolk (plus a little water) to a smooth paint; or bind with gum arabic for watercolor.

**Variations & modifiers.** Different clays give the full ochre range; roasting deepens and reddens any of them. Egg tempera cures hard and matte; gum arabic gives a re-wettable watercolor; linseed oil makes an oil paint. Levigate more times for smoother, more saturated color.

**Preservation / curing.** Dry pigment powder keeps indefinitely in a sealed labeled jar. Egg tempera paint must be mixed fresh (yolk spoils in a day); the dried painting is permanent and lightfast.

**Beyond the recipe.** The same washed ochre tints limewash, casein milk paint, and gesso, and colors slip for pottery. Coarse grit rejected during levigation returns to the garden.

**Safety**

- PIGMENT DUST: dry ochre is a respirable dust and some earths carry manganese — wear an N95 when grinding and handling powder; keep it wet where possible.
- Roasting releases fumes/dust — do it ventilated, and never assume a wild earth is free of contaminants.
- Don't ingest; keep pigment out of food areas and away from children.
- Occurrence is not permission — clay is mapped, but digging it still needs landowner/agency permission.

**Sources**

- [What are Earth Pigments? — Natural Earth Paint](https://naturalearthpaint.com/blogs/blog/what-are-earth-pigments-a-guide-to-ochres-oxides-and-minerals)
- [Instructions for Making Egg Tempera — Earth Pigments](https://www.earthpigments.com/artists-egg-tempera/)
- [Earth Pigments for Artists — Natural Pigments](https://www.naturalpigments.com/pigments/pigments-by-color/earth-pigments.html)

### Primitive pit-fire / simple updraft kiln

`mineral-primitive-kiln-pit-fire` — **Viability 4/5** (flagship) · **Intermediate** · **Result:** Smoke-flashed earth tones, black to blush `#8a5a3c`

> The oldest way to turn dug clay into pottery is a fire in a hole. Pit firing dates back over 10,000 years — Jomon, Ancestral Puebloan, and countless others hardened pots at 600–900°C in an open bonfire or a simple clay updraft kiln. It never vitrifies the clay; it sinters it, leaving an open, porous body that survives the very thermal shock of the fire. The reward is a surface no glaze can imitate: carbon blacks, copper blushes, and smoke flashing painted on by the flames themselves.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-clay`, `mineral-silica`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Keeping-quality reflects the low-fired body: pit-fired ware is sintered, not vitrified, so it stays porous and relatively fragile and is NOT waterproof or reliably food-safe. It's durable as decorative ware kept dry, but won't hold water like a stoneware pot.
- **Timeline:** 4–8 hr tending the fire active · overnight slow cool; days to bone-dry pots first passive
- **Yield:** A single pit or small kiln fires a batch of small-to-medium pots at once; plan for a portion to crack, especially early on.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Bone-dry, coarse/grogged wild-clay pots (bisque or greenware)** — as many as fit with space between — must be BONE DRY; open, grogged bodies with sand/silica resist thermal shock
- **Dry firewood / hardwood** — a generous pile — the main fuel — dry only; green wood steams and cracks pots
- **Fine combustibles (straw, sawdust, dung, dry leaves, paper)** — layers around and over the pots — create the reducing atmosphere and surface color
- _(optional)_ **Colorant combustibles (copper carbonate, salt, seaweed, banana skins, coffee grounds)** — sprinkled/wrapped around pots — give blushes, flashes, and blacks on the surface

**Tools** — _Required:_ A dug pit or a simple clay/brick updraft kiln, Dry firewood + fine tinder, Long tongs / iron poker, Heavy heat gloves + eye protection, Water/hose/extinguisher and a cleared firebreak  ·  _Optional:_ Sawdust/dung for a slow smoulder, Kiln bricks or a metal drum shell, Chimney/updraft opening for a simple kiln, A cover (metal sheet) to bank and slow the burn

**Steps**

1. Fire only where open fires are permitted and safe — check burn bans, get any permit, clear a wide firebreak, and have water/extinguisher on hand. Occurrence of clay is not permission to burn.
2. Use bone-dry pots made from an open, grogged/sandy wild-clay body. Warm them thoroughly near the fire first (or pre-bisque low) to drive off all moisture — any trapped water flashes to steam and explodes the pot.
3. Bed the pit: a base layer of sawdust/combustibles, then nestle the warmed pots with space around each, packing fine combustibles (and any colorants) around and between them.
4. Build the fuel up GRADUALLY — light kindling and let heat rise slowly over the first hour so the pots don't thermal-shock; add wood in stages to a full burn, reaching roughly 600–900°C.
5. Hold the burn until the pots glow and the wood collapses to coals (several hours). A cover or extra sawdust creates a smoky reducing atmosphere for blacks.
6. Let it cool SLOWLY, undisturbed, overnight — rushing the cool cracks the ware by thermal shock. Don't pull hot pots into cold air.
7. Once cold, lift pots out, brush off ash, and wash. Expect some losses — cracks and 'pings' are normal to the method.
8. Fully extinguish and drown the ashes before leaving; stir and confirm cold-out.

**Variations & modifiers.** Atmosphere is the palette: an open oxidizing fire gives warm reds/buffs; smothering with sawdust/dung for a reducing smoke gives blacks and greys; copper carbonate and salt flash greens and pinks; seaweed and organic wraps leave line and haze. A simple clay/brick updraft kiln (even built from the same clay as the pots) holds heat better and fires more evenly than a bare pit. Terra sigillata burnished on before firing gives a soft sheen.

**Preservation / curing.** The fired ware is finished but porous — keep decorative pieces dry; don't rely on them for liquids or food unless a proper high-fire/glazed body is used. A post-fire wax or milk/organic burnish can reduce porosity slightly for looks, not food-safety. Store fuel dry; never store combustibles near the firing pit.

**Beyond the recipe.** This is the firing step that actually completes the wild-clay body cards — dug and processed clay becomes usable pottery here. The grog/sand in the body is the same silica material on the map, and wood ash from the fire is a classic glaze ingredient, feeding the ash-glaze side of the studio and the 'remnant becomes ingredient' loop.

**Safety**

- Open-fire hazard: work only where permitted, clear a firebreak, keep water/extinguisher ready, mind wind and dry surroundings, and fully drown/extinguish the ashes before leaving.
- Exploding pots: any moisture in the clay flashes to steam and can burst a pot violently, throwing hot shards — use only bone-dry (ideally pre-bisqued) ware, pre-warm it, and heat the fire up slowly. Wear eye protection and keep bystanders back.
- Severe burn risk from an open high-temperature fire and hot ware/coals — heavy gloves, eye protection, long tongs, and no reaching over the fire.
- Smoke and fumes: reducing fires make heavy smoke and carbon monoxide — fire OUTDOORS only, never in an enclosed or poorly ventilated space, and stay upwind. Some added colorants (copper compounds, salt) release irritating fumes.
- Pit-fired ware is porous, not vitrified — treat it as decorative; do not assume it is watertight or food-safe.

**Sources**

- [Primitive Firing (sintering, thermal shock, temperatures) — Digitalfire](https://digitalfire.com/glossary/primitive+firing)
- [A Pit Firing Color Palette — Ceramic Arts Network](https://ceramicartsnetwork.org/pottery-making-illustrated/pottery-making-illustrated-article/A-Pit-Firing-Color-Palette)
- [Pit fired pottery (history, technique, temperatures) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pit_fired_pottery)

### Serpentine carving (asbestos-aware)

`mineral-serpentine-carving` — **Viability 4/5** (strong) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Deep green to olive, waxy soft-luster `#5d7042`

> Serpentine is one of the most seductive carving stones — soft enough to shape with hand rasps, glowing translucent green when oiled, the classic material of Zimbabwean and Chinese figure carving. But serpentinite is the parent rock of chrysotile asbestos, and California soapstone and serpentine are notorious for it. The honest version of this craft is as much a hazard-assessment protocol as a carving lesson: test the stone, work it wet under a HEPA respirator, and — the hardest skill — know when to put a block down and walk away.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-serpentine`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — The carved object is durable stone that keeps its form and takes a lasting waxed polish. 'Good' here is about the finished carving, not the process: the process carries a genuine long-latency health risk that no durability rating offsets.
- **Timeline:** 10–40+ hr per carving active · days for lab asbestos test before you cut passive
- **Yield:** One block, one carving. This is a slow, single-object craft, not a batch process.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Serpentine / serpentinite block** — hand-sized to a workable block — green carving grade; SOURCE MATTERS — verify low/no-asbestos origin or test it
- **Water (continuous, for all cutting/grinding/sanding)** — flowing or misting the whole time — wet work is non-negotiable — dry serpentine dust may carry asbestos fibers
- **Wet-or-dry sandpaper grit progression** — 120 → 220 → 400 → 600 → 800+ — used WET only
- _(optional)_ **Carving wax or mineral oil** — a rag's worth — for the final soft luster; deepens the green

**Tools** — _Required:_ Rasps/rifflers and files (hand carving), Wet grinder or angle grinder with water feed + diamond cup, P100 / purple-cartridge respirator rated for asbestos (NIOSH), Sealed goggles + Tyvek or dedicated washable coveralls, HEPA vacuum (never a shop-vac) + wet cleanup supplies  ·  _Optional:_ Asbestos lab test (PLM bulk analysis) on your specific block, Outdoor/downdraft ventilated station, Dop/holding fixture, Diamond hand pads

**Steps**

1. TEST FIRST. Serpentinite frequently hosts naturally occurring asbestos (chrysotile, plus amphibole). The only way to be sure about a specific block is a PLM asbestos test at a lab (a small chip). If it comes back positive for asbestos — walk away; there is no safe hobby workaround.
2. If you cannot test, treat the stone as asbestos-bearing by default and either don't cut it or use it only under full asbestos-grade controls (many hobbyists rightly choose a different, verified stone instead).
3. Set up outdoors or in a downdraft/HEPA-ventilated space. Put on the fitted asbestos respirator, sealed goggles, and washable coveralls. No one else nearby.
4. Rough the form WET: every cut, grind, and sanding pass runs with water so nothing goes airborne. Never let the stone dry-dust while you work.
5. Refine the shape with wet rasps and rifflers for detail, keeping the surface wet and re-wetting often.
6. Wet-sand up the grit progression (120→600→800) for a smooth surface; the stone will start to glow green.
7. Finish with wax or oil buffed by hand for a soft translucent luster.
8. Decontaminate: HEPA-vacuum, then wet-wipe every surface, bag the wet sludge and used sandpaper as (potentially) asbestos waste per local rules, launder coveralls separately, and never dry-sweep or blow anything down.

**Variations & modifiers.** Vermont serpentine/soapstone is generally cited as usually asbestos-free while California and some New York material is notorious for it — but 'generally' is not 'this block,' so testing beats geography. Harder serpentine (bowenite) resists scratching and polishes higher; softer massive serpentine carves faster but bruises. Oil vs. wax finish is a look choice: oil reads wetter/greener, wax is more durable.

**Preservation / curing.** The finished, sealed carving is stable and safe to display — the fibers are locked in solid stone, not friable. The hazard lives entirely in the cutting/grinding/sanding dust and the waste sludge. Store no dry serpentine dust anywhere; dispose of process waste as hazardous per local regulation.

**Beyond the recipe.** Verified asbestos-free serpentine and its cousin soapstone are the same family used for carved bowls, beads, and cabochons; a tested-clean offcut can feed the lapidary bench. The main 'byproduct' of this card is the transferable skill: a wet-work + respirator + HEPA protocol that also protects you when cutting silica stone elsewhere in the shop.

**Safety**

- Naturally occurring asbestos is the defining hazard: serpentinite frequently contains chrysotile. Inhaled fibers cause asbestosis, lung cancer, and mesothelioma decades later — there is no safe exposure level.
- TEST the specific stone (PLM lab) before cutting. If positive, do not carve it — walk away. Do not rely on the block 'looking clean.'
- Work only WET, under a NIOSH P100/purple-cartridge respirator fit-tested for asbestos, with sealed goggles and washable coveralls. Keep others away.
- Never dry-sweep, brush, or use a regular shop-vac — use HEPA vacuuming plus wet wipe-down. Dispose of sludge, sandpaper, and rags as asbestos waste per local rules.
- Serpentine also carries ordinary silica/dust hazards; the wet + respirator protocol covers both.
- Occurrence is not permission and serpentine barrens host rare endemic plants — gather only permitted float/loose blocks, never disturb the barrens.

### Calcining gypsum into plaster of Paris

`mineral-plaster-of-paris-calcining` — **Viability 3/5** (solid) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Chalky white powder, sets to hard white `#ece7dd`

> Plaster of Paris is just gypsum that has been cooked. Heat raw gypsum or alabaster (calcium sulfate dihydrate) to around 150°C and it gives up three-quarters of its bound water, turning into the hemihydrate powder that — add water back — sets rock-hard in minutes. That single reversible reaction is the whole basis of mold-making and casting, and it closes a satisfying loop with the studio practice of reconstituting alabaster carving dust back into plaster.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-alabaster`
- **Keeping / durability:** _moderate_ — Keeping-quality of the powder, not permanence: fresh-calcined hemihydrate is hygroscopic and slowly re-absorbs air moisture, going 'stale' (slow or weak set) within weeks to a few months unless sealed airtight. A cast, cured plaster object is durable but soft (Mohs ~2) and water-sensitive.
- **Timeline:** 1–2 hr to calcine + grind active · cooling; use powder within weeks passive
- **Yield:** Calcined gypsum loses ~20% of its weight as water, so a batch of raw stone yields somewhat less plaster powder by weight — enough to cast several small molds.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Raw gypsum / alabaster (calcium sulfate dihydrate, CaSO₄·2H₂O)** — as much as your vessel holds — soft massive gypsum or alabaster carving offcuts/dust; must be reasonably pure gypsum
- **Heat source with controllable ~150°C** — an oven, kiln, or steady low fire — target ~150°C (300°F); overshoot burns it to 'dead' anhydrite that won't set
- **Water (to rehydrate when casting)** — ~2 parts plaster to 1 part water by weight, roughly — added later, at casting time

**Tools** — _Required:_ Oven, kiln, or controlled fire (~150°C), Heat-safe tray/vessel, Oven thermometer, P100/dust mask + goggles, Heat-resistant gloves, Mortar/mill to re-grind clumps to powder  ·  _Optional:_ Kitchen scale (repeatable water:plaster ratios), Airtight storage container with desiccant, Sieve to grade the powder, Fresh gypsum vs. reclaimed alabaster dust for comparison

**Steps**

1. Reduce the raw gypsum/alabaster to a coarse powder or small chips so heat penetrates evenly (mask + goggles — this makes dust).
2. Spread it thinly in a heat-safe tray and heat to about 150°C (300°F). Hold it there; the mineral is 'giving off' water as vapor. Do not exceed ~180°C or you burn it to anhydrite ('dead-burnt' plaster) that sets poorly or not at all.
3. Keep it at temperature until it stops steaming/losing weight (a couple of hours for a real batch) — that signals the dihydrate has converted to hemihydrate.
4. Cool it, then re-grind any clumps back to a fine, uniform powder. This is your plaster of Paris.
5. Store immediately in an airtight container — fresh hemihydrate grabs moisture from the air and goes stale.
6. To cast: sift plaster into water (roughly 2:1 by weight) until an island of dry powder just breaks the surface, let it slake ~1–2 min, mix smooth without whipping in air, pour into your mold. It heats up (exothermic) as it sets in minutes.
7. Let the cast cure and dry fully before use. Never pour leftover plaster slurry down a drain — it sets in the pipe; let it harden in the bucket and bin it.
8. Wear a dust mask throughout: dry gypsum/plaster dust is a respiratory irritant, and mixing releases heat.

**Variations & modifiers.** Time and temperature set the grade: ~150°C makes ordinary beta-hemihydrate plaster; higher/longer 'dead-burns' it to unusable anhydrite. Pottery-plaster and casting-plaster differ mainly in particle size and water ratio (more water = softer, more absorbent mold; less water = harder). Reclaimed alabaster carving dust calcines the same way, closing the waste loop — but verify it's clean gypsum, not mixed with abrasive or resin.

**Preservation / curing.** Store the finished powder airtight with a desiccant, cool and dry; expect weeks-to-months of usable life before it slowly rehydrates and loses set strength. Cured cast objects are stable indoors but soft and not waterproof — keep them dry; a wax or sealer helps.

**Beyond the recipe.** Feeds the plaster-mold card directly (slip-casting molds, sprig molds, press molds for the clay bodies) and closes the studio loop of alabaster carving dust reconstituted into plaster. Set plaster is also a modeling/patternmaking material and a carving stone in its own right.

**Safety**

- Hot burn hazard: calcining runs at ~150°C and the material and vessel stay hot — use heat gloves and eye protection.
- SETTING PLASTER IS EXOTHERMIC — a large mass (or plaster setting on skin/in a body-part mold) can reach scalding temperatures and cause serious burns. Never cast body parts in plaster; keep pours modest.
- Dry gypsum and plaster dust irritate eyes, nose, and lungs — wear a P100/dust mask and goggles when crushing, grinding, and mixing.
- Overheating past ~180°C 'dead-burns' the plaster (a quality failure) and wastes fuel/heat — mind the temperature.
- Never pour plaster slurry down drains — it hardens and clogs pipes. Let waste set solid and dispose in the trash.
- Casual specimen collecting only: bulk gypsum is a federal salable mineral, and occurrence is not permission — use offcuts, quarry waste, or purchased stone.

### Obsidian & chert knapping intro

`mineral-knapping` — **Viability 3/5** (solid) · **Advanced** · **Result:** Glassy conchoidal flake edge `#20201f`

> Obsidian fractures along a conchoidal curve into an edge only a few molecules thick — sharper than surgical steel, which is exactly why it cuts you before you feel it. Knapping toolstone (obsidian, chert, flint, jasper) is an ancient craft, but toolstone outcrops are often archaeological sites: collecting artifacts on public land is a federal crime under ARPA, and even natural stone can sit in a protected context.

⚠️ **Safety-first box** (toxic/hazard)

- **Maps to:** `mineral-obsidian`, `mineral-silica`, `mineral-agate`
- **Keeping / durability:** _good_ — A finished point is durable stone. This is a modern craft object, not a reproduction meant to be passed off as an artifact.
- **Timeline:** 1-3 hours for a first flake tool active
- **Yield:** A few practice flakes and, with patience, one crude point or blade from a first session.

**Ingredients / materials**

- **Knappable toolstone — obsidian, chert/flint, or jasper** — a few pieces — Buy legally sold spalls. Do NOT collect from public or protected land.
- **Leather pad / thick glove** — 1 — The stone rests on your leg pad; the glove protects your holding hand.

**Tools** — _Required:_ Hammerstone or billet (antler/copper), Pressure flaker (antler/copper tip), Heavy leather leg pad and hand pad, Wraparound eye protection, N95/P100 respirator  ·  _Optional:_ Abrader stone for platform prep, Cut-resistant gloves, Tarp to catch flakes

**Steps**

1. Confirm your stone was legally purchased — obsidian, chert, or jasper spalls from a supplier, never picked up on federal/state land.
2. Set up outdoors or over a tarp with a leather leg pad; wear eye protection and a respirator (silica dust), plus cut-resistant gloves.
3. Strike the edge of the core with a hammerstone at the right platform angle to detach a sharp flake — obsidian and chert both fracture conchoidally.
4. Refine the flake's shape with a billet, then pressure-flake fine notches and edges with an antler/copper tip.
5. Grind (abrade) platforms between flakes so strikes bite instead of crushing.
6. Sweep every flake into the tarp — loose obsidian shards are a laceration hazard underfoot. Rinse dust rather than sweeping dry.

**Variations & modifiers.** Obsidian flakes cleanest and sharpest but shatters easily; chert/flint is tougher and more forgiving for beginners; jasper and agate are hardest and most stubborn. Heat-treating some cherts improves flaking, but that is a separate advanced technique.

**Preservation / curing.** Finished tools are stable stone. Store points so their edges do not chip against each other. Never bury or 'age' a piece in a way that could be mistaken for an artifact.

**Beyond the recipe.** Waste flakes (debitage) make excellent hide-scraping and cutting edges. Broken obsidian is also a striking inlay material — set in resin, never left loose.

**Safety**

- LACERATIONS: obsidian edges are microscopically sharp and cut before you feel them. Gloves, controlled strikes, and slow handling are essential.
- SILICA DUST: chert/flint/jasper knapping raises respirable silica — wear a respirator and work outdoors/ventilated.
- EYE INJURY: flying flakes demand wraparound eye protection at all times.
- LEGAL: collecting artifacts or toolstone on federal, tribal, or most state land is illegal (ARPA, Antiquities Act, FLPMA). Occurrence is never permission — buy your stone.
- Contain and dispose of flakes; do not leave shards in soil or trails.
