# New Material Candidates — June 2026 Permissions Pass

Materials with documented harvest permission somewhere in the encoded rules
that are NOT yet in any species catalog. Each needs safety tags, harvest-ethic
labels, iNaturalist taxon IDs, and seasonality before inclusion.

## ✅ Integration status — FOOD pass shipped 2026-06-23 (commit `2b3bff4`)

The contiguous-US food candidates below were researched, adversarially
safety-reviewed, and **20 were added to `foodSpeciesCatalog`** (Hawaiʻi/USVI
excluded this pass): red mulberry, thimbleberry, salmonberry, Himalayan
blackberry, wild cranberry, red elderberry, buffaloberry, juniper berries,
kinnikinnick, wintergreen, chokecherry, pin cherry, rose hips, hawthorn,
mayapple, prickly pear, acorns, pinyon pine nuts, mesquite pods, chinquapin.

**Deferred for explicit owner decision (NOT added) — reasons:**
- **Saguaro fruit** & **barrel cactus fruit** — legally restricted/protected:
  Saguaro is AZ "Highly Safeguarded" (permit required) and tribal-only inside
  Saguaro NP & most public land (2016 NPS gathering rule); *Ferocactus* is AZ-
  protected (permit required) and only the fruit is edible (body/flesh toxic).
  Conflicts with the project's permission-required labeling — add only as
  permit-required/observe entries if desired.
- **Ramps (Allium tricoccum)** — heavily overharvested, slow-growing, *not*
  permitted at GRSM; conflicts with the abundance/low-impact ethic.
- **Beach peas (Lathyrus japonicus)** — lathyrism toxicity risk.
- **Wild rice / manoomin (Zizania palustris)** & **cholla buds
  (Cylindropuntia)** — culturally significant (Ojibwe / Tohono Oʼodham) *and*
  no fitting catalog category (would need a "grains/greens" category — owner call).
- **Rhubarb (naturalized)** & **Death Valley pomegranate/palm** — marginal
  single-park naturalized escapes; rhubarb also has toxic leaves + no "vegetable"
  category.

**Hawaiʻi / US Virgin Islands tropical groups (§C, §D below) remain candidates**
— excluded this pass because the map focuses on the contiguous US.

`permissions-research-2026-06-nps-expansion.md`), these foods have documented
**general-visitor** harvest permission in a current compendium but are **not** in
the food catalog. None added to the app; each would still need safety tags,
harvest-ethic labels, an iNaturalist taxon ID, and seasonality.

**Strategic note for the owner:** the catalog today is temperate-Eastern. Most
genuinely-new candidates fall into two clusters that would *extend the map's
geography*: the desert Southwest and the tropics (Hawaiʻi, US Virgin Islands).
The temperate group (§A) fits the existing footprint with the least friction; the
desert (§B) and tropical (§C, §D) groups are a deliberate scope decision. Several
carry cultural-harvest dimensions (saguaro, cholla, ʻōhelo, wild rice) where the
general-visitor designation coexists with tribal/Native practice — those want a
respect note, not just a limit, consistent with the project's ethics.

# Ink & Herbalism candidates beyond the temperate East (2026-06-23 research pass)

Discovery sweep across the Desert SW, California, Pacific NW, Rocky Mtn/Great Basin, and subtropical Gulf/Florida (contiguous-US only; Hawaiʻi/USVI excluded). **Nothing here is added to the site** — these are for owner review. **Read the FLAGGED tables first.** Per the project's ethics, overharvested/slow-growing/protected/culturally-appropriated/seriously-toxic species are separated out and should not be added as casual recommendations.

## ✅ Integration status — INK/DYE pass shipped 2026-06-25

The owner reviewed these candidates and asked for the qualifying ones to be
encoded under three criteria: (a) harvest is explicitly permitted somewhere, OR
non-damaging, OR invasive/weed removal; (b) plants that are merely poisonous to
themselves or use caustic processing are fine **if** there is no *wider*
community hazard and it can be labeled — those cards are marked **Intermediate**
or **Advanced**; (c) plants failing the harvest ethic because they need specific
regional/historical/cultural knowledge are published with an explicit
**"educational only"** stamp.

**Added to `inkSpeciesCatalog` (35 species)** with taxon IDs, seasonality,
`SAFETY_TAGS_BY_SPECIES`, and `HARVEST_ETHIC_BY_SPECIES`, plus full **project
cards** in `PROJECT_RECIPES` (now split into Inks / Dyes / Modifiers / Binders).
The map mode is renamed **Ink/Dye**. From the *candidates-for-review (37)* all
qualifying rows were added (regional duplicates merged into genus-level entries:
sagebrush+mugwort, rabbitbrush, alder, prickly pear, Oregon grape & barberry).

**From the FLAGGED table** — added with the stated safeguards:
- **Brazilian pepper** (Advanced; urushiol "never burn / gloves" warning) — owner
  approved as the one borderline-hazard invasive to include.
- **Dyer's woad** (Advanced; eradication-only, caustic lye vat) — invasive removal.
- **Oregon grape / Fremont's mahonia / agarita** — folded into one berry-focused
  entry; UI steers to berries, never casual root/bark digging.
- **Mountain mahogany** — pruned/fallen bark only, respectful cultural note.

**Published as EDUCATIONAL-ONLY / observe-only** (stamp, no harvest how-to):
Carolina redroot, tanoak (SOD quarantine), western redcedar (culturally sacred),
wolf lichen (slow-growing + toxic), and a context-only **cochineal** card (an
insect, sourced ethically — no map species).

**Deliberately EXCLUDED this pass (owner-confirmed) — reasons:**
- **Tansy ragwort** — pyrrolizidine alkaloids are cumulatively hepatotoxic to
  people *and* livestock (disposal/compost/livestock = a wider hazard); abundant
  safe yellows already added (rabbitbrush, coreopsis, etc.).
- **Chinaberry** — all parts (esp. berries) poisonous and the dye is weakly
  documented / poor color; thin craft justification for a home poisoning risk.
- **Giant coreopsis (sea dahlia)** — range-restricted CFP endemic; the catalog now
  carries the broadly-grown plains coreopsis as the substitute. Leave it wild.

# 3D / Objects-mode material candidates (2026-06-25 research pass)

Candidate materials for a fourth map mode — **Objects / 3D materials**: physical craft materials you shape into objects (baskets, carved/turned ware, cordage, mats, containers, brooms, stone and bone work), as distinct from the Food, Ink/Dye, and Medicine modes. Contiguous US only (excludes HI, AK, PR, USVI). **Nothing here is added to the site yet — this is for owner review.** Every entry below would still need an iNaturalist taxon id (where applicable), seasonality, safety tags, and a category mapping confirmed before inclusion.

**KEY PERMISSION INSIGHT (read first):** craft-material harvest is governed *differently and generally more strictly* than food foraging. The federal 36 CFR 2.1 exception that lets the public gather **fruits, nuts, berries, and mushrooms for personal consumption on NPS land does NOT extend to collecting stems, bark, roots, needles, withes, wood, or whole plants for craft.** A plant whose berries are legally edible in a national park is still illegal to cut for basketry there. Realistic craft harvest happens on **USFS/BLM personal-use land (often by free-use permit), private/cultivated land, and in invasive-removal contexts** — never by assuming the food permission carries over. Occurrence data is never harvest permission; keep that explicit in every popup.

## Permission & geolocation structure for a 3D map

**Geolocation works only for materials whose source organism is mappable in iNaturalist.** The site's mechanism plots live iNaturalist observations of the source organism over the USGS PAD-US public-access layer with hand-encoded rules. That works cleanly for a plant/tree/shrub with reasonable US observation density (a willow, a pine, an invasive vine). It does **not** work for materials that are not a mappable organism: **loose beach-cast seashells, "dead-and-down" firewood as a generic category, shed antler/bone/horn, and stone/minerals (chert, obsidian, soapstone, clay)**. For seashells and shed antler the iNat record plots the *living animal*, not the collectible loose part — there is no annotation that reliably separates an empty shell or a cast antler from a live sighting, and many records are subtidal or off-range. For stone and clay there is no biological taxon at all. These materials must be represented by **manually-placed points at named, permitted localities layered over the permission layer**, never as species pins. Shed antler additionally needs **date-aware seasonal-closure logic** (e.g. Colorado/Wyoming winter-range closures Jan 1–Apr 30) that the food map has never required.

**Where craft harvest is realistically permitted vs. not:**
- **USFS National Forests** — commonly allow personal-use "special forest products" (cones, boughs, some plant material, withes, fallen needles), sometimes by free-use permit. The most defensible public-land path for most materials here.
- **BLM** — small personal-use amounts, often under a forest-product permit; ~25 lb/day casual rockhounding for stone.
- **Private / cultivated land** — with landowner permission; the honest default for "cultivated preferred" materials (basket willow farms, broomcorn, gourds, yucca leaf from ornamentals).
- **Invasive-removal contexts** — actively encouraged across most land types; the strongest "harvest is a public good" case (honeysuckle, bittersweet, kudzu, ivy, broom, Arundo, etc.), subject to state transport/quarantine limits.
- **NPS units — generally NOT permitted** for any craft-part collection. The 36 CFR 2.1 food exception does not cover stems/bark/roots/needles/wood. The lone exception in this whole set is **unoccupied seashells**, which 36 CFR 2.1(c)(1) explicitly lets a superintendent designate for hand collection (park-variable, live shells never).
- **Tribal land, culturally-tended gathering sites, and protected riparian/wetland buffers** — off-limits without the stewarding community's or manager's consent.

## Indigenous craft practices (contiguous US) — featureable vs. flagged

The governing principle throughout: **feature the MATERIAL + identification + a respectful cultural-context note when the material is abundant, secular, and widely-taught; mark it observe-only when it is sacred, ceremonially-stewarded, tied to a closed practice, legally harvest-restricted, or already over-commercialized/appropriated.** White sage (*Salvia apiana*) is the canonical do-not case the site already carries; several materials below sit in that same tier.

- **Eastern Woodlands (Haudenosaunee, Wabanaki, Anishinaabe).** Featureable with credit: *basswood bast* cordage (wiigob), *cattail* and *bulrush* matting, *cornhusk* dolls/mats/baskets, *bottle gourd* rattles/containers, folded *red/yellow/river birch* and *maple* bark containers. **Observe-only:** *black ash* splint (sacred, IUCN-CR, EAB-collapsing, tribally stewarded); *paper birch* canoe-grade bark (honorable-harvest protocols — steer to fallen/shed bark); *slippery & American elm* bark (at-risk/endangered, tree-killing); *sweetgrass* (four sacred medicines, over-commercialized); *northern white cedar* (Grandmother Cedar); *basswood/black-ash WOOD for False Face masks* (sacred regalia); *sweet flag* rhizome (ceremonial); *spruce/larch/jack-pine root* watap (stewarded — present permission-based). The distinction within a single species matters: basswood **bast** is openly-shared cordage and featureable, while basswood **mask wood** is restricted.
- **Southeast (Cherokee, Chitimacha, Choctaw, Gullah Geechee).** Featureable: *white oak* and *hickory* splint, *honeysuckle* vine baskets (living Cherokee tradition — credit weavers), *longleaf/slash/loblolly pine-needle* coiling (Coushatta/Gullah lineages). **Observe-only:** *rivercane* and *switch cane* (~98% canebrake loss, active tribal restoration); *bloodroot*, *butternut*, and *yellowroot* dyes where declining/at-risk (yellowroot itself is featureable cultivated; bloodroot/butternut are flagged); *Lowcountry sweetgrass* (access lost to development — cultivated only).
- **Plains (Lakota, Kiowa, Omaha, Ponca, Pawnee).** Featureable: *prairie cordgrass* earth-lodge thatch (architectural/utilitarian), *softstem bulrush* matting (note possible ceremonial dimension). **Observe-only:** *prairie sage* smudge (sacred, Sun Dance); *eastern redcedar FOLIAGE* (sacred smudge — feature only the WOOD); *bison horn* (sacred horn-craft, byproduct-only); *porcupine quillwork* (sacred, gendered art); *pipestone* (tribal-reserved by law).
- **Southwest (O'odham, Hopi, Zuni, Navajo, Apache, Pueblo).** Featureable with credit: *three-leaf sumac*, *Texas mulberry* rods, *devil's-claw cultivar* (grow O'odham landrace), *yucca/agave/sotol* leaf fiber on cultivated/permitted land, *mesquite* wood/gum. **Observe-only / harvest-restricted:** *banana & soaptree yucca* and *Nolina* beargrass (AZ Native Plant Law harvest-restricted + stewarded fiber); *desert ironwood* (Near Threatened, salvage-restricted, Seri carving overharvest); *Fremont cottonwood root* (sacred Hopi katsina carving — religious objects). The recurring Southwest nuance: **Arizona's Native Plant Law specifically restricts fiber/wood harvest of yucca, agave, sotol, Nolina, mesquite, and ironwood**, so even abundant species require a permit + landowner consent there.
- **California (Pomo, Hupa, Yurok, Karuk, Maidu, Miwok, Cahuilla).** Featureable with credit: *deergrass* coiling foundation, *willows* (arroyo/sandbar), *western redbud* red weft, *three-leaf sumac*, *bracken* and *gray-pine-root* black/brown elements (openly taught). Handle with care/cultivated: *white root sedge* (*Carex barbarae* — tended beds, scarce, CIBA access concerns). **Observe-only:** *basket rush* (*Juncus textilis* — narrow endemic, declining, defining SoCal coiling material); *western maidenhair fern* black stipe (ceremonial caps/regalia, state-listed at edges); *redwood burl* (endangered, poaching). Enforce respectful naming (gray/foothill pine, three-leaf sumac — never the slurs).
- **Great Basin / Plateau (Washoe, Paiute, Nez Perce, Yakama).** Featureable: *sandbar* and *Pacific/shining willow* (with stewardship note on tended gathering sites), *tule*. The willows are foundational and secular but many productive stands are actively tended across generations — credit the stewardship and steer users to cultivated/clearly-public ground.
- **Pacific Northwest (Coast Salish, Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian, Nuu-chah-nulth).** Featureable: *red alder* (the workaday carving wood), *vine maple* (bentwood hoops/handles, salmon tongs), *bigleaf maple* inner bark (an ethical non-cedar alternative). **Observe-only:** *western redcedar* (sacred "Tree of Life," bark-pulling ceremony-bound and treaty-reserved); *Sitka spruce root* (stewarded, women-led skilled harvest); *bear grass* (*Xerophyllum* — fire-managed, commercial-vs-tribal-gatherer conflict). The PNW pattern is the clearest example of the within-genus distinction: red alder and bigleaf maple are featureable workaday materials, while redcedar in the same forests is the sacred, restricted one.

## Re-evaluation of the four existing 3D candidates

- **Seashells — KEEP-WITH-CAVEATS (and the geolocation question answered directly: NOT cleanly geolocatable via our pipeline).** iNaturalist has mollusk taxa in good volume (quahog *Mercenaria mercenaria* 125758, whelks *Busycon* 82805), but those observations record the **living organism**, not an empty, legally-collectible beach-cast shell. iNat has no annotation that reliably separates a vacant beach shell from a live animal, many records are subtidal/off-beach, and Falling Fruit is food-forage-only. **A loose seashell is therefore not a species observation** and cannot be plotted as one. Implement it as **curated hand-placed points at named legal beaches over the permission layer** with per-park rule popups. Notably, this is the **one craft material with a clean federal personal-use hook** — 36 CFR 2.1(c)(1) lets superintendents designate unoccupied seashells for hand collection (park-variable; e.g. Padre Island ~1 gal/day, Cape Cod restricts; live shells never). Ethic "fallen material preferred"; flag wampum-grade quahog/whelk with a respectful Indigenous cultural note (no "make your own wampum" framing); live/occupied shells never collectible; state shellfish rules govern any live take.
- **Firewood — DROP.** No source organism or iNaturalist taxon — "dead-and-down" is a generic multi-species land-use category, incoherent to plot as observations, and its real-world framing is **fuel, not shaped objects** (USFS/BLM personal-use cord permits, NPS campfire-only). Fold the USFS/BLM-vs-NPS rule into the permission layer; do not create a craft species entry.
- **Wildflowers — DROP as a broad category; KEEP-WITH-CAVEATS only as a narrow native-everlastings niche.** The marquee everlastings (strawflower *Helichrysum*, statice *Limonium*) are non-native cultivated annuals → "cultivated preferred," not wild-mappable. The one genuinely native, wild, mappable everlasting is **pearly everlasting (*Anaphalis margaritacea*, 52833, ~26,201 obs)**. Durable-object craft use is thin (pressed/dried/wreath work is decorative and ephemeral) and overlaps the existing Ink/Dye mode. Wild flower-picking is broadly prohibited on NPS land. Do not build a broad wildflower catalog for the objects mode.
- **Pine materials — KEEP (strongest of the four).** Strongly geolocatable (longleaf 81901, loblolly 59921, slash 69985, eastern white 52391, ponderosa 48461) with clearly tiered sub-uses: **fallen needles (pine straw)** for coiled basketry is the strongest and a real USFS special-forest-product; **fallen cones** are low-impact decorative material; **pitch/resin** is the weakest (requires wounding living trees → hardened-from-wounds only). Surface the longleaf IUCN-endangered-*ecosystem* context (harvest the dropped material, not the tree), pine-straw raking BMPs, and credit Coushatta/Gullah coiling lineages. Ethics: needles + cones "fallen material preferred"; pitch "fallen material preferred" with hardened-only guardrails.
