Agate & jasper cabochon

The oldest cut — a smooth polished dome

Safety — read before you start
  • SILICA DUST: never grind these stones dry. Water is coolant AND dust control; wear a respirator (P100 preferred over N95) around spray/mist.
  • Clean up grinding slurry while still wet (wet-wipe or HEPA vacuum, never dry-sweep) — dried slurry becomes airborne respirable silica.
  • Plug the cabbing machine into a GFCI-protected outlet since it runs water over an electric motor.
  • Wheels and saws — eye protection and careful finger placement; diamond wheels bite skin.
  • Dop-wax alcohol lamp is an open flame — keep it clear of solvents.
  • Petrified wood carries specific federal rules: never from national parks/monuments; BLM lands have free-use weight limits and prohibit commercial collection. Occurrence is not permission — source slabs and rough responsibly, not from protected land.

About

A cabochon is the oldest way to cut a stone: no facets, just a smooth polished dome that shows off agate banding, jasper landscapes, or the cell structure of petrified wood. You grind it wet on silicon-carbide or diamond wheels — and wet is not optional, because the water is both coolant and the only thing keeping silica dust out of your lungs.

Ingredients

  • Slab of agate, jasper, or petrified wood — 1 · Pre-sawn slab, ~4-6 mm; choose banding/pattern for the dome face.
  • Cabochon template + aluminum dop stick and dop wax — As needed · Wax-mount the stone to a stick to hold it against the wheels.
  • Water — Continuous flow · Wheels must run wet — coolant and dust suppression.

Tools

Required: Cabbing machine or diamond wheels (coarse→fine), Diamond scribe/template, Trim saw or nippers, Dop stick + wax + alcohol lamp, Safety glasses, P100 respirator (preferred over N95), GFCI-protected outlet for the cabbing machine

Optional: Flat lap for the back, Cerium/diamond polish + felt/leather wheel, Magnifier

Steps

  1. Choose the pattern on the slab and scribe the outline with a template; trim close to the line with a saw or nippers.
  2. Grind the outline true on a coarse diamond wheel, running water continuously. Plug the cabbing machine into a GFCI-protected outlet since it runs water over an electric motor.
  3. Warm dop wax, mount the stone on a dop stick, and grind the dome — coarse to fine — checking the curve as you go.
  4. Sand up through the fine wheels until all scratches vanish; keep everything wet and wear a respirator around any spray.
  5. Polish on a felt/leather wheel with cerium or diamond compound to a mirror dome.
  6. Release the stone from the dop (freezer or heat), clean off wax, and finish the flat back.
  7. Clean up grinding slurry while still wet (wet-wipe or HEPA vacuum, never dry-sweep) — dried slurry becomes airborne respirable silica.

Variations & modifiers

Diamond wheels cut faster and cleaner than silicon-carbide but cost more. Softer or fractured stone needs lighter pressure. Petrified wood and banded agate reward orienting the slab to center the best pattern under the dome.

Preservation & storage

Finished cabs need no upkeep; store so domes do not rub. Set into wire or bezel jewelry when ready.

Yield

One cabochon per session as you learn; faster once dopping and doming feel natural.

Beyond the recipe

Cab offcuts feed the tumbler; small scraps become inlay or mosaic. Practice cabs make sample sets for teaching lapidary.
