Hawthorn haw jelly & haw 'ketchup'

Hedgerow jelly and a fruity brown sauce

Safety — read before you start
  • Seeds first: hawthorn seeds contain cyanogenic amygdalin - cook and strain them out, and never crush, grind, or eat the seeds. Risk from a few intact strained-out pips is low, but the rule stands.
  • Heart-medication caution: hawthorn can add to the effects of digoxin, beta-blockers, and blood-pressure drugs. Culinary jelly is a small dose, but anyone on cardiac medication should check with their clinician before eating hawthorn preparations regularly, and should not treat this as medicine.
  • Identify carefully - confirm the plant is a true hawthorn before harvesting, and don't confuse the fruit with toxic red berries.
  • For shelf-stable jars, follow a tested boiling-water process for the sweet jelly only; keep the vinegar ketchup refrigerated. Occurrence is not permission - forage only where legal.

About

Hawthorn haws (Crataegus spp.) are small, seedy, and mild - a classic hedgerow jelly fruit and the base of a savory 'haw ketchup' that eats like a fruity brown sauce. The whole trick is flesh only: you simmer, then push the pulp through a sieve so the seeds stay behind and go in the compost. Hawthorn seeds, like other rose-family pips, contain cyanogenic amygdalin, so they're cooked and strained out, never crushed or eaten.

Ingredients

  • Ripe hawthorn haws, de-stemmed — 1.5-2 lb · Flesh only is used; the seeds are strained out and discarded - never crushed.
  • Water — to just cover
  • Sugar — about 1 cup per cup of strained juice (for jelly) · Measure juice first, then match sugar.
  • Lemon juice — 1-2 Tbsp (optional) · Haws are low-acid and pectin-moderate; lemon aids set and acidity.
  • Cider vinegar, sugar, chili, salt, spices — to taste (for the ketchup version) (optional) · Vinegar makes the savory sauce and adds acidity.

Tools

Required: Heavy non-reactive pot, Sieve, food mill, or jelly bag, Potato masher, Jars with lids, Ladle

Optional: Boiling-water canner, Candy thermometer, Blender (for smooth ketchup), Bottles

Steps

  1. Forage haws only where permitted; identify carefully (confirm a true hawthorn, and avoid confusing with other red berries).
  2. De-stem, wash, and simmer the haws in just enough water to cover, mashing occasionally, ~30-40 minutes until soft and pulpy.
  3. For jelly: pour the softened mash into a jelly bag and let it drip overnight - don't force it, or the jelly clouds. Measure the clear juice.
  4. Boil the juice with roughly equal sugar (plus lemon juice) to the jellying point, ~220 F at sea level, then jar.
  5. For haw ketchup: press the softened mash through a sieve so seeds stay behind, then simmer the sieved pulp with vinegar, sugar, salt, and spices to a pourable sauce.
  6. Fill hot jars with 1/4-inch headspace, seal. Water-bath process only the sweet high-sugar jelly to a tested jam/jelly time for your altitude; default the haw ketchup to refrigerator storage.
  7. Serve jelly with cheese and game; use the ketchup like a fruity brown/HP-style sauce.

Variations & modifiers

Blend haws with crab apple or wild apple to boost pectin and guarantee a firm jelly. The ketchup takes well to chili, garlic, ginger, or star anise. A splash of the jelly into pan sauces makes a quick gastrique.

Preservation & storage

Strained sweet jelly is acidic and high-sugar; for pantry storage, process filled jars in a boiling-water canner to a tested jam/jelly time (1/4-inch headspace, altitude-corrected). The savory haw ketchup is a lower-sugar vinegar sauce with no established tested water-bath recipe - keep it refrigerated rather than assuming a jam/jelly process makes it shelf-stable. Refrigerate opened jars and use within weeks; discard any jar that fails to seal or smells off.

Yield

Roughly 3-4 half-pints of jelly, or a couple of jars of ketchup, from ~2 lb haws.

Beyond the recipe

Hawthorn is a well-studied heart herb - leaf, flower, and berry preparations are traditional cardiotonics. That makes the food-vs-medicine line worth naming: culinary jelly is food, but concentrated hawthorn extracts can interact with heart drugs (see safety). Dried haws also make a mild tea.
