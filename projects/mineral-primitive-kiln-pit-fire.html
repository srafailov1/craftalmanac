Safety — read before you start
  • Open-fire hazard: work only where permitted, clear a firebreak, keep water/extinguisher ready, mind wind and dry surroundings, and fully drown/extinguish the ashes before leaving.
  • Exploding pots: any moisture in the clay flashes to steam and can burst a pot violently, throwing hot shards — use only bone-dry (ideally pre-bisqued) ware, pre-warm it, and heat the fire up slowly. Wear eye protection and keep bystanders back.
  • Severe burn risk from an open high-temperature fire and hot ware/coals — heavy gloves, eye protection, long tongs, and no reaching over the fire.
  • Silica dust: dry wild clay, grog, and silica sand contain crystalline silica. Mix and process clay wet, don't dry-sweep, and wear a NIOSH N95/P100 respirator when handling dry clay/grog or dry-cleaning fired ware — repeated exposure causes silicosis.
  • Smoke and fumes: reducing fires make heavy smoke and carbon monoxide — fire OUTDOORS only, never in an enclosed or poorly ventilated space, and stay upwind. Some added colorants (copper compounds, salt) release irritating fumes.
  • Pit-fired ware is porous, not vitrified — treat it as decorative; do not assume it is watertight or food-safe. Cracked fired fragments have sharp edges — handle with gloves and blunt them before use/display.

About

The oldest way to turn dug clay into pottery is a fire in a hole. Pit firing dates back over 10,000 years — Jomon, Ancestral Puebloan, and countless others hardened pots at 600–900°C in an open bonfire or a simple clay updraft kiln. It never vitrifies the clay; it sinters it, leaving an open, porous body that survives the very thermal shock of the fire. The reward is a surface no glaze can imitate: carbon blacks, copper blushes, and smoke flashing painted on by the flames themselves.

Ingredients

  • Bone-dry, coarse/grogged wild-clay pots (bisque or greenware) — As many as fit with space between · Must be BONE DRY; open, grogged bodies with sand/silica resist thermal shock.
  • Dry firewood / hardwood — A generous pile · The main fuel — dry only; green wood steams and cracks pots.
  • Fine combustibles (straw, sawdust, dung, dry leaves, paper) — Layers around and over the pots · Create the reducing atmosphere and surface color.
  • Colorant combustibles (copper carbonate, salt, seaweed, banana skins, coffee grounds) — Sprinkled/wrapped around pots (optional) · Give blushes, flashes, and blacks on the surface; copper compounds and salt release irritating fumes.

Tools

Required: A dug pit or a simple clay/brick updraft kiln, Dry firewood + fine tinder, Long tongs / iron poker, Heavy heat gloves + eye protection, Water/hose/extinguisher and a cleared firebreak, NIOSH N95/P100 respirator for handling dry clay/grog or dry-cleaning fired ware

Optional: Sawdust/dung for a slow smoulder, Kiln bricks or a metal drum shell, Chimney/updraft opening for a simple kiln, A cover (metal sheet) to bank and slow the burn

Steps

  1. Fire only where open fires are permitted and safe — check burn bans, get any permit, clear a wide firebreak, and have water/extinguisher on hand. Occurrence of clay is not permission to burn.
  2. Use bone-dry pots made from an open, grogged/sandy wild-clay body. Warm them thoroughly near the fire first (or pre-bisque low) to drive off all moisture — any trapped water flashes to steam and explodes the pot.
  3. Bed the pit: a base layer of sawdust/combustibles, then nestle the warmed pots with space around each, packing fine combustibles (and any colorants) around and between them.
  4. Build the fuel up GRADUALLY — light kindling and let heat rise slowly over the first hour so the pots don't thermal-shock; add wood in stages to a full burn, reaching roughly 600–900°C.
  5. Hold the burn until the pots glow and the wood collapses to coals (several hours). A cover or extra sawdust creates a smoky reducing atmosphere for blacks.
  6. Let it cool SLOWLY, undisturbed, overnight — rushing the cool cracks the ware by thermal shock. Don't pull hot pots into cold air.
  7. Once cold, lift pots out and wet-wipe or rinse off ash rather than dry-brushing, to avoid raising silica/ash dust. Expect some losses — cracks and 'pings' are normal to the method; cracked fired fragments have sharp edges, so handle with gloves and blunt them.
  8. Fully extinguish and drown the ashes before leaving; stir and confirm cold-out.

Variations & modifiers

Atmosphere is the palette: an open oxidizing fire gives warm reds/buffs; smothering with sawdust/dung for a reducing smoke gives blacks and greys; copper carbonate and salt flash greens and pinks; seaweed and organic wraps leave line and haze. A simple clay/brick updraft kiln (even built from the same clay as the pots) holds heat better and fires more evenly than a bare pit. Terra sigillata burnished on before firing gives a soft sheen.

Preservation & storage

The fired ware is finished but porous — keep decorative pieces dry; don't rely on them for liquids or food unless a proper high-fire/glazed body is used. A post-fire wax or milk/organic burnish can reduce porosity slightly for looks, not food-safety. Store fuel dry; never store combustibles near the firing pit.

Yield

A single pit or small kiln fires a batch of small-to-medium pots at once; plan for a portion to crack, especially early on.

Beyond the recipe

This is the firing step that actually completes the wild-clay body cards — dug and processed clay becomes usable pottery here. The grog/sand in the body is the same silica material on the map, and wood ash from the fire is a classic glaze ingredient, feeding the ash-glaze side of the studio and the 'remnant becomes ingredient' loop.
