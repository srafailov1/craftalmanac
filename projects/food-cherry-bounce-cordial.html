Wild Cherry & Rose-Hip Cordial (Cherry Bounce)

Alcohol-preserved wild cherry cordial

  • Food map

  • Garnet infused liqueur

Safety — read before you start
  • NEVER crush, grind, or blend cherry/chokecherry/plum pits - they are cyanogenic. Infuse whole fruit only, for a bounded time, then strain and discard the pits. Intact pits keep cyanide release low but do not eliminate it.
  • Even correctly made whole-fruit cordial can carry trace cyanogenic compounds leached from the pits, so keep servings small - reinforcing the not-for-children/pregnant rule below.
  • Chokecherry and pin cherry especially: cook or infuse only; never eat raw pits or foliage.
  • Prickly-pear glochids: singe/peel before handling; the tiny barbed hairs embed in skin. Rose hips: strain out the irritant internal hairs (double-strain).
  • This is an alcoholic product - not for children, pregnant people, or anyone avoiding alcohol; label clearly.
  • Occurrence is not permission - harvest where allowed and leave a wildlife share.

About

Cherry bounce - George Washington packed it for a trip across the Alleghenies - is preservation by alcohol: sugar and spirit pull the fruit's flavor and color out and hold it indefinitely. Wild sour, black, and choke cherries all lend a deep garnet and almond-tinged flavor. That almond note is a warning label: it comes from the same cyanogenic compounds in the pits, so the rule that keeps cyanide release low is to infuse whole, un-crushed fruit for a bounded time and never grind the stones.

Ingredients

  • Whole wild cherries (sour, black, or chokecherry), stemmed — ~2 cups · Leave whole - do NOT crush or grind pits (cyanogenic); infuse a bounded time (a few weeks up to ~1 month with pits), then strain.
  • Neutral spirit, brandy, or whiskey (~40% ABV) — ~2-3 cups · The alcohol both extracts and preserves.
  • Sugar — ~1/2-1 cup · Sweetens and helps preserve; a cordial by definition is sweetened.
  • Rose hips, prickly-pear fruit, or spices — To taste (optional) · Rose hips add tartness/color; strain rose hips well (irritant internal hairs).

Tools

Required: Clean glass jar with tight lid, Fine strainer + cheesecloth, Bottles for the finished cordial, Funnel

Optional: Gloves and tongs for prickly pear (glochids), Kitchen scale, Labels

Steps

  1. Harvest ripe cherries where permitted; leave a share for wildlife. Stem and rinse. Keep the fruit WHOLE - do not pit-crush, grind, or blend, because the pits are cyanogenic.
  2. For prickly-pear tuna: singe/scrub off the glochids and peel first - never handle bare-fingered. For rose hips, trim and de-seed or plan to strain out the irritant internal hairs later.
  3. Layer fruit and sugar in a clean jar, then cover completely with spirit. Seal.
  4. Store in a cool, dark place and shake gently every few days to dissolve the sugar.
  5. Keep the pits-in infusion bounded - a few weeks up to about a month, not open-ended, since longer alcohol contact slowly leaches more cyanogenic compounds. Always strain the fruit out at the end rather than leaving pits sitting; for longer aging, strain the fruit out early.
  6. Strain through cheesecloth (double-strain rose-hip versions to catch every fine hair). Taste and adjust sweetness.
  7. Bottle the clear cordial, label, and store. It only improves with a little age.

Variations & modifiers

Sour and chokecherry give the brightest garnet and tartness; black cherry is softer and winier. Add a strip of rose hip or a splash of prickly-pear juice for color and tang, or warm spices (cinnamon, clove, vanilla) for a holiday cordial. Use less sugar for a drier 'ratafia' style. Cornelian cherry makes an excellent tart substitute.

Preservation & storage

Finished, strained cordial is shelf-stable for a year or more thanks to alcohol and sugar - store cool and dark. Straining the fruit out is both a flavor and a safety step (keeps pit compounds from accumulating over long storage). No refrigeration required, though it doesn't hurt.

Yield

About 2-3 cups of cordial from 2 cups fruit and 2-3 cups spirit. A little goes a long way - it's a sipping/mixing liqueur.

Beyond the recipe

The alcohol-soaked strained cherries (pits still in - never eat the pits) can flavor desserts or be discarded; the safest path is to compost the spent fruit. Prickly-pear peels and rose-hip strainings compost. The cordial itself is a base for shrubs, cocktails, and glazes.
