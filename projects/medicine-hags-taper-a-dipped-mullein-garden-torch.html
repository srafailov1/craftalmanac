Hag's Taper: a dipped mullein garden torch

A dead winter stalk built up in beeswax until it stands like a fat candle.

  • Medicine map
  • Garden
  • Intermediate
  • Toxic, read safety
Safety, read before you start
  • Positively identify the plant before you cut. Confirm mullein by its single tall stalk topped with a dense woolly seed-capsule spike and a felted, fuzzy leaf rosette at the base. Other tall biennials leave similar dead stalks standing through winter. Poison hemlock (Conium maculatum) has a smooth, hairless, hollow stalk blotched and streaked with purple or maroon, never woolly, and it stays toxic when dead and gives off poisonous smoke when burned. Teasel leaves a rigid, prickly, egg-shaped spiny seed head, unlike mullein's soft felted spike. Never cut, dip, or burn a stalk you have not positively identified as mullein.
  • Strip the seed heads before you dip or burn. Shake or comb the mature seed capsules into a closed bag and put them in household trash, and never scatter them on the ground, which keeps this weedy seed from spreading and keeps you from burning the plant's most concentrated part. Mullein seeds contain rotenone and saponins, so keep them away from children and pets and never eat them.
  • Burn with open flame outdoors only. Use it in the open air, never indoors and never on an enclosed or screened porch. Keep the torch well away from dry brush, overhanging branches, and structures, set the base in a metal holder on bare soil, and keep water within reach. The pith wicks fast and the head can drip hot wax.
  • Stay out of the smoke. Stand upwind, do not lean over the flame or fan it, and keep people with breathing difficulties, pregnant people, children, and pets out of the smoke plume. Burning any plant material produces fine-particle smoke and combustion irritants.
  • Handle the dry stalk with care. Cut and process it outdoors, wear gloves and eye protection, avoid crushing the fuzzy stalk near your face, and wash your hands afterward. The dry, branched (stellate) leaf and stem hairs shed easily and can irritate skin, eyes, and airways.
  • Dispose of ash, burnt debris, and stripped seed in household trash, never near ponds, streams, or storm drains, because mullein saponins are toxic to fish.
  • Occurrence is never harvest permission. The map shows where mullein grows, not where you may gather it or light a flame. Cut dead plant material only where it is allowed, preferring your own garden, waste ground, or disturbed roadsides where mullein is abundant, and remember that many parks and preserves prohibit all collection and all open flame.
  • This is a garden craft object, not for any use on the body. Keep the finished torch and all materials away from children and pets.

About

Long before wax candles were common, a dried mullein stalk dipped in fat made a cheap standing torch. The Romans dipped mullein stalks in tallow (rendered beef or mutton fat) and carried them in funeral processions, and the plant kept a whole shelf of torch-and-candle folk names down the centuries: Hag's Taper, Candlewick, King's Candle, Torches. This card revives that old outdoor craft with beeswax, which burns cleaner than tallow, using the plant's dead second-year seed stalk after it has finished its life and dried standing in the field. Presented purely as a botanical craft that reuses the plant's tall dead stalk, not for any use on the body.

Ingredients

  • Dried second-year mullein flower stalk (Verbascum thapsus), standing dead seed stalk, 1 stalk, about 2 to 5 feet long · Cut only a bone-dry, fully dead stalk. The woolly pith of the seed-head end is what holds the wax.
  • Beeswax (blocks or pellets), 1 to 2 pounds · Pellets melt faster. You want enough depth in the pot to submerge the seed-head end of the stalk.
  • Water, Enough to fill the outer pot of the double boiler · For the water bath only. Wax is never heated directly over the flame.
  • Cotton wick, natural fiber, A 2 to 3 inch length (optional) · Optional. Pressed into the warm wax at the tip, it makes the torch easier to light.

Tools

Required: Tall double boiler, or a tall clean metal can set inside a pot of water, Heat source (stovetop or hot plate), Heat-resistant gloves and eye protection, A closed bag or lidded container for the stripped seed capsules, Metal torch holder or a metal ground spike to stand the base in

Optional: Candy or dipping thermometer, Pruning saw or loppers, A second tall metal vessel deep enough to dip into, A comb or gloved hand for stripping seed heads

Steps

  1. Positively identify the plant, then cut. Confirm mullein by its single tall stalk topped with a dense woolly seed spike and, at the base, a felted fuzzy leaf rosette. Cut one bone-dry dead stalk near ground level with a saw or loppers. Do this outdoors, wearing gloves and eye protection, and avoid crushing the fuzzy stalk near your face.
  2. Strip the seed heads outdoors. Working over a closed bag or lidded container, shake and comb the mature seed capsules off the spike so they cannot scatter. Seal the bag and put it in household trash. Keep the stripped seed away from children and pets.
  3. Set up the water bath and melt the wax. Fill the outer pot with water, set the inner vessel of beeswax inside, and warm over medium heat until the wax is fully liquid, about 150 to 160 F. Keep the wax over the water bath the whole time, never directly over the flame.
  4. Dip the seed-head end. Lower the woolly top of the stalk into the melted wax, hold it a few seconds so the pith soaks, then lift it out and let the coat set for one to two minutes until it clouds and firms.
  5. Build up the head. Repeat the dip-and-cool cycle 10 to 20 times, letting each coat cool between dips, until a waxed shell about a quarter to a half inch thick has built up around the seed-head end. If using the optional wick, press it into the warm wax at the very tip during the last few dips.
  6. Harden the torch. Stand or lay the finished torch in a cool, dry spot and let it set undisturbed for 48 hours before burning so the wax fully hardens.
  7. Burn it outdoors only. Set the base in a metal holder or ground spike on bare soil, well away from dry brush, branches, and structures, with water within reach. Scrape a small bare spot at the very tip, light it, and stand upwind. Expect roughly 10 minutes of burn per inch of waxed head; the pith wicks fast and the head can drip hot wax.

Variations & modifiers

Traditional Roman torches used rendered tallow instead of beeswax. Tallow is the authentic material but it smokes more, so beeswax is the cleaner-burning choice here. More dips build a thicker shell that burns longer, while a slimmer shell lights and catches faster. Cutting the stalk shorter (12 to 18 inches) makes a tabletop-height garden torch that is easier to seat firmly in a holder.

Preservation & storage

Store the finished torch upright or flat in a cool, dry place out of direct sun and away from any heat source, so the wax does not soften or slump. Kept dry, a waxed stalk holds for years. Keep the stored torch and any leftover seed away from children and pets.

Yield

1 garden torch, about 30 to 60 minutes of open-air burn (roughly 10 minutes per inch of waxed head).
