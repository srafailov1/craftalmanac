Silica / Quartz Glass-Former Addition

The lever that pulls a crazing glaze into fit

  • Minerals map
  • Glaze
  • Intermediate
  • Harder, brighter, craze-free glaze
  • Toxic — read safety
Safety — read before you start
  • Free crystalline silica is the single biggest chronic hazard in a ceramics studio — inhalation causes irreversible silicosis. Use commercial pre-ground silica, work wet, wear a P100 respirator, and wet-clean surfaces.
  • Never dry-mill foraged quartz, chert, novaculite, or petrified wood without full containment, HEPA, and P100 protection; these are near-pure silica.
  • Do not dry-sweep glaze dust — it re-suspends respirable silica.
  • Petrified wood is legally protected in many places — collection is prohibited in national parks, limited on BLM land (43 CFR 3622), and covered by paleontological-resource law; verify land status and limits before collecting any mapped stone.
  • Correcting fit improves food-safety (no craze-line bacteria harbor) but does not by itself make a colored glaze non-leaching — still leach-test.
  • Wet-cut mapped stones (agate/chert/petrified wood) to avoid both silica dust and sharp fracture edges.

About

Silica is the glass in glass — SiO2, the same building block of quartz crystal, chert, novaculite whetstones, and petrified wood. Alone it needs ~1700 degrees C to melt, so glazes borrow flux to coax it into liquid at kiln temperatures. But the silica you add does one more quiet, crucial job: it lowers the glaze's thermal expansion, pulling a crazing glaze back into compression on the pot. This isn't a recipe so much as the lever that makes every other glaze fit.

Ingredients

  • Silica (325-mesh quartz/flint) — Add in 5% steps, typically 20-35% of a base · Commercial 325-mesh quartz is safest and dissolves better than coarser grades; foraged quartz/chert/petrified wood must be milled extremely fine — a serious silica-dust job.
  • A crazing base glaze to correct — Remainder · Any glossy glaze that fine-line crazes is the candidate for a silica line blend.
  • Kaolin (EPK) — 0-5% (optional) · Add alongside silica to keep alumina/silica balanced and glaze glossy rather than dry.

Tools

Required: P100 respirator, Gram scale, Test tiles, 80-mesh sieve, Gloves

Optional: Ball mill (essential if milling foraged quartz), Thermal-shock kettle, Dilatometer (lab)

Steps

  1. Identify a glaze that crazes (fine cracks) — that means its expansion is too high relative to the clay body.
  2. Mix a line blend adding silica in 5% increments (e.g. +5, +10, +15%) to separate cups of the base.
  3. If foraging quartz, chert, or petrified wood: verify land status and collecting limits first (petrified wood is prohibited in national parks under 36 CFR 2.1 and limited on BLM land under 43 CFR 3622, and may fall under the Paleontological Resources Preservation Act). Then cut and grind WET, then ball-mill to a fine flour. Dry silica milling is one of the most dangerous studio tasks for silicosis — commercial 325-mesh silica is strongly preferred.
  4. Glaze test tiles from each blend and fire to the target cone.
  5. Thermal-shock the fired tiles using the standard IWCT (heat tile to ~300F, plunge into ice water, repeat several cycles) and inspect for craze lines under a loupe. Wear eye protection — tiles can shatter.
  6. Pick the lowest silica addition that eliminates crazing without dulling the surface (too much silica goes stony/under-melted).
  7. If added silica makes the glaze dry, add a little more flux (feldspar/whiting) or a touch of kaolin to rebalance.
  8. Recheck fit and, for food ware, leach-test the final recipe.

Variations & modifiers

Silica and kaolin added together in a ~2:1 ratio keep the alumina/silica balance so gloss survives. If you overshoot and the glaze under-melts (matte, pinholed), pull silica back or add flux. Coarser quartz can be used deliberately for micro-texture in art glazes.

Preservation & storage

Store silica sealed and dry. Mixed glaze keeps in a lidded bucket. Fired correction is permanent and improves ware longevity by preventing water ingress through craze lines.

Yield

A line blend is a handful of test tiles, not a batch of pots — but it can rescue an entire glaze recipe and every future pot glazed with it.

Beyond the recipe

Fine milled quartz is also a body filler to open a clay body and reduce warping, and a polishing abrasive (novaculite is literally whetstone quartz). Screened-out coarse quartz makes grog.
