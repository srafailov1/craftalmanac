Wood-Ash Glaze (40/40/20)

Rebuild the first accidental glaze from fireplace ash

Safety — read before you start
  • Dry wood ash is CAUSTIC, and the dry glaze batch (flint, feldspar, clay) contains crystalline free silica — sieve and weigh outdoors or under exhaust wearing a P100 respirator, goggles, and gloves; silica dust causes irreversible silicosis.
  • Wash water and wet ash are strongly alkaline (lye-like) — wear chemical-resistant gloves and eye protection; can burn skin and eyes.
  • Never use ash from treated, painted, or manufactured wood — it concentrates arsenic, chromium, copper, and chlorides.
  • Runny ash glazes can weld pots to shelves — glaze thinly and protect kiln shelves.
  • Food-safety: a well-fitted ash glaze can be food-safe on liner surfaces only after per-batch testing for crazing and leaching (e.g., 24-hour lemon/vinegar test); high-iron or barium-bearing variants and crazed surfaces are decorative-only.

About

For a thousand years before anyone measured a glaze, the wood ash that drifted onto pots in the kiln melted into an accidental glass — the first glazes were pure serendipity. Sieve out your fireplace ash, blend it 40:40:20 with feldspar and clay, and you rebuild that ancient accident on purpose: the calcium and potash in the ash flux the silica into a runny, streaky celadon that pools amber in the throwing marks. No two batches match, because no two fires burn the same wood.

Ingredients

  • Wood ash (hardwood, sieved 30-80 mesh) — 40% by dry weight · Unwashed ash keeps more soluble flux but is caustic and variable; washed ash is gentler and more repeatable. Hardwood (oak, ash, fruitwood) gives more flux than softwood.
  • Potash or soda feldspar (e.g. Custer, Minspar) — 40% · The alumina/silica backbone; substitute nepheline syenite to lower melt temp.
  • Ball clay or kaolin (EPK) — 20% · Adds alumina, suspends the slurry, and stiffens a too-runny melt.
  • Silica (quartz/flint) — 0-15% (line-blend addition) (optional) · Add to raise melting point and reduce running; helps glaze fit to cut crazing.
  • Red iron oxide — 0-4% (optional) · Deepens celadon green in reduction; keep low for classic pale celadon.

Tools

Required: NIOSH N95/P100 respirator, Chemical-resistant rubber gloves, Safety goggles, 80-mesh sieve, Gram scale, Buckets

Optional: Ball mill, Spray booth, Hydrometer, Test tiles

Steps

  1. Collect ash only from clean, untreated wood — never painted, pressure-treated, or glued scrap, which carry heavy metals and chlorides.
  2. Wearing a P100 respirator and goggles, dry-sieve ash through 30-mesh outdoors or under local exhaust to remove charcoal and nails. Dry ash is caustic and lung-damaging.
  3. Optional wash: soak ash in water in a lidded bucket for a week, pour off the caustic (soapy-feeling) water, refill, and repeat until the water runs clear and non-slippery. Wear gloves; the wash water can be as caustic as lye.
  4. Weigh 40 ash : 40 feldspar : 20 clay by dry weight. Add colorant/silica per your line blend.
  5. Mix into water to a thin-cream consistency, sieve through 80-mesh, and hydrometer to a repeatable specific gravity.
  6. Glaze bisqueware thinly — ash glazes run. Leave the bottom 1/2 inch bare and stilt or wad if needed.
  7. Fire to cone 9-10; in reduction the iron shifts to celadon green, in oxidation toward amber/tan.
  8. Test every new wood source and wash-state on tiles before committing pots.

Variations & modifiers

Line-blend the ash:feldspar:silica triangle to tune the melt: more ash = runnier and glossier; more clay = stiffer and more matte; more silica = less running and better fit. Softwood ash and straw/grass ash carry more silica and phosphorus for opalescent 'oil-spot' effects. Layer thin over an iron slip for classic hare's-fur streaking.

Preservation & storage

Store dry ingredients sealed and labeled; store mixed glaze in a lidded bucket and re-sieve if it settles. Fired ware is permanent. Do not store wash water — neutralize and dispose per local rules; it is caustic.

Yield

A 1000 g dry batch glazes roughly 15-25 mugs depending on thickness. Expect real variability batch-to-batch; ash glazes reward test tiles over hope.

Beyond the recipe

Spent, neutralized ash-wash sludge can be composted in small amounts (it is essentially lime/potash). Sieved-out charcoal is fine for the garden. Leftover glaze slurry blends into other high-fire bases as a flux booster.
