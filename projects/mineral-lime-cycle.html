Lime cycle: limewash & putty

Rock to caustic putty to stone-on-the-wall again

Safety — read before you start
  • CAUSTIC: quicklime and fresh lime are strongly alkaline and cause chemical burns to skin and eyes; slaking is exothermic and can boil and spatter. Full PPE is non-negotiable.
  • Lime burns can feel painless at first — flush any skin or eye contact with running water for 15+ minutes, remove contaminated clothing, and get medical help for eye exposure.
  • Always add lime to water in stages — never water to a mass of quicklime.
  • P100 respirator against lime dust; never breathe quicklime powder.
  • Home calcining requires a real high-temperature kiln — most should buy quicklime or putty, not burn stone.
  • Occurrence is not permission — do not quarry limestone or marble from mapped or protected land.

About

The lime cycle is the oldest chemistry in building: burn limestone or marble (calcium carbonate) above ~900°C to drive off CO2 and get quicklime, slake that quicklime in water — a violently exothermic reaction that can boil and spit — into a smooth putty, then thin it to a limewash that slowly re-absorbs CO2 from the air and turns back to stone on the wall. It is beautiful and it is genuinely dangerous: quicklime and fresh putty are strongly caustic and will burn skin and eyes.

Ingredients

  • Quicklime (calcium oxide) OR mature lime putty — As needed · Most crafters start from purchased quicklime or ready putty rather than burning their own stone. Quicklime is the hazardous step.
  • Limestone or marble — For the burn (advanced only) (optional) · Calcining your own requires a lime kiln reaching ~900°C+ — a serious undertaking, not a casual step.
  • Clean water — Roughly 2-3x lime by volume for slaking · Always add lime to water in stages, never the reverse, to control the boil.
  • Natural earth pigment (optional) — Up to ~5-10% by volume (optional) · Only alkali-stable pigments (ochres, oxides) survive lime's high pH.

Tools

Required: Chemical goggles and face shield, Heavy rubber/neoprene gauntlets, P100 respirator, Non-reactive slaking tub (steel or heavy-duty HDPE — never aluminum or galvanized; slaking heat can soften thin plastic), Long stir stick, Full skin cover

Optional: Sieve for straining putty, Masonry brush for limewash, Sealed maturing bucket

Steps

  1. SAFETY FIRST: goggles, face shield, heavy rubber/neoprene gauntlets, respirator, covered skin, and running water nearby before any lime touches water.
  2. Slaking (if starting from quicklime): add quicklime to water in small increments. It will hiss, steam, and can boil — stand back and stir; never lean over the tub.
  3. Let the slaked putty rest under a layer of water; mature it sealed for weeks to months (older putty works better).
  4. For limewash: dilute mature putty with water to a thin-milk consistency (roughly 1 part putty to several parts water), tint with alkali-stable pigment if desired.
  5. Wet the wall, then brush on many thin coats, letting each carbonate before the next. Thin coats bond; thick coats crack.
  6. Keep the fresh work damp and out of direct sun/frost so it carbonates slowly and hard.

Variations & modifiers

Thicker putty plus sand becomes lime plaster; on wet plaster, painting with lime-fast pigment is buon fresco. Add casein or linseed for durability tweaks. Pigment load stays low — high-pH lime kills fugitive and many organic colors, so stick to ochres and oxides.

Preservation & storage

Mature putty keeps indefinitely under water in a sealed tub. Lime finishes tolerate hairline movement and can be renewed with a fresh coat yearly. Store quicklime absolutely dry.

Yield

A few pounds of putty makes liters of limewash — enough for a small wall or several test panels.

Beyond the recipe

Lime putty is also a mortar, a fresco ground, a tannery and hide-processing agent, and a soil amendment. Leftover carbonated wash is inert chalk.
