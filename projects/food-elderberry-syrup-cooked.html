Cooked Elderberry Syrup (Freezer & Fridge Keeping)

Cooked elderberry syrup for the freezer

  • Inky purple-black syrup

Safety — read before you start
  • MUST cook: raw elderberries, stems, leaves, and unripe fruit contain cyanogenic glycosides; raw juice has caused hospitalizations. Simmer 15-20+ minutes.
  • Red elderberry is more toxic - cook AND remove the seeds via a food mill; never eat red elderberry seeds.
  • Use only fully ripe, dark berries; discard all green berries and any green plant material.
  • Do not give elderberry syrup made with honey to children under 1 year old - honey can cause infant botulism. Use sugar for any batch that might reach an infant.
  • This is a refrigerator/freezer product, not a tested shelf-stable canning recipe - do not store at room temperature.
  • Educational/culinary use only; elderberry syrup is not a medical treatment. Keep clearly labeled and out of reach of children.

About

Elderberry is the one wild fruit where the recipe is a safety instruction: raw berries, stems, and leaves carry cyanogenic glycosides, and people have been hospitalized from raw elderberry juice. Cooking is the fix - research shows processing drops those compounds sharply (up to 96% in cooked spreads and liqueurs). This syrup is built around a hard simmer and total stem removal. Blue elderberry (Sambucus cerulea) and American elderberry (S. canadensis) are the workhorses; red elderberry is more toxic and must be both cooked AND deseeded.

Ingredients

  • Ripe dark elderberries (blue Sambucus cerulea or American S. canadensis), fully stemmed — 3-4 cups · Green berries, stems, and leaves are toxic - use only dark-ripe fruit, no green.
  • Water — 3 cups
  • Honey or sugar — ~1 cup, added after cooking · Sweetener helps preserve and rounds the tannic edge; add off heat if using honey. Do NOT give honey-sweetened syrup to children under 1 year old (infant botulism risk) - use sugar for any batch that might reach an infant.
  • Fresh ginger, cinnamon, cloves — to taste (optional) · Traditional aromatics.
  • Bottled lemon juice — 1-2 Tbsp (optional) · Brightens flavor; helps color.

Tools

Required: Pot, Fine sieve or jelly bag, Fork or berry comb for destemming, Clean jars or freezer containers, Gloves (elderberry stains)

Optional: Food mill (essential for red elderberry deseeding), Masher, Funnel

Steps

  1. Harvest only fully ripe, dark berries where permitted. Comb every berry off the green stems and umbels - the green plant parts are the most toxic. Discard any green or underripe fruit.
  2. For RED elderberry only: you must also remove the seeds. Cook then pass through a food mill; never use red elderberry seeds. Blue/American elderberry seeds are fine once cooked.
  3. Put stemmed berries and water in a pot. Bring to a boil, then simmer hard at least 15-20 minutes - this is the step that reduces the cyanogenic compounds. Mash as it cooks.
  4. Strain through a fine sieve or jelly bag, pressing to extract juice. Discard solids.
  5. Return juice to the pot; simmer to reduce slightly. Remove from heat and stir in honey or sugar (adding honey off-heat preserves its character) and any lemon juice.
  6. Cool, bottle in clean jars, and refrigerate - or freeze in portions/ice-cube trays for long keeping.
  7. Label clearly 'cooked elderberry - refrigerate/freeze.' Never bottle raw elderberry juice, and never leave green material in the batch.

Variations & modifiers

Blue/American elderberry gives a mellow, deep syrup; red elderberry (deseeded) is sharper and less common. Add ginger/cinnamon for a spiced version, or reduce further with more sugar toward a pourable jam. For long shelf storage, freeze - do not attempt open-kettle canning of this syrup, as it isn't a tested water-bath recipe.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate and use within ~2-3 weeks; freezing in small portions extends it for months with little quality loss. Dried and frozen elderberries also store well raw - but must still be cooked before eating. Discard if it ferments or smells off. This is NOT a tested shelf-stable canning recipe.

Yield

About 2-3 cups syrup from 3-4 cups berries. Elderberries are small and seedy; a large harvest reduces to a modest yield.

Beyond the recipe

Cooked elderberry is also a superb natural ink and textile dye (it appears on the ink map), so a craft batch and a food batch come from the same simmer - just keep food and craft vessels separate. The spent pulp composts or, if blue and cooked, sweetens into a fruit-leather base.
