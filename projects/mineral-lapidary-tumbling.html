Agate & jasper tumbling

Patience in a barrel — weeks of grit to glass

Safety — read before you start
  • SILICA DUST: agate, jasper, quartz, and petrified wood are all SiO2. Keep every stage wet; mask (N95 minimum, P100 preferred) when handling dry grit or dried slurry; dust causes silicosis.
  • Never pour slurry down drains — settle and bin the solids.
  • Keep the tumbler away from food-prep areas; dedicate strainers and buckets to lapidary.
  • Petrified wood specifically is limited to small free-use quantities on BLM land and prohibited in national parks (e.g., Petrified Forest NP).
  • Occurrence is not permission — collect rough only where allowed, leave protected sites and quantities alone.

About

A rock tumbler is patience in a barrel: rough agate, jasper, and petrified wood ride for weeks through coarse-to-fine grit and water until abrasion rounds and polishes them to glass. The catch is that these are all silica (SiO2) — dumping a barrel or handling dry grit slurry can free respirable silica dust, so the whole process stays wet and the sludge never goes down the drain.

Ingredients

  • Rough agate, jasper, quartz, or petrified wood — Fill barrel ~2/3 · Best at Mohs 6-8; keep pieces similar in hardness and size.
  • Silicon-carbide grit set (coarse 60/90, medium 120/220, fine 500/600) — Per barrel instructions · Each stage in its own sequence; never mix stages.
  • Polishing compound (cerium/tin/aluminum oxide) — Final stage · Optional pre-polish (1200) before the polish step.
  • Ceramic media / plastic pellets — As filler (optional) · Cushions stones and carries slurry in later stages.

Tools

Required: Rotary (or vibratory) tumbler, Silicon-carbide grits + polish, Strainer (dedicated, not kitchen), N95 minimum (P100 preferred) respirator for handling dry grit/dried slurry, Bucket

Optional: Plastic colander, Old toothbrush for cleaning stones, Settling bucket for slurry disposal

Steps

  1. Sort rough by hardness and size; load the barrel about two-thirds full with stones plus water and coarse (60/90) grit.
  2. Run the coarse stage ~1 week, then STOP: rinse stones and barrel thoroughly so no coarse grit contaminates the next stage.
  3. Repeat with medium (120/220) grit, then fine (500/600), rinsing completely between every stage.
  4. Run the polish stage with polishing compound and (optionally) cushioning media until the stones gleam.
  5. Handle grit and slurry wet; wear an N95 (P100 preferred) when opening or cleaning anything that could have dried to powder.
  6. Let slurry settle in a bucket and dispose of the solids in the trash — never pour silica sludge down the drain (it clogs and is a dust source when dry).

Variations & modifiers

Vibratory tumblers speed the medium-to-polish stages but the coarse shaping stage is still best done in a rotary. Softer stones (obsidian) need gentler, shorter runs and lots of cushioning media. Agate and jasper are the most reliable beginners' stones; petrified wood polishes beautifully but can undercut along growth rings.

Preservation & storage

Polished stones need no upkeep. Store grits sealed and labeled by stage; cross-contamination ruins a batch.

Yield

A barrel of polished stones — dozens of small pieces — per multi-week run. Expect some to chip or fail to polish.

Beyond the recipe

Polished stones become jewelry tumbles, cabochon blanks, mosaic and terrarium stone, or worry stones. Failed pieces reload into the next coarse batch.
