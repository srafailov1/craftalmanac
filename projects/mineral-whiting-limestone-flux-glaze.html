Limestone / Whiting Flux Glaze

The calcium flux behind nearly every stoneware glaze

Safety — read before you start
  • Grind limestone/marble WET and mask up — quartz/silica impurities and fine carbonate dust are respiratory hazards.
  • All glaze mixing raises silica dust from the silica, clay, and feldspar — P100 respirator, wet-clean, never dry-sweep.
  • Calcined lime (quicklime) is caustic; limestone glaze materials are not quicklime, but avoid heating raw limestone dust and skin contact with any lime.
  • Colored variants (copper, cobalt, manganese) can leach, and underfired/immature high-calcia surfaces should also be kept off food contact — use tested liners and leach-test before food use.
  • Zircon opacifiers are safe fired but the raw dust is a respiratory hazard; handle wet where possible.
  • Mapped limestone/marble occurrences are not permission to collect; rock/mineral collecting is prohibited in NPS units and regulated on most public lands — check land-manager rules first.

About

Whiting — the humble white powder in nearly every stoneware glaze — is just finely ground limestone or marble: calcium carbonate, the same rock that builds cliffs and cathedrals. Above cone 8 that calcium is one of the most reliable, durable fluxes in ceramics; below it, the same material can go stony and matte instead. Learning to place calcium in a glaze is learning to place the mapped limestone of your own region into the melt.

Ingredients

  • Whiting (calcium carbonate, ground limestone/marble) — 8-22% · Higher amounts push toward calcia matte above cone 8; commercial whiting is purest, but clean marble/limestone can be milled fine.
  • Feldspar (potash/soda) — 35-45% · Alkali flux + alumina/silica backbone.
  • Silica (quartz/flint) — 20-30% · Glass former; balances thermal expansion for fit.
  • Kaolin / ball clay (EPK) — 15-22% · Alumina for durability and suspension.
  • Tin oxide or zircon — 0-10% (optional) · Opacifier for a cream/white matte.

Tools

Required: P100 respirator, Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve, Buckets, Gloves

Optional: Ball mill, Hydrometer, Test tiles

Steps

  1. Weigh a balanced base — a classic Leach 4:3:2:1-style clear is ~feldspar 40 / silica 30 / whiting 20 / kaolin 10, adjustable. This specific base is a cone 9-10 recipe; cone 6 users should substitute part of the whiting with a calcium borate/boron frit (e.g., Ferro 3134) rather than firing it low.
  2. If milling your own limestone or marble, cut and grind WET to avoid dust, then dry and screen to a fine flour. Respirator on for any dry handling.
  3. Mix dry ingredients into water to a light cream, sieve twice through 80-mesh.
  4. Hydrometer to a repeatable specific gravity and glaze test tiles.
  5. Fire to the base's cone (9-10 for the Leach base above). Note that below ~cone 8 raw whiting may not fully melt and produces an immature, potentially soft/leachable satin or pinhole surface — this is not a finished food surface; fix by fritting some calcium.
  6. Increase whiting toward 20-22% (with high firing) to intentionally make a durable calcium/lime matte; decrease toward 8-12% for a clean gloss.
  7. Check fit for crazing; add silica to lower expansion if it crazes.
  8. Leach-test any colored version — and any matte or underfired surface — before food use.

Variations & modifiers

This is the calcium-flux workhorse: 8-12% whiting reads glossy, 18-22% at cone 9-10 reads a true crystalline lime matte. Do not confuse that intentional matte with an underfired whiting surface below ~cone 8, which is immature and not food-safe. Swap part of the whiting for dolomite to add magnesium for a silkier matte. Add 1-3% iron for celadon, 0.5-2% copper (oxidation) for green — copper cautions apply to food surfaces.

Preservation & storage

Store dry ingredients sealed; keep milled limestone dry to prevent caking. Mixed glaze keeps for months to years if lidded but can grow mold or thicken; stir, re-sieve, and check specific gravity before reuse. Fired result is permanent.

Yield

A 1000 g batch glazes ~15-20 pieces. A reliable clear base you'll reuse as the parent for many colored variants.

Beyond the recipe

Ground limestone/marble whiting is also the calcium source for glass, lime plaster, and a soil sweetener. Leftover clear base is the ideal liner glaze over decorative outer glazes.
