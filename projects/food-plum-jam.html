Wild & beach plum jam

Tested water-bath jam from tart plums

  • Food map
  • Preserve
  • Intermediate
  • Deep magenta-red
Safety
  • Plum, cherry, and apricot pits/kernels contain cyanogenic compounds - discard whole pits and never crush, grind, or blend them into the jam.
  • This is a tested water-bath recipe because plums are reliably below the pH 4.6 high-acid threshold; do not lower the sugar or omit the lemon juice, which keep it safely acidic.
  • Occurrence is not permission - forage only where legal and take a light, sustainable share.
  • Use the correct headspace and processing time for your altitude; discard any jar that fails to seal or shows signs of spoilage.

Foraged from

  • Plums →Food map — Prunus americana and Prunus mexicana

About

Beach and wild plums (Prunus maritima and its cousins) are among the most rewarding foraged fruits for the canner: small, mouth-puckeringly tart, and reliably acidic enough for safe boiling-water processing. The NCHFP tested plum jam needs no commercial pectin - plum skins and slightly underripe fruit carry their own. Follow a tested recipe exactly and the pits stay whole and discarded, never crushed.

Ingredients

  • Chopped tart wild or beach plums (pitted, skins on) — 2 quarts chopped (about 4 lb whole fruit) · Use ~3/4 ripe, 1/4 slightly underripe for natural pectin; discard whole pits - never crush them.
  • Sugar — 6 cups · Sugar level is part of the tested set and shelf stability - don't cut it freely.
  • Water — 1 1/2 cups
  • Bottled lemon juice — 1/4 cup · Adds guaranteed acidity plus pectin activation; use bottled for a known, consistent pH.

Tools

Required: Boiling-water canner or deep stockpot with rack, Half-pint canning jars, new lids, bands, Large heavy non-reactive pot, Jar lifter and canning funnel, Ladle and long spoon, Candy/jelly thermometer

Optional: Cherry/plum pitter, Potato masher, Kitchen scale, Timer

Steps

  1. Forage plums only where harvesting is permitted - occurrence is not permission. Take a light share and leave fruit for wildlife.
  2. Wash, halve, and pit the plums, keeping skins on. Discard the whole pits; do not crush or blend them (the kernels are cyanogenic). Chop to measure 2 quarts.
  3. Pre-sterilize half-pint jars (required for the 5-minute, sea-level to 1,000 ft process tier per NCHFP); keep lids and jars hot.
  4. Combine plums, sugar, water, and 1/4 cup bottled lemon juice in the pot. Bring slowly to a boil, stirring until the sugar dissolves.
  5. Boil rapidly, stirring constantly to prevent sticking, until it reaches the jellying point - 220 F at sea level, or 8 F above your local boiling point.
  6. Ladle hot jam into hot sterile jars leaving 1/4-inch headspace. Wipe rims with a clean damp cloth, set lids, and finger-tighten bands.
  7. Process in a boiling-water canner: 5 minutes at 0-1,000 ft; 10 minutes at 1,001-6,000 ft; 15 minutes above 6,000 ft.
  8. Remove jars, cool undisturbed 12-24 hr, and check that every lid sealed (no flex). Refrigerate any that didn't seal and use those first.

Variations & modifiers

Beach plums run smaller and tarter than inland wild plums but behave the same. A pouch or box of commercial pectin lets you shorten the boil and use the pectin-added ratios if your fruit is very ripe. A split vanilla bean or a cinnamon stick during the cook reads well against the tartness; a mix of wild plum species deepens the color.

Preservation & storage

Water-bath canning is the whole point: sealed jars keep 12+ months in a cool dark cupboard. Plums are reliably below the 4.6 high-acid threshold, and the tested recipe's lemon juice keeps it there. Do not reduce the sugar or skip the lemon juice below the tested recipe - both underpin the safe acidity and shelf stability. Once opened, refrigerate and use within about 3 weeks. Discard any jar that unseals, leaks, spurts, or smells off.

Yield

About 8 half-pint jars from 4 lb of fruit.

Beyond the recipe

Pit-in wild plum 'jam' variants and thick plum butter use the same fruit; the strained skins and cooked pulp can go into fruit leather. Slightly underripe plums are the best natural-pectin source, so save a handful of green ones to help less-pectic fruit set.
