Wild Grape Verjus

Tart green-grape juice, a local lemon

  • Food map
  • Fresh
  • Intermediate
  • Pale green tart juice
Safety
  • Distinguish wild grape from toxic Canada moonseed (Menispermum canadense): moonseed has one crescent seed, no tendrils, and untoothed leaves, and is poisonous (potentially fatal); true grape has 2-4 round seeds and branched tendrils. When unsure, do not use.
  • Green grapes are safe cooked or raw as juice; this is a condiment, not a beverage to chug.
  • Standard clean-handling and cold storage.
  • Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

About

Before lemons were cheap, cooks reached for verjus - the sour juice of unripe grapes - to brighten a sauce without the sledgehammer of vinegar. Wild grapes hang in reach across much of the country, and you catch them just as a few berries begin to blush, when the acid is high and the sugar barely started. One caution travels with wild grape: learn to tell it from the toxic lookalike Canada moonseed, whose single crescent seed and lack of tendrils give it away.

Ingredients

  • Unripe wild grapes (Vitis species), stemmed — ~5 lb per quart · Pick when hard and green, as the first few berries just start to color. Confirm true grape, not moonseed: true grape has 2-4 round seeds and branched tendrils; moonseed has a single crescent seed and no tendrils.
  • Citric acid — 1/2 tsp per pint (optional) · Extends refrigerated life to up to ~6 months.

Tools

Required: Food mill or masher, Jelly bag or fine strainer, Bottle

Optional: Cheesecloth

Steps

  1. Confirm identification: wild grapes have branched (forked) tendrils, 2-4 round seeds per berry, and shreddy bark. Canada moonseed has a single flat crescent seed, no tendrils, and untoothed leaves - discard anything uncertain.
  2. Crush or run the green grapes through a food mill to release the juice.
  3. Strain first through a coarse sieve, then through a jelly bag for a clean juice.
  4. Let it settle overnight in the fridge, then pour off the clear juice from any sediment.
  5. Bottle. Add 1/2 tsp citric acid per pint to keep it for months. Use anywhere you would use lemon juice.

Variations & modifiers

Use it to deglaze the morel cream saute, to dress salads, or to make a nonalcoholic spritz. Reduce with honey for a shrub-like syrup.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate 2-3 weeks plain, or up to ~6 months with citric acid alone (per Backyard Forager); longer keeping needs additional preservatives. Freeze in ice-cube trays for single-splash portions.

Yield

About 1 quart from ~5 lb of grapes (plan ~5 lb per quart; for 2-3 quarts use 10-15 lb). Wild grape is abundant and vigorous.

Beyond the recipe

Pressed skins and seeds compost. Verjus is a house-acid that ties this whole collection together - it replaces lemon in the sumac-ade, shrub, or morel pan.
