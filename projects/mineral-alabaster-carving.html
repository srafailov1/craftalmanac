Alabaster carving (translucent)

Soft gypsum that glows when held to a window

Safety — read before you start
  • GYPSUM DUST: irritating to lungs and eyes. Work damp, wear an N95, and keep ventilation moving.
  • Use a wooden mallet; steel-chisel blows crack alabaster invisibly and ruin the polish.
  • Occurrence is not permission — do not collect gypsum from mapped or protected sites.
  • Wet-clean the workspace; bag dust rather than sweeping.

About

Alabaster is fine-grained gypsum soft enough to shape with a horseshoe rasp, yet polished thin it glows when you hold it to a window — the reason medieval carvers made lamp panels and small saints from it. It has no asbestos risk, but gypsum dust is still an irritant, and worked dry it makes a fine cloud you should not breathe.

Ingredients

  • Alabaster block — 1-4 lb · Sold as translucent white, pink, or veined stock; Mohs ~2, noticeably softer than marble (harder 'Egyptian alabaster' is calcite, a different stone).
  • Microcrystalline or carnauba wax — As needed · Brings up the translucent shine; deepens veining.
  • Water — Small amount for wet-sanding (optional) · Use sparingly — too much water pits gypsum. Many carvers sand alabaster damp, not soaking.

Tools

Required: Wood/horseshoe rasp, Riffler files, Wet/dry sandpaper 120-600, Dust mask (N95) or respirator, Safety goggles

Optional: Wooden mallet and flat chisel, 0000 steel wool, Small hand saw

Steps

  1. Sketch your form. Remove bulk with a rasp — alabaster cuts fast, so go slow near finish lines.
  2. Refine with rifflers. Use a wooden mallet, not steel, if chiseling; a hard blow drives cracks deep below the surface.
  3. Sand damp from 120 up through 600 grit until the surface is glassy; keep dust down with a mask and light misting.
  4. Wipe clean and let the stone dry fully — trapped moisture clouds the polish.
  5. Buff on wax with a soft cloth and polish to a glow. Hold the finished piece to light to check translucency.
  6. Clean the bench wet; do not dry-sweep gypsum dust.

Variations & modifiers

Thin walls (under ~1 cm) maximize translucency for lamp shades or votive shields. Pink and honey-veined alabasters carve identically to white. For crisper edges, step up to marble (calcite, Mohs 3) — harder, opaque, and far more weather-tolerant.

Preservation & storage

Keep dry and indoors. Re-wax to restore luster. Never use alabaster where it meets water, acid, or weather — gypsum dissolves.

Yield

One small figure, dish, or lamp panel per block.

Beyond the recipe

Gypsum offcuts can be calcined (gently heated) into plaster of Paris, then mixed with water for casting molds — the same CaSO4 chemistry potters use for slip molds (a plaster mold, not a glaze ingredient).
