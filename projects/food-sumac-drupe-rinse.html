Cold-Steeped Sumac 'Lemonade'

Whole-use tart pink drink

Safety — read before you start
  • Safety first - identification: never confuse edible red-coned true Rhus sumac with poison sumac (Toxicodendron vernix), which has drooping white berries and causes severe rashes. When unsure, do not harvest.
  • Always filter through fine cloth or a coffee filter to remove the tiny irritating hairs on the drupes.
  • Avoid plants along busy roads or mowed/sprayed rights-of-way — the tart surface coating you steep can't be washed off before use, so pick from clean field edges.
  • Steep cold, not hot - hot water extracts bitter compounds (tannins/alkaloids) from the seeds and stems. Sumac is high in tannins, which are astringent and can upset some stomachs; drink in moderation.
  • Sumac is in the cashew/mango/pistachio family (Anacardiaceae); if you have a known allergy to those, skip it or taste a tiny amount first, as rare cross-reactions are documented.
  • Occurrence is not permission: a mapped sumac stand isn't harvest consent; sumac spreads readily, so light picking leaves plenty for wildlife.

About

The tartness on staghorn sumac's fuzzy red cones isn't inside the seed - it's a dust of malic acid on the outside of the drupes, which is why you steep them in cold water, never hot. Hot water drags bitter compounds out of the seeds; cold water lifts only the bright, lemony acid. It's the whole-use lesson in one glass: use the surface, leave the seed, waste nothing.

Ingredients

  • Ripe staghorn/smooth sumac drupe clusters (true Rhus) — 3-4 clusters per quart of water · Must be upright, dense, deep-red, velvety cones. NOT poison sumac (Toxicodendron vernix), which has drooping white/green berries in wet ground.
  • Cold non-chlorinated water — 1 quart · Cold only - hot water extracts bitter seed compounds.
  • Honey or sugar — to taste (optional) · Sumac is intensely tart on its own.

Tools

Required: Large jar or bowl, Fine strainer, Coffee filter or muslin

Optional: Wooden spoon to bruise clusters, Ice

Steps

  1. Identify carefully: harvest only upright, dense, red, velvety-hairy cones of true Rhus sumac. Poison sumac has smooth stems and drooping white berries and grows in wet swampy ground - if in any doubt, don't pick.
  2. Choose clusters that haven't been rained on recently (rain washes off the surface acid). Do NOT wash the clusters - the flavor lives on the outside.
  3. Break the clusters into a jar of cold water. Gently bruise or rub them to release the acid; more rubbing gives more tartness (and more astringency).
  4. Cover and steep in the refrigerator 2-6 hours - cold only. Taste as it goes; pull it when it's pleasantly tart.
  5. Strain first through a coarse sieve, then through a coffee filter or muslin to catch the fine irritating hairs and seed debris.
  6. Sweeten to taste and serve cold over ice. Drink within a couple of days.

Variations & modifiers

Longer steeping and more bruising intensify tartness and pull more astringency; back off for a softer drink. A sprig of mint, lavender, or a little rose-hip syrup rounds it out. Concentrate a strong batch as a 'sumac shrub' base by adding a splash of the apple-scrap vinegar from the other card.

Preservation & storage

A fresh beverage - refrigerate and drink within 1-2 days; it does not keep. To store the flavor, dry whole clusters and grind the drupe surface into ground sumac spice (sieve out seeds and hairs), which keeps for months in a jar.

Yield

About 1 quart of tart pink 'lemonade' per batch. Expect a bright, citrus-like drink - cloudier and more rustic than lemonade, with a rosy blush.

Beyond the recipe

Strained drupes and seeds compost. Dried clusters become ground sumac spice (a za'atar staple); the same true-Rhus tannin also makes a natural dye and, historically, a tanning agent - a bridge to the ink/dye cards.
