Apple & Crabapple Pectin Stock

Homemade pectin from cores and peels

  • Food map

Safety — read before you start
  • Safety first - canning: only water-bath can this in a tested, correctly-acidified jelly recipe. Improperly processed low-acid or under-acidified home canning risks botulism; when unsure, freeze instead.
  • The rubbing-alcohol pectin test is toxic - mix 1 tsp stock into 1 Tbsp alcohol, never taste it, keep it away from the food, and discard the sample.
  • Apple, crabapple, and hawthorn seeds are cyanogenic - strain and discard incidental seeds; never crush or grind seeds into the stock.
  • Occurrence is not permission: mapped apple, crabapple, and hawthorn are often on land with their own harvest rules.

About

Commercial pectin comes in a foil packet; the same setting power is already hiding in the cores, peels, and underripe drops you'd throw away. Tart apples and crabapples are so loaded with pectin that two pounds of sliced crabapples in three cups of water can make enough liquid to gel jellies for weeks. Save the scraps from a season of pie-making and you never buy pectin again.

Ingredients

  • Tart apple / crabapple cores, peels, and slices — about 2 lb of scraps, or 2 lb sliced crabapples · Underripe and tart fruit has far more pectin than sweet, ripe apples. Hawthorn haws can add pectin too - strain and discard their (cyanogenic) seeds.
  • Water — about 3 cups (just to cover) · Barely cover the fruit - too much water dilutes the set.
  • Lemon juice — 1-2 Tbsp (optional) (optional) · A little acid helps extract pectin.

Tools

Required: Large pot, Colander or jelly bag, Cheesecloth, Bowl, Clean jars or freezer containers

Optional: Candy/jelly thermometer, Rubbing alcohol for the pectin-strength test, Water-bath canner

Steps

  1. Put tart apple/crabapple scraps (or sliced crabapples) in a pot with just enough water to cover, ~3 cups. Do not peel or seed further - the peel and core are where the pectin lives.
  2. Bring to a boil, then simmer 30-40 minutes until the fruit is very soft and the liquid has thickened slightly.
  3. Ladle into a cheesecloth-lined colander or jelly bag set over a bowl and let it drip. For clear stock, don't squeeze - squeezing gives more yield but cloudier stock.
  4. Test the strength: mix 1 tsp cooled stock into 1 Tbsp rubbing alcohol (do NOT taste this test; discard the sample). A firm, single clot means strong pectin; scattered small clots mean simmer the stock down further to concentrate it.
  5. Use immediately in low-pectin fruit jellies (start around 4-6 Tbsp stock per cup of prepared juice), or preserve for later.
  6. To can: reheat stock to a boil, jar with 1/4-inch headspace, and process in a boiling-water bath ~10 minutes (adjust for altitude) per a tested recipe. To freeze: cool and pack in labeled containers.

Variations & modifiers

Crabapple gives the strongest set and a rosy tint; regular apple scraps stretch a crabapple batch. Underripe fruit beats ripe every time. Reduce the strained stock by simmering to concentrate weak pectin. Note the alcohol test is a quick strength gauge - discard the test sample.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate finished stock ~1 week, freeze several months (best for keeping the set), or water-bath can with 1/4-inch headspace using a tested ~10-minute process time. Label with the batch strength so you know how much to use later. Discard if it smells fermented or shows mold.

Yield

Roughly 2-3 cups of pectin stock per 2 lb of scraps - enough to help set several small batches of low-pectin fruit jelly (elderberry, strawberry, wild grape).

Beyond the recipe

The cooked-out pulp can be pushed through a food mill for a rustic apple butter base (re-sweeten and cook down). Spent solids compost well. Pair this with any low-pectin mapped fruit - elderberry, wild strawberry, wineberry - to gel jellies without store pectin.
