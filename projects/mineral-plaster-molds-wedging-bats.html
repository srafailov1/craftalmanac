Gypsum Plaster: Molds & Wedging Bats

The 100:70 recipe behind every studio mold and bat

Safety — read before you start
  • Never mix or immerse a hand in a large plaster batch that could set around it — plaster sets exothermically and hard, a documented burn/entrapment hazard; keep body parts out of setting plaster.
  • Dry gypsum/plaster dust irritates lungs and eyes (bagged plaster can contain trace crystalline silica) — wear a NIOSH N95 respirator and safety glasses, don't dry-sweep, and never pour plaster slurry or washout down a drain (it sets and clogs pipes).
  • Keep plaster out of clay bodies and off wedging clay surfaces used for other work — embedded plaster crumbs cause lime-pop blowouts in fired ware.
  • Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped alabaster/gypsum only with consent; and this is a studio tool, not a food-contact surface.

About

Every plaster mold, drying bat, and wedging table in a pottery studio is calcined gypsum — the same soft stone as alabaster — ground, cooked to drive off water, and re-hydrated to set rock-hard in minutes. Pottery plaster is mixed by an exact recipe: 100 parts plaster to 70 parts water by weight. Get the ratio right and you get a strong, thirsty mold; get it wrong and it sets soft, weak, or too dense to pull the water out of clay.

Ingredients

  • Pottery/molding plaster (calcined gypsum; buy USG-type pottery plaster rather than self-calcining alabaster) — 100 parts by weight · Alabaster is the natural gypsum stone; self-calcining is finicky, so studio pottery plaster is the reliable route.
  • Water (potable, 70-100°F) — 70 parts by weight (standard 70-73 parts water per 100 plaster, per USG consistency spec) · Use ~70°F water as the default. Cold water slows the set and increases setting expansion — which can make demolding harder.

Tools

Required: Accurate scale, Flexible mixing bucket, Cottle boards / mold form or bat ring, Mold soap or release for multi-part molds, NIOSH N95 respirator and safety glasses for handling dry plaster (bagged plaster can contain trace crystalline silica)

Optional: Drill mixer (low speed), Surform/rasp for trimming, Sponge for finishing the working face

Steps

  1. Weigh water and plaster to the 70:100 ratio (water:plaster by weight). This ratio sets strength and absorbency, so measure — don't eyeball.
  2. Sift the plaster into the water by hand (plaster into water, never water onto plaster), letting each handful sink until a peak of dry plaster rises just above the surface — that indicates roughly the right ratio.
  3. Let it slake undisturbed for a couple of minutes, then mix slowly and thoroughly, breaking up lumps without whipping in air.
  4. Pour into your soaped mold form or bat ring while the slurry is still fluid (before it thickens); tap/vibrate to release bubbles.
  5. Wait for the set exotherm — the plaster warms as it hardens; demold after it cools back down and is firm (about an hour).
  6. Dry the finished mold/bat fully for several days to a week (a warm, ventilated spot, not so hot it calcines the surface) before putting it to work; wet plaster won't absorb from clay.

Variations & modifiers

More plaster (less water) = faster set, harder, less absorbent mold; more water = softer, more absorbent, weaker. Use ~70°F water (up to 100°F) as the default; cold water slows the set and increases setting expansion, which can make demolding harder. Add a splash of vinegar to retard set, or a pinch of salt/potassium sulfate to accelerate — test first.

Preservation & storage

Cured, fully dried plaster molds and bats last for hundreds of uses if kept from chipping and from soaking too wet. Never let plaster crumbs get into a clay body — even a tiny lodged bit causes a lime-pop blowout in fired ware. Store dry; re-dry molds between heavy casting sessions.

Yield

The 70:100 ratio makes a predictable volume: ~2.8 lb water + 4 lb plaster fills roughly a small drying bat. Mixed plaster has a short pot life (minutes) — mix only what you can pour before it stiffens.

Beyond the recipe

The same gypsum plaster makes press molds, hump/slump molds, and sprig molds, plus super-absorbent reclaim slabs for reconditioning slop clay. Natural alabaster's other craft life is carving (the alabaster carving card in the mineral set) — a soft, translucent stone.
