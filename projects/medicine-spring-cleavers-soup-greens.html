Spring Cleavers Soup Greens

A mild, cucumber scented spring green that turns soft and grassy once it simmers in the pot.

  • Medicine map
  • Greens
  • Intermediate
  • Toxic, read safety
Safety, read before you start
  • Identify your plant before eating. True cleavers has thin square stems, whorls of six to eight narrow leaves, and backward hooked hairs that make it cling to skin and clothing. Do not confuse the tangle it climbs through: poison ivy has woody stems, leaves in groups of three, and does not cling, and nightshade has broad leaves with small round berries. Pick only stems you can positively confirm as cleavers.
  • Cook it fully. Simmer cleavers in the pot rather than giving it a quick wilt, so the hooked bristles soften all the way. Undercooked cleavers can scratch the mouth and throat.
  • Harvest strand by strand, never a whole tangled mat. Cleavers is a clinging scrambler that hooks onto and grows through its neighbors, so a grabbed handful can sweep in whatever it was climbing on, including poison ivy or nightshade. Inspect every stem, confirm it is cleavers, and discard anything it was clinging to.
  • External contact caution: wear gloves and long sleeves while gathering, because the fresh plant causes a contact rash on some skin. If handling it makes your skin react, do not eat it.
  • Use it as one ordinary green in a mixed pot, in modest amounts and only now and then, not as a strong standalone brew and not as a daily habit.
  • If you take a diuretic (water pill) or lithium, or have a kidney or fluid balance condition, keep cleavers minor and occasional and check with a clinician first. Cleavers can increase urination, and a documented case of severe lithium toxicity has been linked to herbal diuretics.
  • During pregnancy or while breastfeeding, do not make cleavers a large or frequent part of the diet. It is a wild green with no established safety record at that amount.
  • Keep gathered cleavers and the finished pot away from children and pets until you have confirmed the greens and removed anything the plant was clinging to.
  • Map occurrence is not harvest permission. Confirm that gathering is allowed on the specific piece of land before you pick.

About

Long before it picked up garden nicknames like sticky willy and goosegrass, cleavers was a humble spring pot green across Britain and northern Europe, simmered into a plain country soup that cooks called goosegrass broth. Its hooked, clinging hairs make the raw plant a nuisance to eat, so cooks always cooked it right down until soft. This card follows that tradition: young cleavers as one seasonal wild leaf, gathered before it sets seed and dropped into a spring soup pot. This card is about the food and the flavor, nothing else.

Ingredients

  • Young cleavers aerial parts, 2 to 3 packed cups · The youngest tender tips only, gathered in early spring before any fruits appear.
  • Olive oil or butter, 1 to 2 tbsp · For sweating the aromatics.
  • Onion or leek, chopped, 1 medium · A mild allium base for the broth.
  • Garlic, chopped, 1 to 2 cloves (optional) · Optional, for depth.
  • Potato, diced, 1 medium (optional) · Optional, gives body and a creamy texture when blended.
  • Vegetable or chicken broth, 4 cups (about 1 liter) · Enough to cover the greens with room to simmer.
  • Salt and pepper, to taste · Season at the end.

Tools

Required: Gloves, Gathering basket or bag, Colander, Cutting board, Knife, Soup pot

Optional: Long sleeves, Blender or immersion blender, Kitchen scissors

Steps

  1. Gather 2 to 3 packed cups of the youngest cleavers tips in early spring, picking strand by strand and inspecting each stem as you go. Take only the soft, tender growth from before the plant flowers.
  2. Back in the kitchen, rinse the cleavers well in a colander under cold running water. Pick out and discard any stray leaves, stems, or debris the plant was clinging to, keeping only stems you have confirmed as cleavers. Chop into rough 1 inch pieces.
  3. Warm the oil or butter in the pot over medium heat. Add the chopped onion or leek (and garlic, if using) and sweat until soft, about 5 minutes. Stir in the diced potato if you want a creamy soup.
  4. Pour in the broth, bring to a gentle simmer, and cook until the potato is tender, about 10 minutes.
  5. Add the chopped cleavers and simmer gently for 15 to 20 minutes, until the hooked bristles have fully softened and the greens are limp and tender. Do not shortcut this simmer: the long cook is what makes the greens pleasant to eat.
  6. Season with salt and pepper. For a smooth soup, blend until creamy. For a rustic pot, leave it chunky.
  7. Serve hot as a spring green soup, treating the cleavers as one leafy vegetable among the others in the bowl.

Variations & modifiers

Blend the finished soup with a cooked potato for a smooth, cucumber like texture, or leave it chunky and rustic. Cleavers pairs well with other mild spring greens in the same pot, such as chickweed or young dandelion leaf, and with cooked nettle. A squeeze of lemon or a spoon of cream at the end rounds out the grassy flavor.

Preservation & storage

Cleavers is a spring green, best used fresh the day you gather it while the tips are tender. Raw cleavers wilts and toughens quickly, so do not hold it for long. The finished soup keeps in a covered container in the refrigerator for up to 3 days, or freezes for up to 3 months.

Yield

One pot, about 4 bowls.
