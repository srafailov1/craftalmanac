Safety — read before you start
  • This is an ink, not food. Don't ingest it, label the jar clearly, and keep it away from children and pets.
  • Boston ivy / Parthenocissus berries are toxic — they contain calcium oxalate crystals and can cause mouth/throat pain, drooling, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, and the plant is a skin irritant. Use them for color only, wear gloves, and use a dedicated non-food pot — or simply omit them and use more grapes/blackberries.
  • Wild grape look-alike hazard: moonseed (Menispermum canadense) mimics grape clusters but is poisonous. Distinguish by moonseed's single crescent-shaped seed versus grapes' round seeds, and by grape vines' forked tendrils. When unsure, don't harvest.
  • Anthocyanin juices stain skin, nails, fabric, counters, and grout — protect surfaces, wear gloves, and wipe spills promptly.
  • Simmering makes hot, splattering, staining liquid and vinegar fumes; ventilate, keep your face back from the steam, and consider eye protection.
  • If anyone ingests Boston ivy or moonseed berries or this ink, contact Poison Control (US: 1-800-222-1222) or seek care. Occurrence on a map is not permission to harvest — favor fallen, abundant, or invasive material.

About

Crushed wild grapes give a moody blue-purple from anthocyanins — the same pigment that makes the juice a living pH meter: a splash of vinegar swings it red-pink, a pinch of baking soda turns it blue-green. The lookalike berries of Boston ivy are tempting but toxic, so know which vine you're picking.

Ingredients

  • Ripe wild grapes (Vitis) — 1.5 to 2 cups (about 250-350 g) · store blackberries, blueberries, or concord grapes give a very similar ink
  • White vinegar — about 1/4 to 1/2 cup · use vinegar as the liquid, not water — it extracts color, acts as the acid modifier, and preserves
  • Boston ivy / Parthenocissus berries (for deeper blue-purple only — toxic, never edible) — up to 1/2 cup, only if positively identified (optional) · optional and toxic; omit and use more grapes/blackberries to deepen color without the risk
  • Salt — 1/4 to 1/2 tsp (optional) · preservative / mild mold deterrent
  • Whole cloves — 2 to 4 (optional) · natural preservative; a drop of clove oil also works, or refrigerate
  • Gum arabic — 1.5 to 2 tsp per cup of finished ink (optional) · binder for dip-pen flow; a little honey adds body, or omit for a watery wash
  • Baking soda or iron acetate — a pinch / a few drops, to taste (optional) · baking soda pushes toward blue; iron water gives grey/slate tones

Tools

Required: A small pot reserved for craft use (strongly recommended, especially if you add Boston ivy), Potato masher, fork, or spoon, Fine strainer plus coffee filter or cheesecloth, A bowl to strain into, A glass jar with lid; a craft-only stir spoon

Optional: Gloves (recommended; prevents staining and skin contact with Boston ivy), Eye protection if working near steam, Small jars or a palette for testing pH modifiers, Dip pen or brush for testing, Hand lens for verifying grape identity (round seeds, not crescent)

Steps

  1. Identify first. Confirm your wild grapes are true Vitis: forked tendrils on the vine and berries with several small round seeds. The dangerous look-alike, moonseed (Menispermum canadense), has no tendrils and a single crescent-shaped seed — if in doubt, don't use it. If using Boston ivy berries, be certain of the ID and remember they're toxic and for color only.
  2. Set up safely: work with ventilation, keep children and pets away, put on gloves, and use a pot set aside for craft (not food). Rinse and de-stem the grapes; remove leaves and unripe or moldy fruit.
  3. Crush the fruit in the pot with a masher or fork to release the juice. (If adding Boston ivy berries, add them now, for pigment only.)
  4. Add the vinegar (about 1/4 to 1/2 cup) — not water. Bring to a gentle simmer and cook 10-15 minutes, mashing and stirring so it doesn't scorch.
  5. Cool completely — anthocyanin color is heat-sensitive, and straining is cleaner cool.
  6. Strain through a fine strainer, then a coffee filter or cheesecloth into a clean bowl. Press gently and re-strain until smooth and particle-free.
  7. Stir in the salt and, if using, the cloves; the vinegar plus salt is your basic preservative. For more body and better pen flow, stir in gum arabic (or a little honey) until dissolved.
  8. Tune the color (optional) in separate test cups: a drop of vinegar/lemon for warmer magenta, a pinch of baking soda for bluer (expect some dulling), or a few drops of iron water for grey-slate tones. Adjust the main batch only after you like a test.
  9. Funnel into a clean glass jar, label it clearly as ink — not food (toxic if it contains Boston ivy), and store cool and dark (refrigerate to extend life). Test on your actual paper; the dried color is paler than the wet juice.

Variations & modifiers

Wild grape and Boston ivy color is anthocyanin, a natural pH indicator, so the ink is reactive and unstable. Acid (vinegar or lemon) shifts it toward magenta/pink-purple; a base (a pinch of baking soda) shifts it toward blue/blue-green but tends to dull and muddy and shorten shelf life; iron (iron water) reacts with the fruit's tannins to give greyed violets, slate, and grey-black. Because the pigment responds to pH, the same ink can look different on acidic versus alkaline paper and can drift as it ages.

Preservation & storage

White vinegar is the primary preservative (it's why you cook with vinegar, never water); reinforce with 1/4-1/2 tsp salt and 2-4 whole cloves (or a drop of clove oil). Keep the ink in a sealed glass jar, cool and dark; refrigeration meaningfully extends life. Even so, treat it as fresh and short-lived — it can ferment or mold over weeks to months, and the written color is fugitive regardless of preservation.

Yield

Roughly 1/2 to 1 cup of ink from 1.5-2 cups of fruit, depending on reduction. Refrigerated with vinegar/salt/cloves it keeps weeks to a few months; discard if it smells off or molds. The written color is fugitive — visibly fading within weeks to months in light — so it's best for journaling and sketching, not archival work.

Beyond the ink

Not effective as a durable textile dye — anthocyanin berry juice stains fiber readily but fades badly even stored in darkness. If you experiment anyway, scour the fiber, pre-mordant with alum, simmer gently, and post-treat with iron or tannin for a slightly more stable grey-purple, but expect a fugitive result. It does make a great kitchen-chemistry pH indicator and a food coloring (wild grapes only, never Boston ivy).
