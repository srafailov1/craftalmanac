Wild-Clay Slip Glaze (Albany style)

A high-iron wild clay that becomes a glaze on its own

  • Advanced

Safety — read before you start
  • Dry clay processing releases fine free silica — respirator (P100), wet-clean surfaces, never sweep dry; silicosis is permanent and cumulative. Slake whole dry lumps rather than dry-crushing to minimize dust.
  • Iron-rich wild clays can carry unknown minerals — leach-test before any food-contact use.
  • Some regional clays contain gypsum/lime lumps that pop or lime-blow in the fire; screen thoroughly.
  • Ventilate the kiln when firing organic- and sulfur-rich wild clays (burnout fumes/sulfur).
  • Occurrence is not permission: verify legal collecting rights and leave the bank stable and un-eroded.
  • Wear gloves; prolonged wet-clay contact dries and cracks skin.

About

For most of the 1800s and 1900s, American stoneware crocks wore a single dark glaze dug from one clay bank near Albany, New York — a naturally iron- and lime-rich clay that melts almost entirely on its own into a glossy tobacco brown. When that pit closed, potters spent decades reverse-engineering it. You can chase the same thing under your own boots: a high-iron, high-flux wild clay that becomes a glaze at 60-100% of the recipe.

Ingredients

  • Wild iron/lime-rich clay (Albany-style) — 60-100% · Dark red-brown clays from lake/glacial deposits melt best. High-clay slip glazes over ~40-50% raw clay shrink, crack, and crawl in the fire — calcine (bisque-roast to ~1000F/540C) half the clay powder and blend roasted:raw to stop crawling.
  • Feldspar or nepheline syenite — 0-25% (optional) · Add flux if the raw clay won't fully melt at your cone.
  • Whiting (calcium carbonate, from limestone/marble) — 0-15% (optional) · Boosts calcium flux for gloss and durability; mapped as ground limestone/marble.
  • Ferro Frit 3195 or 3134 — 0-20% (optional) · The reliable modern route to bring a stubborn wild slip down to cone 6.
  • Red iron oxide — 0-3% (optional) · Deepens color toward near-black tenmoku territory.

Tools

Required: P100 respirator, Gloves, Goggles, 80-100 mesh sieve, Gram scale, Mortar or crusher

Optional: Ball mill, Test tiles, Line-blend cups

Steps

  1. Confirm you have permission to collect the clay — occurrence on a map is never harvest permission, and many banks are on protected or private land.
  2. Fully dry the clay, then slake whole dry lumps in water — they break down on their own; avoid dry crushing/grinding, which creates fine free-silica dust. If you must crush stubborn material, wear a P100 and wet-clean afterward.
  3. Wet-sieve the slaked slurry through 80-100 mesh to remove grit and organics.
  4. Calcine (bisque-roast to ~1000F/540C) half the clay powder and blend roasted:raw before glazing — high-clay slips over ~40-50% raw clay shrink, crack, and crawl in the fire; this is the standard Alberta/Albany-style fix.
  5. Run a melt test at your target cone: brush the blended slip on a test tile and fire. If it stays dry/matte, it needs added flux.
  6. Line-blend flux additions (feldspar, whiting, or frit) in 5-10% steps until the tile gives a smooth glassy surface.
  7. Check fit on a fired tile — flex and thermal-shock test; look for crazing or shivering.
  8. Once dialed in, glaze ware, keeping feet clean, and fire to the tested cone; ventilate the kiln during bisque/glaze firing of organic- and sulfur-rich wild clays.
  9. Send a dialed-in food-contact recipe for leach testing before using on dinnerware.

Variations & modifiers

Higher clay % = darker, drier, more matte; more frit/feldspar = glossier and lower-firing. Add 2-4% titanium or rutile for streaky 'kaki' rust breaks. Layer a thin ash glaze over the top for hare's-fur. Substitute Alberta Slip (the commercial Albany stand-in, 80% slip + 20% Frit 3195 at cone 6) when you can't dig your own.

Preservation & storage

Store dry, screened clay in sealed labeled containers to control silica dust. Mixed glaze keeps indefinitely in a lidded bucket; re-sieve after settling. Fired glaze is permanent.

Yield

A 1000 g batch glazes roughly 15-20 pieces. Expect to spend more time testing than glazing; the reward is a glaze that costs almost nothing and is unmistakably local.

Beyond the recipe

The same wild clay, less refined, can temper a stoneware body or make a slip for decoration. Coarse grit screened out is good pathway fill or grog if re-fired and crushed.
