Lake pigment (dye → solid pigment)

Make solid pigment from a plant dye

Safety
  • These are art materials, not food — label everything 'NOT FOOD' and keep from children and pets.
  • Wear a fitted dust mask when grinding or handling dry pigment powder; do not inhale it.
  • Alum and soda ash are only mildly irritating but should not be ingested and should be kept off eyes and broken skin; rinse splashes with water.
  • A toxic dye plant makes a pigment that is still from a toxic plant — keep toxic-plant pigments out of reach and never ingest.
  • Reserve a non-food pot and tools for craft use once used for pigment.

About

This is the bridge recipe. It turns a soluble plant dye into an insoluble solid pigment you can grind, store for years, and bind into watercolor, gouache, pigment sticks, or oil paint — the same "laking" that gave medieval painters their yellows and reds. You precipitate the dye onto a mineral carrier (alum plus soda ash or chalk), filter, and dry. Best with the map's lightfast dyers — weld, goldenrod, coreopsis, marigold, madder-types, and dyer's polypore; skip fugitive berry and other anthocyanin colors, which lake to a dull, short-lived gray.

Ingredients

  • Dye plant material (a strong dyebath) — a lightfast dyer — enough for ~1 quart / liter of strong dye · weld, goldenrod, coreopsis, marigold, madder root, or dyer's polypore work well; avoid berry/anthocyanin colors
  • Alum (potassium aluminum sulfate) — ~20 g per liter of dye (about 4 tsp) · the mordant/carrier the dye precipitates onto; dissolve in a little hot water first
  • Soda ash (washing soda) OR calcium carbonate (chalk / whiting) — ~10 g soda ash, or ~8 g chalk, per liter · raises pH to drop the pigment out. Chalk is gentler and dries faster/more opaque; soda ash gives a more translucent pigment
  • Clean water — for the dyebath and for rinsing · distilled avoids minerals that dull the color

Tools

Required: Nonreactive pot (stainless or enamel) reserved for craft use, Heat source, Strainer + coffee filters or fine cloth, Jars for settling, Spoon for stirring

Optional: pH strips (to watch the precipitation), Mortar and pestle or muller (to grind the dried pigment), Dust mask (for grinding dry pigment), Gloves

Steps

  1. Extract the dye: simmer your plant material in water 30-60 min (hot, not hard-boiling), then strain out the solids. You want a strong, deeply colored bath.
  2. Add alum: while the dye is warm, stir in the dissolved alum until fully mixed (about 20 g per liter).
  3. Precipitate: dissolve the soda ash (or chalk) in a little warm water and add it to the dyebath slowly — it will fizz and the liquid will cloud as the pigment drops out. Add until the reaction stops and the liquid above the sludge runs pale.
  4. Settle: let it stand several hours or overnight; the pigment settles as a colored sludge under a clearer liquid.
  5. Filter and rinse: pour off the clear liquid, then pour the sludge through a coffee filter or fine cloth. Rinse it two or three times with fresh water to wash out excess salts (filtering is slow — give it time).
  6. Dry: spread the filter of wet pigment out flat, out of direct sun, for several days until bone dry.
  7. Grind and store: scrape up the dry pigment and grind it smooth in a mortar (wear a dust mask). Store in a sealed, dark jar — dry pigment keeps indefinitely.
  8. Use it: bind the pigment into watercolor pans, gouache, pigment sticks, egg tempera, glair, or oil paint (see those recipes).

Variations & modifiers

Chalk (calcium carbonate) yields a more opaque, faster-drying, pastel-leaning pigment; soda ash yields a more translucent, saturated one — pick by the paint you want. The dye's own iron/pH modifiers should be set in the dyebath before you lake it. Anthocyanin dyes shift gray through the alkaline step — don't lake them.

Preservation & storage

The finished dry pigment is a stable powder that keeps indefinitely in a sealed jar away from light and damp. Only the wet dyebath spoils — lake it fresh.

Yield

A strong liter of dye yields roughly a small jar (a few teaspoons to a couple of tablespoons) of dry pigment, depending on the dye's strength.

Beyond the ink

This is the gateway to solid color: one jar of lake pigment can become watercolor pans, gouache, soft pastels / pigment sticks, egg tempera, glair, or (with drying oil) oil paint. It is the plant-color counterpart to the earth pigments in Heidi Gustafson's Book of Earth.
