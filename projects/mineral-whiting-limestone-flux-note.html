Limestone & Marble as Whiting Flux

Grind the same rock as cliffs into a workhorse flux

  • Minerals map
  • Clay
  • Advanced
  • Warm, tightened low-fire body
  • Toxic — read safety
Safety — read before you start
  • Grinding limestone/marble makes respirable dust that frequently contains free silica — grind wet, wear P100, never dry-sweep.
  • Calcining carbonate to quicklime (CaO) produces a strongly caustic material that burns skin, eyes, and lungs and reacts violently with water — do not attempt without proper training and PPE.
  • Coarse lime grains cause lime pops in fired ware — always grind fine.
  • Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped limestone/marble only with consent; glaze food-safety depends on a properly fluxed, tested glaze (never lead).

About

Limestone and marble are the same molecule — calcium carbonate — the metamorphic one just recrystallized. Ground fine it becomes 'whiting,' the most common calcium flux in ceramics: about 5% in a porous earthenware body helps counter the moisture expansion that makes glazes craze, and it is the backbone calcium of countless glazes. Fire it and the carbonate burns off as CO2 (the lime cycle in miniature), leaving reactive calcium oxide behind.

Ingredients

  • Whiting (calcium carbonate) ground from mapped limestone or marble — ~5% as a body addition (much more as a glaze flux) · Grind fine (~200 mesh); marble and limestone are both CaCO3. At low-fire temperatures whiting in the body works as a filler that suppresses moisture expansion and reduces fired shrinkage; its true flux action (hard, durable glass) applies to glazes at mid/high fire.
  • Base clay body — Balance · Any porous earthenware body prone to moisture-expansion crazing benefits.
  • Water — As needed · Process wet to control dust.

Tools

Required: Crusher + ball mill/muller, 200-mesh screen, Scale, P100 respirator, Kiln + glazed test tiles

Optional: Acid-fizz test kit (vinegar) to confirm carbonate, Test kiln for glaze-fit ladders

Steps

  1. Confirm the stone is carbonate: a drop of vinegar on limestone or marble fizzes (CO2). That fizz is the working chemistry.
  2. Crush and grind to a fine powder (~200 mesh), processing wet or masked — calcium carbonate dust is a nuisance/respiratory irritant and often carries free silica.
  3. For a body addition, blend ~5% whiting into a porous earthenware body to reduce delayed moisture-expansion crazing.
  4. For glaze use, weigh whiting as the calcium flux in a tested glaze recipe (calcium is a high-temperature flux giving hardness and durability).
  5. Fire and note that carbonate decomposition starts around 1300-1500°F and is essentially complete by ~1650°F, releasing CO2 and leaving calcium oxide — vent the kiln and don't seal thick calcium layers that could bloat.
  6. Test glaze fit on tiles; adjust whiting to tune the craze/shiver balance.

Variations & modifiers

As a glaze flux, more calcium = harder, more matte, more durable glass (to a point); too much crawls or matte-crazes. As a body addition keep it low (~5%). Dolomitic limestone adds magnesium (different, more matte effect). Calcined lime is caustic — see safety.

Preservation & storage

Dry whiting keeps indefinitely, sealed. In fired ware calcium is a permanent, durable flux. Beware 'lime pops': coarse limestone grains left in a body absorb moisture after firing, slake, and pop chips off the ware — grind fine to avoid this.

Yield

A little goes far: hundreds of grams of whiting flux a large batch of glaze or body. Self-grinding limestone is laborious; this card is about a mapped source for a workhorse additive.

Beyond the recipe

The same limestone/marble, burned hotter to quicklime and slaked, is the lime of lime plaster, limewash, and mortar — the full lime cycle. Marble also carves (the mineral-set carving card), and marble/limestone dust is a filler in gesso and clay-plaster.
