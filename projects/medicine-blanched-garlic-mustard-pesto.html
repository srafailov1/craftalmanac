Blanched Garlic Mustard Pesto

A vivid green, garlicky spoonable sauce made from a plant you are helping pull.

  • Medicine map
  • Greens
  • Beginner
  • Toxic, read safety
Safety, read before you start
  • Identify the plant before you pull or eat it. Crush a leaf: it should smell clearly of garlic. Discard any similar rosette that does not smell of garlic.
  • Rule out understory lookalikes: wild violet, white avens (Geum), toothwort (Cardamine), fringecup, piggyback plant, and ground ivy (creeping charlie). None of these smell of garlic, so a leaf that lacks the garlic scent is not garlic mustard.
  • Blanching is a required cook step, not an optional flavor step. Drop the leaves into boiling water for 2 to 3 minutes until vividly green. Garlic mustard carries cyanogenic compounds (higher than in cultivated brassicas, and highest in first-year plants), and the boil greatly reduces them along with the bitterness. Do not skip or shorten it.
  • Do not make a raw version. Many pesto recipes online blend the leaves raw. This project is deliberately different: it uses no raw leaf preparation.
  • Discard the blanching water. Never reuse it as broth or stock, because the cyanogenic compounds and soluble oxalates leach into it. The ice water plunge is a separate cooling step and does not replace pouring the boiling water out.
  • Use this as an occasional condiment, not a staple green. Garlic mustard contains oxalates, so do not eat large quantities on consecutive days.
  • Allergen note: this recipe contains hard grating cheese (dairy) and, if you add them, tree nuts or pine nuts.
  • Keep the finished sauce and the raw plant material away from children and pets.
  • Occurrence on the map is not permission to harvest or to run a removal. Pull or remove only on land you own, with explicit land manager permission, or through an organized invasive pull or weed warrior program. National Park Service units (36 CFR 2.1) and most state parks and preserves prohibit removing plants, even invasives, without authorization.
  • Control seed spread: carry pulled plants in sealed bags, take flowering tops before seed set, never compost them, and do not move seeding plants offsite.

About

Cooks across the Mid-Atlantic have turned an aggressive woodland invader into a kitchen ingredient. Garlic mustard was carried to North America in the 1800s as a culinary herb and escaped into forest understories, where volunteers now pull it by the bagful each spring. State agencies, including Maryland's Department of Natural Resources, publish a blanched pesto so the plants you help remove end up in the kitchen instead of the compost pile.

Ingredients

  • Young garlic mustard leaves and tender flowering tops (first- and second-year plants, spring), 5 cups packed · Gather while pulling plants for removal. Use leaves and tender tops only, not the root.
  • Olive oil, 1 to 1.25 cups · Start with 1 cup and add more for a looser sauce.
  • Hard grating cheese, Parmesan style, grated, 1/2 cup · Dairy allergen. Leave out for a dairy-free sauce and add a little extra salt.
  • Garlic cloves, 3 cloves · Peeled.
  • Lemon juice, 1 tablespoon · Added at the end, to taste.
  • Salt, To taste
  • Tender herbs (parsley, mint, or basil), 1 cup packed (optional) · Optional, to round out the flavor.
  • Toasted nuts (pine nuts, walnuts, or hazelnuts), 1/2 cup (optional) · Optional. Tree nuts or pine nuts are a common allergen. Sunflower seeds work as a nut-free swap.
  • Black pepper, To taste (optional)

Tools

Required: Large pot, Bowl of ice water, Slotted spoon or colander, Food processor or blender, Sealed bags for the pulled plants

Optional: Small skillet for toasting nuts, Clean storage jar

Steps

  1. Confirm the plant while pulling it for removal: crush a leaf and check that it smells clearly of garlic. Discard any similar rosette that lacks the garlic scent. Pick young leaves and tender flowering tops, and leave the root in the ground.
  2. Rinse the leaves and tender stems thoroughly in cool water, then measure about 5 cups packed.
  3. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Drop the leaves in for 2 to 3 minutes, until they turn vividly green. This boil is the required cook step, not an optional one.
  4. Lift the leaves out with a slotted spoon straight into the bowl of ice water to stop the cooking. Pour the boiling water down the drain and do not save it. Once cool, gather the leaves in your hands and squeeze them as dry as you can.
  5. In a food processor, combine the garlic, optional herbs, optional toasted nuts, grated cheese, salt, and pepper. Pulse to a coarse paste.
  6. Add the squeezed blanched leaves and 1 cup of olive oil. Blend until smooth, scraping down the sides and adding more oil for the consistency you want. Finish with the lemon juice and adjust salt to taste.
  7. Spoon into a clean jar, cover the surface with a thin film of olive oil, and refrigerate. Serve on pasta, crusty bread, or cooked vegetables.
  8. Bag the remaining pulled plants in sealed bags, take any flowering tops before they set seed, do not compost them, and do not carry seeding plants offsite.

Variations & modifiers

Swap the optional herbs (parsley, mint, or basil) or the optional nuts (pine nuts, walnuts, hazelnuts, or sunflower seeds) to change the flavor. Leave out the cheese and nuts for a lighter, allergen-friendly version. For single servings, freeze the finished sauce in an ice cube tray, then pop the cubes into a bag. The root is a restricted part on this map, so keep it out and use leaves and tender tops only.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate in a sealed jar with a thin film of olive oil over the surface and use within about 5 days, or freeze in small portions for up to 3 months. The base is cooked by blanching, not raw.

Yield

About 1.5 to 2 cups of sauce.
