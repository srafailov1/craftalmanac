Autumn olive berry ink

Pink from an invasive berry

  • Ink & Dye map
  • Ink
  • Beginner
  • Pale pink
Safety
  • This is ink, not food. Even though ripe autumn olive berries are edible, the finished ink isn't — it may contain cloves, gum arabic, salt, or come from a craft pot. Label the jar and keep it from children and pets.
  • Unripe (green, firm) berries are intensely astringent and can upset stomachs — use only fully ripe red fruit and never snack from the craft batch.
  • The juice stains strongly: wear gloves and protect clothing, counters, and porous surfaces. Skin and nail stains fade over a few days.
  • Positively identify the plant (bright red, silver-speckled berries; silvery-scaled leaf undersides). It has no common deadly lookalikes, but never substitute an unidentified red berry.
  • If you simmer the juice, ventilate the kitchen and reserve the pot for craft use.
  • Harvest only where permitted — invasive status encourages removal but doesn't override land rules or landowner permission.

About

This invasive "super-berry" packs roughly 7-17 times the lycopene of a ripe tomato, so a handful of crushed fruit bleeds a startling pale pink-crimson juice. Picking it does double duty: every cluster you strip is seed that won't get scattered by birds across the next hillside.

Ingredients

  • Ripe autumn olive berries (bright red, soft, silver-speckled) — about 2 cups (1 lb) · use only fully ripe berries; freezing then thawing makes them crush more easily
  • Water — 1/4 to 1/2 cup, just to loosen the mash
  • White vinegar — 1 to 2 tsp (optional) · not needed, but keeps the pink a bit brighter and mildly preserves; lemon juice works too
  • Salt — 1/4 tsp (optional) · light preservative / discourages mold
  • Whole clove — 1 per small jar (optional) · anti-mold; a few drops of isopropyl alcohol or vodka also help
  • Gum arabic — about 10 drops liquid (or 1/8 tsp powder) per 2 oz ink (optional) · smoother flow and a little more cling; a pinch of sugar adds body but invites mold sooner

Tools

Required: A non-reactive (glass, stainless, or enamel) bowl or container for crushing, Potato masher, fork, or sturdy spoon, Fine strainer or sieve, Coffee filter, cheesecloth, or clean cotton cloth, A small glass jar or bottle with a tight lid, Gloves (the juice stains hands and nails)

Optional: A small dedicated (non-food) saucepan if you reduce the juice, Dip pen, brush, or fountain-pen converter for testing, Newspaper or a wipeable surface, Small funnel for bottling

Steps

  1. Collect only fully ripe, soft, bright-red autumn olive berries where harvesting or removal is permitted. The shrub is invasive across the eastern and central US, so removal is encouraged — but occurrence on a map is never permission, so confirm you may take material. Rinse and pick out leaves, stems, and green fruit.
  2. Put on gloves and protect your work surface; the juice stains skin, nails, fabric, and porous counters.
  3. Crush the berries thoroughly in a non-reactive container to break the skins, adding just 1/4 to 1/2 cup water to loosen the mash. (Freezing the berries first, then thawing, makes them mush easily.)
  4. Strain through a fine sieve, pressing to leave seeds and skins behind, then strain again through a coffee filter or cheesecloth into your jar. Let a little fine red pulp through — that's where the pink lives, so don't over-clarify.
  5. Optional, for a deeper (still soft) red: gently simmer the strained juice in a dedicated non-food saucepan 20-40 minutes until slightly reduced. Don't boil hard. Much of the red is lycopene locked in oily pulp and won't dissolve, so it stays pink-to-warm-red, not scarlet.
  6. Stir in vinegar and a pinch of salt, drop in a clove, and (optionally) about 10 drops of liquid gum arabic per 2 oz for smoother flow. The vinegar brightens and protects the pink but won't make it lightfast.
  7. Test a swatch on the paper you'll use and let it dry — it dries lighter than it looks wet. A dab of baking-soda water or vinegar shifts it only faintly (dulling or browning), not the dramatic flip anthocyanin inks show, because this red is a carotenoid.
  8. Cap tightly and refrigerate in a labeled jar ("Autumn olive ink — not food"). Shake before use, since the pulp settles. Use within a couple of weeks; discard at any sign of mold, fizzing, or off smell, and keep finished art out of light.

Variations & modifiers

Autumn olive's red comes mainly from lycopene, a fat-soluble carotenoid — not a pH indicator, so the dramatic acid/base flips of anthocyanin inks (red cabbage, pokeberry) do not reliably happen. A drop of vinegar keeps the pink slightly brighter; a pinch of baking soda tends to dull or brown it rather than turn it blue/green. Reducing with heat shifts it toward a warmer red-brown and concentrates it. Iron (iron water) saddens it toward dull brown-purple. None of these makes the ink lightfast.

Preservation & storage

Add 1-2 tsp white vinegar plus a pinch of salt and a clove (or a drop of clove oil) per jar, and refrigerate sealed. This is a water-and-pulp ink with no strong preservative, so treat it as perishable: use within about 1-2 weeks (a few weeks at most refrigerated), and discard at the first sign of mold, fizzing, or sour smell. A splash of isopropyl alcohol or vodka extends life modestly. Preserving the liquid doesn't stop the painted color from fading.

Yield

About 2 cups of berries yields roughly 1/2 to 3/4 cup of pink-red ink (less if reduced). Shelf life is short — about 1-2 weeks refrigerated, a few weeks with vinegar and a clove. The painted color is fugitive and will visibly fade or brown within weeks in light even if the jar is fine.

Beyond the ink

This one is effectively ink/wash only — the berries are a poor textile dye, because lycopene is fat-soluble and won't bond into washed cloth even with a mordant; any pink rinses and fades out. If you want fiber color from this plant, dye with the leaves instead (scour and alum-mordant, long simmer), which yields brown to gold. The berries' best second life is culinary: jam, fruit leather, and lycopene-rich sauce.
