Persimmon & pawpaw pulp freezing

Bank a short-season glut in the freezer

  • Food map
  • Waste
  • Beginner
  • Golden-orange puree
Safety
  • Use only fully soft-ripe fruit. Unripe/astringent persimmon is not just mouth-drying - its tannins can, especially eaten in quantity or on an empty stomach, form a diospyrobezoar (a firm stomach mass) in rare cases. Full ripeness eliminates this.
  • Pawpaw seeds and skin are not food - screen them out and discard; the seeds contain toxic acetogenins. Some people get stomach upset from pawpaw, and pawpaw flesh contains annonacin, so eat it in moderation (the caution matters most for frequent/large-quantity consumption) rather than in large daily quantities.
  • These low-acid pulps are not safe to home-can - freeze them; do not water-bath or 'cold pack' pulp for the pantry.
  • Occurrence is not permission - forage only where legal and take a light, sustainable share.

About

Persimmons and pawpaws share a problem: they ripen all at once, go from perfect to mush in days, and have too short a window to eat fresh. The standard save is to screen the ripe pulp and freeze it in portions. It's the single most useful waste-reduction move for these short-season natives - capture a glut at its peak and bake, blend, or churn it all winter.

Ingredients

  • Fully soft-ripe persimmons and/or pawpaws — as much as your glut · Must be dead-ripe: astringent persimmons soft and jelly-like, pawpaws fragrant and yielding.
  • Ascorbic acid (vitamin C powder) — 1/8 tsp (about 375 mg) per quart of puree (optional) · Prevents browning; more effective than lemon juice, and useful for near-neutral-pH pawpaw.

Tools

Required: Sieve, food mill, or colander (to screen out seeds/skin), Bowl and spoon, Freezer containers or bags with headspace, Freezer at 0 F

Optional: Blender (for smooth pulp), Ice-cube trays or portion containers, Labels for date and portion size

Steps

  1. Forage/harvest where permitted, and only fully soft-ripe fruit - under-ripe astringent persimmon is mouth-drying, and pawpaw is only worth eating dead-ripe.
  2. For persimmon: sort, wash, peel, and press the soft flesh through a sieve to a smooth puree, leaving skin and seeds behind.
  3. For pawpaw: cut, scoop the custardy flesh, and press through a screen to remove the large toxic seeds and the skin; keep only the pulp.
  4. Optional but recommended: stir 1/8 tsp ascorbic acid per quart into the puree to prevent browning (especially for near-neutral pawpaw).
  5. Portion into freezer containers or bags, leaving 1/2-inch headspace for expansion; freezing in small usable portions (cups or cubes) means you thaw only what you need.
  6. Label with fruit and date, and freeze at 0 F.
  7. Use within about 6 months for best quality: persimmon pulp for puddings, breads, and cookies; pawpaw pulp for ice cream, custards, and quick breads.

Variations & modifiers

Freeze in recipe-sized portions (1-cup blocks, or ice-cube-tray dollops for small amounts). Persimmon from native varieties needs no sugar; cultivated persimmon or pawpaw can be packed with 1 cup sugar per quart if you like. Puree is also the honest fallback for any low-acid fruit that has no tested canning recipe.

Preservation & storage

Freezing only - both fruits are low-acid and not safe to home-can as pulp. Keep at a steady 0 F; persimmon holds up to ~6-12 months, pawpaw about 6 months. Ascorbic acid guards color; press air out and leave 1/2-inch headspace. Don't refreeze fully thawed pulp for quality.

Yield

Scales with the glut - a good picking easily fills several 1-2 cup portions.

Beyond the recipe

This is the waste-reduction backbone: it turns a two-week glut of the most perishable native fruits into a year of ingredients. Screened-out persimmon peels can be dried; pawpaw skin and seeds are discarded (seeds are toxic and never eaten). Pairs with the hoshigaki card - firm persimmons get dried, soft ones get pulped.
