Elderflower Cordial

Muscat-scented flower cordial

  • Food map
  • Fresh
  • Beginner
  • Pale straw-gold cordial
Safety
  • Only the blossoms are edible raw; leaves, stems, bark, and berries (ripe or unripe, raw) contain cyanogenic glycosides - keep green parts out, and never eat raw berries; cook berry preparations.
  • Confirm the shrub is common or black elder (flat-topped cream umbels on a woody shrub). Avoid red elderberry (Sambucus racemosa, cone-shaped flower clusters) and never confuse with herbaceous white umbels like poison hemlock or water hemlock, which grow from the ground rather than a woody shrub.
  • Avoid roadside foraging near traffic exhaust.
  • Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

About

Pick elder flowers from late morning on a dry day, when the cream umbels smell of muscat and honey and not the faintly catty note they take on past their prime. Only the blossoms are used; the green stems carry the same cyanogenic compounds as the rest of the plant, so you snip each floret free. Citric acid does double duty here - it pulls flavor from the flowers and holds the cordial safe in the fridge for weeks.

Ingredients

  • Fresh elderflower heads (Sambucus canadensis/nigra), fully open — 25-30 heads · Shake out insects; do not wash off the fragrant pollen. Snip blossoms off green stems.
  • Sugar — 1.5 kg (about 7 cups)
  • Water — 1.5 L
  • Citric acid — 50-75 g · Extracts flavor and preserves. Substitute juice of 2-3 extra lemons for shorter shelf life.
  • Lemons, sliced — 2-3 (optional) · Adds brightness.

Tools

Required: Large pot or bucket, Fine strainer or muslin, Bottles

Optional: Funnel, Kitchen scale

Steps

  1. Forage cream-white, sweet-smelling blossoms on a dry day; discard any browning or off-smelling heads. Confirm the shrub is common or black elder (flat-topped cream umbels on a woody shrub), not a lookalike.
  2. Snip individual florets from the green stems into a bowl - green parts are toxic and bitter.
  3. Boil water and sugar into a syrup; remove from heat.
  4. Add citric acid, lemon slices, and the flowers to the warm syrup. Cover and steep 24-48 hours in a cool place.
  5. Strain through muslin, bottle, and refrigerate. Dilute about 1 part cordial to 4-8 parts water.

Variations & modifiers

Steep with a few sprigs of lemon balm or mint for a greener profile. The same base freezes into a floral sorbet, or lengthens a gin-and-tonic.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate 4-6 weeks with citric acid; freeze in bottles or ice-cube trays for a year. Cloudiness or fizzing means fermentation has started - discard.

Yield

About 1.5-2 L of cordial. One good shrub yields plenty in a single June morning.

Beyond the recipe

Spent flowers compost cleanly. Come autumn the same shrubs give the berries for the cooked elderberry cordial in this set - a two-season plant.
