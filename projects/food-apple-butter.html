Wild apple butter & dried apple rings

Slow-cooked butter from tart wild apples

  • Food map
  • Preserve
  • Intermediate
  • Deep burnished brown
Safety
  • Follow the tested NCHFP ratios - the cider, vinegar, and sugar keep apple butter safely acidic for water-bath canning; don't cut the acid or sugar freely.
  • Stir frequently during the long reduction; thick fruit butters scorch and can splatter (burn hazard) as they thicken.
  • Dry apple rings to leathery-dry and store airtight; discard any that mold. Occurrence is not permission - forage only where legal.
  • Use correct headspace and altitude-corrected processing; discard any jar that fails to seal.

About

Wild apples and crab apples are usually too tart or small to eat out of hand, but they cook down into a dark, spiced apple butter that's better than anything from the store. It's a long, slow reduction - cider and vinegar, then hours of gentle cooking until a spoonful mounds and no liquid weeps at the edge. The peels and cores that come off go straight into a scrap batch of cider or jelly.

Ingredients

  • Wild apples and/or crab apples — 8 lb · Tart, high-pectin fruit is ideal; wash and quarter, no need to peel for the butter.
  • Apple cider — 2 cups
  • Vinegar — 2 cups · Part of the tested recipe; also reinforces acidity.
  • White sugar — 2 1/4 cups
  • Packed brown sugar — 2 1/4 cups
  • Ground cinnamon — 2 Tbsp (optional)
  • Ground cloves — 1 Tbsp (optional)

Tools

Required: Large heavy non-reactive pot, Food mill, sieve, or colander, Boiling-water canner, Half-pint or pint jars, lids, bands, Ladle and funnel, Long spoon

Optional: Slow cooker (for the long reduction), Dehydrator or low oven (for rings), Immersion blender, Ascorbic acid or lemon water (to keep rings pale)

Steps

  1. Forage apples where permitted. Wash and quarter (cores in is fine for the butter); no peeling needed since you'll sieve.
  2. Cook the fruit slowly in the cider and vinegar until soft, then press through a food mill or sieve to make a smooth pulp; discard skins, cores, and seeds.
  3. Return the pulp to the pot with both sugars and the spices. Cook slowly, stirring frequently to prevent sticking, until thick.
  4. Test doneness: spoon a little onto a plate - when no rim of liquid separates around it (or it mounds on a spoon held from the steam for 2 minutes), it's ready.
  5. Fill hot into sterile half-pint or pint jars, 1/4-inch headspace. Wipe rims, set lids, finger-tighten bands.
  6. Process in a boiling-water canner: 5 minutes for half-pints/pints at 0-1,000 ft; 10 minutes to 6,000 ft; 15 minutes above 6,000 ft. Or skip canning and freeze the butter in containers.
  7. For dried apple rings: peel/core and slice sound apples ~1/4-inch, optionally dip in lemon or ascorbic-acid water to stay pale, then dry at ~135 F until leathery and pliable, and store airtight.

Variations & modifiers

Crab apples add tartness and extra pectin; a mix of wild varieties gives complex flavor. Swap in maple syrup for some sugar, or add allspice/nutmeg. Reduce further for a stiffer 'apple leather.' A slow cooker with the lid ajar makes the long reduction hands-off.

Preservation & storage

Two safe paths: water-bath-can the butter (1/4-inch headspace, altitude-corrected times) for best quality within about a year, or freeze it. Dried rings keep for months airtight and dark; condition them a week and check for moisture. Refrigerate opened jars and use within a few weeks.

Yield

About 9-10 pints of apple butter from 8 lb apples; dried rings scale with what you slice.

Beyond the recipe

This is a whole-fruit, low-waste project: butter uses the pulp, rings use sound slices, and the peels/cores can be simmered into scrap-apple jelly or fermented into vinegar. Pomace after pressing can go to compost or animals.
