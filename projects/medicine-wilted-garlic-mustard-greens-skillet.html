Wilted Garlic Mustard Greens Skillet

Spring greens with a mild garlic bite, gathered on an invasive removal outing and cooked down in a hot skillet like spinach.

  • Medicine map
  • Greens
  • Intermediate

Safety, read before you start
  • Always cook garlic mustard thoroughly before eating and never eat it raw. The young first-year leaves this dish targets carry the highest hydrogen cyanide load, so raw nibbling is exactly the wrong move.
  • Chop the greens, let them rest a few minutes, then cook until fully wilted and darkened with no raw crunch left. For larger batches, blanch in boiling water and pour off the water before cooking, since discarding the blanch water most reliably carries off the water-soluble compounds.
  • Identify your plant with 100 percent certainty before you pull or eat anything. Crushed leaves smell distinctly of garlic, and the leaves are smooth and hairless with scalloped kidney or heart shapes. If it does not smell of garlic, do not eat it.
  • Do not confuse native lookalikes while pulling. Cut-leaf toothwort (Cardamine concatenata) has deeply cut, divided leaves, not rounded scalloped ones. Sweet cicely (Osmorhiza claytonii) has soft fuzzy, fern-like compound leaves and no garlic smell. Native violets (Viola spp.) have simple heart-shaped leaves but no garlic smell. Ground ivy (Glechoma hederacea) has a square stem and a minty, not garlicky, smell. Confirm the garlic smell and leaf shape before pulling any plant.
  • Garlic mustard contains oxalates, so handle it like spinach: eat normal food-size portions rather than one enormous serving. Anyone advised to limit dietary oxalate, for example people who form kidney stones, should handle it as they would spinach.
  • Do not feed raw trimmings or freshly pulled plants to children or pets. Cook the greens before serving them to anyone.
  • Keep raw trimmings, cooked greens, and pulled plants where children and pets cannot reach them.
  • Occurrence on the map is not permission to remove or harvest. Gather only at a sanctioned removal event or a site where you have the land manager's written permission, and note that some jurisdictions require a permit even for hand-pulling.
  • Respect the harvest ethic: eat only the young leaves and tender shoots and never harvest the root. Bag and trash all pulled plants, including roots and any forming seedpods. Do not compost them and do not leave pulled material on site, because seedpods keep maturing and spreading seed.

About

European settlers carried garlic mustard to North America in the 1860s as a garden pot-herb, and cooked spring greens are one of its oldest kitchen uses. Today the same plant spreads aggressively through eastern woodlands, so pulling it during an organized removal outing and cooking the young leaves is a way to put the day's haul to use. This is a simple skillet green with a mild garlic flavor, cooked down the way you would cook spinach or kale. Think of it as a seasonal side, not a wild novelty.

Ingredients

  • Young garlic mustard leaves and tender shoots, 4 packed cups (about 6 oz), stems trimmed · First-year basal leaves and second-year shoots picked March to May before flowering. Wash in two changes of cold water.
  • Olive oil, 2 tablespoons
  • Fresh garlic, 2 cloves, thinly sliced (optional) · Optional; the greens already carry a mild garlic flavor.
  • Salt, 1 pinch (about 1/4 teaspoon)
  • Lemon juice or vinegar, 2 teaspoons, to finish (optional) · Cider or white wine vinegar both work; brightens the finished greens.

Tools

Required: Cutting board and knife, Large skillet or saute pan, Colander for washing

Optional: Salad spinner, Medium pot for blanching larger batches, Slotted spoon

Steps

  1. On a sanctioned removal outing, confirm each plant before pulling: crush a leaf and check for a clear garlic smell, and look for smooth hairless leaves with scalloped kidney or heart shapes. If it does not smell of garlic, leave it. Pull whole plants, but keep only the young leaves and tender shoots for the kitchen and bag every root and seedpod for the trash.
  2. Back home, strip the leaves and tender tops from the stems and wash them well in two changes of cold water, then drain in a colander.
  3. Chop the greens coarsely and let the chopped pile rest for about 5 minutes. Chopping and resting begins driving off the plant's volatile compounds before cooking.
  4. For a larger batch or milder flavor, blanch the greens in boiling water for 1 to 2 minutes, then lift them out and discard the cooking water. Discarding the water is what carries off the water-soluble compounds. Squeeze the greens dry before the next step.
  5. Warm 2 tablespoons olive oil in a large skillet over medium heat. If using, add the sliced garlic and cook 30 to 60 seconds until fragrant but not browned.
  6. Add the chopped greens and a pinch of salt. Cook, tossing often, for 3 to 5 minutes until fully wilted and darkened with no raw crunch left.
  7. Take the pan off the heat and finish with a squeeze of lemon or a splash of vinegar. Serve warm as a side, or fold into eggs, pasta, or a grain bowl.

Variations & modifiers

For a brighter plate, finish with lemon instead of vinegar and a little grated hard cheese. The blanch and discard step gives the mildest, least bitter result and is worth it for larger batches. Fold the cooked greens into scrambled eggs, toss with pasta and olive oil, or spoon over a grain bowl. A pinch of chili flakes with the garlic adds warmth without changing the method.

Preservation & storage

Cook the greens fully before storing. Keep cooked greens covered in the refrigerator and use within 3 to 4 days. To hold longer, blanch, discard the water, squeeze dry, and freeze in a sealed bag; use within 3 months. Store cooked greens away from where children and pets can reach them.

Yield

2 side servings (about 1 packed cup cooked)
