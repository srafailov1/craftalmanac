Witch Hazel Cut Branches for a Winter Vase

Cut just a stem or two from your own garden shrub or from nursery stems: spidery yellow blooms and a light citrusy scent for a cool, bright winter room.

Safety, read before you start
  • This is a decorative cut-branch craft only. Do not steep, distill, dab, or apply the stems, leaves, or bark to skin, and do not ingest any part.
  • Keep the vase water out of reach of children and pets, and do not let anyone drink it, since swallowed plant tannins in the water can cause stomach upset. The foliage itself is listed as non-toxic to cats and dogs by the ASPCA, so the concern is the standing water rather than the plant.
  • Cut only from your own cultivated garden shrub, a shrub cut with the owner's explicit permission, or nursery bought cut stems. Never cut from wild or mapped plants, because the catalog ethic for witch hazel is observe only.
  • The occurrence map shows where a plant grows, never permission to harvest it.
  • Use standard care with sharp pruning tools. Cut just above a bud and take only a stem or two so you guide rather than harm the shrub's shape.
  • Identify your plant before cutting. Witch hazel (Hamamelis) shows spidery, ribbon-petaled yellow flowers along bare or nearly bare stems in fall through late winter, with wavy edged oval leaves.
  • External, non-body use only: this arrangement is for looking at, not for touching to skin or taking internally.

About

Witch hazel (Hamamelis) is one of the very few North American shrubs that flowers in the dark half of the year, sending out spidery, ribbon-petaled yellow blooms along bare or nearly bare stems. The native common witch hazel (Hamamelis virginiana) flowers in fall to early winter, so its branches are simply cut while already in bloom for a vase. True forcing of tight dormant buds is a trick for the late winter hybrids (Hamamelis x intermedia), whose stems can be cut in midwinter and coaxed open indoors. Either way, cut branches have long been a favorite way to bring a little yellow and a light citrusy scent into a home during the gray months.

Ingredients

  • Cut witch hazel stems (garden or nursery), 1 to 2 stems · From your own cultivated garden shrub, a shrub cut with the owner's permission, or nursery bought cut stems only. Cut just above a bud.
  • Cool fresh water, enough to fill the vase halfway · Room temperature to warm water helps the stems drink; refresh every 2 to 3 days.
  • Clear lemon-lime soda (optional preservative), 1 tablespoon per quart of water (optional) · A common cut-branch trick to help the water stay fresh; plain water works too.

Tools

Required: Sharp pruning shears or secateurs, Vase

Optional: Hammer or small mallet (to split the woody stem base), Sharp knife

Steps

  1. Source your stems first. Cut only from your own cultivated garden witch hazel, a shrub cut with the owner's explicit permission, or buy nursery cut stems. Take only a stem or two and cut just above a bud to guide the shrub's shape.
  2. Match the plant to the season. For the native common witch hazel (Hamamelis virginiana), which flowers in fall to early winter, cut stems that already show open or opening blooms. For the late winter hybrids (Hamamelis x intermedia), you can cut stems with tight dormant buds in midwinter and force them.
  3. Back inside, recut the base at an angle and split the bottom inch of each stem with your pruners or a light tap of a hammer so the woody stem can take up water.
  4. Stand the stems in a vase filled halfway with cool to warm water, and set it in a bright, cool room away from direct heat and drafts.
  5. If you like, add 1 tablespoon of clear lemon-lime soda per quart of water to help the water stay fresh; plain water is fine too.
  6. Over several days the ribbon-like petals unfurl and lightly scent the room. If a warm then cold swing curls them shut, they reopen once it warms again.
  7. Every 2 to 3 days, pour out the old water, refresh it, and recut the base about an inch to keep the stems drinking.
  8. Keep the vase where children and pets cannot reach the water, and do not let anyone drink it. When the stems are spent, compost them.

Variations & modifiers

For a midwinter version, use the late winter hybrids (Hamamelis x intermedia) in yellow, orange, or red: cut stems with tight dormant buds and let them truly force open indoors over a week or two. To build out an arrangement, stand a witch hazel stem with bare red twig dogwood or a few evergreen sprigs in the same vase.

Preservation & storage

A cool room, out of direct heat and sun, plus fresh water every 2 to 3 days keeps the blooms open longest, often 1 to 2 weeks. This is a decorative arrangement only: when it is spent, compost the stems. Do not dry, save, or process any part for skin or body use.

Yield

One vase arrangement from a stem or two.
