Wineberry ink

Magenta from an invasive berry

  • Ink & Dye map
  • Ink
  • Beginner
  • Rose-pink
Safety
  • This is ink, not food — even though wineberries are edible, the finished ink contains vinegar, preservative, and possibly gum arabic. Don't taste or ingest it; label the jar not food.
  • Keep ink, fruit-in-progress, and preservatives away from children and pets.
  • Strong staining: wear gloves and protect skin, eyes, clothing, grout, and wood; wipe spills immediately.
  • Use a dedicated craft pot once preservative or gum arabic is added; don't return it to cooking.
  • Ventilate while simmering vinegar (sharp vapors) and avoid leaning over the pot.
  • Wineberry canes are bristly — wear long sleeves and watch your hands and eyes while harvesting. Don't confuse this beginner-safe berry with the toxic ink plants on this site (pokeweed, privet, Boston ivy, elderberry leaves/stems).

About

An aggressive Asian raspberry brought to the US around 1890 to breed better berries, wineberry now blankets eastern woods edges — recognizable by canes and fruit-cups furred in sticky red glandular hairs. Crush the ripe berries and you get a gorgeous magenta-rose juice that writes beautifully and, sadly, fades almost as fast as you can admire it.

Ingredients

  • Ripe wineberries (bright orange-red), favoring invasive stands — 2 cups (about 250-300 g) · ripe black or red raspberries, blackberries, or mulberries behave almost identically
  • White vinegar — a little under 1/2 cup (about 100 ml) (optional) · extracts color and mildly preserves/acidifies; a splash of water can stand in but the color keeps less well
  • Salt — 1 tsp (optional) · helps draw out pigment; optional
  • Whole clove — 1 per small jar (optional) · preservative against mold; a drop of clove oil also works
  • Gum arabic — about 1.5-2 Tbsp per 1 cup finished ink (optional) · binder for smoother dip-pen flow; skip for a thinner watercolor wash

Tools

Required: A pot reserved for craft use, Potato masher, fork, or sturdy spoon, Fine strainer plus cheesecloth or a coffee filter, A bowl to strain into, A small glass jar or bottle with a lid (sterilize with boiling water first)

Optional: Rubber/nitrile gloves (the juice stains), Funnel for bottling, Apron and surface covering, Dropper for adding preservative or pH modifiers

Steps

  1. Harvest about 2 cups of fully ripe, bright orange-red berries only where harvesting is permitted. Wineberry is invasive (its sale/possession is banned in CT and NY), so taking ripe fruit from established stands is low-impact and even helpful — but get landowner or agency permission first, and pick fruit rather than cutting living canes.
  2. Set up a stain-safe workspace: cover the surface, wear gloves, and use a pot you'll keep for craft only. The juice stains skin, clothing, counters, and grout.
  3. Put the berries in the craft pot, add the vinegar (and salt if using), and crush thoroughly with a masher or fork.
  4. Warm over medium-low and simmer gently 10-15 minutes, stirring and mashing so it doesn't scorch. Ventilate the room (simmering vinegar is sharp), and don't boil hard. Never taste the ink.
  5. Remove from heat and let it cool completely.
  6. Strain through a fine strainer, then again through cheesecloth or a coffee filter into a clean bowl, until no pulp or seeds remain (so it won't clog a pen).
  7. Optional: stir in gum arabic (about 1.5-2 Tbsp per cup) for dip-pen use, or leave it thin for washes. To play with pH, brush a little vinegar (brighter pink) or baking-soda water (toward purple/blue-grey) over a test swatch — keep these separate from the storage jar.
  8. Bottle in a sterilized jar, drop in a clove to discourage mold, label it clearly as ink / not food, and refrigerate. The color is fugitive: it fades and browns over months, faster in direct light, so keep finished art out of sunlight and scan work you want to keep.

Variations & modifiers

Wineberry color is anthocyanin, a natural pH indicator. Acid (the vinegar, or a drop of lemon) keeps the ink in its brightest pink-to-red range. A base such as baking soda raises the pH and shifts it toward purple then a dull blue-grey; these basic shifts on red-berry inks tend to look muted. Iron (iron water) dulls and saddens the color toward grey-brown rather than brightening it. Heat beyond a gentle simmer dulls and browns the pigment, so keep cooking brief and low.

Preservation & storage

The vinegar gives mild protection; for storage add a clove (or a drop of clove oil) per jar, bottle in a jar sterilized with boiling water, and refrigerate. This guards against mold but does not stop light-fading — the anthocyanin color is inherently fugitive.

Yield

Roughly 3/4 to 1 cup of ink from 2 cups of berries. Refrigerated with a clove it keeps a few weeks to a few months before molding; discard if it smells off or grows fuzz. The written color itself is fugitive and will fade and brown over months, faster in sunlight.

Beyond the ink

Not a durable textile dye: like other Rubus berry juices it doesn't bond to cellulose on its own, and even an alum mordant on wool/silk gives only a brighter pink-red that stays poorly lightfast and washes out. Honestly, ink/stain only — for lasting cloth color, use a different dye plant. The berries' best use is the kitchen: raw, jam, pie, syrup, or wine.
