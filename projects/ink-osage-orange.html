Osage orange fruit ink

Fugitive yellow from fallen fruit

  • Ink & Dye map
  • Ink
  • Beginner
  • Soft yellow
Safety
  • This is ink, not food. Do not taste or drink it; label the jar clearly and keep it away from children and pets.
  • Wear gloves — the fruit exudes a sticky, mildly irritating latex that can cause skin irritation and is hard to wash off; it also stains skin and surfaces yellow.
  • Use a dedicated craft-only pot, board, spoon, and jars; don't return them to kitchen food use.
  • If you make the iron acetate modifier, leave the jar loosely capped (the steel wool and vinegar give off hydrogen gas), keep it away from flames, label it, and store it away from food, children, and pets.
  • Simmer in a ventilated space and keep it to a gentle simmer to avoid splatter from the hot liquid.
  • Harvest only where collecting is permitted — occurrence on a map is not permission. Take only fallen fruit so no living tree is harmed.

About

In autumn, female Osage orange trees drop softball-sized, brain-textured "hedge apples" by the hundreds — a sticky-handed windfall you can simmer into a soft yellow ink that swings to a 1970s avocado-green with a pinch of baking soda. No tree gets touched; you're just beating the squirrels to fruit nobody wants.

Ingredients

  • Fallen Osage orange fruit (Maclura pomifera) — softball-sized, yellow-green, brain-textured "hedge apples" — 1-2 whole fruit, chopped, per 2 cups water · gather windfall from the ground; the fruit gives a soft, experimental yellow, not the wood's strong lightfast gold
  • Water — about 2 cups, enough to cover the chopped fruit
  • Baking soda — a small pinch, added to taste (optional) · alkaline shift — slides the soft yellow toward a 1970s avocado-green
  • Iron acetate (steel wool in vinegar) — a few drops per 1/4 cup ink, to taste (optional) · optional modifier — nudges the color toward olive/khaki; can be made 1-2 days ahead
  • Gum arabic — about 1/4 tsp per 1/4 cup finished ink (optional) · not needed, but gives the watery ink more body and flow on the page
  • Whole cloves — 1-2 per jar (optional) · preservative against mold; a drop of clove oil, a splash of isopropyl alcohol, or refrigeration also work

Tools

Required: Stainless or enamel pot reserved for craft use, Heat source, Knife and board for chopping the fruit (craft-only), Coffee filter, fine sieve, or muslin, Craft-only spoon, Heatproof jar or bottle, Gloves

Optional: Small second jar for the iron acetate modifier, Dropper, Test paper and a pen/brush for swatching, Label and pen

Steps

  1. Gather fallen hedge apples from the ground where harvesting is permitted — occurrence on a map is not permission to collect. The fruit drops in heavy abundance in autumn and is widely treated as litter, so this is pure windfall. Put on gloves: the fruit exudes a sticky, mildly irritating latex that is hard to wash off.
  2. Rinse the fruit, then chop or crush it into chunks on a craft-only board to expose more surface. Drop the pieces into your dedicated (non-food) pot and add about 2 cups of water, enough to cover.
  3. Bring to a gentle simmer (not a hard boil) and hold it for 30-45 minutes, stirring now and then and pressing the fruit against the side of the pot. Work in a ventilated space; expect a soft yellow liquid rather than a deep dye bath — this is an experimental, low-yield color.
  4. Turn off the heat and let it steep as it cools, ideally an hour or more, to pull a little more color.
  5. Strain through a sieve, then again through a coffee filter or muslin into a clean jar, pressing the solids to get all the liquid. Compost the spent fruit.
  6. Optional color test: spoon a little ink into a dish and stir in a pinch of baking soda to watch it slide toward avocado-green; try a separate dish with a few drops of iron water (steel wool soaked in vinegar) for an olive/khaki shift. Iron acts fast, so add a drop at a time, then add your chosen modifier to the main batch to taste.
  7. Optional binder: stir a little dissolved gum arabic into the warm ink for smoother flow and a touch more body. Skip it for a plain watery wash.
  8. Add a clove or two (or a drop of clove oil) per jar and bottle in a clean, labeled jar. Label it clearly, e.g. "INK — NOT FOOD," and store it cool and out of reach of children and pets.
  9. Test on the paper you'll actually use and let it dry. Because the fruit color is soft and likely fugitive, keep finished work out of direct sun and treat this as a sketchbook or ephemeral ink.

Variations & modifiers

The fruit's soft yellow responds to pH and iron, though gently. A pinch of baking soda (alkaline) slides it toward a muted avocado-green — the signature trick of this fruit ink. A few drops of iron acetate (steel wool steeped in vinegar) sadden it toward olive or khaki; add it a drop at a time because it works fast. Acid keeps the color on the clearer yellow side. None of these will turn a low-yield fruit extract into a bold ink — they only nudge a fundamentally soft, experimental color. Note: the strong, lightfast yellow-gold and the iron-modified mossy green that Osage orange is famous for come from the heartwood, not the fruit, so don't expect the fruit to match those.

Preservation & storage

Add a clove or two per jar (or a drop of clove oil, a splash of isopropyl alcohol, or a pinch of salt) and bottle in a sterilized jar. A little gum arabic also helps stabilize the liquid. Fruit ink is perishable — refrigerate it and use within a couple of weeks unpreserved, or a few months refrigerated with a preservative. Discard if it smells off or grows mold.

Yield

About 1/3 to 1/2 cup of soft yellow ink per batch, depending on how far you reduce it. Be honest with yourself: the fruit is a low-yield, experimental colorant, and its color is likely fugitive — the dependable lightfast Osage yellow lives in the heartwood, which this recipe deliberately leaves alone.

Beyond the ink

Fruit-on-fabric is experimental, not a reliable dye: the durable Osage colorant (morin/maclurin) is in the heartwood, and dye references state plainly that the fruit is not used in the textile process, so expect pale, blotchy results of unknown wash- and lightfastness. If you try the fruit on cloth anyway, scour it, pre-mordant with alum (roughly 10-15% weight-of-fiber), simmer long and hot, and use an iron afterbath to push toward green — but for a dependable lightfast yellow on fiber, the honest answer is responsibly-sourced Osage sawdust rather than the fruit. Whole fallen fruit is also sold as a folk indoor insect/spider repellent, though that use is anecdotal and not supported by testing.
