Hickory hull ink

Soft brown from fallen hulls

Safety
  • This is ink, not food. Don't taste or drink it, and keep it from children and pets. (Hickory nutmeats are edible, but the hull extract and any iron or preservative additives are not.)
  • Heavy staining: hull dye stains skin for days, plus clothing, counters, and grout. Wear gloves, use an apron, and cover your work surface.
  • Pet hazard from moldy hulls: fallen, moldy nut hulls can carry the tremorgenic mycotoxin Penitrem A, which causes tremors and seizures and can be fatal in dogs. Don't collect moldy hulls or let dogs eat hull scraps.
  • Use a dedicated non-food pot and utensils; tannin and iron discolor cookware and shouldn't return to food use.
  • Simmer in a ventilated area; the long simmer makes strong, earthy steam. If you make the iron-vinegar modifier, label it, keep its lid loose, and store it away from children and pets.
  • Hickory, like walnut, contains traces of juglone (toxic to many plants); pour spent dye and hulls in the trash, not onto garden beds.

About

The same shagbark hickory whose wood smokes barbecue drops tannin-rich husks every autumn that simmer down into a soft, warm brown ink — a true waste-stream pigment gathered off the ground, no tree touched. It is the gentler cousin of black walnut: lighter and less aggressive, the color of weak coffee rather than espresso.

Ingredients

  • Fallen hickory hulls (the green-to-brown four-part husk around the nut) — about 1 packed quart of hulls per 3-4 cups water · green or just-fallen hulls give the most color; dried brown ones can give only a weak tan
  • Water (tap or rain) — enough to just cover the hulls, roughly 3-4 cups
  • Iron acetate (rusty nails or steel wool steeped in vinegar) — a few drops to 1 tsp per batch, to taste (optional) · saddens the warm brown toward grey-brown or grey-black; can be made 1-2 days ahead
  • Gum arabic — about 1/4 tsp per 1/4 cup finished ink (optional) · not needed, but adds body and flow; a little honey works too
  • Whole cloves, or a splash of isopropyl alcohol or vinegar — 1-2 cloves, or 10-20% alcohol/vinegar by volume (optional) · preservative against mold; refrigeration also helps

Tools

Required: A non-food pot reserved for craft use, Heat source, Coffee filters or a tightly woven cloth, A heatproof jar or bowl to strain into, Craft-only spoon or stick, Gloves (the dye stains skin brown for days)

Optional: Hammer or mallet to crush the hulls, Fine sieve for a first rough strain, Small jars or dropper bottles for storage, Dip pen or brush for testing, Apron and a covered work surface

Steps

  1. Gather fallen hickory hulls where harvesting is permitted — occurrence on a map is not permission. Take only fallen material so living trees aren't harmed, and skip any moldy, fuzzy hulls (moldy nut hulls can carry Penitrem A, dangerous to dogs).
  2. Put on gloves, rinse off dirt, and crush or chop the hulls to expose more surface — green hulls give much more color than dried brown ones.
  3. Put the hulls in your craft pot and add just enough water to cover. Soak 24 hours to several days; longer soaking deepens the color.
  4. Bring to a gentle simmer (not a hard boil) and hold it 30 minutes for a light ink, up to a few hours for a stronger one. Ventilate — the steam has a strong earthy smell.
  5. For a deeper brown, keep simmering to reduce the liquid by about half. Expect modest color: hickory is naturally paler and more variable than walnut.
  6. Cool, then strain through a coffee filter or tight cloth into a jar, pressing the hulls. Strain again if you see grit.
  7. Optional color shift: stir in a little iron water to sadden the brown toward grey-brown or grey-black — a drop at a time, it works fast. A pinch of baking soda nudges it warmer.
  8. Optional binder: stir in a little dissolved gum arabic (or a touch of honey) for smoother flow. Skip it for a plain matte wash.
  9. Add your preservative — cloves, or 10-20% isopropyl alcohol or vinegar — and bottle in a clean jar. Label it clearly as non-food craft ink and store out of reach of children and pets.

Variations & modifiers

Hickory's color is tannin, so it behaves like other nut-hull inks. Iron (iron water) reacts with the tannins and saddens the warm brown toward cool grey-brown, taupe, or grey-black — the same reaction behind oak-gall ink; add it a drop at a time. A pinch of baking soda pushes it slightly warmer, while acid keeps or lightens it. Green, freshly fallen hulls and longer soaking give far more color than old dried hulls.

Preservation & storage

Tannin inks still mold if stored wet at room temperature. Preserve with a clove or two, or 10-20% isopropyl alcohol or white vinegar by volume, and refrigerate for best life. If iron is added, the ink keeps similarly but may keep darkening slowly in the bottle.

Yield

Roughly 1/2 to 1 cup of ink per quart of hulls, depending on reduction. Refrigerated and preserved, it keeps several months to about a year; discard if it smells off or molds. Unlike fugitive berry inks, tannin nut-hull ink is comparatively lightfast and may even darken with age, though it isn't archival and its acidity can embrittle paper over many years.

Beyond the ink

Hickory also works as a textile dye, just paler than walnut: crack and soak the hulls, then simmer the bath low for a long time (or repeat). Wool and silk take it with little or no mordant (the tannin is somewhat substantive); alum deepens the shade and improves wash- and lightfastness, while cotton and linen need a tannin-plus-alum pre-treat and still give soft beige-browns. It also serves as a homemade wood stain and a leather-friendly tannin source.
