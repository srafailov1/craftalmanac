Saved-Scrap Mushroom Stock

Broth from saved mushroom trimmings

Safety — read before you start
  • Safety first - mushroom ID: only ever use stems from mushrooms you've positively identified as edible. Foraged mushrooms can have toxic lookalikes; a wrong stem is as dangerous as a wrong cap.
  • Never use scraps from false morels (Gyromitra) or from any morel you are not certain of - their toxin (gyromitrin) extracts into the cooking liquid and is not reliably destroyed by boiling; in a stock, the broth itself becomes the hazard.
  • Morels are toxic raw - never taste raw morel or its raw rinse. Raw true-morel stems become safe through the full 45-90 minute simmer.
  • Keep the scrap bag clean: nothing rotten, moldy, or gritty. Cool finished stock quickly and refrigerate promptly to avoid bacterial growth.
  • Occurrence is not permission: a mapped morel spot isn't harvest consent, and morels should be gathered lightly.

About

The sandy, tapered stem-ends and trimmings from a mushroom cook are usually the first thing into the bin - but they're where a lot of the savory depth lives. Keep a freezer bag going all season: every clean trimming from properly-identified edible mushrooms gets tossed in until you have enough for a bag-to-pot broth, no blanching needed. A morel's hollow stem, cleaned of grit, gives one of the most prized stocks in the woods.

Ingredients

  • Clean mushroom stems and trimmings from identified edible species — 2-4 cups packed · Only stems from positively-identified edible mushrooms. Use only true morels you are certain of - never false morels (Gyromitra); their toxin extracts into the broth.
  • Water — to cover generously (~6-8 cups)
  • Aromatics (onion skins, carrot ends, celery, peppercorns, bay) — a couple handfuls (optional) · More whole-use scraps welcome here.
  • Salt — to taste at the end (optional)

Tools

Required: Freezer bag, Large pot, Fine strainer, Storage containers

Optional: Cheesecloth, Ice-cube trays for concentrated portions

Steps

  1. As you cook mushrooms through the season, save only CLEAN stems and trimmings - nothing gritty, rotten, or moldy - in a labeled freezer bag. No blanching needed.
  2. When you have a few cups, tip the frozen scraps into a pot with any vegetable-scrap aromatics.
  3. Cover generously with water, bring to a boil, then reduce and simmer 45-90 minutes until the liquid is deeply colored and savory.
  4. Raw trimmed stems from positively-identified TRUE morels are fine here - the 45-90 minute simmer fully cooks them. Never include false morels (Gyromitra) or any morel you're unsure of.
  5. Strain through a fine sieve (cheesecloth for clarity). Salt to taste.
  6. Cool quickly and refrigerate, or freeze in containers or cubes for later soups and risottos.

Variations & modifiers

A splash of the apple-scrap vinegar or a spoon of miso deepens the umami. Roast the scraps first for a darker, more caramelized stock. Reduce the strained broth by half for a concentrated 'mushroom glace' to freeze in cubes. Combine with nut-shell or corn-cob scraps for a fuller vegetable stock.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate finished stock up to ~4 days (USDA FSIS guidance for broths); freeze several months. Cool it rapidly (shallow containers or an ice bath) before storing to stay food-safe. The raw scrap bag keeps for months frozen until you cook it. Discard stock that smells off.

Yield

About 5-6 cups of stock per pot; the scrap bag itself is 'free' from trimmings you'd discard. Expect a savory, mushroom-forward broth - morel scraps punch well above their volume.

Beyond the recipe

Spent strained solids go to the compost. The reduced glace freezes into flavor bombs. Rinse/soak water is best discarded; if used, add it only at the start so it goes through the full simmer - never into finished stock. Pairs naturally with the vegetable-scrap stock habit.
