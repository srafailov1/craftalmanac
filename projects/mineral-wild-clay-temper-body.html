Tempered Wild-Clay Body

Open a sticky clay with sand or crushed grog

  • Speckled earthy stoneware

Safety — read before you start
  • Silica sand, quartz, chert, and grog are all crystalline silica — crush and screen wet or with a P100 respirator; silicosis is cumulative and irreversible.
  • Never dry-sweep silica or grog dust; wet-mop or HEPA-vacuum.
  • Never crush glazed ware of unknown origin for grog (may contain lead or barium), and never crush archaeological potsherds or artifacts — that destroys the record and is illegal on public land (ARPA); use your own scrap bisque.
  • Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped silica/quartz only with consent and never from protected land.
  • A porous tempered body is not food-safe on its own; use a tested food-safe glaze liner for anything holding food or drink.

About

The oldest fix in pottery is also the simplest: a too-sticky, over-shrinking clay is opened up by mixing in something already fired — grog — or clean sand. The ancients crushed old pots back into their bodies for exactly this. Temper reduces drying shrinkage and cracking (one test dropped drying shrinkage from 8% to 6.5% with 20% aggregate) and buys the thermal-shock resistance a pit or raku fire demands.

Ingredients

  • Processed wild clay body — 80 parts by weight · From the wild-clay processing card, at wedgeable stiffness.
  • Clean silica sand (mapped silica/quartz, crushed & screened) — 10-20 parts (optional) · Screen to a consistent mesh (~40-70). Prepare wet — silica dust causes silicosis.
  • Grog (crushed pre-fired clay/bisque) — 10-20 parts (optional) · Combine sand + grog to roughly 20% total aggregate for hand-building/pit firing. Crush only unglazed bisque or scrap from glazes you know are lead-free; never crush glazed ware of unknown origin — old/imported glazes may contain lead or barium. Never crush archaeological potsherds or artifacts (destroys the record and is illegal on public land under ARPA).

Tools

Required: Scale, Screens for sizing sand/grog, Wedging surface (plaster or canvas), P100 respirator, Water for wet processing

Optional: Mortar & pestle or jaw-style crusher for making grog (from your own scrap bisque only), Test-tile ruler, Pug mill for large batches

Steps

  1. Decide the aggregate load: ~10% for smoother throwing bodies, up to ~20-30% for hand-built or pit/raku work needing thermal-shock resistance and low shrinkage.
  2. Make grog by crushing scrap bisque or old pots — only unglazed bisque or scrap from lead-free glazes, never glazed ware of unknown origin and never archaeological potsherds — then screen to size; prepare or buy clean silica sand and screen it too. Do all crushing/screening wet or masked — this is pure silica dust.
  3. Weigh clay and aggregate. A common starting recipe is clay:sand:grog of 80:10:10 by weight (i.e., ~20% total temper).
  4. Wedge the aggregate evenly into the plastic clay until no dry pockets or streaks remain; add a little water if the mix stiffens.
  5. Rest the tempered body a day so moisture equalizes, then re-wedge before use.
  6. Fire test tiles alongside your first pieces to confirm the temper hasn't pushed the body too porous or shifted its maturing cone.

Variations & modifiers

Coarser sand/grog = more open, tooth-y body good for large forms and thermal shock, but rougher to throw. Finer temper = smoother but less shrink relief. Line-blend temper at 3%, 7%, 15%, and 20% on test tiles to find your sweet spot. Grog adds thermal-shock resistance more than plain sand and shows as speckle in the fired body. Coarse quartz sand can microcrack (dunt) through quartz inversion at stoneware temperatures — prefer grog as the coarse fraction for high-fired or flame-facing ware.

Preservation & storage

Store sealed and moist like any clay body; tempered bodies age fine. Because temper opens the body, tempered ware dries more evenly and cracks less — but stays more porous, so it usually needs a glaze liner for any liquid-holding use.

Yield

Yield ≈ input clay plus the added temper weight; 80:10:10 turns 8 lb of clay into ~10 lb of body. Expect the tempered body to feel firmer and shrink noticeably less than the untempered clay.

Beyond the recipe

Leftover screened sand and grog store indefinitely for the next batch or for a coarse pit-fire/raku body. Very coarse grog also makes a good kiln-shelf wadding and a non-slip additive for wedging bats.
