Sauteed Dandelion Spring Greens

A bitter wild spring green, blanched to tame the bite and sauteed with garlic and a squeeze of lemon.

Safety, read before you start
  • Identify the plant before you harvest. True dandelion is a flat basal rosette with no leaves on the flower stalk, hairless jagged toothed leaves whose teeth point back toward the base, a single unbranched hollow stalk carrying exactly one yellow flower head, and milky white sap that oozes from any broken leaf or stem.
  • Because you pick at the leafless rosette stage, first confirm the patch when it is in flower, or at minimum snap a leaf and verify the milky white latex, before eating any pre-flowering rosette.
  • Know the lookalikes. Cat's ear, hawkbit, and false dandelion have hairy leaves and solid branching stems carrying several heads. Sow thistle and wild lettuce grow tall leafy stems rather than a flat rosette. The rosettes of tansy ragwort and coltsfoot have lobed or ruffled basal leaves, no milky latex, and branched multi head flower stems, and can cause liver damage. If any feature does not match, or the plant lacks milky sap, do not eat it.
  • Harvest only from clean ground. Avoid roadsides, any herbicide, pesticide, or fertilizer treated turf, and areas with dog or pet traffic. Wash the leaves in several changes of water.
  • The milky sap in the stems and leaves can cause contact dermatitis in people with latex sensitivity or eczema, so rinse the sap off your hands after handling. This is external use guidance for the raw sap, not a preparation to apply to skin.
  • People with ragweed or other Asteraceae allergy may react. Reactions can include mouth tingling or stomach upset, not only skin irritation.
  • Eat in normal food amounts, the way you would any cooked leafy green. Because these are leafy greens high in vitamin K, sudden large or irregular quantities can affect warfarin or other anticoagulant control, so consistency matters. Dandelion may also interact with diuretics, lithium, and blood sugar medication. If you take any of these, check with a clinician.
  • A pin on the map shows where a plant grows, not that you may harvest there. Gather only on land you own or have explicit permission to harvest, and never in parks or preserves that prohibit collecting.
  • Take only young leaves as a light harvest and leave the taproot in the ground. Do not dig the root.
  • Prefer the cooked preparation, blanch and then sautee, which cuts bitterness and reduces contamination and pathogen exposure. If you keep any raw salad use, apply the same clean ground and wash well cautions.
  • Keep harvested greens and trimmings away from children and pets until they have been washed and identified.

About

Dandelion greens are one of the oldest wild spring vegetables, gathered from lawns and field edges across Italian, Greek, Korean, and Appalachian kitchens long before they reached the grocery aisle. In southern Italy the young leaves are cooked ripassata style, blanched first to tame their bite, then finished in olive oil and garlic. This card presents the plant purely as seasonal foraged food: a bitter green cooked much like spinach, a weedy and abundant lawn plant whose leaf harvest is low impact.

Ingredients

  • Young dandelion leaves, about 8 oz, a large colander full, packed · Picked before the plant flowers, milky white sap confirmed on a snapped leaf.
  • Extra virgin olive oil, 4 tablespoons
  • Garlic, 1 to 2 cloves, crushed
  • Lemon, half a lemon (optional) · For a squeeze at the finish.
  • Salt, to taste · Plus a pinch for the blanching water.
  • Water, a large pot · For blanching.
  • Red pepper flakes, a pinch (optional) · Optional heat with the garlic.

Tools

Required: Large pot, Colander, Saute pan, Knife, Cutting board

Optional: Salad spinner, Kitchen towel

Steps

  1. Harvest young, tender leaves in early spring before the plant flowers, when the bite is mildest. Snap one leaf to confirm the milky white sap, cut the leaves at the base, and leave the taproot in the ground.
  2. Sort the leaves, discard any flower stalks and tough older outer leaves, and wash in three or four changes of cold water until no grit remains.
  3. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil, add a pinch of salt, and blanch the leaves for 2 to 3 minutes to temper the bitterness.
  4. Drain in a colander, rinse under cold water to stop the cooking, then squeeze out as much water as you can.
  5. Warm 4 tablespoons olive oil in a saute pan over medium low heat, add 1 to 2 crushed garlic cloves, and cook about 5 minutes until softened but not browned.
  6. Add the blanched greens, toss to coat in the oil, and cook over medium heat for 3 to 5 minutes until silky and tender.
  7. Finish with a squeeze of lemon and salt to taste. Serve as a side, or fold into eggs, pasta, or beans the way you would cooked spinach.

Variations & modifiers

For younger, milder leaves you can skip the blanching and toss a small handful raw into a mixed salad, applying the same clean ground and wash well cautions. Add a pinch of red pepper flakes with the garlic for heat, or a spoonful of pasta cooking water to loosen the greens. The cooked greens also work stirred into scrambled eggs, tossed through pasta, or folded into white beans.

Preservation & storage

Best eaten fresh. Store washed and dried raw leaves in a sealed container in the refrigerator and use within 2 to 3 days. Cooked greens keep in the refrigerator for up to 3 days, or freeze the blanched and squeezed greens in a sealed bag for up to 3 months.

Yield

Serves 2 as a side
