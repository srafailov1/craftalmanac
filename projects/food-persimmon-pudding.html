Wild Persimmon Pudding

Dense spiced pudding from ripe wild fruit

  • Food map
  • Fresh
  • Intermediate
  • Dark spiced pudding
Safety
  • Only ripe fruit: unripe persimmons are intensely astringent and, eaten in quantity, tannins can rarely bind into a stomach mass (bezoar). Risk is higher for people with prior stomach surgery or delayed gastric emptying. Wait for full ripeness.
  • Rinse fallen fruit and discard any that are moldy, fermenting, or insect-infested.
  • Remove seeds and skin via mill or sieve.
  • Standard baking food-safety for the egg-and-milk custard.
  • Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

About

An American persimmon eaten a day too early will chalk your mouth with tannin so fierce you never forget it - the fruit must be so ripe it is nearly liquid inside a fragile skin, usually only after it drops. Get the timing right and the wild native (Diospyros virginiana), far smaller than its grocery cousin, bakes into a dense, dark, spiced pudding that is Indiana's unofficial state dessert. The old-timers' test still holds: pick it up off the ground, not the branch.

Ingredients

  • Ripe wild persimmon pulp (Diospyros virginiana), seeds removed — 2 cups · Only fully soft, jelly-ripe fruit - unripe is intensely astringent. Push through a mill/sieve to remove seeds and skin.
  • Sugar — 1 cup
  • Flour — 1/2-1 cup · Use less flour with the juicier native pulp.
  • Eggs — 2
  • Milk or buttermilk — 1.5 cups
  • Butter, melted — 1/4 cup
  • Baking soda + spices (cinnamon, nutmeg, clove) — to taste (optional) · Baking soda is near-universal in traditional persimmon pudding for texture and darkening.

Tools

Required: Food mill or sieve, Mixing bowls, Baking dish, Oven

Optional: Whisk

Steps

  1. Gather only ground-fallen, jelly-soft persimmons. Rinse fallen fruit and discard any that are moldy, fermenting, or insect-infested - jelly-ripe persimmons spoil quickly on the ground. Taste one: if it puckers your mouth at all, the batch is not ripe.
  2. Push the pulp through a food mill or sieve to separate the flat seeds and skin - you want smooth pulp.
  3. Whisk pulp with sugar, eggs, milk, melted butter, then fold in flour, soda, and spices.
  4. Pour into a buttered dish and bake at 325-350 F for 45-80 minutes until set and dark; it will sink as it cools (that is correct).
  5. Cool and serve with whipped cream.

Variations & modifiers

Fold in toasted black walnuts or hickory nuts from the map's nut layer. Buttermilk deepens the tang; more flour makes a firmer, cakier pudding.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate baked pudding 3-4 days or freeze. Freeze surplus raw pulp in cups for winter baking - it is the easiest way to bank a short season (NCHFP has a freezing-persimmons method).

Yield

One 8x8 pudding. Note: it can take ~100 tiny wild persimmons to reach 2 cups of pulp.

Beyond the recipe

Extra pulp becomes persimmon bread, cookies, or a ripple for the pawpaw ice cream. Seeds and skin compost.
