Rose-Hip Flesh Syrup (Seeds Removed)

Tangy vitamin-C syrup, hairs removed

  • Food map
  • Preserve
  • Intermediate
  • Warm orange-red syrup
  • Toxic — read safety
Safety — read before you start
  • Safety first - irritating hairs: rose hips contain fine internal hairs that irritate skin, eyes, and throat (historic 'itching powder'). The hazard here is mechanical irritation from the seed hairs, not poisoning. Double-strain through fine muslin and keep the hairs from going airborne; consider gloves.
  • Use a gentle heat and short cook to protect vitamin C, and don't force fibrous solids through the strainer.
  • Skip ornamental and roadside roses that may have been treated with systemic pesticides or herbicides; harvest from unsprayed wild or known-clean plants.
  • If water-bath canning, follow a tested recipe for headspace and process time; do not improvise low-acid canning.
  • Occurrence is not permission - mapped Rosa may be on managed land, and hips feed wildlife into winter, so pick lightly.

About

A rose hip is really two foods stacked together: a tart, vitamin-C-rich flesh you want, wrapped around a core of seeds and fine hairs you very much don't. Those hairs were once the active ingredient in schoolyard 'itching powder.' The whole-use move is precise separation - capture the flesh into a bright, tangy syrup and send the irritating seed core to the compost.

Ingredients

  • Ripe rose hips (after first frost is sweetest) — 4 cups · From any mapped Rosa; deep red/orange and slightly soft. Skip ornamental and roadside roses that may have been treated with systemic pesticides.
  • Water — 2 cups
  • Sugar — about 1 part sugar to 2 parts juice (up to equal parts for longer keeping) · For flavor and keeping quality.
  • Lemon juice — 1-2 tsp (optional) · Brightens color and helps stability.

Tools

Required: Knife, Saucepan, Jelly bag or fine-mesh sieve lined with muslin, Bowl, Bottles

Optional: Gloves, Food mill, Candy thermometer

Steps

  1. Work in a way that keeps the fine seed hairs out of the air and off your skin: top-and-tail the hips. You can either quarter and scoop out the seeds and hairs now, or (cleaner) cook them whole and strain the hairs out later.
  2. Simmer the hips in the water about 20-30 minutes until very soft, mashing as they cook to release the flesh and juice.
  3. Pour through a jelly bag or a muslin-lined sieve and let it drip. Then strain a SECOND time through fresh fine muslin - this double strain is what removes the irritating hairs. Don't force fibrous solids through.
  4. Measure the strained liquid, return to the pan with sugar (about 1 part sugar to 2 parts juice, up to equal parts for longer keeping) and optional lemon, and heat, stirring, until the sugar dissolves.
  5. Keep the syrup at a gentle simmer and keep the boil time short (about 5 minutes once the sugar is dissolved) to limit vitamin C loss - avoid prolonged hard boiling.
  6. Bottle and cool. To store longer, freeze in portions or water-bath can per a tested recipe (accepting some vitamin-C loss).

Variations & modifiers

A splash of the hickory syrup or a cinnamon stick makes a warmer, cordial-style syrup. Reduce further for a thick drizzle, or keep it thin for a vitamin-C cordial to dilute in water. Blend with tart mapped berries (currant, cranberry) for depth.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate a few weeks in sterilized bottles; freeze in portions for months (best vitamin-C retention). Water-bath canning works with a tested recipe (e.g. UAF's 5-minute boiling-water-bath process) but degrades vitamin C. Discard if fermented or moldy.

Yield

About 1.5-2 cups of syrup from 4 cups of hips. Expect a bright, tangy cordial-style syrup rather than a thick pancake syrup.

Beyond the recipe

The strained seed-and-hair mash goes to the compost, NOT into leather or tea (the hairs irritate). Dried de-seeded hip flesh can be powdered into a tart vitamin-C sprinkle; the rose-hip flesh also pairs into fruit leather once fully de-haired.
