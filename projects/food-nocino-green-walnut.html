Safety — read before you start
  • Alcohol only - this is a spirit for adults, not a food preserve; keep away from children. The 'toxic' flag here refers to green-hull handling and staining plus spent-hull plant/horse toxicity, not the finished, properly-alcohol-preserved liqueur, which is safe to drink in moderation for adults.
  • Juglone in green black-walnut hulls stains skin near-black for weeks and marks clothing, wood, and counters permanently - wear gloves and protect every surface.
  • Don't compost spent walnuts or pour steeping liquid onto garden soil; juglone is toxic to many plants and to horses.
  • Airborne nut proteins and the nut itself are a tree-nut-allergy hazard - keep allergic people away. Occurrence is not permission; forage only where legal.

About

Nocino is the dark, bittersweet Italian liqueur made from green, unripe walnuts picked in early summer before the shell hardens inside. You quarter the soft green nuts, steep them in high-proof spirit with sugar and spices for weeks, strain, and rest the bottle for months as the tannins mellow. Black walnuts (Juglans nigra) stand in for the Old-World nut - and the juglone in those green hulls stains everything it touches jet-black, gloves mandatory.

Ingredients

  • Green, unripe black walnuts (soft enough to cut through) — about 25-30 nuts · Pick in early summer (around late June) while a knife passes through the whole nut; juglone stains heavily.
  • High-proof neutral spirit or vodka — 1 liter (about 1 bottle) · Higher proof extracts better and preserves; many recipes start from ~190-proof spirit diluted after steeping, for stronger extraction. 100 proof works but under-extracts relative to that.
  • Sugar — 1-2 cups (to taste) · Added during or after steeping.
  • Spices - cinnamon stick, cloves, citrus zest, vanilla — to taste (optional) · Traditional aromatics.

Tools

Required: Large glass jar with lid, Nitrile or rubber gloves (juglone stains skin for weeks), Sharp knife and cutting board you don't mind staining, Fine strainer and cheesecloth, Bottles for the finished liqueur

Optional: Funnel, Apron and surface protection, Labels (date the batch)

Steps

  1. Forage green walnuts where permitted, in early summer while the nut is still soft enough to cut clean through - occurrence is not permission.
  2. Gloves on, on a protected surface, quarter the green walnuts. The cut nuts and juice stain skin, clothes, wood, and counters near-black.
  3. Pack the walnuts into a glass jar with the sugar and any spices, then cover completely with the spirit. Seal.
  4. Steep in a cool, dark place about 4-6 weeks, swirling occasionally; the liquid turns from green to deep brown-black.
  5. Strain out and discard the solids through cheesecloth. Taste and adjust sweetness.
  6. Bottle and rest at least 6 months (longer is better) to let the harsh tannins soften into a smooth, bittersweet digestivo.
  7. Serve in small amounts, neat or over ice. Adults only.

Variations & modifiers

Adjust sugar for a drier or sweeter amaro style; maple syrup gives a rounder sweetness. Traditional/commercial nocino lands around 30-40% ABV in the glass and many recipes start from higher-proof spirit (e.g. 190-proof) diluted after steeping. Citrus zest, star anise, coffee beans, or a split vanilla bean all suit it. Longer resting (a year or more) makes it noticeably smoother.

Preservation & storage

Alcohol is the preservative - no canning needed. Finished, bottled nocino keeps for years and improves with age; store sealed, cool, and dark. Because it's high-proof and sugared, spoilage isn't a concern the way it is with a water preserve.

Yield

About 1 liter of liqueur from a 1-liter spirit base.

Beyond the recipe

The same green black-walnut hulls are a classic brown dye and ink base - this liqueur and the walnut-hull ink card draw on the very same juglone-rich material, one for the still and one for the pen. Spent, strained walnuts should be discarded (not composted onto garden beds - juglone is toxic to many plants).
