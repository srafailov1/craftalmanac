Cornelian cherry preserves & sharbat

Ruby preserve and syrup from a dogwood

  • Food map
  • Preserve
  • Intermediate
  • Bright ruby-red
Safety
  • Use fully ripe (soft, deep red) fruit; underripe cornelian cherries are intensely astringent, not dangerous but unpalatable.
  • Cornelian cherry is a dogwood, not a true stone fruit; its pits are not cyanogenic, but still strain them out - they are hard and inedible.
  • For shelf-stable jars, follow a tested boiling-water jam process (1/4-inch headspace, altitude-correct times) - otherwise refrigerate or freeze.
  • Occurrence is not permission - many cornelian cherries are ornamental plantings; confirm you may harvest.

About

Cornelian cherry (Cornus mas) is a dogwood, not a true cherry - olive-shaped, brilliant red, and startlingly tart when ripe. Its flesh is among the most pectin-rich of any fruit, so a jam can set almost too fast, turning to a firm jelly if the syrup is overcooked. One pot gives you two things at once: a whole-fruit preserve (moraba) and a clear ruby sharbat syrup for cordials and soda.

Ingredients

  • Ripe cornelian cherries (soft, deep red) — 2 lb · Must be fully ripe/soft - underripe fruit is mouth-drying and very sour; pits stay whole and are strained out.
  • Sugar — 3-4 cups (to taste and set) · The fruit is very tart; higher sugar also supports shelf stability.
  • Water — 2 cups · For extracting flesh from the large pits.
  • Lemon juice — 1-2 Tbsp (optional) · Brightens and reinforces acidity; the fruit is already acidic.
  • Ground cardamom — 1/4 tsp (optional) · Traditional in Persian sharbat.

Tools

Required: Heavy non-reactive pot, Food mill, sieve, or jelly bag, Canning or storage jars with lids, Ladle and long spoon

Optional: Boiling-water canner (for shelf-stable jars), Candy thermometer, Bottles for syrup, Cheesecloth

Steps

  1. Forage only where permitted; cornelian cherry is often a planted ornamental, so confirm you may harvest.
  2. Wash the fruit and simmer with the water, covered, 20-40 minutes until the flesh softens and loosens from the pits.
  3. Press through a food mill or sieve to separate the ruby pulp/juice from the large whole pits. Discard the pits.
  4. For preserves: return pulp to the pot with sugar, bring to a boil, and cook - watching closely - just to a soft set. The pectin is very high, so stop early to avoid a hard jelly.
  5. For sharbat syrup: simmer the strained juice with sugar (roughly equal volumes) a few minutes to a pourable syrup; add cardamom and a squeeze of lemon at the end.
  6. Jar hot with 1/4-inch headspace. Refrigerate, freeze, or - for shelf-stable jam - process in a boiling-water canner per the general NCHFP jam guidance for your altitude.
  7. Dilute sharbat with cold water or soda to drink; use preserves on yogurt, cheese, or toast.

Variations & modifiers

Leave some fruit whole for a chunkier moraba-style preserve. Cook the syrup a touch longer and it sets into jelly - a feature, not a bug, if that's what you want. Blends beautifully with tart apple or quince (both high-pectin) to stretch a small harvest.

Preservation & storage

High sugar and acidity carry it: refrigerated syrup and preserves keep for months; frozen syrup keeps longer. For pantry storage, water-bath-can jars of the jam following tested jam headspace (1/4 inch) and processing times. Treat homemade syrup as fridge/freezer unless canned to a tested process. If drying strained skins/pulp into fruit leather, dry fully until leathery with no tacky/moist spots, store cool/dry, and discard if any mold appears.

Yield

Roughly 3-4 half-pints of preserves, or a preserve plus a couple cups of syrup, from 2 lb fruit.

Beyond the recipe

Sharbat syrup becomes cordials, sodas, and cocktail mixers; the fruit is also traditionally dried, pickled, or fermented. The strained skins and pulp can be dried into a tart fruit leather. Traditional across Persian, Turkish, and Eastern European kitchens.
