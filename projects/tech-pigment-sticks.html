Pigment sticks & soft pastels

Roll pigment into drawing sticks

Safety
  • Art materials, not food — label 'NOT FOOD', keep from children and pets.
  • Wear a dust mask and gloves — dry pigment and chalk make fine dust that should not be inhaled, and pigment stains skin.
  • Sticks made from toxic dye-plant pigments remain toxic — do not let children mouth them; these are not for young kids.
  • Work over a wipeable surface; clean up dust with a damp cloth, not by sweeping it into the air.

About

Mix pigment with a little gum-tragacanth (or honey) binder and a chalk extender into a stiff dough, roll it into sticks, and dry — soft pastels and pigment sticks you can draw with, straight from your own lake pigments. More binder makes a harder stick; less makes a softer, more velvety one that lays down more color.

Ingredients

  • Dry pigment — a lake pigment or earth pigment — the color base, to taste · see the lake-pigment recipe
  • Gum tragacanth (powder) OR honey — the binder — tragacanth: 1 part powder to ~30 parts water; or a 1:5 honey-to-pigment mix · tragacanth soaked in water 48 h to a gel is the classic pastel binder; honey is the pantry alternative
  • Chalk / whiting (precipitated calcium carbonate) — extender — blend with pigment to set softness and tint strength (optional) · more chalk = paler, softer, more 'pastel'; less = stronger color
  • Clean water — as needed to form a dough · distilled preferred

Tools

Required: Glass plate or tile + palette knife, Small jar for the binder gel, Paper towels (to dry the rolled sticks on), Gloves (pigment stains hands)

Optional: Mortar and pestle, Dust mask, A grooved board or stiff paper to roll even sticks

Steps

  1. Make the binder gel: stir 1 part gum-tragacanth powder into ~30 parts water, then refrigerate 48 h until it swells to a clear gel. (Or skip ahead with honey.)
  2. Blend the dry color: on the plate, mix your pigment with chalk to the tint and softness you want — more chalk for a paler, softer stick.
  3. Make a dough: make a well in the powder, add a little binder gel, and work it in with the knife, then gloved hands, adding pigment or a drop of water until you have a stiff, clay-like dough that holds a shape without crumbling or sticking.
  4. Roll sticks: roll the dough into pencil-thick sticks on a paper towel (a stiff paper or grooved board helps make them even).
  5. Dry: set the sticks on a fresh paper towel and air-dry 24-48 h, turning once, until hard all the way through.
  6. Test and adjust: if a stick crumbles in use it needs a bit more binder; if it is too hard to leave a mark, the next batch needs a touch more water or less binder.

Variations & modifiers

Binder is the main lever: more gum/honey = harder stick and less color release; less = softer, more crumbly, more pigment on the paper. Chalk both softens the stick and lightens/extends the color into tints. Set the pigment's shade before rolling.

Preservation & storage

Dried sticks keep indefinitely if stored dry and unbroken; wrap them or box them so they don't rub. Finished pastel marks are delicate — frame under glass or store flat in a folio, and use a fixative if you want to handle the work.

Yield

A tablespoon or two of pigment plus extender makes several sticks.

Beyond the ink

Turns lake pigments into dry drawing media. The same dough pressed into a shallow mold makes pigment cakes; the same pigment bound in oil and wax makes oil-pastel-style sticks (an Advanced variation). Pairs with the lake-pigment recipe.
