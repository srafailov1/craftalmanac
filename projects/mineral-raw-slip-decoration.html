Draw with the map itself, one clay on another

Safety, read before you start
  • Dry clay and oxide powders raise fine free silica and toxic metal dust, respirator (P100), mix wet where possible, wet-clean surfaces.
  • Colorant oxides (cobalt, manganese, copper, chrome) are toxic, wear gloves and a mask; keep out of food-contact glaze layers or leach-test.
  • Fire manganese- or copper-bearing slips in a ventilated kiln area, these metals volatilize as fumes during firing.
  • Grind any foraged limestone/silica additions WET and masked.
  • Unglazed slip is porous and not food-safe, glaze food surfaces with a tested clear.
  • Occurrence is not permission: confirm rights before digging body or slip clays.

About

The oldest decoration on pottery is just clay on clay: a slip, liquid clay, brushed, trailed, or combed onto a leather-hard pot so a light body shows through a dark one, or the reverse. It is the slipware of English country pottery and the mishima of Korea, and it needs no glaze chemistry at all, only a clay that shrinks at the same rate as the pot beneath it. Dig one clay for the body and another for the slip and you can draw with the map itself.

Ingredients

  • Base clay (ball clay/kaolin or wild clay), 50-90% (slip) / 25-50% (engobe) · Match the slip's shrinkage to the body; slips are higher-clay, engobes lower-clay with calcined additions.
  • Calcined clay (calcined kaolin), 0-25% (engobe) (optional) · Reduces shrinkage so the slip fits drier ware without cracking.
  • Silica (quartz/flint), 0-15% (optional) · Adjusts shrinkage and hardness.
  • Feldspar / whiting (limestone), 0-15% (optional) · Flux for adhesion and to harden the slip at temperature.
  • Colorant oxides or stains, Per colorant (see note) (optional) · Slips need more colorant than glazes, but rates differ by material: cobalt 1-4%, iron/copper ~8%, manganese 5-15%, commercial stains 10-15%.

Tools

Required: P100 respirator (for dry mixing), Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve, Brushes / slip trailer, Gloves

Optional: Rubber comb, Sgraffito tool, Bulb syringe, Banding wheel

Steps

  1. Mix a slip at 50-90% clay with water to a smooth pourable cream; sieve through 80-mesh. Wear a respirator while handling dry clay and oxides.
  2. For color, blend in oxide or stain at the right rate for the material (cobalt 1-4%, iron/copper ~8%, manganese 5-15%, commercial stains 10-15%).
  3. Test the slip's fit: brush it on a scrap of the same body at the same moisture and dry it, if it flakes or crawls, add calcined clay/silica to cut shrinkage.
  4. Apply to WET-to-leather-hard ware for best bond (high-clay slips can crack on bone-dry pots). Brush, trail, dip, or pour.
  5. Decorate: trail lines with a bulb, comb through for feathering, or carve back through for sgraffito.
  6. Dry slowly and evenly to avoid the slip pulling off at edges.
  7. Bisque, then cover food surfaces with a tested food-safe clear glaze and glaze-fire. Fire manganese- or copper-bearing slips in a ventilated kiln area, these metals volatilize as fumes during firing.
  8. Leach-test if you used copper/cobalt/manganese under a food surface.

Variations & modifiers

White slip on red body (or the reverse) is the classic contrast; combing two wet-trailed colors gives feathered slipware. Add more calcined clay/silica to apply on drier ware. Underglaze-style detail comes from higher stain loads. Wild-clay slips let the body and decoration both come from mapped material.

Preservation & storage

Store slip in a lidded container; re-sieve if it settles or smells sour (harmless, just re-blend). Unglazed fired slip is porous and decorative; glazed slip is permanent and food-safe under a sound clear.

Yield

A 1000 g dry batch of slip decorates dozens of pots, decoration uses little material. The limiting factor is fit, not quantity.

Beyond the recipe

The same slip thinned becomes casting or joining slip for handles and attachments. Wild-clay slip doubles as the starting point for a wild-clay slip glaze at higher temperatures.
