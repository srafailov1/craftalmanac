Calcining gypsum into plaster of Paris

Cook gypsum, add water back, set rock-hard in minutes

  • Minerals map
  • Craft
  • Advanced
  • Chalky white powder, sets to hard white
  • Toxic — read safety
Safety — read before you start
  • Confirm the stone is gypsum, not calcite: only gypsum alabaster (calcium sulfate) calcines to plaster; calcite alabaster (calcium carbonate) will not, and heating calcite far higher to force it decomposes it to caustic quicklime (CaO).
  • Hot burn hazard: calcining runs at ~150°C and the material and vessel stay hot — use heat gloves and eye protection.
  • SETTING PLASTER IS EXOTHERMIC — a large mass (or plaster setting on skin/in a body-part mold) can reach scalding temperatures and cause serious burns. Never cast body parts in plaster; keep pours modest.
  • Dry gypsum and plaster dust irritate eyes, nose, and lungs — wear a P100/dust mask and goggles when crushing, grinding, and mixing. Calcine in a ventilated space and never use a food-preparation oven for reclaimed/contaminated dust, which can off-gas resin or binder residues.
  • Never pour plaster slurry down drains — it hardens and clogs pipes. Let waste set solid and dispose in the trash.
  • Casual specimen collecting only: bulk gypsum is a federal salable mineral, and occurrence is not permission — use offcuts, quarry waste, or purchased stone.

About

Plaster of Paris is just gypsum that has been cooked. Heat raw gypsum or alabaster (calcium sulfate dihydrate) to around 150°C and it gives up three-quarters of its bound water, turning into the hemihydrate powder that — add water back — sets rock-hard in minutes. That single reversible reaction is the whole basis of mold-making and casting, and it closes a satisfying loop with the studio practice of reconstituting alabaster carving dust back into plaster. Note: this works only for GYPSUM alabaster (calcium sulfate) — calcite alabaster (calcium carbonate) will NOT calcine into plaster, and must not be over-heated in an attempt to force it (calcite decomposes to caustic quicklime).

Ingredients

  • Raw gypsum / alabaster (calcium sulfate dihydrate, CaSO₄·2H₂O) — As much as your vessel holds · Soft massive gypsum or alabaster carving offcuts/dust; must be GYPSUM alabaster (calcium sulfate dihydrate, soft, Mohs ~2), NOT calcite alabaster (calcium carbonate) — calcite will not convert to plaster at these temperatures and must not be over-heated to force it.
  • Heat source with controllable ~150°C — An oven, kiln, or steady low fire · Target ~150°C (300°F); hemihydrate is usable up to ~170-180°C, and true dead-burning to unusable anhydrite sets in well above that (~250°C).
  • Water (to rehydrate when casting) — ~2 parts plaster to 1 part water by weight, roughly · Added later, at casting time.

Tools

Required: Oven, kiln, or controlled fire (~150°C), Heat-safe tray/vessel, Oven thermometer, P100/dust mask + goggles, Heat-resistant gloves, Mortar/mill to re-grind clumps to powder

Optional: Kitchen scale (repeatable water:plaster ratios), Airtight storage container with desiccant, Sieve to grade the powder, Fresh gypsum vs. reclaimed alabaster dust for comparison

Steps

  1. Confirm the stone is GYPSUM alabaster (calcium sulfate dihydrate), not calcite alabaster (calcium carbonate) — calcite will not make plaster. Reduce the raw gypsum/alabaster to a coarse powder or small chips so heat penetrates evenly (mask + goggles — this makes dust).
  2. Spread it thinly in a heat-safe tray and heat to about 150°C (300°F) in a ventilated space — never use a food-preparation oven for reclaimed/contaminated dust, which can release fumes from resin or binder residues when heated. Hold it there; the mineral is 'giving off' water as vapor. Do not push toward ~250°C or you dead-burn it to unusable anhydrite.
  3. Keep it at temperature until it stops steaming/losing weight (a couple of hours for a real batch) — that signals the dihydrate has converted to hemihydrate.
  4. Cool it, then re-grind any clumps back to a fine, uniform powder. This is your plaster of Paris.
  5. Store immediately in an airtight container — fresh hemihydrate grabs moisture from the air and goes stale.
  6. To cast: sift plaster into water (roughly 2:1 by weight) until an island of dry powder just breaks the surface, let it slake ~1–2 min, mix smooth without whipping in air, pour into your mold. It heats up (exothermic) as it sets in minutes.
  7. Let the cast cure and dry fully before use. Never pour leftover plaster slurry down a drain — it sets in the pipe; let it harden in the bucket and bin it.
  8. Wear a dust mask throughout: dry gypsum/plaster dust is a respiratory irritant, and mixing releases heat.

Variations & modifiers

Time and temperature set the grade: ~150°C makes ordinary beta-hemihydrate plaster; well above ~250°C 'dead-burns' it to unusable anhydrite. Pottery-plaster and casting-plaster differ mainly in particle size and water ratio (more water = softer, more absorbent mold; less water = harder). Reclaimed alabaster carving dust calcines the same way, closing the waste loop — but verify it's clean gypsum, not calcite, and not mixed with abrasive or resin.

Preservation & storage

Store the finished powder airtight with a desiccant, cool and dry; expect weeks-to-months of usable life before it slowly rehydrates and loses set strength. Cured cast objects are stable indoors but soft and not waterproof — keep them dry; a wax or sealer helps.

Yield

Calcined gypsum loses ~20% of its weight as water, so a batch of raw stone yields somewhat less plaster powder by weight — enough to cast several small molds.

Beyond the recipe

Feeds the plaster-mold card directly (slip-casting molds, sprig molds, press molds for the clay bodies) and closes the studio loop of alabaster carving dust reconstituted into plaster. Set plaster is also a modeling/patternmaking material and a carving stone in its own right.
