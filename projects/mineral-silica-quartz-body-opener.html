Safety — read before you start
  • Crystalline silica dust from quartz/chert/flint/novaculite is the leading silicosis hazard in ceramics — grind and screen wet, wear P100, and never dry-sweep.
  • Chert, flint, and novaculite outcrops are often prehistoric quarry sites — if you see flaking debris, worked stone, or artifacts, do not collect; disturbing such sites on federal or state land violates ARPA. Collect only raw, unworked float with landowner permission.
  • Calcining/quenching quartz throws hot fragments and can spall — do it outdoors with eye protection and heavy gloves.
  • Too-coarse or too-much free quartz causes dunting (cracked ware) and cristobalite conversion; keep it fine and moderate.
  • Occurrence is not permission — collect mapped silica only with landowner consent; leave protected outcrops untouched.

About

Quartz is the quiet skeleton inside almost every clay body. Added as fine silica it fills the body, stiffens it against slumping, and — crucially — its own thermal expansion 'squeezes' the glaze on cooling to help keep it from crazing. Flint, chert, and Arkansas novaculite are all just microcrystalline SiO2; ground fine, they do the same job that bagged 'flint' does in a studio recipe.

Ingredients

  • Mapped silica: chert / flint / quartz / novaculite (SiO2) — 5-25% of dry body weight · Must be ground to a fine powder (target ~200 mesh) — coarse quartz causes dunting. Quartz is macrocrystalline SiO2; flint, chert, and novaculite are the microcrystalline forms — all behave as free crystalline silica in the body.
  • Plastic clay body (wild or ball clay) — Balance of the recipe · Supplies plasticity and alumina the pure silica lacks.
  • Water — As needed · Process everything wet to control silica dust.

Tools

Required: Jaw crusher or heavy mortar (calcining optional to ease crushing), Ball mill or muller to grind fine, 200-mesh screen, P100 respirator + wet setup, Eye protection and cut-resistant gloves for crushing, Scale

Optional: Test kiln for fit tests, Dilatometer access (advanced) or simple craze/shiver test tiles

Steps

  1. Identify the silica: clear/white quartz, banded chert/flint, or Arkansas novaculite (whetstone-grade microcrystalline quartz) all work; keep it clean of contaminants. Chert, flint, and novaculite outcrops are often prehistoric quarry sites — if you see flaking debris, worked stone, or artifacts, do not collect.
  2. Crush coarse, then grind to a fine powder. Conchoidal fracture of chert and flint throws razor-sharp flakes and flying chips during crushing — wear eye protection and cut-resistant gloves. Some potters heat (calcine) hard quartz to ~red heat and quench to make it crumble more easily — do this carefully and outdoors, mindful of steam bursts when quenching red-hot quartz.
  3. Screen to ~200 mesh; discard oversize back into the crusher. Coarse quartz particles left in the body cause cracks (dunting) on cooling.
  4. Do all crushing, grinding, and screening wet or inside a respirator — SiO2 dust is the classic silicosis hazard.
  5. Blend the fine silica into the plastic clay: start around 10% by dry weight for a filler/opener, wedging or slip-mixing to full uniformity.
  6. Make craze/shiver test tiles glazed with your standard glaze; adjust silica up (less crazing) or down (less shivering/dunting) to dial in glaze fit.

Variations & modifiers

As an 'opener' quartz reduces shrinkage and warping like grog but is finer. Line-blend 5/10/15/20/25% silica against a fixed glaze to map the craze-to-shiver window. Chert and flint behave like quartz; novaculite is prized for its extreme fineness and purity. Free quartz above ~25% risks cristobalite/dunting problems at stoneware temperatures.

Preservation & storage

Dry silica powder keeps indefinitely, sealed and dry (mask when opening). In the fired body silica is permanent; correct proportions give durable, craze-resistant ware, while excess can leave ware prone to cracking under thermal or mechanical stress.

Yield

A small test body (e.g., 2 kg) uses only 100-500 g of fine silica; grinding your own is slow, so this card is about a targeted additive, not bulk production. Expect meaningful loss to oversize during screening.

Beyond the recipe

The same fine silica is a glaze former (the primary glass-maker in most glazes) and a kiln-wash ingredient. Coarser rejected quartz becomes body temper or pit-fire aggregate. Novaculite's other life is as a whetstone — the sharpening card in the mineral set.
