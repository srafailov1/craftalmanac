Chokecherry Syrup (No Pits)

Deep red syrup from wild cherries

  • Food map
  • Fresh
  • Intermediate
  • Deep garnet syrup
  • Toxic — read safety
Safety — read before you start
  • Cyanogenic pits: never crush, blend, or grind chokecherry, pin cherry, plum, or wild-cherry stones. Juice gently and discard whole.
  • Cook the fruit; do not eat raw pits or bitter kernels.
  • Do not confuse with common buckthorn (Rhamnus cathartica) - its black berries grow singly or in clusters along twigs (not in elongated racemes) and cause severe cramping and diarrhea.
  • For shelf-stable canning use a tested extension recipe and correct water-bath time.
  • Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

About

Chokecherries pucker the mouth raw but cook into a syrup the deep red of stained glass, long a staple across the northern plains. The one firm rule is the pit: like all wild cherries the stones hold amygdalin, which frees cyanide when crushed, so you juice gently and never blend or force the seeds. A food mill turns the fruit without cracking a single pit - a blender does the opposite, which is exactly what you must avoid.

Ingredients

  • Ripe chokecherries (Prunus virginiana), stemmed — 4 cups · Do not crush the pits at any stage. Pin cherry (P. pensylvanica) works the same way.
  • Water — 3 cups
  • Sugar — 3-4 cups · Chokecherry juice is very tart.
  • Lemon juice — 2 tbsp (optional) · Brightens flavor.

Tools

Required: Large pot, Jelly bag or fine sieve, Food mill (optional)

Optional: Candy thermometer

Steps

  1. Stem and rinse the cherries. Do not blend, mash hard, or force fruit through anything that could crack the pits.
  2. Simmer whole cherries with water 20-25 minutes until soft, then rest 10 minutes.
  3. Ladle into a jelly bag and let the juice drip through on its own - do not squeeze so hard you break seeds. Discard the pits and pulp.
  4. Return the strained juice to the pot with sugar and lemon; simmer 8-12 minutes to a light syrup.
  5. Cool and refrigerate. Serve over pancakes, in soda, or diluted as a drink.

Variations & modifiers

Add cinnamon or a vanilla bean to the simmer. Increase sugar and pectin for chokecherry jelly instead of syrup. The same pit-safe method works for pin cherry and wild black cherry. Note that some published recipes mash berries with pits present - this card intentionally follows the stricter UMN no-crush method.

Preservation & storage

Refrigerate 2-3 weeks; freeze for months. Shelf-stable only with an extension-tested canning process - follow UMN Extension's tested chokecherry syrup/jelly recipe and process in a water bath. Keep the acidity/pH guidance solely within the tested-canning-recipe instructions.

Yield

About 2-3 cups of syrup from 4 cups fruit. Wild chokecherry is abundant and often overlooked.

Beyond the recipe

Once the syrup is made, the pulp and pits go to compost - never re-used, because of the seed toxin. The syrup itself glazes meats and sweetens the sumac-ade or berry shrub sodas in this collection.
