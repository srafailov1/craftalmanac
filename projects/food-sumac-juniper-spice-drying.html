Dried Sumac & Juniper Spice

Two wild spices you can air-dry

Safety — read before you start
  • Identity is critical: use only RED, upright-clustered true sumac (Rhus). Poison sumac (Toxicodendron vernix) has white, drooping fruit and causes severe skin reactions - never harvest it.
  • Rhus is in the cashew/mango family; people allergic to those can react to sumac. Wear gloves and try a small amount first.
  • Juniper: use only recognized culinary Juniperus communis. Avoid Juniperus sabina and J. oxycedrus (toxic berries) and do not use Eastern red cedar (J. virginiana) as a spice; juniper is traditionally cautioned against in pregnancy - use as a spice, sparingly.
  • Harvest on dry days; rain leaches the acidic flavor and can promote mold on the clusters.
  • Occurrence is not permission - get consent to harvest.

About

The crimson cones of true sumac (Rhus) are furred with malic acid - the tart tang behind the name 'lemonade tree.' Dried and ground, they become the tart red spice of za'atar. Juniper berries, actually tiny cones, dry into the resinous blue spheres behind gin and game marinades. Both are among the few wild spices you can simply air-dry and grind. The identity rule is absolute: true red-fruited sumac only - the white-drooping berries of poison sumac (Toxicodendron) are dangerous.

Ingredients

  • True red sumac drupe clusters (Rhus, e.g. staghorn) — As many upright red cones as you like · RED, upright clusters only - never the white, drooping fruit of poison sumac.
  • Ripe juniper berries (blue, 2nd-3rd year cones) — A handful (optional) · Use ONLY Juniperus communis (common juniper) for culinary use. Avoid Juniperus sabina and J. oxycedrus (toxic berries), and do not use Eastern red cedar (Juniperus virginiana) as a food spice.

Tools

Required: Dehydrator or airy drying space, Spice grinder or mortar, Fine sieve, Airtight jars, Gloves (Rhus is a cashew relative)

Optional: Bowl for sifting, Low oven (140 F) for a final dry

Steps

  1. Confirm true sumac: red, upright, cone-shaped fruit clusters (Rhus). Poison sumac has whitish, drooping berries and grows in wet ground - never use it. Harvest on a dry day (rain washes away the acidic flavor coating).
  2. Dry the whole sumac clusters completely - hang them or spread on screens for several days, or dehydrate low. They're ready when the drupes crumble easily.
  3. Strip the dried red fuzz from the seeds and stems. Pulse briefly in a spice grinder - just enough to free the red flesh from the hard inner seeds; don't pulverize the seeds.
  4. Sift through a fine sieve: the tart red powder falls through, the seeds and stems stay behind. Spread the powder to air-dry another day, then jar it.
  5. For juniper: dry ripe blue berries of Juniperus communis until they shrivel and harden, then store whole and crush as needed.
  6. Label and store both airtight, cool, and dark. Grind sumac in small batches as flavor is brightest fresh.

Variations & modifiers

Blend ground sumac with sesame and dried thyme/oregano for za'atar, or with salt for a finishing spice. Juniper pairs with black pepper, bay, and allspice in a game rub. Extract sumac's flavor cold (a 'sumac-ade' overnight steep in cold water) if you'd rather have a drink than a powder - hot water pulls out bitterness.

Preservation & storage

Dried whole juniper keeps 6-12+ months; ground sumac fades faster, so store whole-dried and grind as needed, or grind small batches. Keep airtight and dark. Any dampness or clumping means it reabsorbed moisture - re-dry before it molds.

Yield

Sumac is generous: several big cones yield a small jar of powder. Juniper is a season-long slow accumulation of a handful at a time.

Beyond the recipe

Sumac is also a mapped ink/dye and a tannin source - the same clusters give a warm tan-to-grey dye with iron. Spent sumac seeds and stems compost. Cold sumac steep left over from tasting makes a tart drink base or a vinaigrette acid.
