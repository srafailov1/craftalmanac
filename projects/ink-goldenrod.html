Goldenrod ink

The classic dye yellow

Safety
  • This is ink, not food. Don't taste or ingest it, and keep it from children and pets.
  • Use a dedicated non-food pot, spoon, jar, and bottle; alum and especially iron shouldn't be prepared or stored in cookware you eat from.
  • Goldenrod isn't toxic to people, but it's in the aster/daisy family and can cause allergic contact dermatitis; anyone with ragweed or Asteraceae allergies should wear gloves and a mask or skip it.
  • Alum is a mild irritant: avoid eye contact, keep it from kids, and wash hands after use.
  • The iron (steel-wool-and-vinegar) modifier is corrosive and stains; make and store it labeled in a non-food jar with its lid loose, keep it off skin and eyes, and never ingest it.
  • Work in a ventilated area while simmering; don't hard-boil. Fresh ink stains skin and surfaces — protect your work area. Occurrence on a map is not permission to harvest.

About

Goldenrod gets blamed every autumn for hay fever, but it's framed: its pollen is sticky and insect-carried, while the real culprit is the inconspicuous wind-pollinated ragweed blooming alongside it. The same showy flower tops that draw bees give one of North America's most reliable folk dyes a warm, honeyed yellow.

Ingredients

  • Goldenrod (Solidago) flowering tops, in full bloom — about 2 cups loosely packed fresh heads (or ~1 cup dried) per 2 cups water · pick heads in full bright bloom; buds give a greener, paler color
  • Water (tap or distilled) — about 2 cups, enough to just cover the flowers
  • Alum (potassium aluminum sulfate, pickling alum) — 1/4 to 1/2 tsp in the simmering pot (optional) · not needed, but makes a clearer, brighter, slightly more lasting yellow
  • Gum arabic — about 1/2 tsp per 1/2 cup finished ink (optional) · binder for flow and body; a little honey or glycerin works in a pinch
  • White vinegar and a pinch of salt — 1 tsp vinegar; a pinch of salt (optional) · help the color bind to and soak into paper
  • Whole cloves — 1 per small bottle (optional) · preservative against mold; a drop of clove oil or refrigeration also works
  • Iron acetate (steel wool in vinegar) — a few drops to 1/4 tsp, to taste (optional) · shifts the yellow to olive/sage green; can be made 1-2 days ahead

Tools

Required: A dedicated (non-food) pot, A craft-only spoon or stir stick, Strainer plus a coffee filter or fine cloth, A heatproof jar or measuring cup, A small bottle or jar with a tight lid, Scrap white paper for color-testing

Optional: A small jar to make the steel-wool-and-vinegar iron modifier ahead, Funnel for bottling, Gloves (to avoid temporary yellow staining and keep alum/iron off skin), Dropper for dosing binder and preservative

Steps

  1. Harvest goldenrod flowering tops only where harvesting is permitted, choosing heads in full bright bloom. It's abundant and resilient, but take only a small share and leave plenty for pollinators. Snip the flower fronds and discard the woody stalks. Goldenrod is in the aster family — if you have ragweed or Asteraceae allergies, wear gloves and a mask or skip it.
  2. Roughly chop or tear the flower heads into your dedicated pot and add about 2 cups water, just enough to cover. This is craft material, not food.
  3. Heat to a gentle simmer (don't hard-boil) and, if using, add 1/4 to 1/2 tsp alum to brighten and clarify the yellow. Simmer about 40-60 minutes; for a stronger ink, steep overnight and reheat briefly.
  4. Test the color on a strip of white paper. When it clearly marks the paper, it's as deep as it will get. Let it cool enough to handle.
  5. Strain through a coffee-filter-lined strainer into a clean jar, pressing the spent flowers. Compost the flowers.
  6. Optional: stir in 1 tsp white vinegar and a pinch of salt to help the color bind to and soak into paper (keep the ink slightly acidic; alkaline paper can dull the yellow).
  7. Optional binder: while the liquor is still warm, whisk in gum arabic at about 1/2 tsp per 1/2 cup. Add a touch more for thicker ink, less for a watery wash.
  8. Optional olive-green version: to part of the batch, add iron water a few drops at a time, testing on paper — the yellow shifts through gold to olive/sage. Iron is potent, so go slow; too much turns it muddy. Keep the iron version in its own bottle.
  9. Bottle in a clean (ideally sterilized) jar, drop in a clove to discourage mold, cap, and label. Store cool and dark; refrigerate to extend life. The color is fugitive — keep finished art out of direct sun.

Variations & modifiers

Goldenrod's color comes from flavonoids (quercetin, kaempferol). Alum makes the yellow clearer and brighter and helps it last a little longer. Acid (vinegar) keeps it warm and helps it bind to paper; strongly alkaline conditions dull or shift it. Iron is the big modifier: a small dose of iron water saddens the yellow to olive/sage or khaki — a few drops is enough, and overdoing it turns it muddy brown. Picking buds before full bloom yields a greener, paler yellow without any iron.

Preservation & storage

Add a whole clove or a drop of clove oil per small bottle to inhibit mold (a traditional natural-ink preservative), bottle in a sterilized jar, cap tightly, and store cool and dark; refrigerate to extend shelf life. Discard if it smells off or grows fuzz.

Yield

Roughly 1/2 to 3/4 cup of ink from 2 cups of fresh flowers. Refrigerated with a preservative it keeps several months to about a year; watch for mold. The applied color is fugitive — in sunlight it fades toward pale lemon over weeks to months, so keep finished work out of light.

Beyond the ink

Goldenrod is one of North America's classic textile dyes, but it's mordant-dependent: pre-mordant wool or silk with alum (roughly 10-15% weight-of-fiber) for clear, lasting yellows; without a mordant the color washes out. Simmer the flowering tops (don't hard-boil) and finish with an iron afterbath to shift toward olive/sage green. Cotton and linen need a tannin step plus alum to take it well. The same flowers also make a botanical watercolor and feed pollinators in a dye garden.
