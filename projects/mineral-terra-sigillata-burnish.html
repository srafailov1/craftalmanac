Terra Sigillata Burnish

A glaze substitute made of pure, mapped clay

  • Minerals map

  • Satiny burnished earth-red or ivory

Safety — read before you start
  • Dry clay is fine free silica — add clay to water, wear a P100 respirator, and wet-clean; silicosis is cumulative and permanent.
  • Sodium silicate and soda ash are alkaline — wear gloves and avoid eye contact.
  • Terra sig is NOT a vitrified, food-safe glaze — it is porous; keep it off food-contact surfaces unless sealed with a fired liner.
  • Oxide colorants (cobalt, manganese, copper) are toxic dusts raw — mask and handle wet.
  • Verify collecting rights for any wild clay — occurrence is not permission.

About

Long before glaze, Greek and Roman potters gave their ware a soft glassy shine with nothing but clay refined down to its finest particles — terra sigillata, 'sealed earth,' the gloss on ancient Greek and Roman fine ware. You let a clay-and-water slurry settle, pour off only the cloud of ultrafine platelets floating on top, brush that on in whisper-thin coats, and buff it before it dries. Burnished and fired low, it becomes a lustrous skin — a glaze substitute made of pure, mapped clay.

Ingredients

  • Ball clay (e.g. OM4) or red clay (Redart) or wild clay — 1 part dry clay · White clays give ivory sig; iron clays (Redart, wild red clay) give the classic terracotta red.
  • Water (distilled preferred) — ~1.5-2.5 parts by weight · Roughly 1.5-2.5 parts water per part dry clay depending on the clay; final consistency is set by specific gravity, not the mix ratio.
  • Sodium silicate (deflocculant) — ~0.4% of dry clay weight · Keeps fine particles suspended so the coarse grit settles out; soda ash can partly substitute. Do not substitute Calgon water softener — it does not work for terra sig.
  • Soda ash — Trace (with red clays) (optional) · Pinnell's red-clay method uses a little soda ash plus sodium silicate.

Tools

Required: P100 respirator (for dry clay), Gram scale, Tall clear jar or bucket, Soft brush, Turkey baster or siphon

Optional: Hydrometer, Smooth stone or plastic bag for burnishing, Spray bottle

Steps

  1. Weigh clay and water (roughly 1.5-2.5 parts water per part dry clay). Wearing a respirator, add dry clay to water (not the reverse) to limit dust.
  2. Dissolve the deflocculant (sodium silicate, ~0.4% of dry clay; add a little soda ash for red clays) and blend the slurry smooth.
  3. Let it settle undisturbed 12-48 hours. It separates into a bottom grit layer, a middle usable layer, and a top water layer.
  4. Siphon off the top water, then draw off ONLY the fine middle layer — that is the terra sig. Discard the heavy bottom sludge.
  5. Adjust to a thin-milk consistency (hydrometer ~1.10-1.15).
  6. Brush 2-3 whisper-thin coats onto leather-hard or bone-dry ware; over-thick sig flakes.
  7. Before the last coat fully dries, burnish with a soft cloth, plastic bag, or smooth stone for the sheen.
  8. Bisque-fire low — burnished sig keeps its stone-polished sheen best at ~cone 012-010; unburnished/polished sig survives up to ~cone 02-04, but the higher you fire the duller the shine.

Variations & modifiers

Firing temperature controls sheen: the lower the fire, the waxier and shinier. Color the sig with 2-10% oxides/stains (more than a glaze needs). Layer different-colored sigs for terra-sigillata painting. Post-fire waxing or a pit/smoke firing deepens the surface. Wild red clay makes a beautifully local terracotta sig. Deflocculant guide: about 0.5% of the dry clay weight, split as 0.25% soda ash + 0.25% sodium silicate. Yield: roughly 2 lb (900 g) of dry clay makes about a pint to a quart of usable sig; siphon only the middle fraction to a specific gravity near 1.15 (checked with a hydrometer).

Preservation & storage

Store sig in a sealed jar; it keeps for months but re-check consistency (it keeps settling). Fired sheen is permanent but porous — protect food surfaces with a proper liner glaze or keep sig to decorative/exterior areas.

Yield

One jar of settled slurry yields plenty of sig for many pots — a thin coat goes a long way. Only the middle fraction is usable, so expect to discard the grit.

Beyond the recipe

The discarded coarse settlings return to the reclaim bucket for clay body. Terra sig also makes a fine resist/underglaze medium and a smoke-fire surface for raku and pit firing.
