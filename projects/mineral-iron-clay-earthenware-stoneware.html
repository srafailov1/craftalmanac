Iron Clay: Earthenware vs. Stoneware

Fire a ladder of tiles to find your clay's cone

  • Minerals map
  • Clay
  • Advanced
  • Terracotta red to toasty brown
  • Toxic — read safety
Safety — read before you start
  • Firing unknown wild clay can bloat or melt onto the shelf — always fire test tiles on a scrap shelf or catch-tray and vent the kiln (fumes from organics and sulfur).
  • Dry clay and any crushing of overfired scrap are silica hazards — process wet or masked.
  • Do not assume an iron wild-clay pot is food-safe; earthenware needs a tested food-safe glaze liner, and no raw or crazed surface should hold food.
  • Collect clay for food-contact ware away from roadsides, industrial fill, or contaminated ground, and use a lead-free, leach-tested liner glaze.
  • Occurrence is not permission — collect the clay only with consent.

About

The same iron that stains a wild clay red is what caps how hot you can fire it: push an iron-rich terracotta past its range and it bloats, slumps, or turns to a black bubbled slag. Earthenware clays mature low (roughly cone 010-04); stonewares vitrify dense and waterproof higher (about cone 5-10). The only way to know which yours is, is to fire a ladder of test tiles and watch where it tightens up — and where it fails.

Ingredients

  • Processed iron-rich wild clay — Enough for 6-10 test tiles + trial pots · Red/brown color signals iron; iron is a flux that lowers the melting point.
  • Optional flux or temper — To taste (optional) · A little talc or whiting can nudge a body; sand/grog opens it for thermal shock.

Tools

Required: Test kiln with pyrometric cones or accurate controller, Test-tile molds or slab, Ruler + marker for shrinkage line (mark 10 cm), Scale for absorption test, Boiling setup for the fired-absorption test, P100 mask

Optional: Kiln shelf posts to catch bloating/melting drips

Steps

  1. Make identical test tiles, each with a scored 10 cm reference line; record the wet length, then the bone-dry length to get drying shrinkage.
  2. Fire tiles in steps across a range — e.g., cone 06, 04, 1, 5, 8 — placing each on a scrap shelf or in a catch-tray in case it melts.
  3. After each firing, re-measure the line for fired shrinkage: shrinkage climbs to a maximum as the body densifies, then the body swells/bloats if overfired — that peak marks maturity.
  4. Run the absorption test: weigh a fired tile, boil and soak it, weigh again; low absorption (under ~2%, ideally under 1.5%) means it is vitrifying like stoneware and is functionally watertight; 3%+ ware can still weep.
  5. Watch for failure signs: blistering, warping, or slumping mean you have passed the body's ceiling — back off to the last good cone. Black coring is different: it usually means the firing climbed too fast for carbon and sulfur to burn out — slow the bisque and ensure good oxidation rather than lowering the cone.
  6. Fire actual ware at that proven cone; glaze earthenware bodies to make them hold liquid, since they never fully vitrify.

Variations & modifiers

To extend an iron earthenware upward, blend in a more refractory light clay or reduce iron by blending. To lower a stubborn body, a touch of flux (talc, whiting, or feldspar) drops the maturing cone. Reduction firing deepens iron colors and can flux the body further; oxidation keeps brighter terracotta reds.

Preservation & storage

Unfired body stores wet and sealed. Correctly matured stoneware is waterproof and dishwasher-durable; earthenware stays porous and must be glaze-lined for utility. Overfired, bloated ware is scrap — recover only as grog.

Yield

This is a diagnostic card: a few pounds of clay yields a tile ladder plus a couple of trial pots. The payoff is knowing your clay's cone, not volume.

Beyond the recipe

Overfired, bloated, or melted tiles crush into grog and even into a raw slip glaze (some iron clays melt to a glossy brown 'slip glaze' at stoneware temperatures — the origin of Albany-type slips). Well-matured scraps become the sig and temper feedstock for other cards.
