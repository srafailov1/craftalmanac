Wild Clay: Field Test & Processing

Bend a coil around your finger to test a clay bank

Safety — read before you start
  • All clay dust is respirable crystalline silica — process wet whenever possible, wear N95/P100 when handling dry material, and never sweep dry (wet-mop or HEPA-vacuum).
  • Occurrence is not permission: a mapped deposit conveys no right to dig. Get explicit landowner or land-manager consent; digging is prohibited in most parks and protected lands.
  • Do not dig into stream banks or wetlands where it causes erosion or violates water rules; take small, backfill, and leave the bank stable.
  • White specks that fizz in vinegar are limestone — screen them out or fire higher; calcareous inclusions can cause lime-pops that spall fired ware weeks after firing.
  • Unknown wild clay can contain natural contaminants — never assume a raw wild-clay pot is food-safe until it is fully vitrified and, for food surfaces, lined with a tested, lead-free food-safe glaze.

About

Almost any exposed clay bank — a road cut, a creek meander, a builder's excavation — holds a clay that once made a local brick or roof tile. The test for whether it will hold a shape is beautifully simple: roll a coil and bend it around your finger. If it wraps without cracking, it's plastic enough to throw. Occurrence is not permission — a mapped deposit is not a dig site; get the landowner's or manager's yes before you take a bucket.

Ingredients

  • Wild clay (mapped clay deposit) — 1-2 buckets raw · Occurrence is not permission — collect only with the landowner/land manager's consent, and never in parks or protected areas.
  • Water — 2-3x the clay volume · For slaking to a milk-thin slurry.

Tools

Required: Buckets (2-3), Window screen + progressively finer sieves (window screen down to ~80-100 mesh), Stir stick or drill paddle, Plaster bat or old bedsheet on plaster/wood for drying, N95 or better dust mask

Optional: Ball mill or blender for a small test batch, Shrinkage ruler / test tiles, Test kiln access for fire tests

Steps

  1. Field test first: dig a walnut-sized lump, remove pebbles and roots, and roll a pencil-thick coil. Wrap it around your finger — no cracks means it is plastic enough to use; heavy cracking means it needs a more plastic clay blended in or is too sandy.
  2. Dry the raw clay completely — bone-dry clay slakes fastest and most fully.
  3. Slake: drop the dry lumps into a bucket of water (clay into water, not water onto clay) and let them collapse on their own for hours to a day. Stir to a smooth, milk-thin slurry.
  4. Screen out roots and gravel through a window screen, then work down through finer sieves. Wear a mask whenever the material is dry — clay dust contains respirable crystalline silica, and chronic inhalation causes silicosis.
  5. Levigate to drop sand: keep the slurry thin, let it stand ~10 minutes so sand settles, and pour or siphon the fine clay-in-water off the top, leaving the grit behind. Repeat for a smoother body.
  6. Concentrate: let the poured-off slip settle for a day or more, siphon the clear water, then dry the thick slip on a plaster bat or a sheet over plaster until it reaches wedgeable stiffness.
  7. Wedge thoroughly to remove air, then make test tiles: mark a 10 cm line on each, measure after bone-dry and again after bisque and glaze firings to learn the body's shrinkage and maturing cone.

Variations & modifiers

Too sticky (over-plastic): blend 10-30% fine sand or grog as temper (see the temper card). Too short or too sandy: levigate more and/or blend in a more plastic ball clay. White specks that fizz in vinegar are limestone — screen them out or fire higher; calcareous inclusions can cause lime-pops that spall fired ware weeks after firing. A magnet dragged through dry, screened clay pulls out coarse iron. Blend two wild clays to average out their behavior.

Preservation & storage

Store wet clay sealed in plastic to age — aging improves plasticity as bacteria develop. Dried, screened clay powder keeps indefinitely sealed and dry (mask when handling). Fire only after test tiles confirm the maturing range so ware neither stays porous nor bloats.

Yield

A 2-gallon bucket of raw bank clay typically yields 8-15 lb of usable processed body after grit and water are removed — enough for a handful of small pots. Expect real losses to sand and stones; a sandy bank can lose half its volume.

Beyond the recipe

The levigated-out sand and grit is clean temper for the next batch or for a coarser pit-fire body. The finest suspended fraction, siphoned separately, is the starting stock for terra sigillata. Iron-stained wild clay can also be dried and ground as a slip or a raw glaze colorant.
