Pawpaw Custard Ice Cream

Custard ice cream from a native fruit

  • Food map
  • Fresh
  • Intermediate
  • Pale gold custard
Safety
  • Skin and seeds are never eaten - only the ripe pulp. Some people react to the ripe pulp itself with GI upset - try a small amount first if new to pawpaw.
  • Pawpaw pulp contains annonacin (a neurotoxic acetogenin), concentrated further in the seeds and skin, and the acetogenin family has been linked to neurodegeneration with heavy, chronic consumption. Eat pawpaw in moderation — not as a daily staple — and use only the ripe pulp, never the seeds or skin.
  • Use fruit that smells sweet, not boozy or sour - discard pulp that has begun to ferment or turned deep brown/off.
  • Use fully ripe fruit; hard unripe pawpaw is unpleasant and less digestible.
  • Standard cooked-custard care: cook gently to 170-180 F, cool promptly, keep cold.
  • Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission - confirm legal access before collecting.

About

Pawpaw is the largest fruit native to North America and tastes like a banana crossed with mango and custard - which is why it never travels: the ripe pulp browns and ferments within days of falling. That short window is the whole reason to eat it fresh, folded raw into a cooked custard base so its perfume survives. Peel and seed carefully; the skin and the big brown seeds are not eaten, and some people react to the skin.

Ingredients

  • Ripe pawpaw pulp (Asimina triloba), peeled and seeded — 1.5 cups · Fully soft, fragrant fruit only - it should smell sweet, not boozy or sour. Discard skin and the large brown seeds - not eaten.
  • Egg yolks — 5 · For the cooked custard base.
  • Whole milk — 1.5 cups
  • Heavy cream — 1.5 cups
  • Sugar — 3/4 cup
  • Lemon juice — 1 tbsp (optional) · Slows browning of the pulp.
  • Vanilla — 1 tsp (optional)

Tools

Required: Saucepan, Whisk, Fine sieve, Ice cream maker

Optional: Blender, Double boiler

Steps

  1. Slice ripe pawpaws into disks, then lift the skin and pop out the large seeds; keep only the soft pulp. Discard any pulp that has begun to ferment or turned deep brown/off. Puree with the lemon juice.
  2. Make a custard: whisk yolks and sugar, temper in hot milk, and cook gently over low heat, stirring, to 170-180 F (77-82 C), until it coats the back of a spoon (do not boil).
  3. Strain the custard, cool it fully, then whisk in the cream, vanilla, and pawpaw puree.
  4. Chill the base thoroughly, then churn in an ice cream maker.
  5. Freeze until firm and serve within a few weeks for best flavor.

Variations & modifiers

Skip the eggs for a quick Philadelphia-style base (pulp, cream, sugar) if you want to keep the fruit's flavor front and center. A spoon of the elderberry or chokecherry syrup from this set makes a fine ripple.

Preservation & storage

Keep frozen; texture is best within a few weeks. Freeze extra raw pulp in bags immediately - it does not keep fresh past a couple of days.

Yield

About 1 quart of ice cream. Pawpaw fruits heavily but ripens all at once and briefly.

Beyond the recipe

Extra pulp freezes for pawpaw bread or the persimmon-style pudding. Seeds and skin are discarded (skin can irritate; seeds are not eaten).
