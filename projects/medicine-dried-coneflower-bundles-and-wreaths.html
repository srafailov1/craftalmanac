Dried Coneflower Bundles and Wreaths

Stiff stems, papery ray petals, and a dark bristled cone that keeps its shape long after the season ends.

Safety, read before you start
  • This is a decorative botanical craft only. Nothing here is eaten, brewed, or applied to the body.
  • Identify your plant before cutting. Coneflower (Echinacea purpurea) has rough, coarsely toothed lance shaped leaves and a single daisy like flower with a raised, bristly central cone. Do not confuse a young coneflower rosette with foxglove (Digitalis), whose soft, downy, finely scalloped leaves form a low basal rosette and which is dangerous to handle and to keep near food. Foxglove never forms the raised bristly cone.
  • Coneflower is in the daisy and aster family (Asteraceae). People who are sensitive to ragweed, chamomile, feverfew, chrysanthemums, or daisies can get skin irritation from the sap, or a pollen reaction while cutting, stripping, and drying. Wear gloves and long sleeves if you are sensitive.
  • Dry the bundles in a well ventilated space rather than a small closed room. Good airflow limits pollen and dust buildup indoors during the multi week dry.
  • Use cultivated material only: home grown, garden bed, or nursery bought plants. Do not wild harvest coneflower. Some wild coneflowers are protected or declining (Echinacea laevigata is federally listed), and native prairie stands should be left to set seed.
  • Map occurrence data is not harvest permission and never marks a plant as free to cut.
  • Keep finished dried arrangements out of reach of children and of pets that might chew them, and away from food surfaces. Coneflower is listed as non toxic to pets, with only minor irritation possible at worst if a pet chews it.

About

Purple coneflower has been a favorite of cut flower gardeners for generations, prized as much for its stiff seed cone as for its ray petals. Cut and hung to dry, the blooms hold their form for arrangements, wreaths, and ornamental bunches, and the bare cones make a spiky dark accent once the petals fall. This project is decorative only and makes no reference to eating, brewing, or any traditional health use of the plant.

Ingredients

  • Coneflower flowering stems (Echinacea purpurea), cultivated only, 10 to 15 firm stems per bundle · Cut from your own bed or nursery grown plants. Choose freshly open blooms on firm stems, ideally in the morning after dew dries. No roots and no bark.
  • Grapevine or wire wreath frame, 1 frame (optional) · Only needed if you are building a wreath rather than hanging loose bundles.
  • Ribbon or raffia, 1 to 2 yards (optional) · Optional finishing for a hung bundle or wreath.

Tools

Required: Sharp bypass pruners or floral snips, Rubber bands or twist ties, Garden gloves, Twine or hooks for hanging

Optional: Long sleeved shirt, Floral wire, Clear matte spray sealant

Steps

  1. Wearing gloves and long sleeves, cut in the morning once the dew has dried. Choose freshly open blooms on firm stems from your own bed or a nursery grown plant, and cut stems 12 to 18 inches long with clean pruners.
  2. Strip the lower leaves by holding a stem just below the flower and running a gloved hand down toward the cut end. Leaves hold moisture and slow the dry, so remove all of the lower foliage.
  3. Gather small bunches of about 8 to 12 stems, offsetting the heads so the cones do not crush each other. Bind the stem ends with a rubber band or twist tie rather than string, since the band keeps tension as the stems shrink.
  4. Hang the bunches upside down from twine or hooks in a warm, dark, dry, well ventilated space. Darkness holds the petal color and good airflow prevents mold, so space the bundles apart so air moves around each one.
  5. Leave the bundles undisturbed for 2 to 3 weeks, until the stems snap cleanly and the central cones feel hard and dry to the touch.
  6. For a spiky textural accent, cut the cones alone once the petals have dropped and dry them the same way, or wire short dried stems in overlapping fan shaped tufts around a grapevine or wire frame to build a wreath.
  7. Optional: a light pass of clear matte spray sealant helps hold the petals and reduce shatter. Keep finished pieces in a dry indoor spot out of direct sun.

Variations & modifiers

Cones only: wait until the petals have dropped, cut the bare cones, and dry them for a darker, spikier accent. Wreath: wire short dried stems in overlapping fan shaped tufts around a grapevine or wire frame. Color: coneflower petals fade toward tan over the months, so pair them in a mixed bundle with statice or goldenrod, whose blues and yellows hold color longest when air dried.

Preservation & storage

Fully dried bundles keep their form for a year or more in a dry indoor spot out of direct sun. Humidity softens the stems and invites mold, so avoid bathrooms and basements. A light coat of clear matte spray sealant reduces petal shatter. Store the pieces hanging or laid flat, and never box them while any moisture remains in the stems.

Yield

2 to 3 dried bundles, or one small wreath
