Everlasting Goldenrod: Drying Flowering Tops for Autumn Bundles

Loose bunches of late summer gold, hung in the dark until the stems snap crisp.

Safety, read before you start
  • Identify your plant with certainty before cutting. Goldenrod (Solidago) is a genus of many look alike species, and this project is only for common, abundant, weedy kinds such as Canada goldenrod or tall goldenrod.
  • Do not confuse true goldenrod with rayless goldenrod (Isocoma pluriflora), a different and toxic plant in another genus. True goldenrod has showy yellow ray flowers along plume shaped heads, while rayless goldenrod has small rayless flower clusters and grows mainly in the dry Southwest. If you are unsure of the plant, do not cut it.
  • Some Solidago species are rare or legally protected, for example Houghton's goldenrod and Short's goldenrod. Harvest only from large stands of the common weedy kinds, and never from solitary, unusual, or shoreline and dune plants, or from any plant you cannot confidently identify as abundant.
  • Anyone with a ragweed, Asteraceae (also called Compositae), or latex sensitivity, or with asthma, should skip this project or work carefully. Handling the flowers and leaves and breathing in dislodged pollen or fine fluff can cause a skin rash or a breathing reaction in sensitized people. Wear gloves, work in good ventilation, and put on a dust mask when stripping leaves or handling heads near the fluffy seed stage.
  • Keep finished dried bundles out of reach of pets, livestock, and small children, who might chew on dried plant material.
  • Map occurrence is not harvest permission. Public land may require permission from the land manager. Cut only a small fraction of any stand and leave most flowering heads standing, since goldenrod is a magnet for native bees and migrating butterflies late in the season.

About

Goldenrod lights up roadsides and old fields across North America from August into October, and its plumes have long been gathered for dried arrangements that hold their color into winter. This card is purely a decorating craft: cut a few flowering tops from an abundant weedy stand, hang them to dry, and use the golden bundles in wreaths and bouquets. One common misconception worth correcting up front: goldenrod is often wrongly blamed for autumn hay fever, but its heavy pollen is carried by insects rather than the wind, and the real wind pollinated culprit is ragweed, which blooms at the same time.

Ingredients

  • Goldenrod flowering tops with stem, 3 to 5 small handfuls (about 20 to 40 stems) · Cut from a large stand of common weedy goldenrod (Canada or tall goldenrod). Choose heads that are open and freshly gold but not yet gone to fluffy seed, so they hold color and shed less.
  • Garden twine, jute string, or rubber bands, 1 per bundle · Rubber bands keep gripping as the stems shrink while drying, so bundles do not slip loose.

Tools

Required: Bypass pruners or sharp scissors, A warm, dark, well ventilated space with a hook, line, or nail to hang from

Optional: Gloves, Dust mask, Airtight container or box for storing finished bundles

Steps

  1. Go out on a dry morning after the dew has burned off, and pick a large, dense stand of common weedy goldenrod. Look for plumes that are open and bright gold but have not started turning to fluffy white seed, since those hold their color best and shed the least in the house.
  2. Cut only a small fraction of the stand, taking flowering tops with 12 to 18 inches of stem and leaving most of the heads standing. Snip cleanly with pruners or scissors.
  3. Strip the lower leaves off each stem so they will not mold in the bundle, and shake each stem gently to knock off loose pollen and insects before bringing it indoors.
  4. Gather 5 to 10 stems into a small, loose bunch. Keep bunches small and airy so air moves through the middle, which prevents mold and helps the color set.
  5. Tie the base of the stems tightly with twine or wrap them with a rubber band, then hang each bunch upside down from a hook, line, or nail.
  6. Leave the bundles hanging in a warm, dark, well ventilated room for 1 to 3 weeks, longer in humid climates. Drying in the dark keeps the gold from fading. They are done when the stems snap crisply and the heads feel papery.
  7. Once fully dry, work the bundles into wreaths, swags, or dried bouquets, or simply stand them in a vase.
  8. Store any extra bundles flat or upright in an airtight container away from direct sunlight, where they keep their color for about a year.

Variations & modifiers

Mixing in a few stems cut slightly earlier, when the buds are still tight and greenish gold, gives a range of tones in a finished arrangement. For a fuller wreath, dry extra bundles and wire them onto the frame in overlapping layers.

Preservation & storage

Fully dried bundles kept in an airtight box away from direct sunlight and damp hold their gold color for roughly a year. Sunlight fades them and humidity can bring back mold, so keep them cool and dark between seasons.

Yield

3 to 5 small bundles, enough for one wreath or a few arrangements
