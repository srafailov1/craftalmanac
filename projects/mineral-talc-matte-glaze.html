Talc / Soapstone Matte Glaze

Magnesium crystals scatter light into a buttery matte

Safety — read before you start
  • Soapstone/steatite and serpentine MAY contain asbestos — unverified stone must be treated as asbestos-containing; 'verified asbestos-free' means lab-tested (PLM/TEM analysis), not visually inspected. Cut and grind WET with HEPA and a P100 respirator, never dry, or use asbestos-free ceramic-grade talc.
  • All dry glaze handling raises free silica — respirator and wet-clean; silicosis risk.
  • Under-fluxed magnesia mattes can be soft and leach or cutlery-mark — leach-test before food contact and prefer boron-fluxed bases for dinnerware.
  • Barium is sometimes used for mattes — avoid barium on food surfaces; this recipe uses MgO instead by design.
  • Dispose of soapstone grinding slurry as potential asbestos waste per local rules if the source is unverified.

About

Talc is soapstone ground to powder — the same soft, soapy stone people carve into bowls and stove liners, chemically a magnesium silicate. Load enough of that magnesium into a glaze and, on slow cooling, it grows microscopic crystals that scatter light into a soft, dry, buttery matte you want to touch. It is the mineral behind the best magnesia mattes at both cone 6 and cone 10 — but soapstone and its cousin serpentine can carry asbestos, so this is a wet-and-masked material from the very first cut.

Ingredients

  • Talc (from soapstone/steatite) — 12-20% · Use asbestos-free ceramic-grade talc (e.g., Texas-type talc sold by ceramic suppliers); unverified foraged soapstone must be treated as asbestos-containing and lab-tested (PLM/TEM) before any dry handling, and milled WET.
  • Nepheline syenite or feldspar — 25-35% · Alkali flux backbone.
  • Ferro Frit 3134 (boron flux) — 15-22% (optional) · Boron helps the MgO melt cleanly at cone 6 and improves durability/leach resistance. Verify the frit number for your recipe (3124 has alumina, 3134 does not).
  • Whiting (limestone) — 8-12% (optional) · Calcium co-flux.
  • Kaolin (EPK) — 10-15% · Alumina and suspension.
  • Silica — 8-12% · Glass former and fit control.
  • Tin oxide or zircon — 5-8% (optional) · Whitens/opacifies the matte.

Tools

Required: P100 respirator, Gloves, Goggles, Gram scale, 80-mesh sieve

Optional: Ball mill, Programmable kiln (for slow cool), Test tiles

Steps

  1. If sourcing your own soapstone, treat it as asbestos-containing until a laboratory PLM/TEM analysis proves otherwise — visual inspection cannot rule out asbestiform fibers. When in doubt, buy asbestos-free ceramic-grade talc.
  2. Any cutting/grinding of soapstone must be WET with HEPA extraction and a P100 respirator — dry soapstone dust may release asbestos fibers.
  3. Weigh a generic cone 6 talc magnesia-matte base to be tested on tiles — e.g. talc ~15 / frit 3134 ~20 / nepheline ~30 / EPK ~13 / silica ~10 / whiting ~10, plus opacifier. Treat this as an untested variant and prove it on tiles; do not assume it matches a named tested recipe.
  4. Mix into water to a smooth cream, sieve twice through 80-mesh.
  5. Glaze test tiles evenly — mattes show application flaws.
  6. Fire to cone 6 (or cone 10) and program a slow cool through the crystallization range to deepen the microcrystalline matte; a well-formulated magnesia matte is primarily chemistry-driven and should not turn fully glossy on a normal cool.
  7. Check the surface: it should be silky, not chalky. Chalky/dry means under-fluxed — add frit.
  8. Cutlery-mark and leach-test before food use.

Variations & modifiers

Slow cooling deepens the matte; magnesia mattes are chiefly chemistry-driven (high MgO). Trade some talc for dolomite to combine magnesium and calcium for a classic dolomite matte at cone 10. Colorants sit softly in magnesia mattes — cobalt goes lavender/pink, rutile breaks streaky. Keep boron/flux adequate so the matte is durable, not soft.

Preservation & storage

Store talc and dry materials sealed. Mixed glaze keeps lidded; re-sieve when settled. Fired matte is permanent; avoid acidic-food storage on any under-fluxed matte.

Yield

A 1000 g batch glazes ~15-20 pieces. Expect to tune the cooling curve; the matte is made as much in the cool as in the recipe.

Beyond the recipe

Talc is also a low-expansion body flux for craze-resistant earthenware and ovenware bodies. Carved soapstone offcuts become bowls, beads, and stove tiles; scrap grinds (WET) into more talc.
