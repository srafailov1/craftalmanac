Safety — read before you start
  • LACERATIONS: obsidian edges are microscopically sharp and cut before you feel them. Cut-resistant gloves, controlled strikes, and slow handling are essential.
  • SILICA DUST: knapping raises respirable silica primarily from chert/flint/jasper (crystalline silica); obsidian dust is amorphous glass, but a respirator is still recommended. Work outdoors/ventilated.
  • EYE INJURY: flying flakes demand wraparound eye protection at all times.
  • LEGAL — artifacts: never collect artifacts on federal, tribal, or most state land (ARPA, Antiquities Act, FLPMA).
  • LEGAL — raw natural toolstone: rules vary by agency; some BLM/NF lands allow casual collection of small amounts, but never collect in parks, monuments, or archaeological contexts. Check local rules; occurrence is never permission — buy your stone when in doubt.
  • Contain and dispose of flakes; do not leave shards in soil or trails.

About

Obsidian fractures along a conchoidal curve into an edge only a few molecules thick — sharper than surgical steel, which is exactly why it cuts you before you feel it. Knapping toolstone (obsidian, chert, flint, jasper) is an ancient craft, but toolstone outcrops are often archaeological sites: collecting artifacts on public land is a federal crime under ARPA, and even natural stone can sit in a protected context.

Ingredients

  • Knappable toolstone — obsidian, chert/flint, or jasper — A few pieces · Buy legally sold spalls. Do NOT collect from public or protected land.
  • Leather pad / thick glove — 1 · The stone rests on your leg pad; the glove protects your holding hand.
  • Cut-resistant gloves — 1 pair · Obsidian edges cut before you feel them — essential, not optional.

Tools

Required: Hammerstone or billet (antler/copper), Pressure flaker (antler/copper tip), Heavy leather leg pad and hand pad, Wraparound eye protection, Cut-resistant gloves, N95/P100 respirator

Optional: Abrader stone for platform prep, Tarp to catch flakes

Steps

  1. Confirm your stone was legally purchased — obsidian, chert, or jasper spalls from a supplier, never picked up on federal/state land.
  2. Set up outdoors or over a tarp with a leather leg pad; wear eye protection and a respirator (silica dust), plus cut-resistant gloves.
  3. Strike the edge of the core with a hammerstone at the right platform angle to detach a sharp flake — obsidian and chert both fracture conchoidally.
  4. Refine the flake's shape with a billet, then pressure-flake fine notches and edges with an antler/copper tip.
  5. Grind (abrade) platforms between flakes so strikes bite instead of crushing.
  6. Sweep every flake into the tarp — loose obsidian shards are a laceration hazard underfoot. Rinse dust rather than sweeping dry.

Variations & modifiers

Obsidian flakes cleanest and sharpest but shatters easily; chert/flint is tougher and more forgiving for beginners; jasper and agate are hardest and most stubborn. Heat-treating some cherts improves flaking, but that is a separate advanced technique.

Preservation & storage

Finished tools are stable stone. Store points so their edges do not chip against each other. Never bury or 'age' a piece in a way that could be mistaken for an artifact.

Yield

A few practice flakes and, with patience, one crude point or blade from a first session.

Beyond the recipe

Waste flakes (debitage) make excellent hide-scraping and cutting edges. Broken obsidian is also a striking inlay material — set in resin, never left loose.
