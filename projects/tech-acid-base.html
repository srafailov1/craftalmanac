Swing berry inks across the rainbow

Safety
  • These inks and modifier solutions are craft materials, not food, drink, or face/skin paint. Keep them clearly labeled — for example, label a kept jar 'INK — NOT FOOD' — and well away from children, pets, and anything you eat or drink from.
  • Elderberry's leaves, stems, bark, and raw or unripe berries are toxic — treat all of it as do-not-ingest in ink form. Pokeweed is toxic in every part, and its toxins can absorb through skin, so wear gloves whenever you handle pokeberry material or ink.
  • Washing soda (sodium carbonate) is a real irritant: it can irritate or burn skin and eyes, and the powder irritates the lungs. Wear gloves and eye protection, avoid raising dust, and dissolve it carefully.
  • Household ammonia gives off irritating fumes — use only a little, only diluted, and only with ventilation, and don't lean over the jar and inhale. Never mix or store ammonia with bleach or bleach-containing cleaners; the combination makes toxic gas. This is the single most dangerous mistake to avoid here.
  • Even baking-soda solutions are alkaline — keep them out of eyes and off broken skin, and rinse splashes promptly.
  • Berry inks stain skin, nails, clothing, counters, and grout. Work on a protected surface, wear gloves and old clothes, and wipe spills quickly.
  • If any modifier or ink gets in your eyes, flush with plenty of water for at least 15 minutes and seek medical advice; for ingestion, call Poison Control (US: 1-800-222-1222).

About

Anthocyanin berry inks — elderberry, wild grape — are living pH indicators: add a little acid and they warm to red and magenta, add a little base and they walk from purple to blue and on toward green. It's the same chemistry as the red-cabbage experiment, run on your own foraged ink. One honest catch: pokeberry isn't an anthocyanin at all (it's a betalain), so it stays stubbornly pink across the useful range and won't make a clean rainbow.

Ingredients

  • A finished anthocyanin berry ink to modify (elderberry, wild grape, or another blue/purple-berry ink) — small test pours, about 1-2 tbsp (15-30 ml) per color you want to try · the pigment that does the shifting; brewed red-cabbage water is a non-toxic stand-in for practice. Pokeberry ink barely moves — it's a betalain, not an anthocyanin
  • White vinegar (the acid) — a few drops to ~1 tsp per test pour · drives the color toward red, pink, and magenta; lemon juice or a citric-acid solution work too
  • Baking soda, dissolved in water (the mild base) — ~1/2 tsp baking soda in 2 tbsp water; add a few drops at a time · the safe base — walks the color from purple toward blue; gets you most of the blue without anything caustic
  • Washing soda / soda ash, dissolved in water (stronger base) — a pinch in water, a few drops at a time (optional) · optional and caustic — pushes further toward blue-green, but baking soda is far safer and gets most of the way; skip unless you want the deepest shift
  • Household ammonia, well diluted (strongest base) — a drop or two of dilute ammonia per test pour (optional) · optional — reaches the most extreme alkaline greens, but the fumes are hazardous; use only diluted, with ventilation and gloves. Never mix or store near bleach
  • Clean water — as needed to dilute modifiers and rinse (optional) · distilled water gives the most predictable results

Tools

Required: Several small clear jars, shot glasses, or a white palette (clear or white lets you judge color), An eyedropper, pipette, or a clean spoon tip for adding modifiers drop by drop, Nitrile or rubber gloves, White paper towel, watercolor scrap, or coffee filter to swatch-test each color as it dries

Optional: Eye protection (needed if you use washing soda or ammonia), Cheap pH test strips to see where each color sits, Toothpicks or small spoons for stirring, A notebook or chart to record drops-to-color so you can repeat a result

Steps

  1. Forage and source responsibly. Harvest the berries for your base ink only where harvesting is permitted, favoring abundant or invasive material and fallen or over-ripe fruit — a plant growing somewhere is never by itself permission to take it.
  2. Set up in a ventilated spot (open a window) on a wipeable surface and put on gloves. This is craft chemistry, not cooking — keep it away from food, drinks, kids, and pets, and label everything clearly. These berry inks are not food or skin paint.
  3. Pour equal small amounts (about 1 tablespoon) of your berry ink into several clear or white containers — one to leave untouched as a control, and one for each color you want to chase.
  4. Mix your bases first so they're ready as dilute liquids: stir about 1/2 teaspoon baking soda into 2 tablespoons water until dissolved. If you're using washing soda or ammonia, dissolve and dilute them in water too, label them clearly, and make only a small amount.
  5. To go toward red and pink, add white vinegar (or lemon juice) to one jar a few drops at a time, stirring and watching after each addition. The ink warms toward red and magenta. Stop when you like the color — more acid past that point mostly just dilutes it.
  6. To go toward blue, add the baking-soda solution to a fresh jar a few drops at a time, stirring between drops. The ink moves from purple toward blue-purple and blue. Add slowly — bases shift fast and it's easy to overshoot into a dull gray. A little fizzing is normal.
  7. For blue-green (optional), add a few drops of the dilute washing-soda solution to a fresh jar, keeping gloves on and avoiding splashes and breathing the powder. For the most extreme greens you can add a drop or two of dilute ammonia, but only with the window open. Don't lean over the jar and inhale, and never add or store anything containing bleach near it — ammonia plus bleach makes a toxic gas.
  8. After each color you like, dab a swatch onto white paper or a coffee filter and label it (which ink, which modifier, how many drops). The wet color in the jar and the dried color on paper are often different.
  9. Watch your swatches over the next hours and days and note honestly which colors hold. The acid pinks and reds are the most stable; the baking-soda and ammonia blues and greens are the least stable and tend to slide back toward purple or fade to gray, tan, or brown as they dry and meet daylight.
  10. Clean up: these solutions are dilute enough to rinse down a drain with plenty of running water. Wash craft-only tools, wipe surfaces, remove gloves, and wash your hands. Don't keep mixed base solutions long-term — make them fresh next time.

Variations & modifiers

This whole card is the modifier. Anthocyanin pigments are reversible pH indicators: acid (vinegar, lemon, citric acid) pushes them toward red, pink, and magenta — the acid end is also where berry color lasts longest — while base (baking soda is mild; washing soda is stronger; household ammonia is most alkaline) walks them from purple to blue and finally to blue-green and green. Add modifiers a few drops at a time; bases especially shift fast and overshoot into muddy gray. The shifts are largely reversible while wet — re-acidify a blue and it returns toward red — but not permanent once dry, since light, oxidation, and time degrade the pigment regardless of pH. Iron (a rusty nail or iron water) is a separate effect: it saddens berry inks toward gray-brown and black rather than making the clean rainbow, so it's not part of this technique. The key exception: pokeberry pigment is a betalain, not an anthocyanin — it stays pink-to-magenta across the entire safe acid-to-neutral range and only turns purple at strong alkali and dull yellow at extreme pH, so it won't give the elderberry/grape rainbow.

Preservation & storage

This technique is for experimenting and demonstrating, not archival color — the shifted hues are inherently fugitive. To get the most life out of a result, favor the acid (vinegar) end, which is most stable; keep swatches and inks out of direct light and away from heat; and store any retained ink in a small sealed jar in the fridge, expecting it to darken over time. A few drops of white vinegar already in a berry ink act as a mild acidic preservative and color-stabilizer. Don't count on the baking-soda, washing-soda, or ammonia blues and greens lasting — photograph or scan good results promptly, because they may not survive even a few days unchanged.

Yield

Whatever small test volumes you pour — a few tablespoons makes several color swatches. Wet, refrigerated, lightly acidified berry ink may keep days to a couple of weeks before molding or darkening; base-shifted blues and greens are the least stable and can visibly change within hours to days. Dried swatches are keepsakes and reference, not lightfast artwork — expect ongoing fading and color drift.

Beyond the ink

This is the same pH-indicator chemistry behind the classic red-cabbage science experiment, so it doubles as a hands-on demonstration of acids and bases. The acid/base trick also crosses over into textile dyeing: with anthocyanin and other pH-sensitive plant dyes, dyers shift the bath acidic for warmer reds or alkaline for cooler blue-greens, though on fiber you still need an actual mordant (such as alum) to get the dye to bond and last — the acid or base only tunes the hue, it doesn't fix the color.
