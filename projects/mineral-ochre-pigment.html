Ochre pigment from iron clay

The oldest pigment humans ever used

Safety — read before you start
  • PIGMENT DUST: dry clay pigment contains respirable crystalline silica (quartz) — chronic exposure causes silicosis. Some earths also carry manganese. Wear a P100 respirator (not just N95) for dry grinding, or grind wet and dry only the finished cake; keep it wet where possible.
  • Roasting releases fumes/dust — do it in a ventilated space, use a DEDICATED (non-food) roasting vessel, and never assume a wild earth is free of contaminants or roast it in kitchen cookware.
  • Don't ingest; keep pigment out of food areas and away from children. Wash hands and tools after handling raw egg yolk.
  • Occurrence is not permission — clay is mapped, but digging it still needs landowner/agency permission.

About

The oldest pigment humans ever used is iron-stained clay: goethite and hematite tinting a kaolin-and-silica earth into yellows, reds, and browns — the ochres of cave walls and Renaissance flesh tones alike. You can wash a lump of iron-bearing clay into fine pigment by levigation (float off the fines, settle out the grit), then bind it with egg yolk into tempera that cures to a durable film. Roasting yellow ochre drives it toward warm red.

Ingredients

  • Iron-bearing clay / ochreous earth — A few handfuls · Yellow, red, or brown iron-stained clay. Clay is a mapped material — collect only where allowed.
  • Water — Several liters · For levigation (repeated wash-and-settle separation).
  • Egg yolk (binder) — 1 yolk + a little water (about 1:1 yolk to water emulsion, adjusted to a workable consistency) (optional) · For egg tempera; a 1:1 yolk:water emulsion mixed into pigment paste.

Tools

Required: Buckets/jars for settling, Sieve or cloth strainer, Muller & slab OR mortar and pestle, P100 respirator for dry grinding (dry clay pigment contains respirable crystalline silica), Drying trays

Optional: Dedicated pan or crucible for roasting (never reused for food), Palette knife, Gum arabic (alternate binder for watercolor)

Steps

  1. Break up the dry clay and soak it in water; stir to a thin slurry.
  2. Levigate: let the coarse sand/grit settle for a minute, pour the still-cloudy fine suspension into a second container, and discard the grit.
  3. Let the fine suspension settle fully (hours to days), then pour off the clear water and dry the pigment paste into a cake.
  4. Grind the dry cake to a fine powder with a muller or mortar — wear a P100 respirator (dry clay pigment contains respirable crystalline silica), or grind wet and dry only the finished cake.
  5. To make red from yellow: roast the dry ochre in a DEDICATED pan/crucible that is never reused for food — never roast wild earths in kitchen cookware — until it shifts from yellow to warm red (hematite), then cool and regrind. Roast in a ventilated space.
  6. Bind: mull the pigment powder with egg yolk (about 1:1 yolk to water) to a smooth paint; or bind with gum arabic for watercolor. Wash hands and tools after handling raw yolk.

Variations & modifiers

Different clays give the full ochre range; roasting deepens and reddens any of them. Egg tempera cures hard and matte; gum arabic gives a re-wettable watercolor; linseed oil makes an oil paint. Levigate more times for smoother, more saturated color.

Preservation & storage

Dry pigment powder keeps indefinitely in a sealed labeled jar. Egg tempera paint must be mixed fresh (yolk spoils in a day); the dried painting is permanent and lightfast.

Yield

A cupful of raw clay yields a few tablespoons of fine pigment — enough for many small paintings.

Beyond the recipe

The same washed ochre tints limewash, casein milk paint, and gesso, and colors slip for pottery. Coarse grit rejected during levigation returns to the garden.
