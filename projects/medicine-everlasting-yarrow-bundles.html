Everlasting Yarrow Bundles

Flat papery flower heads that keep their shape and color hung to dry for weeks.

  • Medicine map
  • Dried

Safety, read before you start
  • Identify your plant with certainty before cutting. True yarrow grows about 2 to 3 ft tall with soft, finely divided, feathery, fern like leaves and a fuzzy green stem with no purple markings, and it smells sweetly herbaceous. Do not cut white flat topped clusters from tall (5 to 10 ft) plants with smooth, hairless stems bearing purple spots or blotches, which is poison hemlock (Conium maculatum) and deadly, or from plants growing in wet ground with a chambered stem base, which is water hemlock (Cicuta species) and deadly. Both can be fatal, and poison hemlock toxins can pass through skin. If you are not certain, do not cut.
  • Prefer cultivated or purchased yarrow. For this purely decorative craft, garden grown Achillea, stems from your own patch, or florist and dried goods stems remove the field misidentification hazard almost entirely.
  • The finished dried bundle is a toxic household object. The ASPCA lists yarrow as toxic to dogs, cats, and horses, with vomiting, diarrhea, drooling, and skin irritation if chewed. Hang or display arrangements out of reach of pets and small children, and sweep up any dropped flower heads and stem fragments.
  • Wear gloves while cutting and stripping leaves, especially if you are ragweed sensitive, since handling yarrow foliage can cause an Asteraceae family skin reaction. Wash your hands after handling.
  • Watch for co occurring carrot family plants such as wild parsnip, wild carrot, and giant hogweed, whose sap can cause blistering skin burns on skin later exposed to sun. Cover your skin, wash your hands after cutting, and keep sap off bare skin.
  • This is a decorative craft only. Do not burn, smudge, steam, brew, or otherwise use the dried bundles on or in the body.
  • Occurrence on the map is not harvest permission. Take only a light share of any stand, and keep wild collected stems away from children and pets until identity is fully confirmed.

About

Yarrow is a classic everlasting. Its flat clusters of tiny flowers hold their form and color for months once air dried, which is why bunches have long been hung to dry and worked into wreaths, swags, and dried bouquets. Here everlasting carries only its ordinary floristry sense, dried flowers that keep their shape, and the whole project is simply a matter of cutting, tying, and hanging. This card presents it strictly as a decorative drying and arranging craft.

Ingredients

  • Yarrow flowering stems (Achillea millefolium), flat clusters in full open bloom, 16 to 36 stems · Cut in the morning once flower heads are fully open and papery firm. Prefer garden grown, home patch, or florist stems over wild ones.
  • Natural twine or jute string, one 10 in length per bundle · Rubber bands or elastics also work well and grip better as stems shrink.

Tools

Required: Garden snips or pruners, Gardening gloves

Optional: Rubber bands or elastics, Clothespins, S hooks, or a drying rack, A dark, dry, well ventilated room or closet

Steps

  1. Once you have confirmed the plant is true yarrow (see safety), put on gloves and cut flowering stems in the morning after the dew has dried, choosing heads that are fully open and papery firm rather than soft and freshly opened. Take only a light share of any stand.
  2. Strip the lower leaves from each stem so the bottom few inches are bare. This improves air flow inside the bundle and gives the twine clean stems to grip.
  3. Gather 8 to 12 stems into a small bundle, keeping the flower heads at slightly staggered heights so air can move between them.
  4. Wrap the bare cut ends snugly with twine and tie tight, or slip on a rubber band. The stems shrink as they dry, so bind them firmly and plan to re tighten twine after a day or two, or use an elastic that keeps its grip.
  5. Hang each bundle upside down in a dark, dry, well ventilated spot, spacing bundles so air circulates around and through them. Darkness preserves the yellow and white color far better than a bright window.
  6. Leave them for about 5 to 10 days in dry conditions, longer in humid weather, checking every few days for good air flow and no clumping.
  7. The bundles are ready when the flower heads feel papery and the stems snap crisply instead of bending. Damp or bendy stems need more time.
  8. Work the finished everlasting stems into wreaths, swags, and dried arrangements, and display them out of reach of pets and children.

Variations & modifiers

Yarrow comes in white, soft yellow, and warm garden shades of pink, coral, and red. Sort each bundle by color for a clean look, or mix shades for a meadow feel. Cutting longer stems gives you more to work with in tall arrangements. Golden flowered Achillea filipendulina holds an especially strong yellow and reads well in mixed everlasting bunches.

Preservation & storage

Dried yarrow keeps its shape and color for many months, often a year or more, when kept out of direct sun, damp, and heavy dust. Store spare stems flat or upright in a dry box. Faded or dusty heads can be swapped out as arrangements age.

Yield

2 to 3 small bundles of 8 to 12 stems each
