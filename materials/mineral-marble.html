Marble (carving / statuary)

Marble (metamorphic calcite)

Marble is metamorphosed limestone, recrystallized calcite, the classic statuary stone. Finer and more uniform than limestone, it holds crisp detail and takes a high polish, and its offcuts and dust also serve gesso and plaster work.

  • In seasonYear-round
  • Used partsSculpture and relief carving, inlay, and polished accents.

Identification & lookalikes

Marble is a crystalline calcite (or dolomite) rock with a sugary, sparkling grain, usually white but often veined or colored by impurities; it is soft (calcite Mohs 3, scratched by a knife) and fizzes in dilute hydrochloric acid, the test that separates true marble from harder, silica-based 'marbles' sold in the trade (quartzite, serpentine 'verde' marbles) that do not fizz and dull tools. Unlike limestone it is fully recrystallized, so it has no visible fossils and a more even, translucent surface. No toxic lookalikes, but note that some 'marbles' sold commercially are actually serpentine, which can carry asbestos, verify a green 'marble' before dry-working it.

Habitat & range

US statuary and craft marble comes from Danby/Proctor (Vermont), Tate (Georgia), Yule (Colorado), the Tennessee marble district, and Sylacauga (Alabama). Statuary grades are near-exhausted or tightly held; source quarry scrap and reject blocks by purchase or permission. Available year-round.

In the workshop

Marble is the premier stone for figurative sculpture, relief, and inlay, cut with chisels, rasps, and rifflers and finished to a polish. It is harder and less forgiving than limestone or alabaster, and calcite's cleavage means it can bruise or fracture along crystal boundaries under a heavy blow. True calcite marble carries no crystalline-silica hazard, but chips fly and fine dust is an irritant. Ground marble/chalk is also a gesso and clay-plaster filler (see the chalk-gesso recipe); marble also takes fine incised lettering (see the marble-lettering recipe).

Field note

Fine statuary and craft marble, Danby/Proctor (VT), Tate (GA), Yule (CO), Tennessee marble, Sylacauga (AL). Statuary grades are near-exhausted or tightly held; source quarry scrap/reject by purchase or permission rather than pulling outcrop. Calcite, no silica hazard, but fine dust and chips.

Safety

Wear eye protection and a dust mask, carving true (calcite) marble throws sharp chips and raises fine calcium-carbonate dust (an irritant dust, not crystalline silica); if a green 'verde marble' is actually serpentine, treat it as a possible asbestos risk and work it wet.
