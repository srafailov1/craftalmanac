One-seed juniper

Juniperus monosperma

One-seed juniper (Juniperus monosperma) is an abundant, slow-growing juniper of the Southwest pinyon-juniper country. Its shed bark, twigs, and berries give tan and brown, and its green-needle ash is a traditional Navajo alkaline mordant. On this map it is a brown dye plus an ash mordant.

  • In seasonSep-Nov
  • Used partsShed bark, twigs, berries; green-needle ash as mordant.
  • Harvest ethicFallen Material Preferred

Identification & lookalikes

A shrubby, often multi-trunked juniper with stringy shredding bark, scale-like blue-green foliage, and small single-seeded, waxy blue-green female cones (the single seed per cone is diagnostic). Crucially, do NOT substitute the toxic ornamental savin juniper (Juniperus sabina), whose foliage and berries are rich in sabinyl acetate and related compounds and are genuinely poisonous (savin oil is a documented abortifacient and can cause severe gastrointestinal and kidney injury); one-seed juniper is simply unpalatable rather than acutely toxic, but its resinous foliage should still never be eaten. Also do not confuse the ash-mordant step with burning savin foliage — savin smoke and oil are hazardous. Consistent with the existing tags: this is a dye only, do not ingest, and confirm it is not savin juniper.

Habitat & range

Dominant across the Colorado Plateau and Southwest — New Mexico, Arizona, and adjacent Colorado, Utah, and west Texas — in pinyon-juniper woodland on dry, rocky, harsh country, much of it tribal land, national forest, or BLM ground with its own collecting rules. Shed bark, twigs, and ripe cones are gathered in fall, roughly September through November.

As ink & dye

Shed bark, dropped twigs, and fallen cones are simmered to give tan and brown of moderate lightfastness on wool, deepening with an iron dip; the berry-derived colors are the most fugitive. Separately, the ash of burned green needles is a traditional Dine (Navajo) alkaline mordant that shifts and fixes color. Favor fallen material — stringy shed bark, dropped twigs, and fallen cones — over stripping or cutting living trees, since the species is abundant but slow to recover in hard country.

Field note

Shed bark, twigs, and berries give tan and brown; the green-needle ash is a traditional Navajo alkaline mordant. Dye-only — not for ingestion; not savin juniper (J. sabina).

Safety

Dye only, never ingest — and be certain you have one-seed juniper, not the toxic ornamental savin juniper (J. sabina), whose oil is a documented abortifacient.
