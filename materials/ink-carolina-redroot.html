Carolina redroot

Lachnanthes caroliniana

Carolina redroot (Lachnanthes caroliniana) is a Southeastern coastal-plain wetland perennial whose rhizomes hold a vivid orange-red, a documented Indigenous red dye of the Southeast. It is listed here as observe-only: the color lives in the root, the plant is regarded as toxic to graze, and it grows in fragile bog habitat, so harvest is off the table.

  • In seasonJun-Sep
  • Used partsRhizome/root, observe only; do not dig wild.
  • Harvest ethicObserve Only

Identification & lookalikes

A bloodwort-family (Haemodoraceae) perennial of wet places with narrow, iris-like basal leaves and a woolly, branched flower cluster of small yellowish blooms in summer; the diagnostic feature is the rhizome, which is stained deep red-orange (the color of dried blood) when broken open. It is regarded as toxic to livestock and is not a food plant, its pigments have been linked to photosensitization (phototoxic skin/organ reactions after sun exposure) in grazing animals, so it is never a casual material. No safe-vs-deadly foraging confusion applies here because the plant is observe-only: the correct action is a photograph and a record, not a dig.

Habitat & range

A wetland obligate of the Southeastern coastal plain, bogs, wet pine savannas, pond and pool margins, seepage areas, and lakeshores. It becomes less common and is listed as threatened, endangered, or of special concern in several states toward the northern edge of its range. Flowering runs through summer (roughly June-September), but there is no harvest season here: the plant is mapped to be identified and honored, not gathered.

As ink & dye

Historically the red rhizome pigment dyed textiles and craft materials for Native peoples of the Southeastern coastal plain. Crucially, botanical references note the actual method of use for the dye has been lost, and there is no modern lightfastness testing, so any color should be assumed fugitive, there is no reliable, repeatable, low-impact way to get lasting color from it. Digging the root kills the plant and tears open slow-healing wetland habitat. For an actual usable red-brown, the map points to abundant fallen-material substitutes (e.g. one-seed juniper bark) instead.

Field note

Educational only. A documented Indigenous red dye, but the color is in the root of a wetland perennial, digging destroys the plant and its bog habitat, and the plant is toxic. Observe; do not harvest wild.

Safety

Regarded as toxic to grazing livestock (photosensitizing root pigments), do not ingest the root or any part. Observe only: do not dig or harvest this habitat-sensitive wetland plant, which is protected in parts of its range.
