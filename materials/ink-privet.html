Privet

Ligustrum

Privet (Ligustrum) is a widely naturalized invasive hedge shrub whose ripe blue-black berries once colored 18th-century French playing cards. On the ink map it gives a moody red-purple ink that lurches toward green when you add a pinch of baking soda — and every berry picked is invasive seed removed.

  • Used partsRipe berries.
  • Harvest ethicInvasive Removal

Identification & lookalikes

An evergreen-to-semievergreen shrub with OPPOSITE, dark-green glossy oval leaves, smooth grayish-tan bark, and oblong blue-black drupe fruit in clusters. Common invasive species are Chinese privet (L. sinense), European/common privet (L. vulgare), and glossy/Japanese privet (L. lucidum). The berries are toxic and not edible; do not confuse the fruit with any edible berry, and note the opposite glossy leaves and clustered blue-black drupes. The plant is toxic to dogs, cats, horses, and livestock as well as people.

Habitat & range

A non-native invasive naturalized across the eastern, southern, and parts of the western contiguous US in old hedges, forest edges, floodplains, fencerows, and abandoned lots. Ripe berries persist September through winter; frost-touched fruit often gives better color.

As ink & dye

Crush or simmer ripe black berries (about an hour) and strain for a muddy red-purple ink. pH is the main lever: alkali (baking soda/washing soda) walks it toward purple, blue, teal, and green — the historic 'sap green' trick — while acid tends to strip the color and iron saddens it to grey/grey-green. Like all anthocyanin berry inks it is fugitive, fading and browning within weeks to months of light, so keep work dark. As a wool dye it needs scouring, alum, and a pH shift, and still fades.

Field note

Berries can shift blue, purple, or green; many privets are invasive and fruits are not edible.

Safety

Toxic — do not ingest: privet berries and leaves contain terpenoid/secoiridoid glycosides that can cause mouth and throat irritation, stomach pain, vomiting, and diarrhea. Wear gloves, keep the labeled non-food ink away from children, pets, and livestock, and bag spent pulp for the trash.
