Garlic mustard

Alliaria petiolata

Garlic mustard (Alliaria petiolata) is an aggressive Eurasian invasive biennial of shaded woodlands across much of the contiguous US, edible and traditionally used as a pungent digestive green. It is a 'digestive' entry here, flagged to confirm ID, to cook (cyanide/oxalates), and to bag pulled plants because it is invasive.

Educational reference only. Historical and traditional use, not medical advice.

  • In seasonMar-Jun
  • Used partsLeaves, roots, flowers, and seeds.
  • Harvest ethicInvasive Removal

Identification & lookalikes

A biennial: first-year plants are ground-level rosettes of kidney-shaped, scalloped-edged leaves; second-year plants send up a 1-4 ft stalk with triangular, coarsely toothed leaves and clusters of small four-petaled white flowers (spring). The single most useful ID test is smell, crushed leaves smell distinctly of GARLIC. First-year rosettes are easily confused with native violets, with young lesser celandine (Ficaria verna, toxic), and with creeping charlie / ground ivy (Glechoma hederacea), but none of those has the garlic odor (and ground ivy has square mint-family stems, lesser celandine has glossy leaves and yellow buttercup flowers). If a crushed leaf does not smell clearly garlicky, do not treat it as garlic mustard.

Habitat & range

A widespread woodland invasive across the eastern, midwestern, and parts of the western contiguous US, thriving in shaded forest floors, trail edges, and disturbed woods. Leaves, flowers, and roots are gathered in spring (roughly March-June); pulling it also serves ecological restoration.

Traditional & historical use

In folk use the pungent leaves, flowers, and roots are eaten as a bitter, garlicky green (small amounts of young raw leaves, or cooked). Because the leaves release hydrogen cyanide when cut or chewed and also contain oxalates, cooking or blanching is recommended and it should not be eaten in large raw quantities; cyanide levels are highest in first-year plants. Harvesting it doubles as invasive removal, but bag and dispose of pulled plants so seeds don't spread. Educational, historical framing only.

Field note

Invasive biennial; bag and remove pulled plants where local rules allow, because seeds can spread.

Safety

Confirm the garlic smell to separate from lookalikes (including toxic lesser celandine); cook or blanch (leaves release cyanide when cut and contain oxalates); do not eat large raw amounts, especially of first-year plants. Invasive, bag pulled plants where local rules require.
