Yarrow

Achillea millefolium

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium) is an aromatic composite of dry meadows and roadsides whose feathery leaves and flat white flower clusters have deep roots in Western herbal tradition (its genus name references Achilles and battlefield wound care). It is a topical entry here and carries lookalike, pregnancy, and drug-interaction cautions.

  • In seasonMay-Sep
  • Used partsFlowering tops and leaves.
  • Harvest ethicLight Harvest

Identification & lookalikes

Grows 1-3 ft tall with soft, finely divided, ferny leaves that are hairy and strongly aromatic (sweet/spicy) when crushed, topped by dense flat-topped clusters of small white (sometimes pink) flower heads (May-September). Its safety-critical danger is that, from a distance, the flat white flower cluster resembles deadly members of the carrot family — poison hemlock (Conium maculatum) and water hemlock (Cicuta species). Tell them apart up close: yarrow's leaves and stems are HAIRY, its foliage is soft and feathery, and the plant smells sweet/spicy; the hemlocks have SMOOTH, hairless stems (often with purple blotches), lacy carrot/parsley-like foliage, true compound umbels, are usually much taller (5-10 ft for poison hemlock), and smell foul/musty or carroty. Queen Anne's lace and wild parsnip are other umbel lookalikes to know. Poison and water hemlock are among the most lethal plants in North America — if the stem is smooth or purple-blotched, or the flower is a true umbel, do NOT treat it as yarrow.

Habitat & range

Found across the contiguous US in sunny, well-drained, disturbed ground: fields, meadows, roadsides, pastures, and lawns. Flowering tops and leaves are gathered through the summer bloom (roughly May-September).

Traditional & historical use

In tradition the flowering tops and leaves are used chiefly as an external material — fresh crushed leaf as a field poultice, or dried tops infused into oils, salves, and washes for skin care. It dries and stores well, making it a practical topical to keep on hand. This map keeps it in topical use; note the flags below. Educational, historical framing only — no efficacy, dosing, or 'treats/heals' claims.

Field note

Confirm the aromatic, finely divided leaves; avoid confusing with toxic lookalikes in the carrot family.

Safety

Confirm the hairy, aromatic, soft feathery foliage to rule out smooth/purple-blotched-stemmed poison and water hemlock (deadly). Traditionally avoided in pregnancy, may cause skin photosensitivity or allergic reaction in Asteraceae-sensitive people, and may interact with medications.
