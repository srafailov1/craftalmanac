Tanoak

Notholithocarpus densiflorus

Tanoak (Notholithocarpus densiflorus) carries one of the highest bark-tannin loads of any West Coast tree; its bark steeps into a warm brown and, with iron, an iron-gall black. It is observe-only on this map: only already-dead or already-felled wood may be used, because Sudden Oak Death has killed the tree at massive scale and it is an Indigenous cultural keystone.

Identification & lookalikes

An evergreen tree of the coastal forests with hard, leathery, sharply toothed leaves that are pale/woolly beneath when young, and acorns set in a distinctive bristly (spiny-scaled) cap — hence 'tanbark oak,' though it is not a true Quercus (it is the sole Notholithocarpus). It has no toxic dye lookalike, but the real hazard is regulatory and ecological, not a poisonous twin: tanoak is the species most susceptible to Sudden Oak Death (Phytophthora ramorum), so cutting or moving live tanoak risks spreading the pathogen. Use only wood that is already dead or has been felled for other reasons, and follow SOD quarantine rules on moving bark, wood, and even soil.

Habitat & range

Native to the coastal forests of California and southwestern Oregon, in mixed evergreen and redwood/Douglas-fir stands. There is no true harvest season for a live tree here; bark for dye comes only from wood already dead or felled for other reasons, worked with attention to quarantine boundaries.

As ink & dye

Bark tannins give a warm, substantive brown on wool and silk at roughly 1:1 bark-to-fiber; met with iron they form iron-tannate and slide through gray to a deep iron-gall black — the same chemistry as centuries-old iron-gall manuscript ink, so the plain-tannin brown is moderately lightfast and the iron black is durable. The catch is the fiber, not the hue: iron-tannate is acidic, and iron accelerates acid hydrolysis of cellulose and protein, so heavily iron-dyed cloth grows brittle over time — go light on iron and neutralize after. Thickened with gum arabic, the liquor also behaves as a traditional brown-to-black drawing ink. Tanoak's industrial history was leather tanning; treat it as craft-only, with dedicated non-food tools, and honor its standing as a Karuk, Yurok, Hupa, and other Indigenous acorn staple.

Field note

Educational only. Bark gives brown to iron-black, but the tree is in steep decline from Sudden Oak Death and is a cultural acorn staple — use only already-dead or felled wood, and mind SOD quarantine rules.

Safety

Use only already-dead or already-felled wood — never cut a living tanoak — and follow Sudden Oak Death quarantine rules for moving bark, wood, and soil. Iron modifiers are staining and, used heavily, weaken the finished fiber over time.
