Oregon grape & barberry

Berberis aquifolium, B. repens, B. fremontii, B. trifoliolata

Oregon grape and barberry (Berberis/Mahonia aquifolium, B. repens, B. fremontii, B. trifoliolata) are spiny-leaved native shrubs whose ripe dusky-blue berries make a pH-shifting blue-to-purple ink. On this map the ink comes from the berries — deliberately not the bright-yellow berberine roots.

  • In seasonJul-Sep
  • Used partsRipe dusky-blue berries (not the roots).
  • Harvest ethicLight Harvest

Identification & lookalikes

Holly-like pinnately compound leaves with stiff, spine-tipped leaflets (5-9 in tall Oregon grape; more in Cascade Oregon grape, B. nervosa), dense yellow spring flower clusters, and clusters of dusty blue-black grape-like berries. Scratch a root or inner bark and it is vivid yellow — the berberine. There is no deadly lookalike; the tart blue berries are in fact edible (traditionally eaten and made into jelly and wine), so this is a safe plant to gather — the spiny, holly-like evergreen leaves distinguish it from unrelated true grapes. The berries are safe as food in moderation, but the concentrated berberine-bearing root/bark dye should not be swallowed and is very bitter.

Habitat & range

Across the western US (and B. trifoliolata in Texas/the Southwest) in dry woodlands, chaparral, foothills, and rocky slopes; the berries ripen and darken in mid-to-late summer (roughly July-September).

As ink & dye

Ripe dusky-blue berries are crushed and strained for a blue-purple ink whose hue shifts with pH (leaning pink/red in acid, blue/purple in base). The famous bright-yellow berberine dye lives in the roots and bark — but digging kills these slow-growing native shrubs, so this map uses berries only and treats the root-dye as off-limits. The berry ink is on the fugitive side; expect some shift over time.

Field note

Ripe dusky-blue berries make a pH-shifting blue-purple ink. The bright berberine yellow lives in the roots and bark, but digging kills these slow-growing shrubs — use berries, not roots.

Safety

Use berries, not roots — digging destroys slow-growing shrubs, and the concentrated root/bark berberine dye is very bitter and should not be swallowed.
