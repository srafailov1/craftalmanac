Pawpaws

Asimina triloba

Pawpaw (Asimina triloba) is the largest edible fruit native to North America, with soft custard-like pulp tasting of banana, mango, and vanilla. On this map it is a prized short-season fresh fruit behind recipes like pawpaw custard ice cream, valued precisely because ripe fruit is too perishable to ship. Only the ripe pulp is eaten; skin and seeds are not, and even the ripe pulp is best enjoyed in moderation.

Identification & lookalikes

An understory tree or thicket-forming clonal patch with large, drooping, tropical-looking leaves (6 to 12 inches, unpleasant-smelling when crushed) and smooth green fruit in clusters that ripens yellowish-green to brown and softens like a ripe avocado. The pulp holds two rows of large brown lima-bean-shaped seeds. There is no dangerous toxic tree lookalike, but the fruit itself carries hazards: the SEEDS contain toxic acetogenins (notably asimicin) and were traditionally used as an emetic and are inedible; some people react to the SKIN, especially on underripe fruit.

Habitat & range

Native across the eastern US, roughly from New York and the Great Lakes south to the Gulf and Florida panhandle and west to eastern Nebraska, Oklahoma, and Texas. It grows in rich, moist bottomlands, floodplains, stream banks, and shaded slopes as a forest-understory tree. Fruit ripens in late summer into fall, roughly August through October.

In the kitchen

Pawpaw is used only as ripe pulp: peeled, de-seeded, and folded raw into custards, ice cream, quick breads, or frozen to extend the glut. Because ripe fruit browns and ferments within days of falling, the whole craft is about speed. Fresh, gentle handling preserves the perfume, which is why it is added raw rather than heavily cooked. A note on scale: it fruits heavily but ripens all at once and only briefly.

Field note

Soft custardlike fruit from understory trees, often near streams and rich slopes.

Safety

Eat only ripe pulp, and treat pawpaw as an occasional seasonal treat rather than a daily staple. Do not eat the skin or the large seeds (seeds are emetic and contain toxic acetogenins). Some people get GI upset or a skin reaction, more so with unripe fruit. The ripe pulp also contains neurotoxic acetogenins (annonacin) that, like the related soursop/custard-apple, have been associated with atypical Parkinsonism where these fruits are eaten heavily and chronically; regulators regard ordinary, moderate consumption as safe, but heavy daily intake is not advised.

  • skin/seeds not for eating
